Infant researchers face a fundamental methodological fork: habituation methods expose each infant to stimuli until reaching an individually-determined criterion (typically three consecutive trials below 50% of baseline), while familiarization methods expose all infants to identical fixed trials regardless of individual looking patterns. This choice trades off exposure control against interpretive certainty.

Habituation Method: The primary advantage is interpretive clarity‚Äîby ensuring every included infant reaches habituation criterion, researchers can confidently interpret test-phase novelty preference as evidence of discrimination. The costs are substantial, however. First, differential exposure confounds processing speed with learning opportunity: fast habituators might see 6 trials while slow habituators see 20, meaning any group differences could reflect either cognitive ability or cumulative exposure. Second, attrition rates of 30-50% occur when infants fuss out or never reach criterion, creating sampling bias toward compliant, fast-processing infants. Third, the criterion itself (50%, three consecutive trials) is arbitrary, introducing researcher degrees of freedom.

Familiarization Method: Equal exposure enables clean group comparisons without confounding group differences (age, clinical status) with processing speed differences. However, without criterion verification, infants may enter the test phase in different preference states‚Äîsome in novelty preference, others still in familiarity preference, and some at the transition point. This makes null results inherently ambiguous: no test preference could indicate discrimination failure, insufficient familiarization, or averaging across infants in opposite preference states.

Appropriate Applications: Use habituation when establishing whether an ability exists at all (can newborns discriminate phonemes?)‚Äîinterpretability trumps exposure concerns. Use familiarization when comparing groups where equating exposure is methodologically critical, or when studying learning from stimuli where exposure duration is the independent variable. Hybrid approaches exist (familiarization-with-monitoring, habituation-with-caps) but each introduces its own compromises. Critically, the method must be pre-registered and never switched post-hoc.
