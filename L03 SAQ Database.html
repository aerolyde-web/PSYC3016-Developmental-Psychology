CORE THESIS: Twin studies constitute a "naturalistic experiment" exploiting the biological accident that MZ twins share 100% genes while DZ twins share 50%, yet both share 100% common environment. This asymmetry allows decomposition of phenotypic variance into A (additive genetic), C (common environment), and E (unique environment). The BIGGEST INTERPRETIVE HINGE: heritability (h¬≤) quantifies population-level variance, NOT individual composition ‚Äî saying "60% of my personality is genetic" is fundamentally incorrect.

‚ö° THE LOGIC: Why MZ vs DZ Comparison Works

Key insight: Common environment (C) is the SAME for both twin types ‚Üí when you subtract rDZ from rMZ, the C cancels out!
rMZ ‚àí rDZ = (h¬≤ + C) ‚àí (¬Ωh¬≤ + C) = ¬Ωh¬≤ ‚Üí Therefore: h¬≤ = 2(rMZ ‚àí rDZ)
If MZ twins are MORE similar than DZ twins, the difference must be due to the extra 50% shared genes (100% vs 50%).
LO3-A Interpreting Twin Data: Correlation vs Variation
"Explain the difference between correlation and variation in twin studies and what each reveals..."
Concept Definition Twin Study Interpretation
Correlation (r)
Twin Similarity		 Systematic LINEAR relationship between Twin 1 and Twin 2. Visualized as tightness of "cloud" around regression line. Higher r = knowing one twin's score better predicts co-twin. MZ: tight cluster (high r)
DZ: loose cluster (lower r)
Difference signals genetic influence
Variation
Spread/Distribution		 How trait values spread across measurement scale. Range (min to max) may appear similar between MZ and DZ, but distribution shape differs. MZ: extremes in pairs (if one twin very tall, co-twin also tall) ‚Üí compressed distribution
DZ: more spread (one extreme, other may be average) ‚Üí normal curve
‚ö†Ô∏è COMMON CONFUSION: High MZ CORRELATION does not automatically mean high HERITABILITY. If rMZ = 0.80 but rDZ = 0.75, then h¬≤ = 2(0.80‚àí0.75) = 0.10 (only 10%!). The small MZ-DZ difference means most similarity comes from shared ENVIRONMENT, not genes. Always check the DIFFERENCE, not just absolute values.
LO3-B The ACE Model: Decomposing Variance
"Describe the components of the ACE model and how each is estimated from twin correlations..."
Symbol Component Meaning Calculation
A (h¬≤) Additive Genetic Genetic effects that sum linearly. 1 allele = +R risk, 2 alleles = +2R risk. "Narrow-sense heritability" h¬≤ = 2(rMZ ‚àí rDZ)
C (c¬≤) Common Environment Shared environmental influences affecting BOTH twins THE SAME WAY. Family, SES, school, parenting. c¬≤ = 2rDZ ‚àí rMZ
or c¬≤ = rMZ ‚àí h¬≤
E (e¬≤) Unique Environment Non-shared influences + measurement error. Events affecting ONE twin only OR affecting twins DIFFERENTLY. e¬≤ = 1 ‚àí rMZ
WORKED EXAMPLE: Height (from lecture)
Given: rMZ = 0.96, rDZ = 0.32
h¬≤ = 2(0.96 ‚àí 0.32) = 2(0.64) = 1.28 ‚Üí PROBLEM! h¬≤ > 1 is impossible
This signals VIOLATION of additive-only assumption
Reality: Height involves DOMINANT genetic effects (D) ‚Üí use ADE model instead
e¬≤ = 1 ‚àí 0.96 = 0.04 (4% unique environment)
‚ö†Ô∏è MODEL ASSUMPTION: ACE assumes ADDITIVE genetic effects only. When h¬≤ > 1 (mathematically impossible), the calculation isn't wrong ‚Äî the MODEL is wrong. This signals dominant or recessive effects, gene-gene interactions, or gene-environment interactions. Switch to ADE model (dropping C, adding D for Dominance).

‚ö° CRITICAL CLARIFICATION: Same Event ‚â† Same Effect

Classification depends on EFFECT, not occurrence. Example: A parent dies (same event for both twins). If both react with equal grief ‚Üí COMMON environment (C). If one becomes depressed, other resilient ‚Üí UNIQUE environment (E). The KEY is whether the influence makes twins similar or different.

Age matters: Shared environment (C) is more important in CHILDHOOD (more time at home, same school). As children age, unique experiences accumulate (different friends, careers) ‚Üí E increases, C decreases.
LO2 Criticisms & Limitations of Twin Studies
"Critically evaluate the limitations of twin study designs for estimating heritability..."
Limitation Problem Consequence for h¬≤ Estimate
Equal Environments Assumption (EEA) Assumes MZ twins experience no GREATER environmental similarity than DZ. VIOLATED: MZ dressed identically, confused by others, share more friends due to appearance. INFLATES h¬≤ ‚Äî excess MZ environmental similarity absorbed into genetic component
Assortative Mating Assumes random mating. VIOLATED: People choose partners based on similar traits (ethnicity, education, values). INFLATES h¬≤ ‚Äî increases DZ genetic similarity beyond 50%, paradoxically decreasing rDZ under additive model
Population-Specificity h¬≤ is tied to the specific population studied. Most twin registries: White, middle-class, Western. LIMITED GENERALIZABILITY ‚Äî h¬≤ in affluent sample ‚â† h¬≤ in poverty (environmental variance differs)
Restricted Unique Environment Most twin pairs raised under highly similar conditions ‚Üí low variation in E INFLATES h¬≤ ‚Äî reduced E variance makes genetic variance proportionally larger
MZ Separated at Birth "Gold standard" but rare AND non-random placement (often placed with similar families). REDUCED POWER ‚Äî non-random placement confounds environmental "separation"
‚ö†Ô∏è THE ECOLOGICAL FALLACY (Most Common Exam Error):
h¬≤ = 0.60 for personality does NOT mean "60% of your personality is inherited from your parents."
h¬≤ describes variance decomposition across individuals in a specific population at a specific time ‚Äî NEVER within an individual.
"It is incorrect to say that since the heritability of personality traits is about .6, that means that 60% of your personality is inherited from your parents and 40% comes from the environment" ‚Äî Turkheimer (2000)

‚ö° EXAM TAKEAWAY: Shared Environment IS NOT Zero

Traditional twin study wisdom: "C doesn't matter." Burt (2009) overturned this for child psychopathology. 10-16% C is modest but clinically significant ‚Äî in population of 1 million children, 100,000-160,000 have symptoms attributable to modifiable family factors. ADHD is the exception ‚Äî zero shared environment, high dominant genetic effects (44%), consistent with neurobiological substrate (dopamine).
LO4-B HOW Shared Environment Influences Psychopathology: Parenting
"Explain specific parenting mechanisms that account for shared environmental effects on child psychopathology..."
Parenting Style Mechanism Child Outcome
Overprotection Prevents exposure to manageable challenges; child learns feared situations ARE dangerous ‚Üë ANXIETY (internalizing)
Validates fear, maintains avoidance
Overcontrol Prevents development of autonomy and mastery experiences ‚Üë ANXIETY, ‚Üì self-efficacy
Child never learns they CAN cope
Hostility/Aggression Models aggressive behaviour; disrupts attachment security ‚Üë CONDUCT PROBLEMS (externalizing)
Child learns aggression is acceptable

Child doesn't learn limits
‚ö†Ô∏è NOT "BAD PARENTS": Overprotection and overcontrol often feel like the RIGHT thing to do (protective instincts). These are well-intentioned behaviours with unintended consequences. The clinical implication is that family-based interventions targeting these specific behaviours can reduce the 10-16% shared environmental variance identified by Burt (2009).

‚ö° KEY CLINICAL INSIGHT

Conduct problems in childhood = BEST PREDICTOR of all common adult mental health problems (except anxiety).
This means early intervention targeting parenting (hostile, poor boundaries ‚Üí conduct) has downstream effects on multiple adult outcomes. The 15% shared environment for conduct problems represents a modifiable target with broad long-term impact.
LO3/LO4 Turkheimer's Parallel: The Missing Specification Problem
"Explain Turkheimer's observation about the parallel difficulty in specifying genetic and environmental influences..."
üí° Turkheimer's Key Insight

"There is an interesting parallel between the search for individual genes that influence behavior and the failed attempt to specify the nonshared environment in terms of measured environmental variables."


Translation: We know h¬≤ exists (twin studies show genetic influence) but can't find the specific genes (GWAS identifies only 2-3% of variance). Similarly, we know E (unique environment) matters (it's 25-40% of variance) but can't specify WHICH unique experiences actually cause the effects.


Implication: Twin studies estimate THAT genes/environment matter, not HOW they matter. The mechanism question requires different methods (L04: molecular genetics, GxE interactions, epigenetics).
