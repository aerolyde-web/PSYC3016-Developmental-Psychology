PSYC3016 - Lecture 04: Behaviour Genetics II

Overarching Question: How does the missing heritability problem—where twin studies demonstrate approximately 50% heritability for complex psychological traits yet genome-wide association studies (GWAS) identify genetic variants explaining only 2-3% of phenotypic variance, leaving 47 percentage points mechanistically unexplained—resolve through three complementary mechanisms: (1) mediation explaining HOW genetic effects operate through intermediate neurobiological pathways (genotype → neurotransmitter function → brain structure → behaviour), (2) moderation explaining WHEN genetic effects emerge by demonstrating that the same alleles produce divergent outcomes across environmental contexts as formalized through differential susceptibility theory where orchid children (NR3C1 A-allele carriers) show catastrophically poor outcomes in adversity (75% externalizing disorders without intervention) yet exceptionally good outcomes in enrichment (18% disorders with Fast Track intervention services, yielding 57-percentage-point effect) while dandelion children (GG genotype) show environmentally-stable moderate outcomes (50% disorder rate regardless of intervention, 0-percentage-point effect), and (3) epigenetic modifications—particularly DNA methylation adding CH₃ groups to cytosine bases without altering DNA sequences—that alter gene expression through blocking transcription factor access to promoter regions (visualized as bulldogs clipping onto rope impeding bug movement in the bug-on-a-stick analogy) and respond dynamically to environmental stressors (Quebec ice storm prenatal stress producing altered methylation patterns in offspring immune and insulin genes persisting into adolescence) in ways invisible to sequence-based molecular genetics yet providing the molecular substrate for gene-environment interactions?

Comprehensive Summary

The missing heritability problem describes the systematic failure of molecular genetic methods to account for trait variance identified through quantitative genetic designs, where twin studies employing the classical twin method (comparing MZ twin correlations with DZ twin correlations under equal environments assumption) consistently estimate heritability h² ≈ 0.50 across diverse psychological phenotypes including intelligence, personality, and psychopathology, yet genome-wide association studies (GWAS) scanning millions of single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) identify common genetic variants collectively explaining only 2-3% of phenotypic variance even after aggregating hundreds of small-effect loci into polygenic risk scores, creating a 47-percentage-point explanatory gap between quantitative estimates and molecular findings, with empirical consequences including endemic replication failures in candidate gene studies (5-HTTLPR serotonin transporter, MAOA monoamine oxidase, DRD4 dopamine receptor variants) that proliferated in the early 2000s yet failed to reproduce across independent laboratories due to publication bias, inadequate statistical power (typical N < 200), and uncorrected multiple comparisons, alongside frustratingly small effect sizes where individual SNPs account for less than 0.5% variance requiring samples exceeding 10,000 participants for adequate power, explained through three complementary hypotheses where (1) "wrong genes" reflects hypothesis-driven research limitations (focusing exclusively on serotonergic genes for depression when inflammation, HPA axis, and neurotrophic factors also critical), gene functionality misunderstandings (same sequence produces different proteins across tissues through alternative splicing), and gene network complexity (executive function requires coordinated effects across prefrontal development genes, dopamine signalling genes, synaptic plasticity genes producing epistatic interactions invisible to single-gene analyses), (2) "wrong samples" emphasizes phenotypic heterogeneity where convergent phenotypes like impulsivity arise through distinct aetiological pathways (ADHD neurodevelopmental deficits, antisocial personality reward hypersensitivity, traumatic brain injury acquired damage, substance use altered reinforcement)—aggregating mechanistically distinct groups into single "high impulsivity" samples guarantees genetic heterogeneity diluting associations toward null—compounded by ethnic stratification (allele frequencies varying across populations due to evolutionary selection) and developmental timing issues (chronic infection versus congenital hearing loss appearing identical at age 3 yet requiring longitudinal follow-up distinguishing environmental versus genetic aetiologies), and (3) interactions and epigenetics revealing genetic effects invisible to main-effects analyses yet emerging powerfully when analysing mechanistic pathways or environmental dependencies. The critical distinction between mediation and moderation—the single biggest hinge students mishandle—centres on causal questions each addresses: mediation asks "HOW or WHY does the independent variable affect the dependent variable?" by identifying intermediate mechanisms transmitting effects along causal chains where X causes M which causes Y (sequential causation with temporal requirements that M occurs after X but before Y), while moderation asks "WHEN or UNDER WHAT CONDITIONS does the independent variable affect the dependent variable?" by identifying boundary conditions that amplify, attenuate, or reverse effects where X's impact on Y depends on level of Z (conditional causation with no temporal sequence requirements), operationalized through mediation example where homework → practice papers → exam performance creates sequential pathway (homework increases practice, practice improves exams, homework's exam benefit diminishes when controlling for practice) answering HOW homework affects outcomes through intermediate mechanism, contrasting with moderation example where negative social contacts × drinking-to-cope motivation → alcohol use demonstrates conditional effects because stress-drinking relationship shows steep positive slope for high-coping individuals (increasing from 0.25 drinks to 1.90 drinks as social stress rises) yet flat slope for low-coping individuals (remaining at 0.25 drinks regardless of stress), creating non-parallel lines indicating effect depends on moderator level answering WHEN stress triggers drinking (only when individuals endorse coping motivations), with behaviour genetics applications where mediation identifies neurobiological pathways linking genotypes to phenotypes (5-HTT gene variant → reduced serotonin transporter expression → altered amygdala reactivity → anxiety disorders explains HOW genetic effects operate) while moderation identifies gene-environment interactions (5-HTT variant × stressful life events → depression explains WHEN genetic vulnerability manifests). Differential susceptibility theory (Ellis et al., 2011) reframes behaviour genetics' most influential moderation framework by proposing individuals differ genetically not in vulnerability versus resilience but in environmental sensitivity, where some individuals respond strongly to both negative and positive environmental inputs (highly susceptible "orchid" children) while others show stable outcomes across diverse environments (low susceptibility "dandelion" children), fundamentally challenging traditional diathesis-stress models conceptualizing genetic variants as "risk alleles" predisposing to psychopathology under adversity by reframing these as "plasticity alleles" conferring heightened sensitivity to environmental quality generally—the same genetic profile producing catastrophic outcomes in adverse environments (abuse, neglect, poverty, chaos) produces superior outcomes in supportive environments (stable attachment, cognitive enrichment, intervention services), captured through the orchid-dandelion botanical metaphor where orchids wither in harsh conditions yet flourish spectacularly in meticulously maintained environments while dandelions grow adequately across diverse conditions showing neither catastrophic failure in adversity nor exceptional success in ideal circumstances, translating to human development where orchid children carrying plasticity alleles show dramatically divergent outcomes (worst in adversity, best in enrichment) while dandelion children carrying stable alleles show consistent moderate outcomes (resilience to adversity but insensitivity to intervention benefits). The Fast Track Randomized Control Trial (Albert et al., 2015) provides developmental psychology's most compelling empirical demonstration of gene-environment interactions by recruiting high-risk first-graders from low-SES neighborhoods then randomly assigning participants to intensive intervention (parent training, social skills curricula, academic tutoring, peer interventions, home visiting across multiple years) versus control conditions, with genotyping at NR3C1 glucocorticoid receptor gene (three groups: GG homozygotes common allele, AG heterozygotes, AA homozygotes rare allele) revealing predicted crossover interaction where GG homozygotes (dandelion genotype) showed no intervention effect—50% developed externalizing disorders by age 25 in both control and intervention conditions (0-percentage-point effect indicating environmental insensitivity)—while A-allele carriers (orchid genotype combining AG and AA groups) showed dramatic intervention effects—75% of control-condition A-carriers developed disorders (markedly worse than GG controls indicating genetic vulnerability under adversity) yet only 18% of intervention-condition A-carriers developed disorders (markedly better than all other groups indicating genetic advantage under support), demonstrating that intervention benefit magnitude differed by 57 percentage points between A-carriers (57% reduction: 75% to 18% representing 76% relative risk reduction) versus GG carriers (0% reduction: 50% to 50%), establishing that genetic variation moderated intervention effectiveness rather than representing fixed risk, with the crossover pattern—A-allele predicting worst outcomes in control yet best outcomes in intervention—exemplifying differential susceptibility's "for better and for worse" sensitivity profile contrasting with diathesis-stress models predicting vulnerability without corresponding advantage, raising profound ethical questions about resource allocation where genotype-targeted interventions maximizing return-on-investment (delivering 57-point reductions for responsive orchid children while withholding services from non-responsive dandelion children) create troubling scenarios involving genetic determinism (GG children receiving reduced services based on probabilistic predictions), genetic discrimination (allele status determining service access), and missed opportunities (dandelions might respond to alternative untested interventions). Epigenetics encompasses functionally relevant changes to the genome that do not involve alterations in underlying nucleotide sequences (ATCG base pairs), formally defined as "dynamic alterations in the transcriptional potential of a cell"—changes in whether, when, and how much genes are expressed without changing genetic code itself, distinguishing epigenetic modifications from genetic mutations where mutations change DNA sequences (cytosine → thymine substitution) permanently altering encoded information across cell divisions, while epigenetic modifications change DNA accessibility and transcriptional machinery binding without altering sequences, potentially reversing under different environmental conditions or developmental stages, with the most extensively studied mechanism involving DNA methylation—addition of methyl groups (CH₃) to cytosine bases particularly at CpG dinucleotides (cytosine-guanine pairs) concentrated in gene promoter regions where transcription factors initiate gene expression, mechanistically explained through the bug-on-a-stick analogy where DNA functions like rope with green tags (promoter regions) indicating gene start sites, transcription factor complexes ("bugs") walk along DNA rope ("stick") and upon reaching promoters bind and read gene sequences producing messenger RNA (gene expression), while methylation adds obstacles (methyl groups visualized as bulldogs clipping onto rope) physically impeding transcription factor movement and binding—when methyl groups accumulate in promoter regions, transcription factors struggle to access gene start sites reducing transcription rates and decreasing protein production—with crucial feature that methylation doesn't permanently destroy genes because removing methyl groups (demethylation) can restore gene expression, demonstrating dynamic potentially reversible nature contrasting with permanent genetic mutations. The 1998 Quebec ice storm provided an unplanned natural experiment demonstrating environmental influences on human epigenetic programming during critical prenatal periods, where meteorological disaster subjected Quebec residents to extended power outages during subzero winter temperatures creating profound environmental stress (hypothermia risk, resource scarcity, social isolation), enabling researchers to identify women pregnant during the ice storm and conduct longitudinal follow-up assessments of offspring through childhood and adolescence examining whether prenatal exposure to extreme stress (mediated through maternal cortisol, inflammatory cytokines, disrupted placental function) would alter offspring DNA methylation patterns predicting later outcomes, with results published in PLOS ONE demonstrating extraordinary methylation differences where ice storm offspring collected T-cells showed significantly altered methylation (compared to non-exposed controls) at gene loci involved in (1) immune system regulation with hypermethylation at inflammation-related genes potentially predisposing to autoimmune conditions and altered infection responses, and (2) insulin signalling pathways with methylation changes at glucose metabolism genes potentially increasing diabetes risk through altered pancreatic beta-cell function and peripheral insulin sensitivity—these signatures persisting into adolescence more than a decade after original prenatal stress exposure suggesting durable epigenetic programming rather than transient responses, though methodological limitations warrant interpretive caution including peripheral blood methylation (measured in T-cells for accessibility) imperfectly reflecting brain tissue methylation (actually relevant for psychological outcomes) due to tissue-specific patterns, observed differences representing correlational associations rather than demonstrated causal effects on disease outcomes (adolescents hadn't yet developed predicted conditions), and confounding variables (maternal genetics, postnatal environments, socioeconomic factors) preventing definitive causal attributions to prenatal stress specifically. Epigenetic mechanisms contribute to resolving missing heritability through four complementary pathways: (1) genotype-epigenotype interactions where genetic variants influence methylation patterns creating genetically-determined differences in transcriptional plasticity invisible to standard GWAS examining only main effects of sequence variants, (2) environment-driven methylation patterns independent of genotype contributing variance that twin studies attribute to "genetic" effects (because MZ twins share more similar environments than DZ twins creating gene-environment correlation) yet molecular studies cannot capture through sequence analysis alone, (3) developmental timing effects where methylation established during sensitive periods (prenatal development, early childhood, puberty) has lasting influences despite absent associations with adult methylation measured in research studies, and (4) providing molecular mechanisms for gene-environment interactions that differential susceptibility requires, where orchid children carrying NR3C1 A-alleles showing dramatically different outcomes depending on context (75% disorder rate in adversity versus 18% in enrichment) likely involves differential methylation of glucocorticoid receptor genes in response to chronic stress versus support—adverse environments (abuse, neglect, chaos) trigger sustained HPA axis activation and cortisol exposure potentially increasing methylation at NR3C1 promoters and reducing glucocorticoid receptor expression impairing stress response regulation and increasing psychopathology risk, while supportive environments (stable attachment, cognitive enrichment, intervention) maintain normative methylation patterns and receptor expression preserving effective stress regulation, critically with GG genotype individuals showing minimal methylation responses to environmental variation where their genotype confers stable baseline methylation resistant to environmental programming explaining dandelion-like stability across contexts through genotype-by-environment-by-epigenotype three-way interaction invisible to both twin studies (measuring only phenotypic variance components) and GWAS (examining only sequence variants) yet explaining substantial developmental outcome variance. The hopeful implications centre on intervention potential because unlike genetic mutations—permanent sequence changes requiring experimental gene therapy to alter—epigenetic modifications represent dynamic potentially reversible regulatory changes responsive to environmental interventions including exercise, dietary modifications (methyl-donor nutrients like folate, B-vitamins, choline), stress reduction interventions (mindfulness, cognitive-behavioural therapy), and pharmacological agents (histone deacetylase inhibitors, DNA methyltransferase inhibitors) capable of altering methylation patterns suggesting possibilities for "epigenetic therapy" targeting pathological gene expression configurations, with Fast Track study implicitly demonstrating epigenetic intervention where comprehensive support services likely prevented stress-driven hypermethylation of stress-response genes in orchid children maintaining healthy gene expression patterns and preventing psychopathology emergence, though challenges remain including methylation tissue specificity (altering peripheral blood methylation doesn't guarantee brain changes), individual differences in methylation plasticity (not everyone responds equally to interventions), and potential unintended consequences (globally reducing methylation might activate oncogenes or disrupt necessary gene silencing), leading current research to pursue precision epigenetics targeting specific gene loci for methylation modification rather than genome-wide changes to minimize off-target effects while maximizing therapeutic benefits. The lecture's concluding framework addresses why psychologists studying behaviour and mental processes should care about genetic variants and molecular mechanisms seemingly distant from psychological phenomena through multi-level integration constructing complete causal pathways linking biological substrates to psychological outcomes across hierarchical levels: (1) Genetics (DNA sequences, gene variants, epigenetic modifications), (2) Neurochemistry (neurotransmitter systems, receptor densities, signalling cascades), (3) Brain function (neural circuit activity, regional activation, connectivity), (4) Cognitive and emotional abilities (executive functions, emotion regulation, memory), (5) Personality traits (stable individual differences in temperament and character), and (6) Behaviour (observable actions, symptom expressions, diagnostic categories), with environmental factors influencing every level while time/development operates vertically and the entire system feeding back through causal pathways from behaviour to biology, recognizing that psychologists typically work at upper levels (measuring behaviours, personality, cognitive abilities) where hundreds of overlapping causal pathways converge producing massive phenotypic heterogeneity (impulsivity resulting from ADHD dopamine deficits, antisocial reward hypersensitivity, substance use altered reinforcement, traumatic brain injury prefrontal damage, or temperamental reward sensitivity) that dilutes genetic associations and obscures mechanisms, while moving down the explanatory pyramid reduces overlap where neurochemistry narrows to specific neurotransmitter abnormalities and genetics identifies particular variants until reaching maximally specific mechanistic nodes where fewer pathways converge, enabling more precise phenotype definition, more powerful genetic associations, more targeted interventions, and more accurate prediction of individual treatment response differences through understanding complete pathways (genes → neurochemistry → brain → cognition → personality → behaviour), justified by the scientific imperative involving explaining phenomena through mechanistic understanding rather than merely describing correlational patterns where identifying childhood maltreatment correlating with adult psychopathology (upper-level observation) provides limited explanatory or interventional leverage, while understanding that maltreatment operates through altered HPA axis function and glucocorticoid receptor methylation depending on NR3C1 genotype (complete causal pathway) enables mechanistic theories, targeted prevention identifying high-risk individuals, and precision interventions addressing specific biological pathways, ultimately revealing that simple environmental main effects often misrepresent reality because intervention effects in Fast Track appear modest when averaged across genotypes (approximately 28-point reduction) yet reach massive magnitudes for orchid children specifically (57-point reduction) while showing zero effect for dandelions, such that policy decisions allocating intervention resources based on average effects would systematically mis-target services benefiting neither group optimally, establishing behaviour genetics as essential for mechanistic psychological science aligning with precision medicine's recognition that universal interventions often underperform targeted approaches accounting for biological heterogeneity.
