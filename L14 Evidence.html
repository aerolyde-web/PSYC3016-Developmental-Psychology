Jacqueline Anticipation (Grape Juice vs Soup) Piaget diary studies 9 mo Piaget's daughter Jacqueline Naturalistic observation: present spoon with grape juice vs soup; record anticipatory responses (mouth open vs refuse) Stage 4: Coordination of schemes. Infant anticipates outcome based on visible cue (spoon) ‚Üí demonstrates means-ends separation: spoon = means, juice/soup = end. Not just reflex. Consistent differentiated response: juice ‚Üí accept; soup ‚Üí reject Evidence for intentionality without symbols. Physical action schemes coordinate to predict outcomes. "At 9 months, the spoon tells the future ‚Äî grape juice yes, soup no."
Lucienne Matchbox Problem (Mental Representation) Piaget diary studies 18 mo Piaget's daughter Lucienne Hide chain in matchbox with small opening; child must retrieve it. Observe strategies: physical trial-error vs pause-then-solve. Stage 6: Mental combinations. Lucienne pauses, opens/closes her MOUTH (physical analogue), then solves problem ‚Üí internalized representation of "opening." Semiotic function emerges: mental trial-and-error replaces physical. Pause duration before solution; mouth-opening gesture = symbolic stand-in Transition from action to thought. Physical exploration ‚Üí mental simulation. Symbol created through action schemes. "Matchbox opens when mouth opens ‚Äî body becomes the first symbol system."
Stuck Carriage Problem (Problem-Solving Insight) Piaget diary studies 18 mo Lucienne Lucienne tries to pull carriage through doorway; it's stuck. Observe whether trial-error or sudden insight. Stage 6 insight: After initial failure, Lucienne pauses, looks, then adjusts angle and succeeds ‚Üí shows mental representation of spatial problem rather than blind perseveration. Evidence for tertiary‚Üímental transition. Single-trial learning after pause (not gradual shaping) Demonstrates representational thought: child "sees" the solution mentally before acting. End of sensorimotor period. "Stuck carriage, clear mind ‚Äî one look solves what hands couldn't."
Six Sensorimotor Stages Framework Piaget (1936) Origins of Intelligence 0-24 mo Universal sequence Longitudinal observation of own children + clinical method with other infants; code behaviors into hierarchical stages Stage 1 (0-1mo): Reflex modification
Stage 2 (1-4mo): Primary circular (body-centered)
Stage 3 (4-8mo): Secondary circular (object-focused)
Stage 4 (8-12mo): Coordination of schemes
Stage 5 (12-18mo): Tertiary circular (experimentation)
Stage 6 (18-24mo): Mental combinations ‚Üí SYMBOLIC THOUGHT		 Universal age ranges (¬±2mo individual variation); logically necessary sequence (can't skip stages) Constructivism: Each stage builds on prior; sensorimotor action schemes gradually internalize into mental operations. No innate symbols.
Memorize: 1-Reflex, 2-Primary (body), 3-Secondary (object), 4-Coordination (means-ends), 5-Tertiary (experiment), 6-Mental (symbols)
 "Six steps from reflex to thought: body builds mind, 0 to 24 months."
Object Permanence Emergence Piaget (1954) Construction of Reality 8-12 mo Stage 4 A-not-B task: hide object at location A (infant retrieves); then visibly move to B. Measure search location. Stage 4 error: Infants search at A despite seeing object move to B ‚Üí object permanence still action-bound, not fully representational. Stage 6: Search correctly at B ‚Üí object exists independent of action. A-not-B error: ~80-90% at 8-10mo; ~10-20% by 12mo Shows gradual construction of mental representation. Object concept tied to motor schemes ‚Üí decontextualized representation. "Eight months: 'out of sight, out of mind'; two years: object has a life of its own."
Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) Vygotsky (1978) Mind in Society All ages; focus on 2-7 yr Compare child's solo performance vs performance with adult/peer assistance on same task ZPD = gap between what child can do alone vs with help. Development occurs through social interaction in this zone. Learning leads development (opposite of Piaget: development enables learning). Solo score vs assisted score; size of gap predicts learning potential Sociocultural transmission: Adult provides cultural tools (language, strategies) that child internalizes. Scaffolding bridges ZPD.
ZPD = gap between solo vs assisted. Learning LEADS development (opposite of Piaget)
 "The gap between 'I can't' and 'I can' is filled by a teacher."
Scaffolding & Gradual Withdrawal Vygotsky (Wood, Bruner, Ross 1976 operationalization) 3-6 yr Adult assists child on task (puzzle, problem-solving); gradually reduce support as child gains competence Effective teaching = contingent support: maximum help when needed ‚Üí fade as mastery increases. Child appropriates cultural strategy through co-construction. Optimal: high support early (80%‚Üí) ‚Üí low support late (‚Üí20%); child reaches independence Internalization mechanism: External social interaction becomes internal psychological function. Symbols TRANSMITTED, not constructed. "Teacher builds the bridge, then removes the planks one by one until child walks alone."
Egocentric Speech ‚Üí Inner Speech Vygotsky (1934/1986) Thought and Language 3-7 yr Observe children talking aloud during problem-solving; code frequency, content, task-relevance Peak ~3-5yr: Children talk through problems aloud (egocentric speech). Vygotsky: This is internalization in progress ‚Äî social speech becoming inner thought. Increases with task difficulty. Frequency peaks ~age 4-5 (~40% of utterances during tasks); declines by age 7 (~5%) as internalized Critique of Piaget: Not egocentric/autistic; it's social problem-solving turning inward. Language = cognitive tool from culture. "At four they solve problems out loud; at seven the voice goes underground."
Cultural Variation in Cognitive Tools Vygotsky; cross-cultural evidence (Rogoff, Lave) Children across cultures Compare problem-solving strategies, memory techniques, mathematical reasoning across cultures (e.g., abacus vs number line) Different cultures provide different cognitive tools ‚Üí different cognitive profiles. E.g., abacus experts show different mental arithmetic strategies than Western-schooled children. NOT universal stages. Culture-specific tool use predicts performance patterns (e.g., abacus ‚Üí spatial-sequential processing) Refutes Piaget's universalism: Cognitive development is culturally shaped, not biologically predetermined sequence. "Give a child an abacus or a pencil ‚Äî they'll think in beads or symbols."
Parent Speech Adaptation Developmental psycholinguistics (motherese research) Parents of 1-5 yr Record parent speech to children of different ages; code complexity (MLU, vocabulary, syntax) Parents automatically adjust speech complexity to child's level: simpler to 2yr-old, more complex to 5yr-old. Evidence: Adults are sensitive to child's ZPD and scaffold linguistically. MLU to 2yr-old: ~3-4 morphemes; to 5yr-old: ~6-8 morphemes Social responsiveness: Environment adapts to child's level ‚Üí provides input at optimal challenge (ZPD). Supports cultural transmission view. "Parents don't teach grammar ‚Äî they become the grammar the child can just barely handle."
Poverty of Stimulus Argument Chomsky (1965) Aspects of Syntax Children 2-5 yr; cross-linguistic Theoretical argument: analyze linguistic input vs linguistic knowledge. Show child knows MORE than input provides. Core claim: Children acquire complex grammatical rules despite impoverished input: no negative evidence, fragmentary sentences, limited examples. Yet they never make certain errors (e.g., structure-dependent rules). Conclusion: Innate linguistic knowledge (UG) fills the gap. Grammar acquisition complete by ~4-5yr despite limited/degenerate input; error types NEVER observed (e.g., linear-order rules) Nativism: Linguistic knowledge unlearnable from experience ‚Üí must be innate. If language is innate, symbols are innate (refutes Piaget).
Key argument: Complex knowledge + impoverished input = must be innate (UG)
 "Children know rules never taught, never corrected, never violated ‚Äî genes wrote the grammar book."
Pronoun Binding Constraints Chomsky (1981) Lectures on Government & Binding English-speaking children 3-5 yr Test comprehension/production: "John believes he is smart" vs "John believes him to be smart." Ask: who is smart? ‚úÖ "He" can = John OR someone else (ambiguous)
‚ùå "Him" cannot = John (obligatory disjoint reference)
KEY: Children never violate this constraint despite no explicit teaching. Universal across languages.		 Error rate on "him=John" interpretation: ~0-2% by age 4 (effectively never occurs) Universal Grammar: Constraint is part of innate linguistic endowment. Not learned via reinforcement or observation. Evidence for genetic specification of grammar. "John believes him to be smart" ‚Äî every four-year-old knows 'him' isn't John, though no one told them."
Wh-Movement Constraints (Islands) Chomsky; Ross (1967) island constraints Native speakers (adults & children) Grammaticality judgments: legal vs illegal wh-questions. E.g., "Who is Sam waiting for?" vs "Who did Susan ask why Sam was waiting for?" ‚úÖ "Who is Sam waiting for?" (legal)
‚ùå "Who did Susan ask [why Sam was waiting for __]?" (illegal - violates wh-island constraint)
Children obey these constraints despite no negative evidence.		 Acceptance rate of island violations: ~5-10% (near-zero in native speakers); never corrected in input Structural constraints: Rules operate on hierarchical structure, not linear order. Innate UG specifies "islands" where movement is banned. "'Who did Susan ask why Sam was waiting for?' ‚Äî sounds wrong, is wrong, was never taught to be wrong."
Structure Dependence (Auxiliary Questions) Chomsky (1975) Reflections on Language English-learning children 3-4 yr Test question formation: "The man who is tall is in the room" ‚Üí "Is the man who __ tall __ in the room?" (structure-dependent) vs *"Is the man who is tall __ in the room?" (linear-order) Children ALWAYS use structure-dependent rule (move auxiliary from main clause) despite linear-order rule being simpler and consistent with input. Never produce linear-order errors. Error rate on linear-order strategy: ~0% (despite it being simpler and matching surface patterns) Innate constraints: Human language rules operate on abstract structure, not surface order. This constraint is NEVER violated ‚Üí unlearnable, hence innate. "Children move the right 'is' every time ‚Äî because grammar isn't about counting words, it's about seeing trees."
Adjective Ordering (Universal Constraints) Cross-linguistic typology (Chomsky, generative grammar tradition) Children learning any language Document adjective-noun order across languages; test whether children produce unattested orders Languages vary (English: Adj-N "red car"; some languages: N-Adj "car red"), but children acquiring English NEVER say "car red" despite no explicit correction. Acquire language-specific order immediately. Production errors: ~0% for basic word order by age 2-3 Parameter-setting: UG specifies possible orders; child "sets parameter" based on input. Rapid, error-free acquisition = evidence for innate constraints. "English kids say 'red car,' Chinese kids say 'car red' ‚Äî no one teaches it, everyone gets it right."
No Negative Evidence Problem Chomsky; Brown & Hanlon (1970) empirical evidence Parent-child interactions 2-4 yr Analyze parental corrections: do parents correct grammar errors or only truth-value errors? Parents rarely correct grammar (~10% of errors); when they do, children often don't revise. Parents correct truth/politeness more. Yet children converge on correct grammar ‚Üí cannot rely on negative evidence. Grammar corrections: ~10% of errors; truth corrections: ~40% Learnability argument: Without negative evidence, incorrect hypotheses cannot be ruled out ‚Üí unless innate constraints prevent them. Supports UG necessity. "Parents fix lies, not grammar ‚Äî yet kids' grammar fixes itself."

Vygotsky's Critique of Piaget: "Wall of Facts" Vygotsky (1934) Thought & Language Theoretical critique Conceptual analysis of Piaget's theory Vygotsky: Piaget describes symbolic development beautifully but "hides behind a wall of facts" ‚Äî doesn't explain HOW symbols originate. Sensorimotor action ‚Üí symbols is a descriptive gap, not an explanation. Missing: mechanism for transition. N/A (theoretical argument) Vygotsky's answer: Symbols don't emerge from individual action ‚Äî they're provided by culture and internalized through social interaction. "Piaget tells us WHAT happens at 18 months; Vygotsky asks WHERE the symbols come from ‚Äî and answers: culture."
Chomsky's Critique of Piaget: Sensorimotor Cannot Explain Language Complexity Chomsky (1980) Rules and Representations Theoretical critique Logical argument: compare sensorimotor schemes to linguistic structures Chomsky: No bridge from pushing objects to understanding abstract syntactic constraints (wh-islands, binding principles). Linguistic structures are qualitatively different from action schemes. Sensorimotor "construction" cannot generate Universal Grammar. N/A (theoretical argument) Chomsky's answer: Linguistic knowledge is innately specified (mental organ). Rejects Piaget's constructivism AND Vygotsky's cultural transmission (UG is universal, not cultural). "Pushing blocks doesn't teach you island constraints ‚Äî genes do."
Piaget's Critique of Chomsky: "Biologically Inexplicable" Piaget (1983) response to Chomsky Theoretical critique Conceptual analysis of nativism Piaget: "Biologically inexplicable" ‚Äî how can genes specify abstract grammatical rules? Where is the evolutionary pathway? Innateness is a descriptive cop-out, not an explanation. Rejects "preformationism" (genes as complete blueprint). N/A (theoretical argument) Piaget's position: Deny innateness a priori; all complex knowledge must be constructed through interaction with world (epigenesis, not preformation). "Chomsky says 'it's in the genes' ‚Äî Piaget says 'show me the gene, then show me how it codes syntax.'"
