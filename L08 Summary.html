PSYC3016 - Lecture 08: Moral Development I

Overarching Question: How does Kohlberg's stage theory proposing hierarchical moral reasoning development from pre-conventional punishment avoidance (Stages 1-2: "Will I be punished?" and "What's in it for me?") through conventional social conformity (Stages 3-4: "What will others think?" and "What do rules require?" comprising most adults) to post-conventional principled reasoning (Stages 5-6: social contract achieved by only 10-15% of adults and universal ethical principles reached by ~1% like Bonhoeffer attempting Hitler's assassination) face systematic limitations including reasoning-behavior dissociation (identical reasoning producing different behaviors, same behavior stemming from multiple stages), gender bias (Gilligan's 1982 critique revealing male-only samples privileging justice-oriented Stage 4 above care-oriented Stage 3 despite care and justice representing different orientations not hierarchical levels), cultural bias (collectivist societies valuing harmony over justice reversing Western stage rankings), and ecological validity failures (hypothetical dilemmas misrepresenting real moral pressure), ultimately challenged by Social Domain Theory demonstrating that 30-month-old children reliably distinguish moral transgressions (hitting, stealing causing inherent harm—wrong without rules, universal across contexts, not authority-modifiable, very serious) from conventional transgressions (not sitting on mat, not wearing clothes violating social coordination rules—only wrong if prohibited, context-bound, authority-modifiable, less serious) through eight systematic criteria including harm causation establishing that moral understanding emerges dramatically earlier (Smetana 1981 finding no age differences across 30-57 months) than Kohlberg's adolescent conventional morality, with harm principle revealing that conventions causing harm shift to moral domain (wearing clothes conventionally arbitrary yet morally required in Arctic conditions preventing hypothermia), producing developmental paradox where 2.5-year-olds knowing hitting is universally wrong nevertheless hit siblings when angry because moral knowledge precedes behavioral control requiring immature impulse regulation and emotional management capacities developing over years, distinguishing moral reasoning (Kohlberg's cognitive rationalist "What SHOULD be done?" requiring hypothetical dilemma deliberation emerging adolescence) from moral appraisal (Social Domain's evaluative sentimentalist "Is it right or wrong?" based on harm perception emerging 30 months), establishing that neither approach alone predicts behavior because reasoning without emotion produces empty principles while intuition without reasoning cannot handle complex dilemmas, requiring integration where self-awareness prerequisites progress from 18-month Rouge test self-recognition enabling moral agency ("me" as responsible) through 2-year-old self-conscious emotions (pride, shame, embarrassment requiring external audience feedback) to 8-year-old internalized standards (self-appraisal without audience) supporting the transition from external feedback-dependent morality to internal conscience?

Comprehensive Summary

Moral development requires self-awareness as foundational prerequisite because without self-concept no responsibility or accountability can exist, progressing through 18-month Rouge test self-recognition establishing "me" as potentially responsible agent distinct from others, 2-year-old emergence of self-conscious emotions (pride, shame, embarrassment) requiring external audience feedback where children feel proud when praised yet show no emotion for identical achievements without observers indicating external validation dependence, differentiation from basic emotions (fear, anger, joy operating without self-representation) occurring 4-6 years marking beginning internalization, and 8-year-old capacity for self-appraisal without audience where children feel guilty for private transgressions unknown to others demonstrating fully internalized moral standards. Kohlberg's stage theory proposes hierarchical development through six stages organized into three levels: Pre-conventional morality (Stages 1-2) characterizing early childhood where Stage 1 punishment avoidance asks "Will I be punished?" motivating behavior through consequence avoidance (example: not drawing on walls "because I got told off"), while Stage 2 self-interest asks "What's in it for me?" motivating behavior through personal benefit (example: doing homework "to get to go to cinema"), both operating egocentrically focused on external consequences without consideration of social expectations or abstract principles; Conventional morality (Stages 3-4) characterizing adolescence and most adults where Stage 3 social approval asks "What will others think?" motivating behavior through social expectations and interpersonal harmony (example: helping elderly person "because society expects it"), while Stage 4 law and order asks "What do rules require?" motivating behavior to maintain social systems (example: not shoplifting "must uphold laws to maintain order"), both accepting authority and existing social structures as inherently legitimate; and Post-conventional morality (Stages 5-6) characterizing only 10-15% of adults for Stage 5 and ~1% for Stage 6 where Stage 5 social contract recognizes laws as democratic agreements benefiting majority yet modifiable through consensus (example: civil rights activism changing unjust laws), while Stage 6 universal ethical principles transcends specific laws appealing to abstract justice even when contradicting personal beliefs or legal statutes (paradigmatic example: Dietrich Bonhoeffer, Christian pacifist pastor, attempting to assassinate Hitler because universal principles protecting innocent life outweighed both personal pacifism and legal prohibitions). However, Kohlberg's framework faces seven systematic limitations: (1) reasoning-behavior dissociation where identical behaviors stem from different stage motivations (helping motivated by Stage 2 expectation of reward, Stage 3 social approval, or Stage 6 principled commitment to human dignity) while identical reasoning produces different behaviors due to personality, anxiety, and group effects demonstrating reasoning does not determine action, (2) gender bias (Gilligan 1982) stemming from male-only initial samples producing stage hierarchy privileging male-typical justice orientation (Stage 4: abstract rules, fairness, consistent application) over female-typical care orientation (Stage 3: relationships, contextual response, minimizing harm) when care and justice represent different ethical orientations not hierarchical developmental levels, (3) non-hierarchical reality where individuals deploy multiple stages simultaneously depending on context (minor infractions invoking Stage 2 self-interest, legal matters invoking Stage 4 rule-following) contradicting unidirectional stage progression, (4) ecological validity failures where hypothetical dilemmas lacking real consequences poorly predict behavior under actual moral pressure (Eisenberg's criticism), (5) cultural bias where collectivist societies prioritizing harmony value Stage 3 social expectations above Stage 4 justice-based legalism reversing Western developmental assumptions, (6) articulation limits where children below age 7 cannot verbally justify reasoning despite potentially possessing understanding, and (7) Stage 6 rarity (~1% achievement) suggesting either measurement inadequacy or questionable theoretical validity. Social Domain Theory fundamentally reframes moral development through the moral-versus-conventional distinction, where moral transgressions (hitting, stealing, biting, not sharing) cause harm, suffering, or interpersonal tension making them inherently wrong independent of rules (wrong even if authority permits), universal across contexts (wrong at home AND school, across cultures), not authority-modifiable (remains wrong even if teacher says "hitting is allowed today"), very serious deserving punishment, and based intrinsically on event properties (harm to victim), contrasting with conventional transgressions (not sitting on mat, not wearing clothes, not queuing, improper greetings) breaking social coordination rules without inherent harm making them only wrong if prohibited (perfectly acceptable without rule), context-bound (wrong at formal school, acceptable at beach), authority-modifiable (becomes acceptable if authority permits "today you may stand during mat time"), less serious warranting mild responses, and based externally on social organization not event properties, producing eight systematic differentiation criteria including rule contingency, context generality, authority dependence, seriousness, harm basis, universality, deserved punishment, and intrinsic versus extrinsic wrongness. Smetana's landmark 1981 study revolutionized developmental timelines by demonstrating 30-month-old children reliably distinguish moral from conventional transgressions, testing participants ages 30-57 months (2.5 to 4.75 years) and finding no age differences—even the youngest 30-month-olds judged moral transgressions as more wrong without rules, more serious, more deserving of punishment, and wrong everywhere (both home and school) while judging conventional transgressions as only wrong if prohibited, less serious, and context-specific (wrong at school, acceptable at home), with critical implication that moral understanding emerges dramatically earlier than Kohlberg suggests (30 months versus adolescence for conventional morality), challenging stage theory's developmental timeline and establishing that even toddlers possess sophisticated moral-conventional differentiation, supported by adult response quality teaching domain distinction where conventional violations (not sitting on mat) receive mild encouragement ("Would you like to sit?") then letting it go, while moral violations (biting) trigger immediate intervention, parent notification, and serious addressing, with differential response intensity socializing children's category understanding. The harm principle reveals domain boundaries are permeable where convention violations causing harm shift to moral domain: not wearing clothes normally constitutes conventional transgression (social rule without inherent harm, acceptable in appropriate contexts like beaches/homes) yet in Arctic conditions forcing nudity becomes moral transgression (causes harm through hypothermia), not removing shoes in place of worship transitions from conventional rudeness to moral violation if deeply offensive causing emotional harm, while queuing violations remain conventional (social coordination) unless cutting causes missed appointments producing concrete harm, establishing that harm causation functions as primary criterion determining domain membership, with conventions becoming moral issues when harm emerges as consequence. The development paradox describes persistent dissociation between moral knowledge and moral behavior where 2.5-year-old children demonstrating robust understanding that hitting is wrong everywhere nevertheless hit siblings when angry, 3-4 year-olds distinguishing moral from conventional reliably yet frequently violating both domains, and even 8-year-olds with internalized standards (self-appraisal without audience) showing compliance gaps, explained through behavior control lagging understanding because moral action requires not only conceptual knowledge but also immature impulse control, emotional regulation, and internalization processes developing across years, compounded by social anxiety, group dynamics, and personality factors influencing behavioral expression independent of moral reasoning capacity, establishing that moral knowledge represents necessary but insufficient condition for moral behavior, with the knowledge-behavior gap narrowing gradually as executive function, emotion regulation, and internalization mature. The theoretical synthesis distinguishes moral reasoning (Kohlberg's approach) asking "What SHOULD be done?" through cognitive rationalist framework requiring hypothetical dilemma deliberation and verbal justification competence emerging adolescence from moral appraisal (Social Domain approach) asking "Is it right or wrong?" through evaluative sentimentalist framework based on harm perception and rule contingency judgments emerging 30 months, recognizing that neither approach alone predicts behavior because reasoning without emotional engagement produces empty principles unable to motivate action (knowing abstract justice doesn't generate helping behavior without empathic concern), while intuition without reasoning capability cannot handle complex moral dilemmas requiring deliberation about conflicting principles (automatic harm aversion insufficient for navigating abortion, euthanasia, or trolley problems balancing multiple values), demanding integration where early-emerging moral appraisal (30 months distinguishing harm from rule-breaking) provides emotional foundation and intuitive categorization while later-developing moral reasoning (adolescent capacity for principled deliberation) enables handling complex cases requiring balancing competing considerations, collectively establishing that moral development proceeds along multiple parallel trajectories (self-awareness enabling agency, self-conscious emotions providing affective foundation, domain differentiation establishing harm-based categories, reasoning sophistication enabling principled deliberation, behavioral control emerging through executive function maturation) that must integrate for mature moral functioning rather than progressing through fixed hierarchical stages, with cross-cultural variation, individual differences in care-versus-justice orientation, and persistent knowledge-behavior gaps revealing moral development as multifaceted process irreducible to single stage sequence.
