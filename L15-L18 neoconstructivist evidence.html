Cohen & Strauss (1979) establishes the foundational method: habituation can test what infants treat as "same", revealing their level of pattern abstraction, not just perceptual discrimination. The critical finding is that more variability in habituation → more abstract category at test. With narrow exposure (1 face, 1 angle), infants treat any change as novel (viewpoint-specific picture). With moderate exposure (1 face, many angles), they generalize across orientations but dishabituate to new identity (view-invariant identity). With maximum variability (many faces, many angles), they treat even novel faces as familiar (broad "faces" category). This demonstrates infants don't just memorize individual stimuli - they abstract patterns from variation. The scope of generalization matches the scope of variability experienced. This is the base move for the neoconstructivist argument: infant habituation data can index LEVELS of representation, not just perception, and representation level is controlled by input statistics, not fixed developmental stages.

Younger & Cohen (1983) introduces the concept of hierarchical statistical learning. The paradigm uses line-drawing "animals" with programmed feature correlations (e.g., hoof feet ALWAYS appear with giraffe body). If infants learn only features, both Correlated (C) and Uncorrelated (U) test items should look familiar (same features seen before). But if infants learn correlations, U items violate the learned structure and should elicit dishabituation. Results show 10-month-olds learn correlations (C low, U high, N high) while 4 and 7-month-olds learn features only (C low, U low, N high). This establishes the first step of the representational hierarchy: correlation learning (Level 2) depends on first encoding features (Level 1). Age differences reflect capacity growth, not a module switching on. This motivates three of the five neoconstructivist principles: (1) domain-general system for low-level features; (2) higher units built from relations among lower ones; (3) hierarchical dependence.

The Five Neoconstructivist Principles (Cohen, Younger, Oakes)

1 Innate domain-general system for detecting low-level features All studies show feature detection at all ages — features are the starting point
2 Higher-level units built from relationships among lower-level units Correlations require features; relations require correlations; can't skip levels
3 Learning is hierarchical and constructive Must build Level 1 before Level 2 — order matters
4 System prefers highest level available given current capacity 7mo attempt correlations even when overloaded (flat habituation before reverting)
5 Overload → revert to lower level (adaptive simplification) Extended time → REVERT to features; variability → revert to features/motion codes
Younger & Cohen (1986) provides the "engine" of neoconstructivism. By manipulating task complexity, they show same-age infants change their level of processing depending on load. In Exp 1 (easy: 3 features, all correlated), 7-month-olds now succeed at correlation learning — proving they CAN learn correlations when task demands match capacity. In Exp 2 (medium: 3 features, only 2/3 correlated), 7-month-olds show flat habituation curves — they're attempting correlation learning but are overloaded, never reaching criterion. The critical finding comes in Exp 4: when these same 7-month-olds are given extended habituation time, they REVERT to feature-only processing (C low, U low, N high). Extended time produces WORSE outcomes when task exceeds capacity. This proves adaptive strategy shift: infants attempt the highest level, fail, then strategically simplify. This cannot be explained by module maturation — the same infant flips between representational levels based purely on task demands. These findings directly yield all five neoconstructivist principles, and this "overload → reversion" signature becomes the key pattern to look for across domains.

Ferry, Hespos & Gentner (2015) extends the neoconstructivist framework to abstract identity relations (same/different). Under optimal load conditions (4 diverse pairs, novel test objects), 7-9 month-olds successfully discriminate relations — looking longer at the novel relation with ~57% accuracy. But the conditions matter critically. With only 1 pair repeated, there's no relational generalisation — infants encode the specific objects, not the relation. With object-experience only (playing with test objects without relational habituation), infants FAIL despite familiar objects. This is counterintuitive: familiarity with objects should help, but it actually HURTS relational learning. The explanation ties to the natural partitions hypothesis: by default, infants focus on objects (easy partition); extracting relations (hard partition) requires structured multi-exemplar exposure that habituates out object features. Object familiarity WITHOUT habituation increases object salience → consumes capacity → blocks relational processing. This connects the neoconstructivist engine to identity relations and establishes that objects are cognitively easy; relations are cognitively hard — a tension that persists throughout development.

Anderson et al. (2018) demonstrates that same/different ability isn't suddenly "switched on" at 7-9 months. Even 3-month-olds succeed at relational discrimination when load is tuned to their capacity. With 6 pairs, they're overloaded and fail. With 2 pairs, they succeed — looking longer at the novel relation with completely novel objects. The object-experience interference pattern replicates exactly: familiar but non-habituated objects block relational processing. This extends the overload-reversion logic to even earlier ages and to abstract relations. The claim is NOT that 3-month-olds have a "relation module" that 7-month-olds lack — both ages succeed when conditions match their capacity. Age differences reflect graded capacity growth + overload thresholds, not qualitative stage transitions or module maturation. The finding that 3-month human infants outperform adult chimpanzees on relational matching (chimps need extensive symbol training) suggests humans are built for relational abstraction — but it's capacity-gated from the start.

Oakes & Cohen (1990) applies the neoconstructivist framework to causal perception. Leslie (1984) famously showed 6-month-olds perceive causality in Michotte launching displays, interpreted as evidence for an innate causal module. But Oakes & Cohen kept identical kinematics and only changed object complexity: simple shapes vs. complex realistic toys (dinosaur, airplane, jalopy). With simple shapes, 6-month-olds succeed — replicating Leslie. But with complex toys, the same 6-month-olds FAIL — they track delay vs. gap features rather than causal vs. non-causal category. 10-month-olds succeed with complex toys, showing the relation can be abstracted with more capacity. Under low load (simple shapes), it LOOKS like an innate causal module; but same age fails when complexity eats capacity. The improvement from 6→10 months reflects capacity growth for handling complex objects while still representing relations, not module maturation. This shows the same overload signature appears in the causal domain — early success only under low load — weakening the nativist "causal module" story and aligning causal perception with the same/different results.

Cohen & Oakes (1993) delivers the decisive blow to the nativist retreat. A nativist might concede that the causal "module" matures later, but once it's online (by 10 months), it should work reliably. Cohen & Oakes tested 10-month-olds — the age that succeeded with complex toys in the 1990 study — and manipulated only object variability: same complex toys repeated each trial vs. different complex toy every trial. This cannot be explained by module maturity — the "mature" 10-month-old shows the exact same overload → reversion pattern as younger infants on harder tasks. The within-age, between-condition flip proves capacity-dependent representation, not domain-specific module. The same logic applies: features → correlations → relations, with the achieved level depending on available resources after encoding lower levels. This is the same overload signature seen in Younger & Cohen's feature correlations and in same/different paradigms — domain-general capacity, not domain-specific modules.

The Shared Signature Pattern Across All Three Domains

Identity relations, causal relations, and feature correlations ALL show:

  • Early capacity under optimal load — success at younger ages than expected when task is simplified
  • Overload → reversion to lower representational level (features/objects/motion codes)
  • Same age, different representation based on load manipulation (complexity, variability, exemplar count)

The cross-domain convergence is the strongest evidence for neoconstructivist architecture. Across identity relations (same/different), causal relations (launching), and feature correlations, we see the identical signature: early success under optimal load, overload → reversion to lower level, and same-age variation in representation based purely on task demands. Anderson et al. shows 3-month-olds succeed at same/different with 2 pairs but fail with 6. Oakes & Cohen shows 6-month-olds succeed at causal perception with simple shapes but fail with complex toys. Younger & Cohen shows 7-month-olds succeed at correlations with easy tasks but revert to features with medium tasks. Cohen & Oakes shows 10-month-olds succeed at causal categorization with repeated toys but fail with variable toys. This pattern cannot be explained by multiple domain-specific modules that happen to mature at exactly the right times with exactly the same overload signatures. One domain-general capacity account explains all these phenomena more parsimoniously than separate narrow modules. The shared hierarchical system runs both identity and causal relations, with an inverted-U performance curve vs. load at each age — success in the sweet spot, failure at both extremes (too little variation = no abstraction; too much load = reversion).

Gentner et al.'s brace study demonstrates that the same object-vs-relation tension persists later in life. With a single example, children default to object-based encoding (~38% correct transfer). With low-alignability comparison (hard to map structures), performance improves slightly (~47%). With high-alignability comparison (easy structural mapping), performance jumps to ~82%. Comparison is a domain-general tool that boosts relational encoding by forcing structural alignment — mapping objects not by features but by the roles they play in the relational structure. This is the same mechanism that operates in infant habituation: multi-exemplar exposure with systematic variation habituates out object features and highlights relational structure. The "relational shift" from childhood to adulthood is not a magical new system coming online — it's the elaboration of the same constructivist machinery that was present in infancy. Comparison helps climb from objects to relations just like controlled variability did for infants. Adults also default to object matches unless prompted, showing the object-relation competition is never fully resolved — it's managed through cognitive tools like comparison.

Zheng, Goldwater & Gentner (2023) adds the final rung to the ladder: language as a higher-order dynamic system. Adding a relational label ("brace") during high-alignability comparison boosts performance from ~82% to ~85%, but more importantly, it dramatically improves retention and transfer across delays and novel contexts. The verbal label creates a stable symbolic handle for the relational structure, allowing it to be encoded, stored, and retrieved independently of the specific objects that instantiated it. Without language, relational categories are built through perception + comparison but remain tied to the learning context; with language, they become portable abstractions. This connects the infant constructivism story to later language/executive function/culture themes. Language doesn't create relational categories from scratch — it consolidates and extends categories that were already constructed through sensorimotor statistics and comparison. This is the developmental pathway to domain-general abstract knowledge: features → correlations → relations → language-stabilized abstractions, with each level built from the one below through the same hierarchical, capacity-dependent, constructivist machinery.
Section 8: Conclusion — The Neoconstructivist Framework

The Five Core Principles (Cohen, Younger, Oakes, Gentner) — Mapped to Evidence

# Principle Empirical Support from Studies Above
1 Innate domain-general system for low-level features All studies show feature detection at all ages — 4mo encode features (Y&C 1983); 6mo track motion features (O&C 1990); 3mo encode objects (Anderson 2018). Features are universal starting point.
2 Higher units built from relationships among lower units Correlations require features (Y&C 1983); causal relations require object encoding (O&C 1990); same/different requires object comparison (Ferry 2015). Can't skip levels.
3 Learning is hierarchical and constructive Must build Level 1 before Level 2 — shown in all age progressions. Generalization scope matches habituation variability (C&S 1979). Developmental trajectory: features → correlations → relations → language.
4 System prefers highest level available 7mo attempt correlations even when overloaded (flat habituation before reverting — Y&C 1986 Exp 2); 6mo attempt causality with complex toys. System tries highest level first, doesn't start conservatively.
5 Overload → revert to lower level Y&C 1986 Exp 4: extended time → revert to features. C&O 1993: variability → revert to motion codes. Ferry/Anderson: object-experience → interference with relations. Universal across domains.
The neoconstructivist framework unifies Cohen & Strauss (habituation generalisation), Younger & Cohen (feature correlations), Ferry and Anderson (same/different relations), Oakes & Cohen and Cohen & Oakes (causal launching), and Gentner's comparison + language studies under one coherent architecture. The five principles — (1) domain-general feature detection, (2) higher units from lower relations, (3) hierarchical construction, (4) preference for highest level, (5) overload → reversion — provide the theoretical glue to interpret all empirical patterns as manifestations of the same engine. For exam answers: explicitly state these five principles and point back to each empirical section as illustration. The cross-domain convergence (identity relations, causal relations, feature correlations all showing the same overload signature) is strong evidence against hard nativist modularity. One domain-general capacity account explains these phenomena more parsimoniously and mechanistically than positing multiple narrow modules that happen to show identical developmental patterns. The framework extends from 3-month infants through childhood comparison to language-mediated adult abstraction, providing a unified developmental trajectory from sensorimotor statistics → perceptual categories → relational abstractions → symbolic knowledge.
