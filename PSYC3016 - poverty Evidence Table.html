üìù MODEL SAQ SCAFFOLD: "How does SES affect cognitive development, and what interventions show promise?"

[THESIS] SES affects development via two pathways‚Äîlanguage/cognitive deprivation and threat-related stress‚Äîwhich alter PFC + hippocampus circuits, producing the EF + language deficits that mediate achievement gaps.

[NEURAL SETUP] Farah's longitudinal research shows identical brain architecture at birth but 3-4% smaller hippocampus and thinner PFC by age 12 in low-SES children‚Äîbiological differences with environmental causes. Hackman & Farah demonstrate effect is specific to PFC, not global impairment.

[PATHWAY 1: LANGUAGE] Seo & Ginsburg reveal the paradox: equivalent mathematical play behaviors across SES, but divergent verbal reasoning‚Äîchildren can do but can't explain. High-SES advantage reflects more conversational turns and why/how questions that transform implicit procedures into explicit knowledge (Vygotsky's ZPD).

[PATHWAY 2: THREAT] Chronic stress (cortisol ‚Üí HPA dysregulation) impairs PFC maturation; acute stress (worry, anxiety) consumes WM capacity in real-time. Both pathways reduce EF across inhibition, flexibility, and working memory.

[INTERVENTIONS] Diamond et al.'s Tools of the Mind shows dramatic effects: control schools abandoned their own curriculum because behavior improved so much. Hearts & Flowers switching: 72% vs 50% (+22pp advantage). Abecedarian demonstrates 15+ IQ points sustained into adulthood with comprehensive early enrichment.

[SYNTHESIS] These interventions target exactly what poverty harms: language-rich interaction closes the reflection gap, EF practice in meaningful play strengthens PFC circuits. SES vulnerabilities are not fixed deficits‚Äîwith the right input at the right time, children can be brought back toward typical trajectories.
