1️⃣ Missing Heritability Problem Foundation
Q1
What is the "missing heritability problem" in behaviour genetics?
A) Twin studies find no heritability for psychological traits, contradicting genetic theories
B) Twin studies show ~50% heritability but GWAS identify only 2-3% variance from genetic variants
C) Heritability estimates vary between 0-100% across studies, making replication impossible
D) Genome-wide scans find too many genes to analyze, making interpretation impossible
Explanation:
The missing heritability problem describes the empirical discrepancy where quantitative genetic methods (twin studies using MZ/DZ correlation differences) consistently estimate heritability at approximately 50% for complex psychological traits, yet molecular genetic methods (GWAS scanning millions of SNPs) identify common genetic variants explaining only 2-3% of phenotypic variance. This creates a 47-percentage-point explanatory gap between what we know is heritable from family studies and what we can identify at the molecular level. The problem motivates investigation of interactions, rare variants, and epigenetic mechanisms as explanatory candidates.
From Lecture: "Twin studies employing the classical twin method consistently estimate heritability at approximately 0.50 across diverse psychological phenotypes... However, genome-wide association studies identify common genetic variants collectively explaining only 2-3% of phenotypic variance."
Why other options are incorrect:
A: Twin studies DO find heritability (~50% for most traits), not zero heritability. The problem isn't absence of heritability but inability to locate genetic sources.
C: Heritability estimates are remarkably consistent across well-designed studies (~50%), not highly variable. The issue is consistency between methods, not within methods.
D: The problem is finding too FEW genes with large effects, not too many. GWAS struggle to identify sufficient variants to explain known heritability.

Q2
A twin study of aggressive behavior finds MZ correlation = 0.65 and DZ correlation = 0.35. Using Falconer's formula (h² = 2(rMZ - rDZ)), estimated heritability is:
A) 30%
B) 50%
C) 60%
D) 100%
Explanation:
Applying Falconer's formula: h² = 2(rMZ - rDZ) = 2(0.65 - 0.35) = 2(0.30) = 0.60 or 60%. This calculation estimates that 60% of phenotypic variance in aggressive behavior is attributable to additive genetic effects. The logic behind Falconer's formula: MZ twins share 100% of genes while DZ twins share 50%, so the difference in correlations reflects the effect of the additional 50% genetic similarity. Doubling this difference estimates total genetic contribution. If a subsequent GWAS found only 4% variance explained by identified SNPs, the missing heritability would be 60% - 4% = 56 percentage points.
From Study Guide: "h² = 2(rMZ - rDZ) = 2(0.65 - 0.35) = 2(0.30) = 0.60. Twin study suggests 60% heritability... This represents the proportion of genetically-influenced variance that remains mechanistically unexplained by current molecular methods."
Why other options are incorrect:
A: This (0.30) is just the difference between correlations (rMZ - rDZ), not the heritability estimate. Must multiply by 2 to account for DZ twins sharing only 50% of genes.
B: While 50% is the typical heritability across many traits, this specific dataset yields 60%. Always calculate rather than assuming typical values.
D: 100% heritability (completely genetic with no environmental influence) would require rMZ = 1.0 and rDZ = 0.5, not the values given.

Q3
Early candidate gene studies frequently failed to replicate because:
A) Researchers deliberately fabricated results in the early 2000s
B) Initial samples were underpowered, publication bias favored positive findings, and multiple testing wasn't corrected
C) DNA sequencing technology was too primitive to identify correct genetic variants
D) Genes change too rapidly across generations for stable associations to exist
Explanation:
Candidate gene replication failures resulted from methodological issues, not fraud or technological limitations. Small initial samples (typically N < 200) lacked statistical power to detect small effect sizes reliably, producing inflated effect estimates in significant findings (winner's curse). Publication bias meant journals preferentially published positive associations while null results languished in file drawers, creating a literature systematically overrepresenting false positives. Inadequate correction for multiple comparisons (testing many candidate genes, polymorphisms, and phenotype measures without adjusting alpha levels) inflated Type I error rates. Meta-analyses revealed true effects much smaller than initial reports, requiring samples exceeding 10,000 participants for adequate power.
From Lecture: "Candidate gene studies... yet replication failures became endemic as independent laboratories failed to reproduce initial findings... Meta-analyses revealed systematic publication bias favouring positive associations, inadequate statistical power in initial studies (typical N < 200), and failure to correct for multiple comparisons."
Why other options are incorrect:
A: Replication failures resulted from methodological issues and statistical problems, not deliberate fraud. Most researchers conducted honest but underpowered studies.
C: Genotyping technology was adequate for identifying common variants. The problem wasn't detecting genotypes but having sufficient power to detect small effects.
D: Genes are extremely stable across generations (mutation rates ~10⁻⁹ per base per generation). Instability doesn't explain replication failures.

Q5
Why is phenotypic heterogeneity (convergent phenotypes) problematic for genetic association studies?
A) Heterogeneous samples always lack sufficient statistical power regardless of size
B) Different etiological pathways produce similar behavioral outcomes, diluting gene-phenotype associations
C) Phenotypic measures become unreliable when samples include diverse individuals
D) Genetic variants only affect homogeneous populations, not heterogeneous ones
Explanation:
Phenotypic heterogeneity occurs when multiple distinct etiological pathways produce similar observable behaviors—what look like the same phenotype are actually different conditions. The impulsivity example illustrates this: elevated impulsivity characterizes ADHD (dopamine signaling deficits), antisocial personality (reward hypersensitivity), traumatic brain injury (prefrontal damage), and substance use disorders (altered reinforcement learning). Each pathway involves different genes, yet all produce high impulsivity scores. Aggregating these into a single "impulsive" sample guarantees genetic heterogeneity—different causal genes for each subtype—such that associations dilute toward null. Critically, large heterogeneous samples (N=5,000 mixed-etiology) perform WORSE than small homogeneous samples (N=500 pure ADHD) for detecting specific gene-phenotype associations.
From Study Guide: "Elevated impulsivity characterizes individuals with ADHD, antisocial personality disorder, traumatic brain injury, substance use disorders, and personality-based impulsivity... Aggregating these phenotypically similar but aetiologically distinct groups guarantees genetic heterogeneity—different genes influence each pathway—such that gene-phenotype associations dilute toward null."
Why other options are incorrect:
A: Heterogeneous samples can have adequate statistical power for overall effects, but mixing etiologies reduces effect sizes for any specific genetic variant. It's not purely a power issue but a construct validity problem.
C: Phenotypic measures can be perfectly reliable (consistent, reproducible) while still being heterogeneous. The problem is validity (measuring the same underlying construct), not reliability.
D: Genetic variants affect individuals regardless of sample composition. The issue is detecting those effects statistically, not whether effects exist biologically.

C: Genes within networks have specialized, complementary functions, not identical interchangeable functions. Each contributes uniquely to system function.
D: Gene networks operate throughout development and adulthood, regulating neurotransmitter systems, synaptic plasticity, and ongoing brain function, not just embryogenesis.

2️⃣ Mediation vs Moderation: Understanding Interactions Conceptual
Q8
What is the fundamental question that MEDIATION answers?
A) When or under what conditions does X affect Y?
B) How or why does X affect Y through what mechanism?
C) Does X have a stronger or weaker effect depending on level of Z?
D) Will the relationship between X and Y reverse direction in different contexts?
Explanation:
Mediation answers mechanistic questions (HOW/WHY) by identifying intermediate variables that transmit effects from independent to dependent variables. The mediator (M) explains the process through which X influences Y via a sequential causal chain: X → M → Y. In behavior genetics, mediation analyses identify neurobiological mechanisms linking genotypes to phenotypes, transforming abstract correlations into mechanistically interpretable pathways. For example: HTR1B gene variant → altered serotonin neurotransmission → reduced empathy → callous-unemotional traits. Without specifying mediators, genetic associations remain descriptively interesting but scientifically unexplained.
From Study Guide: "Mediation asks 'HOW or WHY does the independent variable affect the dependent variable?' by identifying intermediate mechanisms that transmit effects along a causal chain... explaining the relationship between the genotype to the phenotype."
Why other options are incorrect:
A: This describes MODERATION, which identifies boundary conditions (when/under what conditions) rather than mechanisms (how/why).
C: Effect strength changes depending on moderator levels describe moderation (conditional effects), not mediation (mechanistic pathways).
D: Relationship direction reversals (crossover interactions) indicate moderation, showing effects differ across contexts rather than explaining mechanisms.

Q10
What distinguishes MODERATION from mediation in terms of causal structure?
A) Moderation involves three variables while mediation involves only two
B) Moderation uses experimental designs while mediation uses correlational designs
C) Moderation involves conditional effects (X×Z → Y) while mediation involves sequential effects (X → M → Y)
D) Moderation measures continuous variables while mediation measures categorical variables
Explanation:
The key distinction is causal structure: Mediation has sequential causation (X causes M, which causes Y, forming a chain) while moderation has conditional causation (X's effect on Y depends on level of Z, where Z defines contexts). In mediation, the mediator is an intermediate variable temporally and mechanistically positioned between predictor and outcome. In moderation, the moderator variable interacts multiplicatively with the predictor, visible statistically as significant X×Z interaction terms and graphically as non-parallel lines when plotting X-Y relationships at different Z levels. Both involve three variables, but their roles and relationships differ fundamentally.
From Study Guide: "Mediation involves sequential causation—X causes M, which causes Y—such that the mediator temporally and mechanistically links predictor to outcome. Moderation involves conditional causation—X's effect on Y depends on level of Z—such that the moderator defines contexts where relationships strengthen, weaken, or change direction."
Why other options are incorrect:
A: Both mediation and moderation involve three variables (X, Y, plus M or Z). Variable count doesn't distinguish them; their relationships do.
B: Both can use experimental or correlational designs. Design type doesn't define the conceptual distinction between mechanism vs boundary conditions.
D: Both mediation and moderation can involve continuous or categorical variables. Measurement scale doesn't determine whether an interaction represents mechanism or conditioning.

Differential susceptibility theory proposes that genetic variants determine:
A) Risk for psychopathology only, with no benefits in positive environments
B) Environmental sensitivity "for better AND for worse" across positive and negative contexts
C) Complete protection from environmental influences regardless of context

Explanation:
Differential susceptibility theory proposes individuals differ genetically in environmental sensitivity rather than vulnerability versus resilience. Highly susceptible individuals (orchid children) respond strongly to BOTH negative and positive environmental inputs—producing catastrophic outcomes in adverse environments yet superior outcomes in supportive environments. This "for better AND for worse" pattern distinguishes differential susceptibility from diathesis-stress models that conceptualize genetic variants as pure risk alleles. The theory emerges from evolutionary biology: genetic diversity in sensitivity provides adaptive advantages across varying ecological niches, maintaining both sensitive and stable phenotypes through balancing selection rather than eliminating sensitive alleles as maladaptive.
From Study Guide: "Differential susceptibility theory proposes that individuals differ genetically not in vulnerability versus resilience, but in environmental sensitivity—some individuals respond strongly to both negative and positive environmental inputs (highly susceptible), while others show stable outcomes across diverse environments (low susceptibility)."
Why other options are incorrect:
A: This describes diathesis-stress models (asymmetrical vulnerability only), not differential susceptibility (symmetrical sensitivity to both adversity and enrichment).
C: Complete environmental protection would mean genetic variants eliminate environmental effects entirely. Differential susceptibility proposes heightened sensitivity, not immunity.
D: Responses are highly systematic, not random. Orchid genotypes consistently show environmental sensitivity while dandelion genotypes show stability—predictable patterns, not randomness.
