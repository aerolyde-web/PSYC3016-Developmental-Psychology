LO1-A Triadic Interactions: The Social-Cognitive Revolution (9-15 months)

LO1-B Social Referencing: Visual Cliff (12 months)
"Describe the visual cliff paradigm and explain what it reveals about social referencing..."
# Component Details Statistical Anchor
1 Sorce et al. (1985)
Method		 12-month-olds on plexiglass with apparent drop-off (visual cliff). Creates genuine uncertainty. Mother positioned across cliff displays emotional expression. Measure crossing behavior Same physical setup, only difference = social information
2 Joy/Happiness
Safety Signal		 Broad smile, positive vocalizations. 74% crossed. Mean crossing time = 45s. Increased approach behaviors 74% crossed
3 Fear
Warning Signal		 Wide eyes, open mouth, backward head movement. 0% crossed. Mean retreat time = 1.08 min. Increased distress 0% crossed
4 Anger
Negative Valence		 Furrowed brow, tight lips. 11% crossed. Increased freezing. Generalized negative valence even though inappropriate emotion 11% crossed
5 Interest/Neutral
Default		 Attentive without clear valence. 73% crossed (similar to joy but slower decision latency). Default to exploration when no prohibition 73% crossed

‚ö° 3 PREREQUISITES FOR SOCIAL REFERENCING (Baldwin & Moses, 1996)

1. Signal Decoding: Discriminate facial expressions as affective meanings
2. Referential Specificity: Map emotion to CORRECT environmental referent (cliff, not background)
3. Communicative Appreciation: Understand information can be transmitted between agents
‚ö†Ô∏è REFERENTIAL TRAP: If infant sees mother fearing a dog through the window but there's also a robot toy nearby, infant may misattribute fear to robot. Social referencing requires PRECISE referential alignment ‚Äî joint attention to SAME environmental feature. This explains why pointing/gaze checking typically precedes emotional signaling.

LO1-D Chimpanzee Food Competition: Uninformed vs Misinformed (Brauer et al., 2007)
"Compare chimpanzee performance with uninformed versus misinformed competitors..."
Condition Setup Retrieval % Interpretation
Hidden
(Uninformed)		 Food inside bucket, visible ONLY to subordinate chimp 42% SUCCESS: Understands dominant doesn't know about unseen objects
Visible
(Informed)		 Food on top, visible to BOTH chimps 27% Avoids competition when dominant knows location

(Misinformed)		 Both see food at A; subordinate sees move to B (dominant doesn't) 33% (chance) FAILURE: Cannot track/exploit false beliefs

‚ö° CRITICAL INTERPRETATION: UNINFORMED vs MISINFORMED

SUCCESS with UNINFORMED: Chimps track "saw/didn't see" = binary states (ON/OFF)
FAILURE with MISINFORMED: Cannot maintain DUAL representations (reality + outdated belief)
Cognitive Load Hypothesis: Tracking 2 objects √ó 2 knowledge states + dominance dynamics exceeds capacity
Alternative: Fundamental metarepresentational limit ‚Äî can't represent beliefs ABOUT beliefs
LO1-E Implicit vs Explicit False Belief Understanding
"Explain the implicit-explicit dissociation in false belief understanding..."
Type Age Method Key Study
IMPLICIT
Automatic		 15 months Looking time measures. Longer looking at belief-INCONSISTENT searches (violation of expectation) Onishi & Baillargeon: Watermelon task ‚Äî infant looks longer when agent searches where object IS rather than where agent BELIEVES it to be
EXPLICIT
Deliberate		 4 years Verbal prediction. "Where will Sally look?" Child must articulate prediction based on false belief Sally-Anne Task: Sally puts marble in basket, Anne moves to box while Sally gone. Where will Sally look?
‚ö†Ô∏è THE PARADOX: Why 3-year gap between implicit (15mo) and explicit (4yr)? Two hypotheses: (1) Different cognitive systems (implicit = automatic expectation; explicit = deliberate reasoning), or (2) Same system but performance factors (language demands, inhibitory control, response selection) mask earlier competence.

LO4 Implications for ASD & Child Development
"How does social cognition research inform understanding of autism spectrum disorder?"
Finding Implication for ASD Clinical Relevance
Joint Attention Deficits ASD: Reduced joint attention, gaze following, declarative pointing. Triadic skills impaired early in development Early marker for screening. Target intervention
