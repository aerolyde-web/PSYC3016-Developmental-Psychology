THEORY OF MIND (L05-L07)

Explain the Two-Pathway Hypothesis and evidence for it.

The Two-Pathway Hypothesis proposes that Theory of Mind comprises two computationally independent systems that develop asynchronously. Implicit ToM is fast, automatic, and unconscious, emerging around 15 months as demonstrated by Onishi & Baillargeon (2005), who found infants looked significantly longer at belief-incongruent actions (39.3s vs 29.1s). This system is shared with great apes and relies on the mirror neuron system.

Explicit ToM is slow, deliberate, and conscious, emerging around age 4-5 as measured by verbal prediction tasks like Sally-Anne, where ~85% of 4-year-olds pass versus ~20% of 3-year-olds. This system is uniquely human and requires the mentalizing network (mPFC, TPJ).

The systems' independence is proven by double dissociation: great apes pass implicit but fail explicit tasks, while Senju et al. showed HFASD adults fail implicit (no anticipatory gaze) despite passing explicit tasks verbally. Additionally, Down Syndrome children pass Sally-Anne at 86% despite lower IQ than ASD children who fail at 80%, proving ToM is modular rather than a function of general intelligence.

Describe the developmental precursors to Theory of Mind.

Theory of Mind develops through a sequence of precursor abilities that build upon each other from infancy. The 9-month revolution marks the shift from dyadic to triadic attention, with Carpenter et al. finding joint attention at 9 months predicts language at 24 months (r = .52). Failure at this stage is an early ASD risk marker.

By 12 months, infants demonstrate social referencing—Sorce et al. showed 0% crossed the visual cliff when mothers displayed fear versus 74% with joy. Liszkowski found infants selectively point to inform uninformed adults (2.1:1 ratio).

Implicit false belief emerges at 15 months (Onishi & Baillargeon), followed by desire understanding at 18 months—Repacholi & Gopnik's broccoli task showed 18-month-olds give the experimenter's preference, not their own. Desire precedes belief because desires are always "true" while beliefs require meta-representation to understand they can be false. Explicit false belief finally emerges at 4-5 years when prefrontal maturation enables inhibitory control over reality bias.

What does the ASD vs Down Syndrome comparison reveal about ToM?

The comparison between ASD and Down Syndrome provides critical evidence that Theory of Mind is domain-specific and modular rather than a function of general intelligence. Baron-Cohen et al. (1985) found that children with Down Syndrome passed the Sally-Anne task at 86%—equivalent to typically developing controls—despite having significantly lower IQ than children with ASD.

In striking contrast, children with ASD failed at 80% despite their higher cognitive abilities. If ToM were simply "being smart about people," higher-IQ ASD children should outperform lower-IQ DS children. The opposite result demonstrates that ToM involves dedicated cognitive architecture that can be selectively impaired (ASD) or spared (DS) independent of general intelligence.

This pattern constitutes a double dissociation that proves ToM is modular. Further evidence from Senju et al. shows that even high-functioning ASD adults who pass explicit ToM tasks verbally fail implicit ToM measures (no anticipatory gaze), suggesting they compensate through learned rules rather than automatic social cognition. Teaching explicit rules does not restore implicit processing.

What is shared intentionality and why is it uniquely human?

Shared intentionality, described by Tomasello as "we-mode" psychology, refers to the capacity to establish joint goals, mutual knowledge, and collaborative roles toward common purposes. This capacity underlies human cultural cognition, enabling language, morality, and social institutions.

While great apes share implicit ToM with humans—passing violation-of-expectation paradigms—they lack shared intentionality. Apes collaborate only when each individual action independently yields reward; they cannot coordinate complementary roles toward a shared goal. Critically, Liszkowski demonstrated that human 12-month-olds point declaratively to share information, while chimpanzees never point to inform—only to request (imperative pointing).

The neural basis for this uniqueness lies in the elaboration of the mentalizing network (mPFC, TPJ) in humans, which supports explicit ToM and enables the recursive representation of shared mental states ("I know that you know that I know"). This capacity for establishing common ground is what makes human cooperation, teaching, and cumulative culture possible.

MORAL DEVELOPMENT (L08-L09)

Explain the prerequisites for moral agency and their developmental sequence.

Moral agency—the capacity to be held responsible for one's actions—requires a developmental cascade of cognitive achievements that must emerge in a specific sequence. The first prerequisite is self-awareness, demonstrated by mirror self-recognition emerging around 18-24 months (Rouge test: ~25% at 18mo → ~70% at 24mo). Without recognizing oneself as a distinct entity, the concept "I did something wrong" is meaningless.

The second prerequisite is Theory of Mind, emerging around age 4 with false belief understanding. Moral judgment requires understanding that others are mental agents whose behavior stems from beliefs and intentions. Building on this, intention reading enables distinguishing accidental from intentional harm—crucial for attributing moral responsibility.

Each stage is necessary but not sufficient for the next. Self-awareness enables but doesn't guarantee ToM; ToM enables but doesn't guarantee mature moral judgment. This cascade explains why moral development continues well past basic ToM acquisition—the prerequisites are in place, but the moral reasoning system that depends on them continues maturing through childhood.

Describe Kohlberg's stages and the main critiques of his theory.

Kohlberg proposed six stages of moral reasoning across three levels, measuring the structure of reasoning rather than behavioral outcomes. The Pre-Conventional level (Stages 1-2) focuses on punishment avoidance and self-interest, requiring no ToM. The Conventional level (Stages 3-4) involves social approval and law maintenance, requires ToM, and characterizes most adults. The Post-Conventional level (Stages 5-6) involves social contracts and universal principles, achieved by only 10-15% (Stage 5) and ~1% (Stage 6).

Seven major critiques challenge the theory: (1) reasoning doesn't predict behavior; (2) Gilligan (1982) identified gender bias from the all-male sample, arguing care ethics (Stage 3) was unfairly ranked below justice (Stage 4); (3) cultural bias favors Western individualism; (4) people show different stages across contexts; (5) hypothetical dilemmas lack ecological validity; (6) young children can't articulate reasoning they possess; (7) Stage 6 is too rare to validate empirically.

Gilligan's critique is particularly significant: she argued that care (emphasizing relationships) and justice (emphasizing rules) represent equally valid moral orientations rather than a developmental hierarchy. The theory's greatest limitation remains that knowing the right thing (reasoning) doesn't predict doing the right thing (behavior).

Explain the Moral-Conventional Distinction and what it reveals about early moral cognition.

Social Domain Theory (Turiel, 1983) proposes that moral rules and social conventions constitute distinct cognitive domains distinguished by three diagnostic criteria. Moral transgressions (hitting, stealing) are authority-independent (wrong even if permitted), rule-independent (wrong even without explicit rules), and universal (wrong anywhere). Social conventions (dress codes, table manners) show the opposite pattern—dependent on authority, rules, and context.

Smetana (1981) demonstrated that children as young as 30 months reliably distinguish moral from conventional transgressions, with no significant age differences from 30-57 months. This remarkably early emergence challenges Kohlberg's stage theory, suggesting children possess sophisticated domain-specific moral intuitions before they can articulate stage-based reasoning.

The Harm Principle explains domain boundaries: adding harm to any behavior converts it from conventional to moral. Eating with hands is merely unconventional, but if it somehow harmed someone, it becomes a moral transgression. This early M/C discrimination suggests morality may be foundational rather than derived from general cognitive development, with the presence of a victim or harm serving as the critical factor that triggers moral—rather than conventional—reasoning.

Explain Blair's Violence Inhibition Mechanism and what psychopathy reveals about morality.

Blair's Violence Inhibition Mechanism (VIM) proposes that moral development depends on an innate emotional system that responds aversively to others' distress cues. When typically developing individuals perceive distress signals—crying, fearful expressions—the VIM activates, producing autonomic arousal and aversive affect that inhibits aggression. Through development, this creates associations between harmful actions and negative emotions, providing the affective foundation for distinguishing moral from conventional transgressions.

Psychopathy provides crucial evidence through a natural dissociation. Individuals with high psychopathic traits show 2.14 times more errors (4.73 vs 2.20) recognizing fear and sadness specifically, with normal recognition of other emotions. Critically, Blair demonstrated they show a 33% smaller moral-conventional distinction gap (1.23 vs 1.84), treating moral transgressions as if they were merely rule violations.

This pattern—intact reasoning plus impaired emotion equals moral deficits—proves that emotion is necessary, not merely helpful, for moral development. The Sentimentalist tradition (Hume, Darwin, Blair) argues that Kohlberg's purely cognitive approach missed the emotional substrate that makes moral distinctions possible. Without the aversive response to distress, you can learn rules about what's prohibited but cannot distinguish intrinsically wrong acts from merely rule-dependent ones.

Describe the Happy Victimizer paradigm and what it reveals about moral development.

The Happy Victimizer paradigm (Nunner-Winkler & Sodian, 1988) ingeniously separates moral knowledge from moral emotion by asking children not whether an act is wrong, but how the wrongdoer feels afterward. A protagonist chooses self-interest over morality (e.g., stealing a toy). All ages judge stealing as wrong, but when asked "How does the protagonist feel?", young children predict positive emotions while older children predict guilt.

The developmental trajectory shows: age 4-5 predict "happy" (+1.35 on a -2 to +2 scale), shifting through mixed emotions at 5-7, to guilt at age 8+ (-0.50)—a total shift of 1.85 units. This reveals that moral knowledge precedes emotional binding: young children know stealing is wrong but don't yet anticipate the emotional consequences.

Importantly, HV at ages 4-5 is a normal developmental stage, not early psychopathy—it only indicates problems if it persists past age 8. Krettenauer et al. (2008) showed priming affects responses: focusing children on moral evaluation increases guilt attribution, while focusing on desires increases HV responses. This explains the knowledge-behavior gap: knowing something is wrong is insufficient if that knowledge isn't emotionally resonant enough to generate anticipatory guilt that deters transgression.

How does intention-outcome weighting develop in moral judgment?

Piaget (1932) first documented that young children's moral judgments focus primarily on outcomes rather than intentions—a phenomenon called moral realism. In his classic comparison, children under 7 judge accidentally breaking 15 cups as worse than intentionally breaking 1 cup while misbehaving. Heyman & Gelman (1998) systematically quantified this using crossed intention-by-outcome designs.

Development involves a shift in weighting, not a binary switch. The judgment formula J = α×Intention + β×Outcome shows kindergarteners weight outcomes heavily (5:1 ratio; α=0.3, β=0.7), while adults weight intentions (4:1 ratio; α=0.8, β=0.2). Critically, young children use both information sources—they don't ignore intentions entirely but simply give them less weight.

This shift depends on Theory of Mind—you cannot weight intentions you cannot represent. Killen et al. (2011) showed false belief understanding predicts earlier transition to intention-weighted judgment. Outcomes never become irrelevant even in adults (accidental harm still generates some liability), but intention becomes the primary factor. The developmental achievement is giving intentions priority, not exclusivity, in moral evaluation.

What does the Malti & Krettenauer meta-analysis reveal about moral emotion and behavior?

Malti & Krettenauer's (2013) meta-analysis of 42 studies (n > 8,000) provides the most comprehensive evidence that moral emotions—particularly guilt—predict actual moral behavior. The analysis revealed effect sizes of r = +0.20 for guilt predicting prosocial behavior and r = -0.35 for guilt predicting reduced antisocial behavior.

The asymmetry between these effects (1.75× stronger for antisocial) reveals guilt's functional nature as an inhibitory mechanism—"emotional brakes" that prevent transgression by generating anticipated negative affect. Guilt can stop you from harming but doesn't create motivation to help. This explains why interventions focused solely on guilt may effectively reduce harmful behavior but be insufficient for promoting active prosociality.

These findings validate the Sentimentalist position that moral development involves more than cognitive understanding—emotional engagement with moral rules is necessary for behavioral compliance. The findings also connect to Happy Victimizer research: children who attribute guilt to wrongdoers are themselves less likely to transgress. Building prosocial behavior may require cultivating additional emotions like empathic concern and pride in helping that provide positive motivation beyond mere guilt avoidance.

Compare Rationalist and Sentimentalist approaches to moral development.

The Rationalist tradition, exemplified by Kohlberg, proposes that moral behavior flows from moral reasoning: THINK → BEHAVE. This approach focuses on cognitive stages of moral judgment, using dilemmas to assess reasoning structure independent of content. However, Kohlberg's stages only weakly predict actual behavior (r = .30-.40 per Blasi, 1980), and most adults remain at conventional stages (3-4) rather than reaching post-conventional reasoning.

The Sentimentalist tradition (Hume, Darwin, Blair) proposes that moral behavior flows from moral emotion: FEEL → BEHAVE. Darwin's dual-instinct model suggests guilt emerges when persistent social instincts outweigh fading appetitive instincts after transgression. Blair's VIM model shows distress cues trigger autonomic responses that inhibit aggression—and when this system is impaired (psychopathy), the moral-conventional distinction collapses despite intact reasoning.

Evidence favors integration: neither approach alone predicts behavior. Psychopathy demonstrates reasoning intact + emotion impaired = moral deficits. The Happy Victimizer paradigm shows knowledge without emotional binding fails to deter transgression. Malti's meta-analysis confirms moral emotion predicts behavior (r = -.35 for antisocial). Modern approaches recognize that mature moral functioning requires both cognitive understanding AND affective binding working together.
