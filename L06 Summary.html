PSYC3016 - Lecture 06: Social Cognition II - Theory of Mind

Overarching Question: How does theory of mind development proceed through a hierarchical yet paradoxical sequence—beginning with triadic interactions at 9-15 months (joint engagement at 9 months, gaze following at 10 months, point following at 11 months, declarative pointing at 12 months progressing to imitative learning at 13 months), advancing through social referencing at 12 months (Sorce et al., 1985 visual cliff study showing 74% crossing rate with mother's joy versus 0% with fear and 11% with anger revealing emotion-specific action modulation), establishing perception-knowledge links at 12-14 months (Liszkowski et al., 2007 demonstrating 2.4-fold increase in pointing when adults missed events: M = 1.27 points for uninformed adults versus M = 0.53 for informed, reflecting selective informing), encountering the uninformed-versus-misinformed distinction where 12-14 month infants and chimpanzees successfully track who lacks information but fail when agents hold outdated representations requiring dual metarepresentation (maintaining reality + false belief simultaneously), then surprisingly showing implicit false belief understanding at 15 months (Onishi & Baillargeon, 2005 watermelon paradigm revealing violation detection with Δ = 8.5 seconds for false belief incongruent actions versus Δ = 16.5 seconds for true belief violations)—yet not achieving explicit false belief understanding through verbal prediction until age 4 years (~85% Sally-Anne task pass rate), creating a 2.5-year implicit-explicit gap explained variously as conceptual change (behavioral rules to mentalistic understanding), performance factors (executive function maturation), or dual systems (implicit fast prediction versus explicit deliberate reasoning), while paradoxically demonstrating false belief sensitivity (15 months implicit) before mirror self-recognition (18-24 months) challenging assumed developmental hierarchies where self-awareness should precede other-awareness, with comparative evidence from chimpanzees (Bräuer et al., 2007 food competition study showing subordinates preferring hidden food at 54% versus visible at 26%, with faster approach speeds for hidden M = 8.3 seconds versus visible M = 14.7 seconds, yet performing at chance 33% on false belief conditions) revealing that tracking perception-to-knowledge links ("what others know from what they've seen") represents a shared evolutionary capacity while representing outdated beliefs (false beliefs proper) remains uniquely challenging across species?

Comprehensive Summary

Theory of mind development begins with the critical transition from dyadic to triadic interactions occurring around 9-15 months, where dyadic interaction (< 6 months) involves only infant ↔ caregiver face-to-face reciprocal engagement without external referents or evidence that the infant understands others possess minds, contrasting with triadic interaction (9-15 months) incorporating infant ↔ caregiver ↔ object structure requiring coordinated attention (not parallel play) and understanding intentional agents with attentional states, progressing through five sequential milestones documented by Carpenter et al. (1998) showing nearly universal achievement by 13 months: (1) joint engagement emerging ~9 months through gaze alternation patterns (object → caregiver → object demonstrating awareness of shared attention), (2) gaze following emerging ~10 months where infants track adult's line of sight to locate objects understanding that looking direction indicates intentional focus, (3) point following emerging ~11 months where infants look where adults point comprehending gestural communication, (4) declarative pointing emerging ~12 months where infants point to share interest rather than request objects (proto-declarative versus proto-imperative functions), and (5) imitative learning emerging ~13 months where children copy arbitrary actions beyond goal achievement revealing attention to demonstrator's intentions not merely end-states, establishing the critical conceptual foundation that triadic interaction requires coordinated attention where both partners jointly attend to an external referent with mutual awareness of sharing that attentional focus, distinguishing genuine joint engagement from incidental co-attention where two individuals happen to look at the same object without metacognitive awareness of the sharing. Social referencing emerges at 12 months as the capacity to use others' emotional appraisals to resolve uncertainty about novel situations, paradigmatically demonstrated through Sorce et al.'s (1985) visual cliff study where 12-month-old infants placed on the shallow side of a plexiglass-covered apparent drop-off encounter ambiguous risk (safe structurally yet visually appearing dangerous) prompting them to reference their mother's facial expression before deciding whether to cross toward an attractive toy, with crossing rates showing dramatic emotion-specific modulation: 74% crossed when mother displayed joy/happiness (positive affect signals safety), 0% crossed when mother displayed fear (negative affect matched to environmental threat creates prohibition), 11% crossed when mother displayed anger (negative generalization where general negativity creates avoidance despite anger not being environmentally appropriate), and 73% crossed when mother displayed interest (neutral exploratory signal without prohibition), revealing that social referencing requires three cognitive prerequisites: (1) signal decoding capacity discriminating facial expressions and their affective meanings beyond mere visual pattern recognition, (2) referential specificity mapping the perceived emotion to the correct environmental referent rather than treating all emotional displays as general behavioral cues, and (3) communicative appreciation understanding that information is being intentionally transmitted between agents for the infant's benefit rather than merely observing emotional reactions. The perception-knowledge link establishes that infants understand "seeing leads to knowing" by 12-14 months, tracking who possesses information based on their perceptual access, elegantly demonstrated through Liszkowski et al.'s (2007) selective informing paradigm where 12-month-olds watched exciting puppet events while an adult either attended (looked toward event) or missed (looked away), then measuring infants' declarative pointing rates: when adults attended the event infants produced M = 0.53 points (baseline sharing), but when adults missed the event infants produced M = 1.27 points (2.4-fold increase, selective informing to update uninformed adults), with the increase amplified further to M = 1.65 points when adults missed events and displayed positive affect (combining epistemic motivation to inform with affiliative motivation to share enjoyable experiences) versus M = 1.27 points when adults missed with neutral affect (epistemic motivation alone), establishing that infants demonstrate dual motivations for communicative pointing: epistemic informing (higher when adults lack knowledge) and affiliative sharing (higher when experiences are positive and bonding-relevant), revealing sophisticated understanding that perceptual access determines knowledge states and that communication can selectively update others' representations. The critical uninformed-versus-misinformed distinction reveals profound constraints on early social cognition where uninformed agents lack information entirely (didn't see toy hidden, creating absence of knowledge) requiring only binary tracking of presence/absence of perceptual access with single representational state, while misinformed agents possess incorrect information (saw toy at location A, doesn't know it moved to location B) requiring dual conflicting representations maintaining both reality (toy now at B) and the agent's outdated false belief (agent thinks toy at A), with 12-14 month infants successfully tracking uninformed states (pointing more when adults miss events) yet failing misinformed states (not adjusting behavior when adults hold outdated beliefs), and critically showing the identical pattern in chimpanzees (successfully exploiting uninformed competitors yet failing to exploit misinformed competitors), suggesting that metarepresentation—simultaneously maintaining reality and false belief as distinct yet related mental contents—represents either a fundamental conceptual limit emerging later or a working memory/cognitive load constraint where dual representation exceeds available processing capacity in young infants and non-human primates. Comparative evidence from chimpanzee food competition paradigms illuminates which components of theory of mind are shared versus uniquely human, with Bräuer et al.'s (2007) elegant study placing subordinate chimpanzees in competitive contexts with dominant chimpanzees where food items are either visible (on top of bucket where both dominant and subordinate see it) or hidden (inside bucket where only subordinate knows location), revealing strategic behavioral patterns: subordinates retrieved food only 27% of trials when dominant could see food (high competition risk), 42% when food was hidden from dominant (reduced competition due to dominant's ignorance), and when offered simultaneous choice between visible and hidden food subordinates preferentially chose hidden food 54% versus visible 26% (demonstrating understanding that dominant's perceptual access predicts competitive interference), with approach speed data confirming strategic calculation where subordinates approached hidden food faster (M = 8.3 seconds) than visible food (M = 14.7 seconds) indicating reduced anxiety when dominant lacks knowledge, yet crucially when experimenters created false belief conditions where dominant initially saw food placement then food was moved while dominant's view was occluded (dominant holds outdated belief about location), subordinates performed at chance 33% showing no strategic exploitation of dominant's false belief, establishing that chimpanzees robustly track "what others know from what they've seen" (perception → knowledge causal link enabling uninformed state tracking) but cannot represent outdated representations constituting false beliefs, suggesting this capacity represents the critical evolutionary innovation distinguishing human social cognition. Implicit false belief understanding emerges surprisingly early at 15 months as measured through violation-of-expectation looking time paradigms, revolutionarily demonstrated by Onishi & Baillargeon's (2005) watermelon paradigm employing a 2×2 design manipulating (1) actor's belief state (true belief: actor saw object move to yellow box; false belief: actor's view was occluded during move to yellow so actor maintains outdated belief object remains in green box) and (2) actor's search location (green or yellow), with looking time results revealing conceptual understanding: in true belief conditions when actors searched green box despite seeing the move to yellow, infants looked M = 27.0 seconds (dramatic violation of rational action given knowledge), versus baseline M = 10.5 seconds when actors correctly searched yellow after seeing the move; critically in false belief conditions when actors searched yellow box (reality-matching but belief-incongruent because actor didn't see the move) infants looked M = 20.0 seconds (violation because actor should search based on belief not reality), versus M = 11.5 seconds when actors searched green box (belief-congruent despite reality mismatch), with violation effect magnitudes of Δ = 8.5 seconds for false belief incongruent actions and Δ = 16.5 seconds for true belief incongruent actions both significantly exceeding baseline, demonstrating that 15-month-olds expect actors to search based on their beliefs (what they think is true) rather than reality (what actually is true), constituting genuine metarepresentational understanding. The implicit-explicit gap describes the puzzling 2.5-year developmental window separating implicit false belief understanding at 15 months (measured through automatic violation detection in looking time paradigms requiring only mismatch detection without deliberate prediction) from explicit false belief understanding at 4 years (~85% pass rate on Sally-Anne verbal prediction tasks asking "Where will Sally look for her marble?" requiring deliberate prediction plus verbal justification while inhibiting reality-based prepotent responses), with three competing theoretical explanations: (1) conceptual change accounts proposing that 15-month-olds apply behavioral rules ("search where you last saw it") without genuinely representing mental states as mental, with true mentalistic understanding emerging only at age 4 through conceptual revolution differentiating mental representations from reality, (2) performance factor accounts proposing that conceptual understanding remains constant but executive function development (particularly inhibitory control enabling suppression of reality-based responses) determines explicit task performance, with implicit tasks succeeding earlier because they don't require response inhibition, and (3) dual systems accounts proposing parallel implicit systems (fast automatic prediction mechanisms operating outside awareness) and explicit systems (slow deliberate reasoning requiring conscious effort) that develop asynchronously, with implicit systems maturing by 15 months while explicit systems await age 4. The paradoxical developmental sequence creates hierarchy disruption where false belief understanding (15 months implicit) emerges before mirror self-recognition (18-24 months Rouge test with 50% pass rate), contradicting intuitive building-block models proposing that self-awareness constitutes the conceptual foundation enabling other-awareness, suggesting instead that recognizing that others hold beliefs different from reality (metarepresentation of others' mental states) operates through distinct cognitive mechanisms from recognizing one's own identity in mirror reflections (self-other differentiation and integrated cross-modal body schema), with these capacities developing along parallel pathways without strict hierarchical prerequisites despite their intuitive logical relationships. Methodological considerations highlight that measurement validity questions whether looking time paradigms (implicit measures) and verbal prediction tasks (explicit measures) assess the same underlying construct or fundamentally different cognitive capacities, with skeptics arguing that increased looking to expectation violations might reflect low-level statistical learning about action-outcome regularities rather than genuine mental state attribution, while advocates argue that carefully controlled conditions where violations are perceptually familiar yet conceptually surprising (same motor pattern, different belief-congruence) isolate mentalistic expectations from perceptual preferences, compounded by cognitive load interpretations proposing that failure on misinformed (dual object, dual location, dual representation) but success on uninformed (single absence) reflects working memory limits rather than conceptual limits, though this faces challenges explaining why enriched executive function support in older children doesn't rescue misinformed task performance if load alone explains failure. The cross-species comparative framework reveals that chimpanzees succeed in ecologically valid competitive contexts (food competition activating evolutionarily-prepared social monitoring mechanisms) yet struggle in cooperative paradigms (experimenter-focused helping tasks requiring human-specific cooperative communication), raising questions about whether paradigm design artifacts create species differences or whether genuine cognitive distinctions separate human cooperative-communicative orientation from great ape competitive-strategic orientation, with evidence that both species track perception-to-knowledge links (gaze following, preferential approach to hidden food when competitor uninformed) yet both fail explicit false belief tasks before age 4 in humans and entirely in chimpanzees, suggesting metarepresentation of outdated beliefs represents a uniquely challenging cognitive achievement potentially dependent on language development providing representational tools for encoding conflicting propositions ("the toy is in the yellow box" + "Sally thinks the toy is in the green box") or cultural scaffolding through pretend play and narrative enabling practice with counterfactual reasoning, ultimately establishing that theory of mind development unfolds through hierarchical stages (dyadic → triadic → perception-knowledge → implicit false belief → explicit false belief) yet with surprising non-linearities (false belief before self-recognition) and profound implicit-explicit dissociations requiring integration of conceptual change (what is understood), performance demands (what can be demonstrated), and measurement validity (what tasks actually assess) to explain the full developmental trajectory from early social cognition to mature metarepresentational theory of mind.
