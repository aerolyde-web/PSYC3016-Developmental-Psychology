L01-L04: Nature vs Nurture & Behaviour Genetics | PSYC3016 SAQ-Ready Cheat Sheet

1. PHILOSOPHICAL FOUNDATIONS: The Nature-Nurture Dialectic

  • Nativism/Rationalism (Chomsky, Plato): Innate cognitive modules genetically inherited, present from birth. Poverty of Stimulus argument: Children acquire complex grammar despite impoverished input—no negative evidence provided, only fragmentary sentences heard, yet grammar complete by ~age 5. Universal Grammar (UG) = innate computational capacity fills gap between input received and knowledge acquired. Key distinction: spoken language naturally acquired; reading/writing NOT innate, requires explicit teaching.
  • Empiricism/Tabula Rasa (Watson, Locke): Blank slate; environment shapes ALL outcomes. Watson's claim: "Give me a dozen healthy infants...I'll train any one to become any specialist regardless of talents, tendencies, abilities, vocations, and races of his ancestors." Empirically refuted: Twin studies demonstrate genetic variance in ALL complex psychological traits. If environment alone determined outcomes, identical environments would produce identical people—they don't.
  • Modern Interactionist View: Both contribute; question is HOW they interact dynamically across development. "The versus is dead." Genes affect environment selection (rGE); environment affects gene expression (epigenetics). Bidirectional causation at molecular, cellular, brain, and behavioural levels. P = G + E + G×E.
Why is nature-nurture a false dichotomy? → Neither nativism nor empiricism alone explains development. Genes set reaction range; environment determines position. Bidirectional causation: rGE (genes→environment) + epigenetics (environment→gene expression). Question shifted from WHETHER to HOW.

2. HERITABILITY: The P = G + E Framework

h² = V_G / V_P = proportion of POPULATION phenotypic variance attributable to genetic variance
h² answers: "Why do people DIFFER?" NOT: "How much of MY trait is genetic?" NOT: "Can this trait change?"
Heritable ≠ Inherited. Heritable = variance partitioning statistic. Inherited = genes passed to offspring.
  • High h² ≠ unchangeable: Height h²≈0.80 in wealthy populations—yet Japanese average height increased +15cm in just 3 generations post-WWII through improved nutrition (gene pool unchanged). Same genes + good nutrition = 184cm; same genes + malnutrition = 168cm → 16cm environmental difference despite 80% heritability.
  • Seesaw dynamics (h² is a RATIO): (1) ↓V_E → h²↑ (uniform environments reduce environmental variance, genetic proportion increases "accidentally"). (2) ↑V_G → h²↑ (outbreeding increases genetic variance). h² is population-specific, context-dependent—cannot generalize from wealthy Western samples to other populations.
  • Reaction range concept: Genes set the ceiling (maximum potential); environment determines where within that range the individual actually falls. Growth plates fuse ~age 18 = critical period closes for height.
Height paradox: 80% heritable yet +15cm from nutrition alone. Genes set ceiling, environment determines position. h² in wealthy pop ≈0.80; in resource-scarce ≈0.40—same trait, different context, different h².

3. TWIN STUDIES & ACE MODEL (Burt 2009 meta-analysis, N=490 studies)

Falconer's Formula: h² = 2(r_MZ - r_DZ)
Derivation: r_MZ = h² + c² (all genetic + all common env) | r_DZ = ½h² + c² (half genetic + all common env)
Subtracting: r_MZ - r_DZ = ½h² → therefore h² = 2(r_MZ - r_DZ)
Then: c² = r_MZ - h² | e² = 1 - r_MZ | Verify: h² + c² + e² = 1.0
  • Twin study logic: MZ twins share 100% segregating genes; DZ twins share 50% on average. BOTH share 100% common environment (same household, SES, parenting, neighborhood). If MZ more similar than DZ on trait, excess similarity must reflect greater genetic similarity since C held constant. "Naturalistic experiment"—exploits natural genetic variation without manipulation.
  • C (Common/Shared Environment): Events happening to BOTH twins that influence them in the SAME way. Examples: family SES, neighborhood, parenting style, school quality. Same event with different effects = E, not C. Burt finding: c² = 10-16% for most disorders—NOT negligible, justifies family-level interventions.
  • E (Unique/Non-shared Environment): Events affecting one twin only OR both twins but affecting them differently. Two pathways: (1) One twin only experiences event (bullying). (2) Same event, differential impact (parent death processed differently). Turkheimer's Paradox: We know E matters (~35-40%) but cannot specify WHICH environmental variables—defined negatively, hard to measure prospectively. Includes measurement error.
  • EEA (Equal Environments Assumption): Assumes MZ and DZ twins experience equally similar environments. If MZ treated more similarly (dressed alike, same friends), h² may be overestimated. Critical methodological assumption.
  • ADHD EXCEPTION: c² = 0 (NO shared environment effect—parenting doesn't explain variance!). Requires ADE model with d² = 44% dominance genetic effects. Total h² = 70%—most heritable disorder studied. Suggests fundamentally biological mechanism different from other externalizing problems.

5. G×E INTERACTIONS: Mediation vs Moderation (Common Exam Confusion!)

ConceptQuestion AnsweredStructureKey RequirementExample
MediationWHY/HOW does the effect occur? (mechanism)Sequential: X → M → Y
M transmits effect		Temporal ordering required
X precedes M precedes Y		5-HTT gene → amygdala reactivity → anxiety (mechanism specified)
ModerationWHEN/FOR WHOM does effect occur? (boundary)Interaction: X × Z → Y
Z changes strength/direction		Non-parallel slopes
No temporal ordering needed		MAOA-L × maltreatment → violence (conditions specified, not mechanism)
  • G×E interaction IS moderation—identifies boundary conditions (when/for whom genetic effect appears) without specifying mechanism. Crossover interaction = slopes actually reverse direction (differential susceptibility pattern).
  • rGE (Gene-Environment Correlation) - 3 types: (1) Passive: Parents provide genes AND correlated environment (musical parents → genes + instruments at home). (2) Evocative: Child's genetically-influenced traits evoke reactions from others (irritable child → harsh parenting feedback loop). (3) Active: Child seeks environments matching genetic predispositions (extraverted child joins clubs, seeks stimulation). All three create non-random gene-environment associations → complicates causal inference.
Quiz trap: students confuse mechanism (mediation: sequential, temporal) with conditions (moderation: interaction, non-parallel slopes). G×E = moderation. Mediation requires longitudinal data with temporal sequencing.

6. DIFFERENTIAL SUSCEPTIBILITY: Orchids & Dandelions (Belsky & Pluess; Ellis et al.)

  • Differential Susceptibility Model: Genetic variants determine susceptibility to environmental influence—some individuals highly responsive to BOTH positive AND negative environments (for-better-AND-for-worse), others resilient/stable regardless of environment. Challenges diathesis-stress which predicts only vulnerability (for-worse), not advantage (for-better).
  • Orchid Children (plasticity alleles, high susceptibility): Like orchids—thrive magnificently in optimal conditions (greenhouse) but wilt in poor conditions (neglect). Steep slope on G×E graph. Worst outcomes in adversity, BEST outcomes in enrichment. Not "vulnerable"—SENSITIVE. Environment determines trajectory entirely.
  • Dandelion Children (stable alleles, low susceptibility): Like dandelions—grow anywhere, including concrete cracks. Flat slope on G×E graph. Stable moderate outcomes (~50%) regardless of environment quality. Resilient to adversity BUT unresponsive to intervention. Neither stars nor failures.
  • Diathesis-Stress vs Differential Susceptibility: Diathesis-stress predicts vulnerability only (bad environment → bad outcomes for vulnerable genotype; good environment → no differential benefit). Differential susceptibility predicts crossover pattern: vulnerable genotype does WORST in adversity but BEST in enrichment—"for-better-AND-for-worse."
Fast Track: Without genotype split, intervention looks modest. A-allele orchids: 75%→18% (76% relative risk reduction!). GG dandelions: 50% either way—resilient but unresponsive. Precision medicine vs genetic discrimination ethical tension.

7. EPIGENETICS: Environment Changes Gene Expression

  • Definition: Heritable changes in gene EXPRESSION without changes to DNA SEQUENCE (ATCG base pairs unchanged). DNA is a library, genes are books—epigenetics determines which books get read, how often, and when. Same genetic sequence can produce dramatically different phenotypes depending on epigenetic modifications. Resolves nature-nurture at molecular level.
  • DNA Methylation Mechanism: Methyl groups (CH₃) attach to cytosine bases at CpG dinucleotides in promoter regions. "Bug-on-stick" analogy: DNA is stick, transcription factor is "bug" walking along reading genes; methyl groups are "bulldogs clipping onto rope" blocking bug movement. Promoter methylation → transcription factor cannot bind → gene not transcribed → mRNA not produced → protein not made → phenotype affected. 80-90% transcription reduction with promoter methylation.
  • REVERSIBLE (via demethylation enzymes/TETs) ≠ mutations (permanent sequence changes). DNMTs add methyl groups; TETs remove them. Dynamic process responsive to environment throughout lifespan, though early life = critical period for programming.
  • Causes: Stress (cortisol activates DNMTs), diet (folate, B-vitamins as methyl donors), toxins (heavy metals, pollution), extreme environmental conditions. Tissue-specific: Brain methylation patterns ≠ blood methylation patterns—cannot directly infer brain epigenome from peripheral blood samples (methodological limitation).
  • Missing Heritability Problem: Twin studies consistently show h² ≈ 50% for most psychological traits. GWAS (genome-wide association studies) identify specific genetic variants explaining only ~2-3% of variance. Gap = ~47 percentage points "missing." Six explanations: (1) wrong candidate genes, (2) incomplete gene networks, (3) unknown protein functions, (4) impure phenotype definitions, (5) rare variants not captured, (6) epistasis (G×G interactions).
Quebec Ice Storm: prenatal stress → methylation changes in immunity/insulin genes → persisted >10 years. "Good genes" locked into bad expression by adversity. Environment → epigenome → transcription → protein → phenotype (molecular G×E bridge).

8. FIVE MYTHS DEBUNKED & CLINICAL h² APPLICATION

MythRefutation & Evidence
1. Genes dictate outcomes (genetic determinism)Even Down syndrome (clear chromosomal mutation, trisomy 21) shows massive outcome variation—some individuals live independently, hold jobs; others require constant care. Genes set reaction range (ceiling), not deterministic fate. Height example: same genes ± nutrition = 16cm difference.
2. Tabula rasa = society's faultBoth extremes commit attribution errors. Attribution bias: negative traits → blame environment ("not my fault"); positive traits → credit genes ("natural talent"). Parents prefer ADHD diagnosis (perceived biological) over ODD (perceived parenting failure) despite equal heritability ~40%. Neither pure nature nor pure nurture—always G×E.
3. Warrior gene = violencePPV = 5% (95% of MAOA-L carriers never violent); 48% of violent offenders have normal MAOA-H. Gene neither necessary nor sufficient. G×E required (maltreatment context). Ecological fallacy in courtrooms—population statistics cannot determine individual culpability. Logical trap: if genes reduce responsibility → same genes imply future dangerousness (increased sentence)?
4. h² irrelevant if all traits heritableh² MAGNITUDE guides treatment selection—tells you which lever to pull. See clinical box below: same conduct disorder phenotype, different h², different optimal intervention. 10% vs 80% h² = fundamentally different mechanism.
5. Genetic knowledge = fatalismCommunication failure, not information problem. "Type 2 diabetes is 70% genetic" → reduced diet/exercise adherence. Solution: Pair genetic risk WITH intervention efficacy: "Your genetic profile means THIS specific treatment works best for you." Maintain agency while personalizing.
MYTH 4 CLINICAL KEY (Conduct Disorder + CU Traits): Children with Low CU traits (callous-unemotional: emotional coldness, lack of empathy) show conduct problems with h² = 30% (70% environmental) → 60% respond to parent training (reactive aggression, poor regulation, hostile environment). Children with High CU traits show conduct problems with h² = 81% (biological mechanism, possibly amygdala/empathy circuits) → only 20% respond to parent training. Same phenotype, different mechanism, different optimal treatment. h² variation reveals when to pull environmental lever vs biological lever.

CRITICAL FORMULAS, WORKED EXAMPLE, EXAM STRATEGY & ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS

Core Formulas:
P = G + E (phenotypic variance partitioning)
h² = 2(r_MZ - r_DZ) Falconer's formula
c² = r_MZ - h² common/shared environment
e² = 1 - r_MZ unique/non-shared environment
Constraint: h² + c² + e² = 1.0 (must verify!)
If h² > 1.0 → dominance effects; use ADE model
Worked Example (given r_MZ = .70, r_DZ = .50):
Step 1: h² = 2(r_MZ - r_DZ) = 2(.70 - .50) = 2(.20) = .40 (40% additive genetic)
Step 2: c² = r_MZ - h² = .70 - .40 = .30 (30% common environment)
Step 3: e² = 1 - r_MZ = 1 - .70 = .30 (30% unique environment)
Step 4: Verify: .40 + .30 + .30 = 1.0 ✓
Exam Strategy: (1) Read question fully—identify WHAT is asked. (2) Tackle essay/hurdle question FIRST while fresh. (3) Simple language (Pilates instructor not food critic). (4) Specific examples with study names & stats.
Ethical Dilemma (Precision vs Equity):
FOR Precision Medicine: Genotype children → target intensive resources to orchids who respond dramatically (Fast Track: 76% risk reduction for A-allele). Maximize intervention ROI.
AGAINST (Genetic Discrimination): Deny services to dandelions who won't respond? Self-fulfilling prophecy? False certainty (probabilistic not deterministic)? Different intervention types untested?
Tension: Efficiency (optimize outcomes) vs Equity (equal access)
Unresolved: Should we genotype children to decide who gets help?
