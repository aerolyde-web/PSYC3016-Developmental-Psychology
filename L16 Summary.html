The nativism-constructivism debate in infant cognition fundamentally concerns the architecture of learning mechanisms: whether infants possess innate domain-specific modules containing core knowledge about objects, agents, and number that are triggered by specific perceptual inputs (the nativist position of Carey, Spelke, and Baillargeon), or whether all knowledge emerges through domain-general information processing that detects low-level features and hierarchically constructs higher-order representations through experience (the constructivist position of Cohen and Cashon). This is not a simple "nature versus nurture" debate‚Äîboth camps acknowledge roles for genetics and learning‚Äîbut rather a dispute about whether learning mechanisms are specialized for specific content domains or apply the same statistical learning processes to all inputs. The critical evidence battleground centers on object unity and permanence: Kellman and Spelke (1983) demonstrated that 4-month-olds perceive a rod moving behind an occluder as a unified object when its visible portions exhibit common motion (coordinated movement), looking longer at a broken rod at test‚Äîa pattern nativists interpret as the object module being triggered by common motion to apply innate principles about object boundaries, while constructivists argue infants are gradually learning statistical regularities (surfaces that move together tend to be parts of the same object) as evidenced by Johnson and Aslin's (1998) finding that younger infants require more visible surface to demonstrate unity. Baillargeon's violation-of-expectation paradigms‚Äîincluding the classic drawbridge study where 4.5-month-olds look longer when a drawbridge appears to rotate through a hidden box, and car studies where infants are surprised when objects pass through solid obstacles‚Äîprovide the strongest nativist evidence, with longer looking to perceptually familiar but physically impossible events interpreted as conceptual surprise when core knowledge expectations (solidity, continuity) are violated. However, Cohen and Cashon's methodological critique challenges this interpretation by noting that Baillargeon included infants who did not fully habituate (less than 50% looking time reduction), and according to Hunter and Ames (1988), incompletely habituated infants show familiarity preferences rather than novelty preferences‚Äîmeaning longer looking to the impossible event (which is perceptually familiar and often contains more motion) could reflect perceptual preference rather than physics knowledge. The reaching-versus-looking-time dissociation further complicates interpretation: while infants succeed on violation-of-expectation looking-time tasks at 3.5-4.5 months, they fail Piagetian manual search tasks until 8-9 months, which nativists attribute to performance limitations (motor planning, inhibitory control) masking underlying competence, while constructivists argue that looking time measures shallow perceptual expectations whereas reaching measures true conceptual permanence. The decisive evidence comes from Meltzoff and Moore's (1998) dissociation studies: 5-month-olds show surprise when an object's features change across occlusion (detecting shape mismatches) but do not show surprise when an object disappears behind one screen and emerges from behind another without appearing in the gap between them (spatial discontinuity), whereas 9-month-olds show both‚Äîsuggesting that memory for object features precedes understanding of spatiotemporal continuity, supporting Piaget's constructivist timeline and refuting the nativist claim of unified early object permanence. The interpretive pivot that students most commonly mishandle is conflating early behavioral competence with innateness: nativists argue that failure at younger ages reflects performance limitations in perceptual or motor systems rather than absence of knowledge (competence-performance gap), while constructivists counter that rapid learning from billions of perceptual experiences in the first months can produce sophisticated behaviors without innate knowledge, and point to gradual developmental trajectories (2-month-olds needing small occluders, 4-month-olds succeeding with large occluders) as signatures of incremental construction rather than sudden module activation. Ultimately, the debate remains unresolved because both frameworks can accommodate most findings by adjusting auxiliary assumptions: nativists locate developmental change in the maturation of perceptual input systems that feed innate modules, while constructivists locate it in the gradual hierarchical construction of representations themselves‚Äîthe frameworks differ not in whether development occurs, but in where the locus of development resides (input systems versus knowledge structures).
