Lecture 16: Infant Cognition II
Nativism vs Constructivism

Conceptual Backbone

Thesis: The nativism-constructivism debate in infant cognition centers on whether infants possess innate, domain-specific knowledge systems (core knowledge) that are triggered by perceptual inputs, or whether all knowledge emerges through domain-general learning mechanisms applied to experience. This debate is not about whether genetics or learning matter—both camps acknowledge both—but about the architecture of learning itself: modular versus general-purpose.

Model Structure: Nativists (Carey, Spelke, Baillargeon) propose cognitive modules with specialized circuits for domains like objects, language, and number, activated by specific perceptual triggers (e.g., common motion for object unity). These modules apply core knowledge—innate principles such as "objects persist when occluded" and "solid objects cannot pass through each other." Constructivists (Cohen, Cashon) propose domain-general information processing that detects low-level features (color, motion) and hierarchically constructs higher-order representations through experience, with no initial domain-specific expectations.

Critical Hinge: The biggest interpretive pivot students mishandle is conflating presence of a behavior at a young age with evidence for innateness. Constructivists argue that rapid learning in the first months can produce sophisticated behaviors without innate knowledge, while nativists argue that failure to demonstrate knowledge early may reflect performance limitations (e.g., inability to perceive motion, motor immaturity) rather than absence of conceptual competence. The methodological tension between habituation paradigms (which assume no prior knowledge) and violation-of-expectation paradigms (which assume prior knowledge overrides perceptual novelty) is the second major source of confusion.

The Real Nature of the Debate

What Are Nativists and Constructivists Actually Arguing About?

The nativism-constructivism debate superficially appears to ask "nature versus nurture," but this framing is misleading because everything involves both genes and learning. Humans share 98% of DNA with chimpanzees, yet only humans acquire language—clearly genetics matter. But even Noam Chomsky, the archetypal nativist, acknowledges infants must learn whether they are in an English-speaking or Mandarin-speaking environment. The genuine dispute concerns the structure of learning mechanisms: are they domain-specific (tuned to particular content like objects, faces, or syntax) or domain-general (applying the same associative/statistical processes to all inputs)?

Nativists propose that evolution has equipped infants with domain-specific learning architectures that embody expectations about specific domains. For instance, a putative "object module" expects that when similar-appearing surfaces move together (common motion), they constitute a single unified object that continues to exist when occluded and cannot interpenetrate with other solid objects. This is not learned from scratch; it is an innate computational bias. In contrast, constructivists argue that infants are born only with domain-general perceptual and learning systems sensitive to low-level features like color, edges, and motion. Through experience, infants notice statistical regularities (e.g., surfaces that look similar and move together tend to be parts of the same object) and gradually construct hierarchical representations. The key difference: nativists claim the infant arrives with a "theory" about objects; constructivists claim the infant builds that theory from perceptual primitives.

Decision Layer: How to Evaluate Evidence for Each Position

When to favor nativist interpretation: If you observe (1) very early emergence of a behavior (newborns or first weeks), (2) simultaneous emergence of multiple conceptually-related abilities at the same developmental moment (suggesting module activation), or (3) specific perceptual inputs being necessary for behavior (e.g., only common motion, not static displays, elicit object unity), lean toward domain-specific modules. The cost is potential unfalsifiability—any failure can be attributed to performance limitations rather than competence absence. The power is explanatory parsimony for why conceptually coherent systems emerge together.

When to favor constructivist interpretation: If you observe (1) gradual, incremental developmental changes (newborns differ from 2-month-olds who differ from 4-month-olds), (2) graded effects of task difficulty (infants succeed with more perceptual support, fail with less), or (3) dissociations between seemingly related abilities (memory for object features precedes understanding of spatial continuity), lean toward domain-general construction. The common failure is assuming rapid learning is impossible—the first four months contain billions of perceptual experiences. The fix: demonstrate that the learning timeline is implausibly fast given statistical structure of input, or show neural network models with no innate knowledge fail to replicate developmental trajectory.

Methodological costs: Habituation paradigms (nativist preference) assume novelty preference after full habituation, but Baillargeon included infants who didn't fully habituate—these may show familiarity preferences, confounding interpretation. Violation-of-expectation paradigms (constructivist critique) assume longer looking to impossible events reflects conceptual surprise rather than perceptual preference for complexity. Neither paradigm is immune to alternative explanations.

Cognitive Modularity and Core Knowledge

The Nativist Architecture: Modules Triggered by Specific Inputs

Nativism in modern developmental psychology is typically instantiated as cognitive modularity, the proposal that the mind contains specialized subsystems dedicated to processing specific domains. A module is characterized by information encapsulation (it operates on proprietary inputs and cannot access information from other modules), domain specificity (designed for objects, faces, language, etc.), and innate specification (present early in development, potentially with dedicated neural tissue). The classic example is language: Chomsky's "language acquisition device" supposedly parses speech sounds, identifies syntactic categories, and applies grammatical transformations innately, triggered by exposure to linguistic input. Critically, modules are not "all-or-nothing present at birth"—they may require specific perceptual inputs to activate and apply their core knowledge.

In the object domain, nativists propose an object module that expects certain perceptual cues to indicate objecthood and then applies core principles. The canonical triggering input is common motion: when two visible surfaces move together in a coordinated fashion, the module interprets them as parts of a unified object, even if an occluder hides the connecting portion. Once triggered, the module applies core knowledge: the object exists continuously even when occluded (object permanence), the object cannot pass through other solid objects (solidity constraint), and the object moves along spatiotemporally continuous paths (no teleportation). Importantly, without the triggering input—for example, if the surfaces are stationary rather than moving—the module does not activate, and infants fail to demonstrate object knowledge. This is not because they lack the knowledge, but because the critical input to activate the computation is absent.

Decision Layer: When Does Module-Based Explanation Apply?

Use modular explanation when: (1) A behavior appears suddenly rather than gradually (suggesting an innate system coming online), (2) the behavior depends on specific, narrow perceptual triggers (common motion but not static cues), (3) multiple related abilities emerge simultaneously (object unity, solidity understanding, and numerosity tracking all at 4 months—suggesting a unified object system activating). The interpretive power is that it explains systematicity: why conceptually-related abilities co-develop. The cost to interpretability is post-hoc flexibility: any failure can be attributed to missing trigger rather than missing knowledge, risking unfalsifiability.

Common failure and fix: Students often think "module" means "no development," leading to the error that any developmental change refutes modularity. The fix: Nativists claim modules themselves are innate, but their expression depends on maturing perceptual prerequisites. For example, newborns may possess an object module but cannot perceive common motion across an occluder until 2-4 months of age when motion-tracking circuits mature. Thus, failure at 2 months and success at 4 months can both be consistent with innate modules—the module was present but lacked input to operate on.

Micro-Worked Example: The Common Motion Trigger

Scenario: Kellman & Spelke (1983) habituate 4-month-olds to a rod moving back and forth behind a stationary occluder (yellow box). The infant sees the top portion of a red rod moving rightward, disappearing behind the box, then the bottom portion of the same red rod continuing to move rightward. Both visible portions move in identical direction and speed (common motion).

Prediction if object module activated: The common motion triggers the object module, which infers that the two rod portions are parts of a unified object. At test, when the occluder is removed, the infant expects to see a complete rod. Showing a broken rod (two separate pieces) violates this expectation, producing longer looking (novelty preference for surprising outcome). Showing a complete rod confirms expectation, producing shorter looking (familiarity).

Prediction if no module (pure perceptual novelty): The infant has seen moving rod portions many times during habituation. At test, the broken rod is perceptually similar (two moving pieces), while the complete rod is perceptually novel (single long rod never seen before). Thus, novelty preference should go to complete rod.

Data decision formula: \( \text{Looking}_{\text{broken}} > \text{Looking}_{\text{complete}} \Rightarrow \) module activated (conceptual novelty overrides perceptual familiarity). \( \text{Looking}_{\text{complete}} > \text{Looking}_{\text{broken}} \Rightarrow \) no module, pure perceptual novelty. Result: Infants looked longer at broken rod, supporting module activation. Critically, in stationary rod condition (no common motion), infants showed no preference—supporting claim that common motion is the specific trigger.

Object Unity: Perceiving Whole Objects from Partial Views

Kellman & Spelke (1983): The Common Motion Paradigm and Its Constructivist Challenge

Object unity refers to the ability to perceive spatially separated visible surfaces as parts of a single unified object when an occluder hides the connecting portion. This is distinct from object permanence (understanding that objects exist when fully hidden); object unity concerns representing parts you cannot see of an object you can see. For example, when you see a person's head and legs protruding from behind a desk, you infer the torso exists even though it is occluded. Piaget claimed infants initially lack this ability and perceive the world as disconnected surfaces. Nativists argue that once infants can perceive the critical triggering input (common motion), they immediately demonstrate object unity because their innate object module is activated.

Kellman and Spelke's (1983) classic study habituated 4-month-olds to a red rod moving horizontally behind a yellow occluder, with the top and bottom portions visible and moving in the same direction at the same speed (common motion). At test, infants saw either a complete rod or two separate broken rod pieces, both moving. The perceptual novelty account predicts longer looking to the complete rod (never seen before as a single unit). The core knowledge account predicts longer looking to the broken rod (violates the inferred unity). Results showed infants looked longer at the broken rod, suggesting they represented a unified rod during habituation despite never seeing it fully. Critically, when the rod was stationary during habituation (no common motion), infants showed no preference at test, supporting the claim that common motion is the specific input required to trigger the object module.

Constructivists (Cohen & Cashon, 2006) offer an alternative interpretation: object unity is learned from perceptual regularities, not innately given. In the real world, surfaces that look similar (same color, texture) and move together tend to be parts of the same object, while surfaces that move independently tend to be different objects. Through repeated exposure, infants extract this statistical regularity and apply it as a heuristic. This predicts gradual development as infants require more or less perceptual support depending on their experience and processing capacity. Supporting this, Johnson and Aslin (1998) found that 2-month-olds demonstrated object unity only when a smaller occluder hid less of the rod (more perceptual evidence), while 4-month-olds succeeded even with larger occlusion. Slater et al. (1996) found newborns show a preference suggesting unity, which constructivists interpret as an innate preference for perceptual simplicity (Gestalt grouping) rather than conceptual object knowledge.

Decision Layer: Distinguishing Nativist from Constructivist Explanations of Object Unity

Evidence favoring nativism: (1) Sudden emergence of behavior at a specific age (4 months) when perceptual prerequisite matures, with no intermediate stages. (2) Specific dependence on common motion—other cues (similarity, proximity) insufficient. (3) Co-emergence with other object abilities at same age (object individuation, understanding solidity), suggesting unified system activation. The power is parsimony: one innate module explains multiple abilities. The cost is explaining failures at younger ages—must invoke performance limitations, which is sometimes ad-hoc.

Evidence favoring constructivism: (1) Gradual improvement from newborns (succeed with minimal occlusion) to 2 months (succeed with moderate occlusion) to 4 months (succeed with substantial occlusion). This graded pattern suggests incremental construction rather than all-or-nothing module activation. (2) Statistical learning models (connectionist networks) trained on visual input can learn object unity without innate object knowledge, showing it's learnable in principle. (3) Cross-cultural differences (if found) would support learning over innate universals. The power is compatibility with general-purpose learning mechanisms. The cost is explaining why unity emerges reliably at similar ages across infants—what constrains the learning trajectory?

Common failure: Treating any demonstration of ability at a young age as proof of innateness, ignoring that rapid learning is possible. Fix: Evaluate whether the developmental trajectory is gradual (constructivist signature) or step-like (nativist signature), and whether ability depends on specific narrow triggers (nativist) or broader perceptual support (constructivist).

Micro-Worked Example: Calculating Predicted Looking Times Under Each Theory

Setup: Infant habituates to moving occluded rod for 6 trials. Mean looking time decreases from 50s (trial 1) to 10s (trial 6), indicating habituation. At test, infant sees broken rod and complete rod in counterbalanced order.

Nativist prediction: During habituation, common motion triggered object module, which inferred unified rod. At test, broken rod violates expectation (conceptually novel despite perceptual familiarity), complete rod confirms expectation. Prediction: \( \text{LT}_{\text{broken}} > \text{LT}_{\text{complete}} \). Typical values: broken rod 30s, complete rod 12s.

Constructivist (perceptual novelty) prediction: Infant represented visible surfaces (two moving rod portions). At test, broken rod is perceptually similar to these portions, complete rod is perceptually novel (single long object). Prediction: \( \text{LT}_{\text{complete}} > \text{LT}_{\text{broken}} \). Typical values: complete rod 28s, broken rod 10s.

Constructivist (familiarity preference) alternative: If infant did not fully habituate (stopped at 40% reduction, not 50%), Hunter & Ames (1988) predict familiarity preference for complex stimuli. The complete rod (more motion complexity) should attract longer looking as familiarity preference. Prediction: \( \text{LT}_{\text{complete}} > \text{LT}_{\text{broken}} \), but for different reason than novelty.

Decision rule: If \( \text{LT}_{\text{broken}} - \text{LT}_{\text{complete}} > 5s \) and statistically significant → support nativism. If \( \text{LT}_{\text{complete}} - \text{LT}_{\text{broken}} > 5s \) → ambiguous (could be novelty preference OR familiarity preference). If no difference → failed to trigger module (e.g., stationary condition).

Object Permanence: Do Objects Exist When Unobserved?

Piaget's Timeline vs. Nativist Claims: Reaching Behavior vs. Looking Time

Object permanence is the understanding that objects continue to exist even when completely out of view—not just occluded in the object unity sense, but entirely invisible. This is conceptually deeper than unity: it requires representing the existence and properties of something with no current perceptual input. Piaget famously claimed infants do not achieve object permanence until 8-9 months of age, based on reaching paradigms: when a toy is hidden under a cloth, 7-month-olds do not reach for it (suggesting "out of sight, out of mind"), while 9-month-olds immediately retrieve it. Piaget interpreted this as development of the representational capacity to maintain a mental symbol of the absent object.

Nativists challenged Piaget by arguing that reaching tasks underestimate competence because they require motor planning, means-end reasoning, and inhibitory control—abilities that develop later than object representation itself. They developed looking-time paradigms that require only visual attention, which might reveal earlier competence. The logic: if infants understand object permanence, they should be surprised by physically impossible events involving hidden objects, even if they cannot yet execute the motor plans to retrieve those objects. This methodological distinction—reaching (behavioral measure requiring motor execution) versus looking time (implicit measure requiring only attention)—is central to the debate.

Decision Layer: Interpreting Dissociations Between Measures

When to attribute failure to performance limitations: If an infant fails a reaching task but succeeds on a looking-time task measuring the same concept, nativists argue the competence exists but performance demands (motor skill, working memory, inhibitory control) prevent expression. This is plausible when the looking task is genuinely simpler in non-conceptual demands. The risk: over-attributing failures to performance, leading to unfalsifiable claims (any failure can be dismissed). The power: explains why abilities appear earlier in sensitive measures.

When to attribute failure to genuine competence absence: Constructivists argue that if an infant cannot use knowledge in goal-directed action (reaching), the knowledge is not fully developed—it may be a shallow perceptual expectation rather than a deep conceptual understanding. They require converging evidence from multiple paradigms. The risk: under-attributing competence, missing early forms of knowledge. The power: distinguishes implicit perceptual predictions from explicit conceptual understanding.

Common error: Assuming looking time and reaching measure identical constructs. Fix: Recognize that looking time may measure perceptual expectations or memory (what should happen next given what I've seen), while reaching measures conceptual permanence and goal-directed reasoning (the object exists in location X, so I should search there). These may dissociate developmentally.

Violation of Expectation: Baillargeon's Challenge to Piaget

Drawbridge and Car Studies: When Impossible Events Produce Longer Looking

Renée Baillargeon developed the violation of expectation paradigm specifically to circumvent the performance limitations of reaching tasks while testing for object permanence and physical reasoning. The logic: if infants possess core knowledge about object properties (solidity, permanence, continuity), they should be surprised when impossible events occur, even if they cannot verbally report surprise or manually intervene. Surprise is operationalized as longer looking time. The critical innovation is contrasting perceptual familiarity with physical possibility. In typical habituation studies, infants look longer at perceptually novel stimuli. Baillargeon created scenarios where the perceptually familiar event is physically impossible (if you represent the hidden object) while the perceptually novel event is physically possible.

In the classic drawbridge study (Baillargeon, 1987), infants first saw a drawbridge rotate 180° back and forth (full rotation from flat on table to standing upright to flat again on the opposite side). After familiarization, a box was placed in the drawbridge's path. In the possible event, the drawbridge rotated until it contacted the box (about 120°) and stopped—this is perceptually novel (partial rotation never seen during familiarization) but physically expected (solid objects block motion). In the impossible event, the drawbridge completed the full 180° rotation, apparently passing through the space occupied by the box—this is perceptually familiar (full rotation seen repeatedly) but physically impossible (violates solidity). The finding: 4.5-month-olds looked significantly longer at the impossible event, suggesting they represented the hidden box and expected it to block the drawbridge.

Similarly, in the car-and-ramp study, infants saw a toy car roll down a ramp, pass behind a screen, and emerge from the other side. After familiarization, a box was placed on the track behind the screen. In the possible condition, the car did not emerge (stopped by box). In the impossible condition, the car emerged normally (apparently passing through the box). Again, infants looked longer at the impossible event. Baillargeon argued this demonstrates that infants as young as 3.5 months understand that (1) objects continue to exist when occluded, and (2) solid objects cannot pass through each other. This challenges Piaget's 8-9 month timeline by suggesting core knowledge is present much earlier, but expressed only in sensitive measures.

Decision Layer: Evaluating Violation-of-Expectation Evidence

When violation paradigm supports core knowledge: If (1) infants are fully familiarized to baseline event, (2) the impossible event is perceptually simpler or more familiar than the possible event (ruling out preference for complexity), (3) infants show no preference between events when the object is visible (ruling out intrinsic preference for one motion pattern), and (4) the looking-time difference is substantial and replicable, then longer looking to impossible events is strong evidence for conceptual surprise. The power is that it cleanly separates perceptual from conceptual factors. The cost is that it requires precise stimulus control—any confound invalidates interpretation.

When to doubt the interpretation (constructivist critiques): If (1) infants were not fully habituated (Cohen & Cashon's main critique of Baillargeon), they may show familiarity preferences (looking longer at perceptually familiar = the impossible event) unrelated to physics knowledge. Hunter & Ames (1988) demonstrated that incompletely habituated infants often prefer familiar stimuli, especially with complex displays. (2) If the impossible event contains more motion or greater perceptual complexity (the full 180° rotation has more movement than the partial rotation), infants might prefer it for perceptual reasons. (3) If infants show high variability or small effect sizes, the finding may be fragile. The common failure: accepting violation findings uncritically. The fix: check whether habituation criterion was enforced and whether perceptual confounds were controlled.

Micro-Worked Example: Calculating Whether Longer Looking Reflects Physics Knowledge

Scenario: Infant familiarizes to drawbridge rotating 180° for 8 trials. Mean looking time decreases from 60s to 30s (50% reduction, meeting habituation criterion). Box is placed behind drawbridge. At test, infant sees possible event (120° rotation, stops at box) and impossible event (180° rotation, passes through box).

Perceptual novelty account predicts: Possible event is perceptually novel (partial rotation never seen), impossible event is perceptually familiar (full rotation seen repeatedly). Novelty preference: \( \text{LT}_{\text{possible}} > \text{LT}_{\text{impossible}} \). Expected difference: +15s.

Core knowledge account predicts: Impossible event violates expectation that box blocks drawbridge. Conceptual surprise overrides perceptual familiarity: \( \text{LT}_{\text{impossible}} > \text{LT}_{\text{possible}} \). Expected difference: +10s.

Familiarity preference account (if incomplete habituation) predicts: Incomplete habituation produces familiarity preference for complex stimuli. Full rotation is more complex (more motion), so: \( \text{LT}_{\text{impossible}} > \text{LT}_{\text{possible}} \), but due to complexity preference, not physics knowledge. Expected difference: +8s.

Observed data: \( \text{LT}_{\text{impossible}} = 45s \), \( \text{LT}_{\text{possible}} = 33s \), difference = +12s, \( p < 0.01 \). Interpretation: Consistent with core knowledge account, but familiarity preference account is plausible alternative if habituation was incomplete. Needed control: Verify habituation criterion was met individually for each infant. Also test with visible box (no occlusion) to ensure no intrinsic preference for 180° vs. 120° rotation.

Memory for Object Features vs. True Object Permanence

Meltzoff & Moore (1998): Dissociating Memory from Spatiotemporal Understanding

A critical constructivist response to violation-of-expectation findings is that longer looking to impossible events might reflect memory for object features rather than conceptual understanding of object permanence. This distinction is subtle but important. Imagine you see a blue ball go behind a screen and a red ball emerges. You'd be surprised—but does this surprise require understanding that objects maintain identity and spatial location across occlusion, or just a memory that "the thing I saw was blue, not red"? The former is deep conceptual permanence; the latter is shallow perceptual memory. Meltzoff and Moore (1998) designed experiments to distinguish these.

In the memory task, infants saw an object (e.g., a cylinder) approach a screen and go behind it. After a brief delay, either the same object (cylinder) or a different object (cube) emerged from behind the screen. Both 5-month-olds and 9-month-olds looked longer when the object changed shape, indicating they remembered the object's features and detected the mismatch. This suggests that even 5-month-olds can maintain a short-term memory representation of an occluded object's properties. So far, this is consistent with both nativist and constructivist accounts—nativists say it reflects core knowledge, constructivists say it reflects perceptual memory.

The critical test is the permanence task, which requires understanding that objects must occupy continuous spatial trajectories—they cannot "skip" locations. Infants saw an object go behind the first of two screens (left screen) and then emerge from behind the second screen (right screen) without ever appearing in the visible gap between the screens. This is physically impossible: if the object exists continuously, it must travel through the gap and be visible there. In the non-violation condition, the object appeared in the gap before emerging from the second screen (spatially continuous). The key finding: 9-month-olds looked longer at the violation (object skipping space), but 5-month-olds showed no differential looking. This suggests that understanding spatiotemporal continuity—a core component of true object permanence—develops later than memory for object features, supporting Piaget's timeline.

Decision Layer: Distinguishing Memory from Permanence

When evidence supports memory only: If infants detect feature changes (color, shape) across brief occlusions but do not show surprise when objects violate spatial continuity (appear in impossible locations, skip intervening space), this suggests they have perceptual memory traces without deeper understanding of object existence across space and time. This is consistent with the constructivist claim that early "object knowledge" is shallow. The interpretive cost: requires operationalizing "deeper" vs. "shallower" understanding, which can be subjective.

When evidence supports true permanence: If infants show surprise both to feature changes AND to spatiotemporal violations (objects skipping space, appearing in wrong locations, failing to maintain continuous trajectories), this suggests conceptual understanding that objects exist continuously and must obey physical constraints even when hidden. The power: demonstrates knowledge beyond immediate perception. The risk: perhaps infants just have richer perceptual expectations about motion paths, not a concept of "object."

Common error: Equating any surprise about hidden objects with full object permanence. Fix: Require converging evidence from both feature-memory tasks (can infant track properties?) AND spatial-continuity tasks (can infant track existence across space?). Dissociation between these indicates piecemeal development.

Micro-Worked Example: Quantifying Memory vs. Permanence

Memory Task Setup: Infant sees blue cylinder approach screen and disappear (3s). After 2s delay, either blue cylinder (match) or red cube (mismatch) emerges. Measure looking time.

Memory prediction: If infant retains perceptual memory of "blue cylinder," mismatch should be surprising. \( \text{LT}_{\text{mismatch}} > \text{LT}_{\text{match}} \). Typical effect: +6s difference.

Permanence Task Setup: Infant sees blue cylinder go behind Screen 1 (left), then emerge from behind Screen 2 (right). In violation condition, cylinder never appears in 10cm gap between screens (spatially discontinuous). In non-violation, cylinder appears in gap (spatially continuous). Measure looking time.

Permanence prediction: If infant understands objects must traverse continuous paths, violation should be surprising. \( \text{LT}_{\text{violation}} > \text{LT}_{\text{non-violation}} \). Typical effect: +8s difference.

5-month-old results: Memory task: \( \text{LT}_{\text{mismatch}} = 28s \), \( \text{LT}_{\text{match}} = 22s \), diff = +6s, \( p < 0.05 \). Permanence task: \( \text{LT}_{\text{violation}} = 18s \), \( \text{LT}_{\text{non-violation}} = 17s \), diff = +1s, \( p = 0.6 \). Interpretation: 5-month-olds possess memory for features but NOT understanding of spatial continuity.

9-month-old results: Memory task: \( \text{LT}_{\text{mismatch}} = 30s \), \( \text{LT}_{\text{match}} = 23s \), diff = +7s, \( p < 0.05 \). Permanence task: \( \text{LT}_{\text{violation}} = 35s \), \( \text{LT}_{\text{non-violation}} = 27s \), diff = +8s, \( p < 0.01 \). Interpretation: 9-month-olds possess both memory and spatiotemporal permanence understanding, consistent with Piaget's timeline.

Worked Examples: Quantitative Reasoning in Paradigms

Calculating Effect Sizes, Interpreting Ambiguous Patterns, Evaluating Confounds

Example 1: Determining Whether Habituation Criterion Was Met

Context: Standard habituation criterion is 50% reduction in looking time from mean of first three trials to mean of last three trials, OR looking time drops below absolute threshold (e.g., 10s per trial).

Infant A data: Trials 1-3 mean = 45s. Trials 10-12 mean = 20s. Reduction = \( \frac{45 - 20}{45} = 0.56 = 56\% \). Meets criterion. At test, if this infant looks longer at impossible event, familiarity preference explanation is ruled out—likely reflects conceptual surprise.

Infant B data: Trials 1-3 mean = 50s. Trials 8-10 mean = 32s. Reduction = \( \frac{50 - 32}{50} = 0.36 = 36\% \). Does NOT meet criterion. At test, if this infant looks longer at impossible event, could be familiarity preference (Hunter & Ames), not physics knowledge. Baillargeon including such infants is Cohen & Cashon's critique.

Decision rule: Only interpret violation paradigm results if habituation criterion met. Alternative: analyze separately infants who met vs. didn't meet criterion to see if pattern differs.

Example 2: Calculating Statistical Power to Detect Graded Development

Scenario: Testing whether object unity shows gradual development (constructivist) or sudden emergence (nativist). Test newborns, 2-month-olds, and 4-month-olds with same paradigm.

Nativist prediction: Newborns and 2-month-olds fail (cannot perceive common motion), 4-month-olds succeed (motion perception matures). Pattern: 0%, 0%, 80% success rates. No intermediate values.

Constructivist prediction: Gradual improvement as representation constructed. Pattern: 20%, 50%, 80% success rates. Monotonic increase.

Data: Newborns 15%, 2-month-olds 45%, 4-month-olds 75%. Analysis: Linear trend: \( r = 0.98 \), \( p < 0.01 \). Supports gradual development. But: Nativist could argue newborn 15% is noise (chance 12.5% with 2 test trials), real change is from 2 to 4 months when motion perception matures. Resolution: Need tighter age sampling (e.g., test every month) to distinguish sudden vs. graded.

Example 3: Isolating Perceptual from Conceptual Factors

Problem: In drawbridge study, impossible event (180° rotation) contains more motion than possible event (120° rotation). Could longer looking reflect preference for more motion rather than physics knowledge?

Control 1 (Amount of motion): Create version where possible event has 180° rotation (box placed to side, not blocking) and impossible event has 120° rotation (box partially penetrated). If physics knowledge drives looking, pattern should reverse: longer looking to 120° now because it's impossible. If motion preference drives looking, pattern should stay same: longer looking to 180° regardless.

Control 2 (Visible box): Run same procedure but keep box visible (don't occlude it). If infants intrinsically prefer 180° rotation for perceptual reasons, they should look longer at it even when there's no hidden object to represent. If no preference with visible box, but preference emerges with hidden box, supports representation explanation.

Calculation: If \( \text{LT}_{180°, \text{ hidden box}} - \text{LT}_{120°, \text{ hidden box}} = +12s \) (original finding) but \( \text{LT}_{180°, \text{ visible box}} - \text{LT}_{120°, \text{ visible box}} = -2s \) (no preference or reverses), then the +12s effect depends on occlusion, supporting representation. If both conditions show +12s, confounded.

Practice Questions Targeting Common Confusions and Conceptual Pivots

MCQ 5: A researcher finds that 3-month-olds show object unity when 70% of the rod is visible (small occluder) but not when 30% is visible (large occluder), while 5-month-olds show unity in both conditions. Susan Carey (nativist) and Leslie Cohen (constructivist) both accept these findings. How would each explain the developmental change?

A) Carey: The object module is gradually constructing representations. Cohen: Perceptual systems are maturing.
B) Carey: The object module is innate but requires sufficient perceptual input (visible surface) to activate; younger infants need more input because their motion perception is less sensitive. Cohen: Infants are gradually constructing the representation; younger infants need more perceptual support because their constructed representation is still developing.
C) Both agree this shows learning, not innate knowledge.
D) Carey would reject these findings as incompatible with core knowledge theory.
Correct: B. This question tests whether students understand that BOTH frameworks can accommodate graded developmental changes, but via different mechanisms. Carey attributes graded changes to perceptual prerequisites for module activation (the module is innate, but needs input). Cohen attributes them to gradual construction of the representation itself (no module, just incremental learning). The key is that Carey locates development in input systems, Cohen locates it in the knowledge representation. Option A reverses the positions.

MCQ 6: In violation-of-expectation paradigms, why do nativists claim that longer looking to the perceptually familiar event (e.g., full 180° drawbridge rotation) supports core knowledge rather than just being a familiarity preference?

A) Because familiarity preferences only occur in habituation paradigms, not in violation paradigms.
B) Because the infant should show a novelty preference after habituation, so longer looking to the familiar event goes against perceptual expectation—it can only be explained by conceptual surprise that the event is physically impossible.
C) Because infants always prefer impossible events due to innate curiosity.
D) Because familiarity preferences never occur in infants younger than 6 months.
Correct: B. This tests understanding of the violation paradigm's logic. After familiarization, infants typically show novelty preference (look longer at perceptually novel stimuli). In Baillargeon's studies, the impossible event is perceptually familiar (seen many times), yet infants look longer at it—violating the expected novelty preference. Nativists argue this reversal can only be explained if conceptual surprise (physical impossibility) overrides perceptual familiarity. The constructivist counter (not offered here, but important) is that incomplete habituation can produce familiarity preferences, offering an alternative explanation.

MCQ 7: Carey (2011) argues that one advantage of positing innate core knowledge is that it predicts multiple conceptually-related abilities should emerge simultaneously. If object unity, understanding of solidity, and ability to individuate objects based on spatiotemporal cues all emerge at 4 months, how does this support nativism over constructivism?

A) It shows infants are learning very fast, which is impossible without innate knowledge.
B) It suggests a unified innate object module is coming online, applying core knowledge to multiple tasks simultaneously. Constructivism predicts these abilities should emerge at different times since they are learned independently.
C) It proves that all knowledge is innate and nothing is learned.
D) Constructivism also predicts simultaneous emergence, so this doesn't distinguish the theories.
Correct: B. This tests understanding of Carey's key argument for the explanatory value of core knowledge. If different abilities (unity, solidity, individuation) are conceptually related (all part of "object knowledge") and emerge together, nativism explains this via a single unified module activating. Constructivism, which posits domain-general learning, has no principled reason why these specific abilities should co-emerge—each is learned from different experiences and could develop at different rates. The co-emergence is not proof of nativism, but it is a pattern nativism predicts and constructivism must explain. Option D is wrong—constructivism does NOT predict simultaneous emergence of conceptually-related but experientially-distinct abilities.

MCQ 8: An infant is shown a ball rolling behind a screen and then emerging from the other side. A box is then placed behind the screen on the ball's path. In the "impossible" condition, the ball still emerges normally. In the "possible" condition, the ball does not emerge (stopped by box). The infant looks longer in the possible condition. What is the most likely interpretation?

A) The infant possesses core knowledge of solidity and is surprised by the possible event (this is a mistake in the study design).
B) The infant has habituated to the ball emerging and shows a familiarity preference for this event (the impossible condition), making the non-emergence perceptually novel and thus more interesting.
C) The infant understands physics but prefers possible events over impossible ones.
D) The infant does not represent the hidden box at all.
Correct: B. This tests whether students can interpret results that go against the predicted pattern. If the infant looks longer at the possible event (ball not emerging), this is OPPOSITE to the core knowledge prediction. The most likely explanation is a novelty preference based on perceptual experience: the infant has been familiarized to the ball emerging, so when it doesn't emerge, that's perceptually novel (never seen before), regardless of physics. This could indicate the infant doesn't represent the hidden box (option D is partially correct), OR that perceptual novelty overrides any physics expectations. Either way, this pattern does NOT support core knowledge of solidity.

Diagnostic Reflection Zone

Reflection Drill 1: Rephrase the Thesis

Prompt: In one sentence, state what nativists and constructivists are genuinely arguing about (not "nature vs. nurture" or "genes vs. environment"). Your sentence must include the terms "domain-specific," "domain-general," and "learning mechanisms."

Suggested answer frame: "Nativists argue that infants possess innate domain-specific learning mechanisms (modules) specialized for particular content domains like objects and language, which apply core knowledge when triggered by specific inputs, whereas constructivists argue that infants possess only domain-general learning mechanisms that extract statistical regularities from perceptual experience to hierarchically construct all knowledge representations."

Reflection Drill 2: Reversal Drill

Prompt: Describe a concrete scenario where longer looking time to a perceptually familiar event would be interpreted as evidence AGAINST core knowledge rather than FOR it. Explain the conditions under which the interpretation flips.

Suggested answer frame: "If infants were not fully habituated (less than 50% reduction in looking time), longer looking to the perceptually familiar event could reflect a familiarity preference (Hunter & Ames) rather than conceptual surprise at physical impossibility. The interpretation flips based on whether the habituation criterion was met: if met, longer looking to familiar-but-impossible suggests conceptual surprise (supports nativism); if not met, it may reflect familiarity preference for perceptually complex stimuli (neutral or supports constructivism). Additionally, if control conditions show the same pattern when the object is visible (no occlusion), the effect cannot be attributed to representing hidden objects."

Reflection Drill 3: Teach-Back Drill

Prompt: Using only the numbers from the Kellman & Spelke (1983) moving rod study (habituation trials start at 50s, drop to 10s; test trials show 30s for broken rod, 12s for complete rod), explain why a constructivist would interpret this differently than a nativist, and what single additional measurement would most clearly distinguish the accounts.

Suggested answer frame: "The 30s vs. 12s pattern (longer looking to broken rod) shows the broken rod is more interesting despite being perceptually similar to what was seen during habituation. Nativists interpret this as conceptual surprise: the module inferred a unified rod from common motion, so seeing it broken violates expectations. Constructivists could argue that if habituation was incomplete, this is a familiarity preference—the broken rod has moving parts like the habituation stimulus. The single measurement that would distinguish: test 2-month-olds and newborns with the same paradigm. If they show the same 30s vs. 12s pattern, it suggests innate module (nativist). If they show gradual improvement (newborns need 90% visible, 2-month-olds need 60%, 4-month-olds need 40%), it suggests constructed learning (constructivist)."

Advanced Integration Question

Prompt: Suppose a study finds that infants raised in environments with mostly stationary objects (e.g., a culture where infants are swaddled and positioned facing walls) show delayed object unity compared to infants raised with mobile objects. Would this finding support nativism or constructivism, and what auxiliary assumption would each framework make to accommodate it?

Diagnostic value: Tests whether you understand that neither framework is easily falsified—both can accommodate experience-dependent effects by adjusting auxiliary assumptions. Nativists would argue experience is needed to mature the perceptual systems that provide input to the innate module (experience affects perception, not the module itself). Constructivists would argue experience directly shapes the constructed representation (experience is the raw material for learning). The frameworks differ in where they locate the role of experience: input systems vs. knowledge representation.
