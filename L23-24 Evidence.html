Crain & Nakayama (1987): Structural Dependence in Question Formation Crain & Nakayama (1987) 3-5 years Elicited production: Complex sentences with relative clauses ("The boy who is smoking is crazy"). Elicit question formation. Linear rule would move first auxiliary ("*Is the boy who smoking is crazy?"); structure-dependent rule moves main-clause auxiliary ("Is the boy who is smoking crazy?") Zero structural dependence violations across 600+ questions. Children NEVER produce linear-order errors despite: (1) Relative clauses rare in child-directed speech (minimal exposure), (2) Linear rule simpler, (3) No negative evidence (never hear island violations corrected). Perfect performance despite impoverished input = innate structural dependence constraint (UG prohibits structure-independent rules). 0 errors across 600+ questions from 3-5 year-olds; Elicitation contexts designed to maximize error likelihood (complex embeddings, time pressure); Other error types (tense, agreement) occur freely in same data (NOT rote memorization) UG Constraint Syntactic operations MUST reference hierarchical phrase structure, NEVER linear word order—genetically specified, NOT learned "Crain & Nakayama 1987: 0 structural dependence errors across 600+ questions (3-5yo)—'parade case' for innate constraint—children never move first auxiliary despite simplicity + no negative evidence"
Wh-Movement Island Constraints: Poverty of Stimulus Argument Chomsky; Jackendoff (2002) Adult speakers; children never violate Grammaticality judgments: Legal wh-questions ("Which movie does Susan imagine that Sarah saw __ last night?") vs. illegal coordinate structure islands ("*What did Beth eat peanut butter and __ for dinner?") or complex NP islands ("*Who does Sam know a girl who is in love with __?") Children NEVER produce island violations despite no negative evidence. No one produces ungrammatical sentences to teach what's prohibited. If general learning mechanisms (reinforcement, statistical extraction) sufficient, children should overgeneralize, produce errors, then correct based on feedback—but they do NOT. Conclusion: Constraints on wh-movement must be innate UG, specifying universally allowed vs. prohibited syntactic operations. Acceptance rate of island violations: ~5-10% in non-native speakers (near-zero in native speakers); Never corrected in input (no negative evidence); Children's production: 0% island violations (cannot learn prohibition from absent evidence) Poverty of Stimulus Linguistic knowledge UNLEARNABLE from experience alone → must be innate. If language is innate, symbols are innate (refutes Piaget/Vygotsky) "Wh-movement island constraints: children NEVER violate ('*What did Beth eat peanut butter and __ for dinner?') despite no negative evidence—innate UG specifies prohibited operations across all languages"

Gertner, Fisher, & Eisengart (2006): 21-Month-Olds Use Transitive Syntax Abstractly Gertner, Fisher, & Eisengart (2006) 21 months Preferential looking paradigm. Warm-up: Familiar transitive verbs using exact test nouns ("The frog is washing the bear"). Test: Novel verb "gorp" in transitive frame ("The frog is gorping the bear"). Two scenes: (1) frog acts on bear (agent-patient), (2) mutual action. Both scenes contain same participants performing related actions—only argument structure distinguishes them 21-month-olds looked longer at frog-acting-on-bear scene when hearing transitive syntax, demonstrating they mapped transitive syntax (NP1 V NP2) → agent-patient semantics WITHOUT knowing "gorp." Claims: Children possess verb-general knowledge—transitive syntax (subject-verb-object) → agent-action-patient interpretation, applicable to ANY verb. Competence present by 21 months (well before Tomasello's verb islands dissolve at 36+ months). Production conservatism = performance limitation (retrieval difficulty, lexical gaps, communicative caution), NOT competence deficit. Significantly longer looking at agent-patient scene vs. mutual action scene when hearing transitive syntax; Warm-up with familiar transitives using test nouns ("frog washing bear"); Effect size moderate; Interpreted as abstract syntax activation Early Abstraction Innate verb-general syntax-semantics linking (agents→subjects, patients→objects, one-to-one mapping) BUT must learn language-specific word orders (weaker than full UG, stronger than constructionism) "Fisher et al. 2006: 21-month-olds interpret 'frog gorping bear' (novel verb) as agent-patient via transitive syntax—verb-general abstract knowledge by 21 months, NOT verb islands—production-comprehension dissociation"
Dittmar et al. (2008): Replication Challenges Fisher—Exemplar Priming Confound Dittmar, Abbot-Smith, Lieven, & Tomasello (2008) 21 months (same age as Fisher) Replicated Fisher et al. procedure BUT added critical control condition: Replaced transitive warm-ups with generic descriptions ("This is called washing") that avoided transitive syntax and specific noun combinations. Identical test trials ("The frog is gorping the bear")—only warm-up differs Control condition (no transitive warm-up): 21-month-olds FAILED—no longer showed agent-patient looking preferences despite identical test trials. Demonstrates: Fisher's evidence for abstract syntax depends on SPECIFIC PRIOR EXPOSURE to relevant nouns in transitive contexts, consistent with exemplar-based retrieval rather than abstract schema activation. Knowledge is exemplar-based and context-dependent, requiring structural priming to manifest—NOT freely applicable abstract rules. Argument: 21-month-olds have syntactic knowledge, but it's item-specific ([FROG] [VERB] [BEAR] frame primed by warm-up), NOT abstract verb-general transitive schema. Fisher's original condition (transitive warm-up): Success (replicated); Dittmar control condition (generic warm-up): FAILURE (21-month-olds no longer discriminate); Critical confound: warm-up primed specific noun combinations in transitive frames, NOT abstract syntax Exemplar Priming "Abstract" syntax at 21 months requires recent exemplar activation—knowledge is item-specific, gradually abstracts through comparison (supports Tomasello's gradual abstraction) "Dittmar et al. 2008 Fisher replication: When transitive warm-up removed (generic descriptions instead), 21-month-olds FAILED—Fisher's 'abstract syntax' actually exemplar priming ([FROG][VERB][BEAR] retrieval), NOT verb-general schema"
Saffran, Aslin, & Newport (1996): Statistical Learning of Word Boundaries via Transitional Probabilities Saffran, Aslin, & Newport (1996) 8 months Artificial language: 4 trisyllabic "words" (bidaku, padoti, golabu, tupiro) repeated in random order with NO pauses, creating continuous 2-minute speech stream (bidakupadotigolabutupiro...). Within "words," transitional probabilities (TP) high: P(da|bi) = 1.0, P(ku|da) = 1.0. Across boundaries, TP low: P(pa|ku) ≈ 0.33 (bidaku could be followed by padoti, golabu, or tupiro). Test: Preferential listening to "words" (bidaku) vs. "part-words" (kupado—spans boundary) 8-month-olds successfully segmented artificial language using transitional probabilities ALONE (no pauses, prosody, semantics, phonotactics). Preferentially attended to "words" (high internal TP) over "part-words" (low TP spanning boundaries), demonstrating extracted statistical structure. Argues AGAINST poverty of stimulus: Infants do NOT require word boundaries marked by prosody/pauses; they can infer segmentation from statistical regularities. Critically, this is DOMAIN-GENERAL statistical learning—similar mechanisms extract patterns from visual sequences, tonal sequences, motor actions (NOT language-specific). Looking time significantly longer for words vs. part-words; Mechanism: domain-general statistical learning (works for syllable sequences, tone sequences, visual sequences); Only 2 minutes exposure sufficient; Demonstrates TP computation: Within-word TP = 1.0, across-boundary TP ≈ 0.33; Threshold-based boundary detection Domain-General Learning Undermines nativist claim that language-specific constraints necessary to solve segmentation problem—domain-general TP tracking suffices for at least some linguistic problems "Saffran et al. 1996: 8-month-olds segment 'bidaku-padoti-golabu' using transitional probabilities (high within-word TP=1.0, low across-boundary TP≈0.33)—domain-general statistical learning solves segmentation WITHOUT innate word boundaries"
Mintz (2003): Distributional Analysis Reveals Grammatical Categories Mintz (2003) Children learning English (corpus analysis) Corpus analysis of child-directed speech. Identify frequent frames (frequent word_1 + slot + frequent word_2): e.g., "the ___ is," "you ___ it," "is ___ing." Track which words appear in each frame's slot. Calculate frame accuracy: % of words in frame that belong to same syntactic category (noun, verb, etc.) Frequent frames reliably co-occur with specific syntactic categories. Frame "the ___ is" occurs with 87% accuracy for nouns in child-directed English corpus. Children tracking which words appear in this frame can cluster them as category (nouns) even WITHOUT knowing what "nounhood" means semantically. Distributional cues provide sufficient information to bootstrap noun/verb distinctions purely from co-occurrence statistics. Explains how children generalize novel words: new word in "the toma is red" → immediately patterns with other nouns in production ("I want toma," "I see toma"). This is verb-general knowledge acquired through distributional analysis, NOT innate: children learn that in English, pre-verbal position + certain frames = subject category. Frame "the ___ is": 87% noun accuracy; Frame "you ___ it": high verb accuracy; Corpus: child-directed English speech; Demonstrates distributional cues sufficient for category induction WITHOUT pre-labeled exemplars or innate category labels Distributional Analysis Words in similar contexts cluster into categories—domain-general categorization mechanism applied to linguistic input yields syntactic categories WITHOUT innate labels "Mintz 2003: Frequent frames in child-directed speech ('the ___ is' 87% noun accuracy)—distributional analysis sufficient for bootstrapping syntactic categories purely from co-occurrence statistics, NOT innate pre-specified categories"
Tomasello & Brooks (1998): Verb Islands—Item-Specific Early Syntax Tomasello & Brooks (1998) 2-year-olds (age 2;0 and 2;6) vs. 4-year-olds Novel verb training + generalization test. Training: Novel verb "tam" in INTRANSITIVE frames only ("The sock is tamming"). Test: Elicit TRANSITIVE production ("Make Big Bird tam the sock"). Measure: Do children extend novel verb from trained construction (intransitive) to untrained construction (transitive)? Age 2;0: Only 3/16 children (19%) produced transitive. Age 2;6: 7/16 children (44%) produced transitive. Age 4+: Succeeded readily. Age-graded performance suggests children do NOT initially possess abstract transitive syntax; they must first accumulate exemplars, then generalize. Critically, same children readily used novel NOUNS in diverse constructions ("I want toma," "I see toma"), showing conservatism is syntax-specific, NOT general shyness/task demands. Supports verb-island hypothesis: Early syntactic knowledge is lexically specific ("I kick X," "I throw Y"), NOT abstract (SUBJECT VERB OBJECT). Abstract constructions emerge through analogical comparison across item-based schemas. Age 2;0: 3/16 (19%) extend to transitive; Age 2;6: 7/16 (44%); Age 4+: majority succeed; Novel nouns: readily used in diverse constructions (NOT verb-specific conservatism); Demonstrates gradual abstraction trajectory: verb-specific → verb-class → fully abstract Gradual Abstraction Initial state: verb islands (lexically specific). Abstract constructions built gradually through schematization—detecting commonalities across multiple item-based schemas via analogical comparison "Tomasello & Brooks 1998: 2-year-olds trained 'sock tamming' (intransitive) rarely produce 'tam the sock' (transitive)—only 3/16 at age 2;0, 7/16 at 2;6—verb islands NOT abstract syntax—4-year-olds succeed after gradual abstraction"
Ambridge et al. (2008): Structural Dependence Errors When Statistics Support Them Ambridge et al. (2008) English-learning children Elicited question production with multiple auxiliaries. Critical comparison: "Is" questions ("The boy who is smoking is crazy" → "Is the boy who is smoking crazy?") vs. "Can" questions ("The boy who smoke can drive" → test: "Can the boy who smoke can drive?" [error] vs. "Can the boy who smoke __ drive?" [correct]). Key manipulation: "who smoke" is grammatical bigram in English (people who smoke); "who smoking" never occurs (ungrammatical) "Is" questions: 0% structural dependence errors (replicates Crain & Nakayama). "Can" questions: 7% structural dependence errors ("Can the boy who smoke can drive?")—children DO make linear-order errors when word-level statistics create plausible alternatives. Word-specific variability contradicts UG's claim of universal innate constraints. Constructionist explanation: Transitional probability of "who smoking" ≈ 0 prevents error with "is"; "who smoke" is grammatical pair (appears in input), occasionally produces error with "can." Errors occur when statistics support them—NOT universal prohibition, but emergent from distributional regularities. "Is" questions: 0% errors (TP("who smoking") ≈ 0 prevents error); "Can" questions: 7% errors ("who smoke" grammatical bigram creates plausible alternative); Demonstrates word-specific statistical effects, NOT universal constraint; 7 percentage-point difference between auxiliaries contradicts universality claim Statistical Patterns Structural dependence emerges from transitional probabilities, NOT innate constraint—errors occur when statistics support them, absent when statistics prevent them (word-specific, NOT universal) "Ambridge et al. 2008: 0% errors with 'is' questions BUT 7% errors with 'can' questions—structural dependence violations occur when 'who smoke' is grammatical bigram—word-specific statistical effects, NOT universal UG constraint"
Competence vs. Performance Distinction: Central Diagnostic Framework Theoretical synthesis across nativist-constructionist debate All developmental failures When children fail tasks, diagnose whether lacking knowledge (competence deficit) OR possessing knowledge but lacking resources (performance limitation). Signature patterns: Can state rules but can't execute (performance); Simplified tasks reveal knowledge (performance); Reducing load improves performance (capacity issue); Priming required for success (exemplar-based) NATIVIST INTERPRETATION (Competence-Performance Dissociation): Early competence, delayed performance—abstract knowledge present early but masked by production limitations, lexical gaps, processing constraints. Example: 21-month-olds succeed in comprehension (Fisher preferential looking) but fail production (Tomasello elicitation) → abstract syntax EXISTS but retrieval difficult. CONSTRUCTIONIST INTERPRETATION (Gradual Competence Development): Knowledge itself becomes more abstract over time as children accumulate exemplars and extract commonalities via analogy. Example: 21-month-olds succeed ONLY with transitive warm-up priming (Dittmar) → knowledge is item-specific, NOT abstract. CRITICAL TEST: Does success require recent specific exemplars (exemplar-based) OR generalize without priming (abstract schema)? Fisher: 21-month-olds succeed with transitive warm-up (interpret as competence with performance facilitation); Dittmar: 21-month-olds fail without transitive warm-up (interpret as item-specific competence, NOT abstract); Tomasello: 2-year-olds fail novel verb extension in production (interpret as verb islands); Crain: 0% errors (interpret as innate constraint OR TP-based avoidance) Framework Both factors matter: EF/capacity improvements unlock latent competencies AND conceptual development occurs—continuous capacity×knowledge growth OR verb-specific→abstract trajectory (unresolved empirical question) "Competence-performance distinction: Nativists claim abstract syntax early but performance-limited (Fisher 21mo comprehension success); Constructionists claim gradual competence development (Dittmar: fail without priming, item-specific knowledge)—hinge: priming-dependence"
