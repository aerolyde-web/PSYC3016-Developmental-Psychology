SAQ 1 L19: Relational Learning
Explain how the Natural Partitions Hypothesis and comparison processes together account for children's development of relational abstraction. Use evidence from Christie & Gentner (2010) to support your answer.

Key Points to Include

  • Natural Partitions Hypothesis (Gentner 1982): Objects are ?perceptually cohesive and spatially bounded, making them easy to individuate; relations are ?dynamic, distributed across entities, and context-dependent
  • The problem: Relations don't "stand out" perceptually like objects do - they must be ?actively abstracted through comparison
  • Comparison as engine: Structural alignment of two instances highlights ?common relational structure while suppressing surface differences
Christie & Gentner (2010) Evidence:
Children shown pairs with same relation (e.g., two "on top" scenes) vs single exemplar. Comparison condition: ?57% relational matches. No comparison: ?2% relational matches. This demonstrates comparison ?invites structural alignment that makes relations "pop out".

Model Answer Structure

Opening: The Natural Partitions Hypothesis (Gentner, 1982) proposes that objects and relations differ fundamentally in their perceptual accessibility, creating an asymmetry that comparison processes help overcome.

Body - NPH: Objects are perceptually cohesive, spatially bounded, and temporally stable, making them easy to individuate as distinct entities. Relations, however, are dynamic, distributed across multiple entities, and context-dependent - they don't "stand out" as natural perceptual units.

Body - Comparison: Comparison acts as the "engine" of relational abstraction by forcing structural alignment between instances. When children compare two scenes sharing a relation (e.g., both showing "on top of"), the alignment process highlights the common relational structure while suppressing irrelevant surface differences.

Evidence: Christie & Gentner (2010) demonstrated this mechanism: children who compared two exemplars showing the same relation made 57% relational matches on a subsequent task, versus only 2% for children shown single exemplars. The dramatic difference shows comparison doesn't merely expose children to relations but actively invites the structural alignment that makes relational structure salient.

Marking criteria: NPH definition (2 marks) + Object vs relation asymmetry (2 marks) + Comparison mechanism (2 marks) + Christie & Gentner numbers + interpretation (2 marks)
SAQ 2 L19: Relational Learning
Explain how language supports relational learning through two mechanisms: invitation and reification. Use evidence from homesigner studies to demonstrate the importance of linguistic scaffolding.

Key Points to Include

  • Invitation: Language directs attention - saying "on" invites the child to ?focus on the spatial relation and compare instances sharing that label
  • Reification: Labels make relations ?thing-like - a stable cognitive handle that can be stored, retrieved, and transferred
  • Why both matter: Without labels, relations remain ?ephemeral and context-bound; labels convert them into portable conceptual units
Homesigner Studies:
Deaf individuals without access to conventional sign language (no spatial terms). Spatial reasoning task results:
Neutral trials: Homesigners = ?45%, Hearing speakers = ?73%
Cross-mapped trials: Homesigners = ?35%, Hearing = ?53% (chance = 33%)
This shows language provides ?critical scaffolding for abstracting and transferring spatial relations.

Model Answer Structure

Opening: Language supports relational learning through two complementary mechanisms: invitation (directing attention) and reification (creating stable representations).

Invitation mechanism: When adults use relational terms like "on" or "under," they invite children to attend to the spatial relation between objects rather than the objects themselves. This labeling prompts comparison across instances sharing the same label, triggering the structural alignment that highlights relational structure.

Reification mechanism: Labels transform fleeting, context-bound relations into "thing-like" cognitive entities. The word "on" creates a stable handle that can be stored in memory, retrieved across contexts, and applied to novel situations - converting an ephemeral relation into a portable conceptual unit.

Homesigner evidence: Homesigners (deaf individuals without conventional sign language) lack spatial relational terms. On spatial reasoning tasks: neutral trials showed 45% accuracy vs 73% for hearing speakers; cross-mapped trials (where object similarity conflicts with relational similarity) showed 35% vs 53%, with homesigners near chance (33%). This demonstrates that without linguistic scaffolding, even basic spatial reasoning suffers - language isn't merely labeling relations but is constitutive of robust relational representation.

Marking criteria: Invitation mechanism (2 marks) + Reification mechanism (2 marks) + Homesigner study design (1 mark) + Numbers (2 marks) + Interpretation (1 mark)
SAQ 3 L20: Thinking During Play
Explain children's implicit scientific reasoning during play using evidence from Schulz & Bonawitz (2007). Contrast this with the explicit Control of Variables strategy and explain the implicit-explicit gap.

Key Points to Include

  • Implicit sensitivity: Children's play behavior reveals sensitivity to ?confounded vs unconfounded evidence without explicit awareness
  • Schulz & Bonawitz finding: When evidence was confounded (ambiguous about causation), children ?played longer with the toy to resolve uncertainty
  • COV failure: In explicit tasks (Klahr's ramp experiment), children ?change multiple variables simultaneously, producing uninterpretable results
  • The gap: Implicit = ?"something is wrong with this evidence"; Explicit = ?"test one variable at a time"
Key Evidence:
Schulz & Bonawitz (2007): Showed children toy with confounded evidence (pressed two buttons, toy activated - which button worked?) vs unconfounded evidence (one button clearly causal). Confounded condition = ?longer play duration as children explored to resolve ambiguity.

Klahr Ramp Experiment: Children testing what makes balls roll faster change ?steepness AND surface AND ball type simultaneously, making results uninterpretable - they cannot generate controlled experiments without instruction.

Model Answer Structure

Opening: Children display implicit scientific reasoning in play that doesn't translate to explicit experimental design, revealing an important gap between intuitive sensitivity and articulated strategy.

Implicit competence: Schulz & Bonawitz (2007) demonstrated that preschoolers are sensitive to evidence quality during play. When children observed confounded evidence (two buttons pressed simultaneously when a toy activated), they played significantly longer than when evidence was unconfounded (one button clearly causal). This extended play represented spontaneous hypothesis-testing to resolve causal ambiguity.

Explicit limitation: Despite this implicit sensitivity, children fail at explicit scientific reasoning. In Klahr's ramp experiments, children asked to determine what makes balls roll faster systematically change multiple variables simultaneously (steepness, surface texture, ball weight), producing uninterpretable results. They cannot articulate or apply the Control of Variables strategy without explicit instruction.

The implicit-explicit gap: The gap reflects the difference between sensing "something is uncertain about this evidence" (implicit) and knowing "I should test one variable at a time" (explicit). Implicit intuitions provide raw material that education must convert into explicit, articulable procedures. This suggests play provides foundational scientific intuitions that formal instruction can build upon, rather than children being "blank slates" for scientific reasoning.

Marking criteria: Schulz & Bonawitz study + finding (2 marks) + COV definition + failure pattern (2 marks) + Gap explanation (2 marks) + Educational implication (2 marks)
SAQ 4 L20: Thinking During Play
Discuss the implicit-explicit gap in mathematical thinking during play, including SES disparities. Use evidence from Seo & Ginsburg (2004) and Siegler's number line intervention to illustrate how play-based learning can bridge this gap.

Key Points to Include

  • Seo & Ginsburg (2004): Observed preschoolers' free play; found mathematical behaviors including ?classification, magnitude comparison, pattern recognition, spatial reasoning
  • SES finding: Low-SES and middle-SES children showed ?equal mathematical play behaviors, but low-SES showed ?lower verbal mathematical reasoning
  • The gap: Implicit mathematical behaviors during play don't automatically ?become explicit, articulable mathematical knowledge without scaffolding
Siegler's Number Line Game:
Low-SES preschoolers played linear board game (spaces numbered 1-10) for ?4 sessions x 15 minutes.
Results:
- Linear estimation R²: ?0.22 to 0.94
- Magnitude comparison: ?65% to 89%
- Critical feature: ?Linear spatial layout makes number magnitude visually explicit through embodied movement

Model Answer Structure

Opening: Mathematical thinking emerges during play but requires intentional scaffolding to become explicit knowledge, with SES disparities revealing where this bridge fails.

Implicit mathematical behaviors: Seo & Ginsburg (2004) observed preschoolers during free play and documented substantial mathematical activity: classification (sorting by attributes), magnitude comparison ("I have more"), pattern recognition, enumeration, and spatial reasoning. Critically, these behaviors appeared equally across SES groups - low-SES children engaged in just as much mathematical play as middle-SES peers.

SES disparity: Despite equal play behaviors, low-SES children showed significantly lower verbal mathematical reasoning. This reveals that implicit mathematical behaviors don't automatically become explicit, articulable mathematical knowledge. Middle-SES children benefit from adult scaffolding (naming, questioning, extending) that converts play behaviors into explicit concepts; low-SES children often lack this "mathematicization" of play.

Bridging the gap: Siegler's number line game demonstrates how intentional play-based learning bridges implicit to explicit. Low-SES preschoolers played a linear board game (1-10) for just 4 sessions of 15 minutes each. Results: linear estimation accuracy improved from R²=0.22 to 0.94; magnitude comparison from 65% to 89%. The linear spatial layout made number magnitude visually and physically explicit through embodied movement along the number line, converting implicit magnitude intuitions into explicit numerical understanding.

Marking criteria: Seo & Ginsburg behaviors (2 marks) + SES disparity pattern (2 marks) + Gap explanation (2 marks) + Siegler numbers + mechanism (2 marks)
SAQ 5 L21: Executive Functions
Explain the competence-performance distinction in cognitive development using the Dimensional Change Card Sort (DCCS) task. How does this distinction change our interpretation of young children's cognitive failures?

Key Points to Include

  • DCCS task: Children sort cards by one dimension (shape), then must switch to sorting by ?another dimension (color)
  • The critical pattern: 3-year-olds can ?verbally state the new rule correctly but ?behaviorally perseverate on the old rule
  • Competence interpretation: Would conclude child ?lacks conceptual understanding of rule-switching
  • Performance interpretation: Child ?possesses knowledge but lacks executive function to execute it against competing responses
DCCS Evidence Pattern:
Comprehension check: "Where do red ones go?" 3-year-old ?correctly points to red pile
Post-switch behavior: Presented with red rabbit, 3-year-old sorts to ?rabbit pile (perseverates on shape despite knowing color rule)
Mechanism (Munakata): Child cannot maintain goal representation ?robustly enough in PFC to override stimulus-driven responding

Model Answer Structure

Opening: The competence-performance distinction fundamentally changes how we interpret young children's cognitive failures, shifting from "they don't understand" to "they can't execute what they understand."

DCCS task: Children sort cards varying on shape (rabbit/boat) and color (red/blue). First they sort by one dimension (e.g., shape), then must switch to the other (color). The critical finding: 3-year-olds who correctly sort during the initial phase and correctly verbalize the new rule ("red ones go here") nonetheless perseverate on the old sorting dimension post-switch.

The dissociation: Verbal rule knowledge demonstrates competence - the child conceptually understands what to do. But behavioral perseveration reveals a performance limitation - lacking the executive function capacity to disconnect behavior from the established mental set and reconnect it to the new rule. The 5-year-old succeeds not because they "understand better" (both ages pass verbal comprehension checks) but because they have sufficient cognitive flexibility to reconfigure stimulus-response mappings.

Reinterpretation: This changes our interpretation of many developmental failures. Rather than concluding children lack conceptual understanding, we must consider whether they possess knowledge but lack executive resources to demonstrate it. Munakata's framework proposes that executive function depends on robust goal representation - maintaining task rules actively enough in prefrontal cortex to override stimulus-driven responding. The 3-year-old knows the rule but cannot represent it robustly enough to outcompete the prepotent response.

Implications: Educational interventions should distinguish knowledge gaps (requiring instruction) from executive gaps (requiring scaffolding and reduced demands). Repeating instructions louder addresses the wrong bottleneck when the issue is performance, not competence.

Marking criteria: DCCS task description (2 marks) + Perseveration pattern (2 marks) + Competence vs performance distinction (2 marks) + Munakata mechanism (1 mark) + Implication (1 mark)
SAQ 6 L21: Executive Functions
Explain Halford's Relational Complexity Theory and how it reconceptualizes working memory capacity. Use the example of statistical interactions to illustrate why some cognitive tasks are impossible even for adults.

Key Points to Include

  • Traditional view: WM capacity = ?number of items (e.g., "7 plus or minus 2")
  • Halford's reconceptualization: WM capacity = ?number of elements that must be processed in PARALLEL because they relate to each other
  • Why it matters: Independent items can be ?chunked or processed serially; related items ?force parallel processing, hitting capacity limits
  • Adult ceiling: ?Quaternary (4-way) relations are maximal; 5-way interactions are uninterpretable
Statistical Interaction Example:
Two main effects (serial processing): "Fresh outsells frozen" AND "Chocolate outsells carrot" - can understand ?separately, no parallel binding required
2-way interaction (parallel required): "For chocolate, fresh=frozen; for carrot, frozen beats fresh" - must hold ?both variables simultaneously because effect of one DEPENDS on other
Why 5-way fails: Would need to track ?how a 4-way pattern changes across a 5th variable - exceeds binding capacity

Model Answer Structure

Opening: Halford's Relational Complexity Theory reconceptualizes working memory not as storage capacity but as the limit on how many elements can be processed in parallel when they must be related to one another.

The reconceptualization: Traditional "magic number 7" approaches treat WM as item storage. Halford argues the real constraint is relational binding - how many elements can be simultaneously coordinated when understanding their relationship requires holding them all in mind at once. Independent items can be chunked or processed sequentially, effectively bypassing capacity limits. Related items force parallel processing, hitting hard capacity limits.

Complexity levels: Unary relations (1 variable): "Fresh cakes outsell frozen" - single dimension, minimal WM load. Binary relations (2 variables): "For chocolate, no fresh/frozen difference; for carrot, frozen wins" - must process both dimensions simultaneously because the effect of one depends on the other. Ternary relations (3 variables): "That 2-way pattern differs across bakeries" - approaching adult ceiling. Quaternary (4 variables): Maximum adult capacity with effort.

Why 5-way interactions fail: To understand a 5-way interaction, you'd need to track how a 4-way pattern changes across levels of a 5th variable. Since 4-way already approaches capacity ceiling, embedding it in another level exceeds binding capacity. People who claim to understand 5-way interactions are actually decomposing them into simpler sub-analyses (serial processing), not genuinely binding 5 elements in parallel.

Developmental implication: Children under 5 struggle with even binary relational complexity, explaining difficulty with tasks requiring simultaneous consideration of how two dimensions jointly determine outcomes.

Marking criteria: Traditional vs Halford distinction (2 marks) + Parallel vs serial processing (2 marks) + Interaction example with levels (2 marks) + Why 5-way fails (2 marks)
SAQ 7 L21: Executive Functions
Explain how socioeconomic status affects executive function development through chronic stress. Include the neural mechanisms involved and the test anxiety example to illustrate how stress disrupts working memory.

Key Points to Include

  • SES-stress pathway: Low SES involves chronic stressors (unstable housing, food insecurity, family conflict) that ?activate prolonged stress responses
  • Neural mechanism: Chronic stress specifically impairs ?prefrontal cortex development and PFC-amygdala connectivity
  • Why PFC specifically: Prefrontal cortex has ?prolonged developmental trajectory making it vulnerable to environmental influences
  • Domain specificity: EF and language most affected (both ?frontal regions); sensory/motor less affected
Test Anxiety Model:
Low stress: WM capacity = ?4 units, all available for task
High stress: Worry ("What if I fail?") consumes ?~2.5 units, leaving only ?1.5 units for math
Result: Familiar problems = ?OK (automatized, low WM demand); Novel problems = ?FAIL (high WM demand exceeds reduced capacity)
Chronic parallel: Low-SES children face ?constant WM overhead from monitoring threats, worrying about needs

Model Answer Structure

Opening: Socioeconomic status affects executive function through chronic stress pathways that specifically impair prefrontal cortex development and disrupt working memory availability.

SES-stress pathway: Low-SES environments involve chronic stressors: unstable housing, food insecurity, family conflict, unpredictable caregiving. These activate prolonged stress responses (elevated cortisol, HPA axis activation) that disrupt brain development, particularly in the prefrontal cortex.

Neural mechanism: The PFC has a prolonged developmental trajectory (continuing to age 25), creating extended vulnerability to environmental influences. Chronic stress specifically impairs PFC maturation and weakens PFC-amygdala connectivity - the neural pathway enabling cognitive control over emotional responses. This explains why EF and language (both frontal functions) are most affected by SES while sensory/motor systems are relatively spared.

Test anxiety illustration: Test anxiety provides a clear model of acute stress disrupting WM. Under low stress, full WM capacity (4 units) is available for task processing. Under high stress, worry consumes approximately 2.5 units ("What if I fail?", physiological monitoring), leaving only 1.5 units for the task. This explains why anxious students succeed on familiar, automatized problems (low WM demand) but fail on novel problems requiring integration (high WM demand) - it's not missing knowledge but reduced available capacity.

Chronic parallel: Low-SES children face a chronic version of this disruption. Their daily cognitive load includes monitoring for threats, worrying about basic needs, and processing unpredictable environments - a constant WM overhead that reduces capacity available for learning, creating the appearance of lower ability when it's actually reduced available capacity due to environmental burden.

Marking criteria: SES-stress pathway (2 marks) + Neural mechanism (2 marks) + Test anxiety model with numbers (2 marks) + Chronic stress parallel (2 marks)
SAQ 8 L21: Executive Functions
Describe the Tools of the Mind curriculum and explain how it uses Vygotskian principles to build executive function. Use the Buddy Reading activity to illustrate the progression from external scaffolding to internalized self-regulation.

Key Points to Include

  • Theoretical foundation: Vygotskian principles - ?language as cognitive tool, external scaffolding gradually internalized, peer regulation precedes self-regulation
  • External scaffolds: Concrete visual cues (ear picture = ?listener role, lips picture = ?speaker role) reduce WM demand and provide inhibition cues
  • Internalization process: Through repeated practice, external regulation becomes ?private speech ("I need to listen") then fully internalized self-regulation
  • Transfer evidence: Children showed gains on ?novel computerized EF tasks they'd never practiced, indicating genuine capacity enhancement
Diamond et al. (2007) Results:
Tools children vs literacy-curriculum controls on novel EF tasks:
- ?Dramatic outperformance on Dots, Flanker, Hearts-and-Flowers tasks
- Effects ?largest on most demanding conditions requiring rule-switching and inhibition
- Control schools ?eventually abandoned their curriculum and adopted Tools due to improved classroom behavior
Replication note: 2017 systematic review showed ?weak overall effects - implementation quality matters enormously

Model Answer Structure

Opening: Tools of the Mind is a Vygotskian preschool curriculum designed to build executive function through structured play activities using external scaffolding that gradually internalizes as self-regulation.

Vygotskian principles: Three key principles: (1) Language as cognitive tool - children use private speech to regulate behavior ("I need to listen quietly"); (2) External scaffolding gradually internalized - concrete visual aids support performance initially, then fade as skills strengthen; (3) Peer regulation as intermediate step - children regulate each other before achieving full self-regulation.

Buddy Reading example: Initially, children struggle with turn-taking - both want to talk, neither listens. The scaffold: one child receives an ear picture (listener role), the other lips picture (speaker role). This makes roles concrete and provides inhibition cues without taxing working memory. Over weeks of practice, children use private speech ("I have the ear, I listen"), eventually internalizing regulation such that pictures become unnecessary. By week 12, children negotiate roles verbally without external cues - the external scaffold has been internalized as self-regulation.

Evidence: Diamond et al. (2007) showed Tools children dramatically outperformed literacy-curriculum controls on novel computerized EF tasks (Dots, Flanker, Hearts-and-Flowers) - tasks they'd never practiced. This transfer indicates genuine executive function capacity enhancement, not just specific skill learning. Effects were largest on most demanding conditions requiring rule-switching and inhibition.

Caveat: A 2017 systematic review found weak overall effects across implementations, highlighting that curriculum efficacy depends heavily on implementation quality and fidelity.

Marking criteria: Vygotskian principles (2 marks) + Buddy Reading scaffold description (2 marks) + Internalization progression (2 marks) + Evidence + transfer (2 marks)
