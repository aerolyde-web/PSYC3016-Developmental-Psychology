‚ö° CRITICAL DECISION POINT: Child Shows Withdrawal Signs

Child is looking away, giving monosyllabic responses, fidgeting. Your response depends ENTIRELY on context:

RESEARCH: "Do you want to keep going or would you like to stop?" (probe for continued assent)

FORENSIC: "I notice you're looking away. Remember you don't have to talk if you don't want to." (document, don't pressure)

CLINICAL: "I see it's hard to talk about this. That's okay. Your body is telling me this is difficult." (name resistance as DATA)

LO4 Key Framework: NICHD Investigative Interview Protocol
"Describe the NICHD protocol and explain how it addresses developmental considerations in forensic interviewing..."
üìã Prof. Michael Lamb (Cambridge) ‚Äî NICHD Protocol
Full Name: National Institute of Child Health and Human Development Investigative Interview Protocol
Purpose: Best-practice guidelines for interviewing children in cases of child sexual abuse
Developers: Team of developmental psychologists, linguists, police officers, and legal professionals
Core Principle: Maximize FREE RECALL memory through open-ended prompts; minimize "risky" focused questions
Result: 4-5√ó more details through free recall compared to standard interviews, with lower error rates
Avoids: Anatomical dolls and other risky techniques
Phase What Happens Cognitive/Social Function
1. INTRODUCTORY
(Ground Rules)		 Interviewer introduces self, explains child's task (describe events in detail, tell truth), explains ground rules: can say "I don't remember," "I don't know," "I don't understand," or CORRECT THE INTERVIEWER Meta-cognitive awareness of truth/knowledge boundaries; permission to correct adult; reduces authority effect
2. RAPPORT-BUILDING
(Narrative Training)		 Child talks about events UNRELATED to suspected abuse; practices leading the conversation; experiences the level of detail expected Episodic memory retrieval practice; child leads, adult follows; trains detailed free-recall BEFORE substantive phase
3. SUBSTANTIVE
(Free Recall)		 Begins with invitation: "Tell me everything that happened from beginning to end as best as you can remember." Follow with "Then what happened?" Use cued invitations based on child's own words. Uncontaminated memory retrieval; child as information expert, adult as naive listener; majority of information from free recall
‚ö†Ô∏è RAPPORT PHASE IS NOT ABOUT LIKEABILITY: The purpose of rapport-building is to TRAIN the child in the narrative style expected during substantive questioning. By practicing detailed recall about neutral events, children learn the level of detail expected. This is cognitive preparation, not social bonding.
LO4 Question Hierarchy: Veracity vs Detail Trade-Off
"Explain the hierarchy of question types in forensic interviewing and why order matters..."
1 FREE RECALL PROMPTS ‚Äî "Tell me everything" | Low yield, MAXIMUM accuracy | Always use FIRST, exhaustively
2 CUED INVITATIONS ‚Äî "You mentioned [their word]. Tell me everything about that." | Moderate yield, high accuracy if using child's language
3 DIRECTIVE QUESTIONS ‚Äî "Where/When/Who" (never "Why") | High yield, moderate error risk | For critical missing info ONLY
4 OPTION-POSING ‚Äî "Was it X or Y?" | Forces choice, high contamination risk | Last resort, include "something else" option
‚õî SUGGESTIVE QUESTIONS ‚Äî "He touched you, didn't he?" | Destroys veracity, creates false memories | NEVER JUSTIFIED ‚Äî forensically inadmissible
CRITICAL INSIGHT: CONTAMINATION IS IRREVERSIBLE
The order of question types is IRREVERSIBLE.

Once you ask a suggestive question ‚Üí you CANNOT undo its contaminating effects
                                    ‚Üí returning to open-ended prompts doesn't help
                                    ‚Üí there is NO mechanism to "clean" contaminated testimony

The hierarchy is NOT a menu ‚Äî it's a SEQUENCE.
You must exhaust Level 1 before moving to Level 2.
Each step DOWN trades veracity for detail ‚Äî a Faustian bargain.

LO4 Clinical Context: Silence as Data, Not Refusal
"Explain how working with children in clinical practice differs from research and forensic contexts..."
Clinical Question Answer
Does child need to agree to treatment? Not necessarily ‚Äî parent/guardian consent sufficient. But lack of child buy-in limits effectiveness.
Is non-engagement refusal of consent? NO ‚Äî it's a challenge to be overcome, AND it's clinical DATA about the child's defensive patterns, trust issues, or trauma responses.
Can child request parent presence/absence? Yes, within limits. Consider separation anxiety. Clinician makes final judgment on therapeutic necessity.
Child in care of social services? Who gives consent? ‚Äî Usually the caseworker/designated guardian. Documentation critical.
Should child know nature of participation? Generally yes ‚Äî age-appropriate explanation of therapeutic goals and process.

‚ö° THE CLINICAL PARADOX: Silence Means Different Things

RESEARCH: Child sits silently for 20 minutes ‚Üí Potential withdrawal of assent ‚Üí Must probe: "Do you want to stop?"

CLINICAL: Child sits silently for 20 minutes ‚Üí Clinical DATA about coping mechanisms, trust issues, trauma responses ‚Üí Name it: "I notice it's hard to talk. That silence is telling me something important about how difficult this is."

Same surface behavior, OPPOSITE professional interpretation.
