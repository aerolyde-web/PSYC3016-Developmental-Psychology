Thematic Overview: The Architecture of Early Cognition

Lectures 15-18 address the foundational question of cognitive development: How do infants build knowledge about the world before they can speak or act? The arc moves from methods (L15: habituation paradigm mechanics) to theoretical debate (L16: nativism vs constructivism) to embodied alternatives (L17: dynamic systems) to decisive tests (L18: relational learning). The signature pattern across all lectures is that increasing cognitive load lowers processing level—the same infant succeeds with simple stimuli but fails with complex stimuli, even when the underlying structure being tested is identical. This load-dependent variation supports neoconstructivism (hierarchical construction through statistical learning) over nativism (domain-specific modules triggered by specific input).

The Habituation Paradigm as Interpretive Framework

The habituation paradigm converts looking time from a continuous, ambiguous variable into an interpretable cognitive signal through a two-phase architecture. The habituation phase serves dual functions: establishing each infant's individual baseline engagement level (accounting for temperament/arousal differences) and ensuring the infant has encoded the stimulus sufficiently to become bored. The standard criterion—3 consecutive trials below 50% of first-3-trial baseline—operationally defines "habituated" and enables test phase interpretation. The test phase presents novel stimuli interspersed with familiar stimuli, with the critical interpretive move being that dishabituation (looking time recovery) indicates discrimination while maintained habituation (continued low looking) indicates the infant treats novel as equivalent to familiar. Procedural controls are essential: caregiver wears occluding headphones to prevent social cueing, observer is blinded to stimulus identity, and attention-getters standardize trial initiation. The most dangerous error is including infants who never reached criterion—they enter test phase in unpredictable preference states, contaminating data.

The Generalization Paradigm: Discrimination vs Abstraction

Cohen & Strauss (1979) demonstrated that habituation reveals what infants extract as invariant across varied exemplars, transforming looking time from a perceptual measure into a window on conceptual structure. They habituated 30-week-old infants to line-drawn faces under three between-subjects conditions varying habituation breadth. Condition 1 (narrow: 1 face, 1 orientation) produced dishabituation to both novel orientation and novel face—infants encoded the specific viewpoint-dependent representation. Condition 2 (intermediate: 1 face, multiple orientations) produced no dishabituation to novel orientation but dishabituation to novel face—infants extracted view-invariant identity, generalizing across orientations while maintaining face discrimination. Condition 3 (broad: multiple faces, multiple orientations) produced no dishabituation to either—infants extracted the superordinate category "faces." Younger infants showed novelty preference to everything in all conditions, proving the effect reflects cognitive pattern extraction emerging with development, not low-level confounds. The core principle: breadth of variation in habituation stimuli controls the level of abstraction in the learned representation.

Framing the Nativism-Constructivism Debate Correctly

The nativism-constructivism debate is NOT "nature versus nurture"—this framing is fundamentally misleading because everything involves both genes and learning. Humans share 98% of DNA with chimpanzees yet only humans acquire language—clearly genetics matter. But even Chomsky, the archetypal nativist, acknowledges infants must learn whether they're in an English-speaking or Mandarin-speaking environment. The genuine dispute concerns the structure of learning mechanisms: are they domain-specific (tuned to particular content like objects, faces, or syntax) or domain-general (applying the same associative/statistical processes to all inputs)? Nativists propose that evolution equipped infants with domain-specific learning architectures embodying expectations about specific domains—a putative "object module" expects that surfaces showing common motion constitute unified objects that persist when occluded. Constructivists argue infants are born only with domain-general perceptual systems sensitive to low-level features; through experience, infants notice statistical regularities and hierarchically construct representations. The key difference: nativists claim the infant arrives with a "theory" about objects; constructivists claim the infant builds that theory from perceptual primitives.

Memory vs True Permanence: The Meltzoff & Moore Dissociation

Meltzoff & Moore (1998) provide the critical constructivist evidence distinguishing memory for object features from true object permanence. In the feature memory task, infants see an object go behind a screen; either the same or different object emerges. Both 5-month-olds and 9-month-olds look longer when the object changes shape—they retain perceptual memory of the occluded object's properties. In the spatial continuity task, an object goes behind Screen 1 and emerges from Screen 2 without ever appearing in the visible gap between screens—a physically impossible "teleportation." 9-month-olds look longer at this violation (expect continuous trajectory), but 5-month-olds show no differential looking—they don't expect the object to traverse continuous space. This critical dissociation proves that understanding spatiotemporal continuity—a core component of true object permanence—develops later than feature memory, supporting Piaget's 8-9 month timeline. For exams: don't equate any surprise about hidden objects with full permanence—require both feature tracking AND spatial continuity understanding.

The A-not-B Error Reinterpreted: Embodied Motor Memory

Smith's (1999) postural reset finding provides the most compelling evidence for embodied cognition in infancy and fundamentally reinterprets Piaget's A-not-B error. In the classic A-not-B task, infants repeatedly reach to location A to retrieve a toy, then watch as the toy is hidden at location B—but continue to reach to A. Piaget interpreted this as incomplete object permanence: infants don't represent the object as existing independently of their own actions. Smith demonstrated that having the infant stand up and sit down between trials ELIMINATES the perseverative error—the infant correctly reaches to B. This cannot be explained by object permanence accounts (standing up doesn't teach about objects), but is perfectly explained by embodied motor memory: the infant developed a habit of "reach-left-from-sitting" which is encoded relative to body position. Changing posture breaks the motor habit, freeing the infant to reach based on current object location rather than motor memory. For exam purposes, this is the critical reinterpretation: A-not-B error reflects embodied motor habit, not incomplete object permanence—cognition is tied to body state.

The Younger & Cohen Signature: Load-Dependent Processing Level

The Younger and Cohen paradigm provides foundational evidence for hierarchical construction through statistical learning, demonstrating that infants detect feature correlations and use these to form higher-level representational units. The critical developmental pattern across studies reveals an age × task complexity interaction supporting the constructivist processing principle: infants attempt the highest level of representation their capacity permits, but revert to lower levels when overloaded. Four-month-olds consistently encode features only (C low, U low, N high), never detecting correlations regardless of task difficulty. Ten-month-olds reliably learn correlations (C low, U high, N high) even with moderate complexity. Seven-month-olds are the theoretically crucial transitional group: they succeed with easy tasks (3 features, all correlated), show flat habituation curves when attempting but failing at medium tasks (indicating struggle, not disengagement), and—most critically—revert to feature-level processing when given extended time on too-difficult tasks (Y&C 1986 E4). This last finding is exam-critical because it demonstrates that more processing time can paradoxically produce worse outcomes, proving the adaptive nature of the cognitive system: when the infant recognizes correlation learning exceeds capacity, they strategically simplify to an achievable goal.

The Shape Bias as Learned Meta-Pattern

The shape bias in word learning exemplifies hierarchical statistical learning scaling from perception to language, demonstrating how higher-order correlations emerge from accumulated lower-level patterns. The developmental sequence proceeds through four stages: (1) infants hear words paired with objects; (2) they extract within-category regularities (all cups share cup-shape, all balls share roundness); (3) after accumulating ~50 nouns, they detect a meta-level correlation across categories—object nouns typically refer to shape-based categories; (4) this higher-order pattern becomes a default inference strategy enabling rapid word learning from single exemplars. The Gershkoff-Stowe (2004) finding is theoretically decisive: shape bias correlates with vocabulary size rather than age (<25 nouns: ~30% shape generalization; 26-50: ~60-70%; 51+: ~90%+), proving the bias is learned through word-object pairing experience rather than innately specified. This directly contradicts nativist accounts (e.g., Markman's whole-object assumption) that treat word learning constraints as innate.

The Causal Perception Debate: Decisive Evidence for Neoconstructivism

The causal perception literature provides the most decisive empirical evidence distinguishing nativist modularity from neoconstructivist hierarchical construction, because it demonstrates systematic task-dependent variation that modules cannot explain. Leslie (1984) found that 6-month-olds habituated to causal launching events categorized by causal status with simple geometric shapes—a finding consistent with an innate causal module triggered by spatiotemporal cues. However, Oakes & Cohen (1990) challenged this by changing ONLY object complexity while holding spatiotemporal cues constant: with complex realistic toys instead of simple shapes, 6-month-olds failed to show causal categorization. This is devastating for nativism: if a causal module exists and is triggered by specific spatiotemporal input, it should fire regardless of what objects are undergoing the interaction—the module shouldn't "care" whether it's squares or dinosaurs. By 10 months, increased capacity allowed causal perception even with complex objects—but Cohen & Oakes (1993) showed that even 10-month-olds reverted to featural processing when complex objects VARIED trial-by-trial. Same age, same complexity level, same relational structure, only variability changed—and processing level shifts from causal to featural. Nativism has no mechanism for this; neoconstructivism explains it perfectly: object variability requires encoding multiple identities, consuming capacity and leaving insufficient resources for causal relation extraction.

The Signature Pattern That Distinguishes Frameworks

The cognitive overload pattern is the signature behavioral prediction that uniquely identifies neoconstructivism and distinguishes it from nativist modularity. The pattern is consistent: increasing cognitive load lowers the information processing level, causing the same infant at the same age to succeed with low-load stimuli but fail with high-load stimuli even when the relational structure being tested is held constant. This appears across both same/different learning and causal perception paradigms, with load manipulated through multiple independent dimensions: object complexity (simple shapes vs complex toys), object variability (repeated vs changing objects), exemplar count (2 pairs vs 6 pairs), object salience (novel/habituated vs familiar-not-habituated), and even processing time (standard vs extended trials causing reversion). The theoretical significance cannot be overstated: nativist accounts predict that if the triggering input for a module is present, the module should fire regardless of task variations—there is no mechanism in modularity theory for why Leslie's 6-month-olds succeed with simple shapes but Oakes & Cohen's 6-month-olds fail with complex toys when the spatiotemporal launching cues are identical. Neoconstructivism explains all these patterns with a single principle: relations are hierarchically built on top of objects through statistical learning, and when object-level processing exceeds available capacity, the higher-level relational representation cannot be constructed or accessed.

The Unified Neoconstructivist Account

Across L15-L18, a unified developmental pattern emerges that confirms the neoconstructivist framework: relational abstraction capacity exists from the earliest testable ages, but its expression is entirely contingent on cognitive load remaining below age-specific thresholds. Anderson et al. (2018) demonstrated that 3-month-old human infants can abstract same/different with 2 exemplar pairs; Leslie (1984) showed that 6-month-olds perceive causality with simple shapes; Ferry et al. (2015) established that 7-9 month-olds succeed with 4 diverse pairs; Oakes & Cohen (1990) found that 10-month-olds can handle complex objects. But in every case, increasing load—whether through object complexity, variability, exemplar count, or salience—causes systematic reversion to lower-level featural processing. The pattern is not all-or-nothing based on age-locked module maturation but graded and task-dependent, exactly as hierarchical construction predicts. The infant cognitive system is revealed to be one unified, domain-general statistical learning system rather than a collection of independent domain-specific modules. The critical theoretical insight is that early competence does not mean robust competence: infants have relational capacity from 3 months, but this capacity is fragile, hierarchically dependent on object processing, and subject to overload—a profile that nativism cannot accommodate but neoconstructivism naturally predicts.
