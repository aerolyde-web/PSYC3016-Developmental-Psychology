CORE THESIS: Two complementary approaches to moral development: (1) MORAL REASONING (Kohlberg) = what SHOULD be done in hypothetical dilemmas, develops through 6 stages (most adults Stage 3-4), NOT same as behavior. LO1 What Kohlberg Measures (Critical Definition)
"What does Kohlberg's theory measure and what does it NOT measure?"
Question Type What It IS What It Is NOT
Moral Reasoning What people think SHOULD be done in hypothetical dilemmas NOT what they would actually DO
Method Structured interviews with moral dilemmas (e.g., Heinz dilemma, child drops roll) NOT behavioral observation
Cognitive Component Reasoning, justification, "thinking side" NOT feelings, emotions, or actual behavior
‚ö†Ô∏è EXAM CRITICAL: Moral reasoning ‚â† Moral behavior. The SAME behavior (e.g., helping) can stem from Stage 2 (self-interest: "What's in it for me?"), Stage 3 (social approval: "Others will think I'm good"), or Stage 6 (universal principles: "All humans deserve help"). You CANNOT infer stage from behavior alone.

LO1 Social Domain Theory: Moral vs Conventional Distinction (Turiel, Smetana)
"Explain the distinction between moral transgressions and social conventions..."
Criterion MORAL Transgressions CONVENTIONAL Transgressions
Definition Cause harm, suffering, interpersonal tension (intrinsic to event) Break social rules coordinating group behavior (no inherent harm)
Examples Hitting, stealing, biting, not sharing, pushing Not sitting on mat, not wearing clothes, not queuing, improper greetings
Wrong without rules? YES ‚Äî inherently wrong (harm to victim regardless of prohibition) NO ‚Äî only wrong if prohibited (rule-dependent)
Context-bound? NO ‚Äî wrong everywhere (home AND school) YES ‚Äî depends on setting (wrong at school, OK at home)
Authority-modifiable? NO ‚Äî remains wrong even if authority allows it YES ‚Äî OK if authority permits
Seriousness Very serious, more deserving of punishment Less serious, mild response (encouragement)
Basis Factors INTRINSIC to event (pain, unfairness) EXTERNAL rules, social organization
Universality Universal across cultures (harm always recognized) Arbitrary, alterable by consensus (varies by culture)
DECISION TREE: Does it cause HARM?
YES ‚Üí MORAL (wrong without rules, universal, authority cannot modify)
NO ‚Üí CONVENTIONAL (rule-dependent, context-bound, authority can modify)

‚ö° HARM PRINCIPLE: When Conventions Shift to Moral

If conventional transgression causes HARM ‚Üí shifts to moral domain.
Example: Not wearing clothes = CONVENTIONAL
BUT forced to remain unclothed in Arctic = MORAL (causes harm)
Example: Not removing shoes in place of worship = CONVENTIONAL
BUT deeply offensive to others = MORAL (emotional harm)
‚Üí Context determines whether rule violations remain conventional or become moral through harm causation.
LO1 Key Evidence: 30-Month-Olds Distinguish Moral from Conventional
"What evidence shows that very young children distinguish moral from conventional transgressions?"
Study Method & Finding Implication
Smetana (1981)
Landmark Study		 Children ages 3-10. Present transgressions (hitting vs dress code). Ask: (1) Is it wrong? (2) Would it be OK if there were no rule? (3) How serious? Even 3-year-olds (and evidence at 30mo): Moral violations rated as more serious, wrong even without rule, generalizable across contexts 30 months ‚Äî moral-conventional distinction emerges
Turiel (1979, 1983)
Theory Source		 Concerned with how children APPRAISE (judge) actions in moral terms (right/wrong), not what they SHOULD do. Reasons vs feelings ‚Äî evaluative not prescriptive Different tradition from Kohlberg
‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL PARADOX: 30-month-olds distinguish moral from conventional but are "pre-conventional" on Kohlberg's stages. This shows: (1) Moral APPRAISAL (is it right/wrong?) develops BEFORE moral REASONING (what should I do?), (2) Articulation limits ‚â† understanding limits, (3) Challenges Kohlberg's slow stage progression.
LO1 Moral Reasoning vs Moral Appraisal: Two Traditions
"Distinguish between moral reasoning and moral appraisal approaches..."
Dimension Moral REASONING (Kohlberg) Moral APPRAISAL (Social Domain)
Core Question What SHOULD be done? Is it RIGHT or WRONG?
Theoretical Tradition Rationalist (cognitive, thought) Sentimentalist (evaluative, intuition/emotion)
Development Pattern Slow hierarchical stages (6 stages) Early-emerging distinction (30 months)
Method Hypothetical dilemmas, verbal justification Harm perception, rule contingency judgments
Age of Competence Adolescence for conventional morality (Stage 3-4) 30 months for moral-conventional distinction
Gender Differences Males score "higher" (Stage 4 > Stage 3) No gender differences in distinction ability
Cultural Variation Universal sequence claimed (but biased toward individualist) Universal harm recognition (but conventions vary)
Behavioral Prediction Poor reasoning-behavior link Understanding ‚â† compliance (impulse control needed)
INTEGRATION: Neither predicts behavior alone
Reasoning without emotion = empty principles | Intuition without reasoning = can't handle complex dilemmas
BOTH needed for mature moral psychology

EXAM TRAPS Common Exam Errors to Avoid
Error Correction
Kohlberg measures behavior or feelings NO: Measures reasoning about what "should" be done (NOT what they would do, NOT how they feel)
Stages are fixed/invariant NO: Context-dependent; people use multiple stages simultaneously
30-month-olds are "pre-conventional" = no moral understanding NO: They distinguish moral (harm) from conventional (rules) at 30 months despite being "pre-conventional"
Care vs Justice = Moral vs Conventional NO: Different distinctions. Care/justice = Gilligan's orientations (gender). Moral/conventional = harm vs social rules
Stage 6 is common or everyone progresses to it NO: Very rare (~1%). Most adults remain at Stages 3-4.
