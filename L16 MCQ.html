1Ô∏è‚É£ The Core Debate: What Are We Really Arguing About? Foundation
Q1
According to the lecture, what is the REAL debate between nativists and constructivists (not the oversimplified version)?
Whether infants are born with any knowledge at all versus learning everything from experience
Whether genetics or environment matters more for development
Whether infants have domain-specific learning mechanisms with innate knowledge of specific domains versus domain-general learning mechanisms with no innate content knowledge
Whether infants develop faster or slower than Piaget claimed
Correct Answer: C
The lecturer emphasized that BOTH sides agree everything has genetic components and everything involves learning. The real debate is about the ARCHITECTURE of learning: Do we have specialized modules for specific domains (objects, language, faces) with built-in expectations, or do we have only general-purpose learning mechanisms that work the same way across all domains?
Why other answers are wrong:
A: Too simplistic - even nativists acknowledge learning is essential
B: This is the outdated "nature vs. nurture" framing the lecture explicitly rejects
D: While relevant, this is a consequence, not the core theoretical question

Methodological Awareness Critical Thinking
Q3
The nativist position is often labeled "anti-Piagetian" while constructivism is "neo-Piagetian." What does this mean?
Nativists reject all of Piaget's methods while constructivists use them exactly as he did
Nativists argue infants START with knowledge (contrary to Piaget's claim they must develop it), while constructivists update Piaget's domain-general learning approach
Nativists think development is faster and constructivists think it's slower
Nativists study older children while constructivists study infants
Correct Answer: B
Piaget argued that infants must CONSTRUCT knowledge through experience, lacking innate concepts like object permanence. Nativists (Carey, Spelke, Baillargeon) explicitly challenge this, arguing for core knowledge present very early. Constructivists (Cohen & Cashon) retain Piaget's general learning approach but update it with modern information-processing theory - hence "neo" (new) Piagetian.
Why other answers are wrong:
A: Both sides use modified versions of methods; this isn't the theoretical distinction
C: Timeline differences exist but this doesn't capture the core theoretical divide
D: Both study infants; the age focus isn't the defining feature
HIGH YIELD Theoretical Framework
Q4
Even Noam Chomsky, the classic nativist, acknowledges that infants must learn which language (English, Mandarin, etc.) they're exposed to. How do nativists reconcile this with claims of innate language knowledge?
They don't - this is a fatal flaw in nativism
The innate knowledge is about the STRUCTURE of language (domain-specific learning mechanisms), not the specific vocabulary or grammar of any particular language
Actually, they claim all languages are innately known
They argue genetics determines which language you'll speak
Correct Answer: B
This is the crucial distinction for understanding nativism. The "innate language learning device" doesn't contain English or Mandarin - it contains expectations about HOW language works (it has syntax, phonemes cluster in certain ways, etc.). These domain-specific learning mechanisms let you rapidly acquire whatever language you're exposed to. Similarly, an "object module" wouldn't know about cups vs. balls, but would know objects persist when hidden.
Critical Concept: Innate doesn't mean "fixed and unchanging" - it means "specialized learning architecture tuned to specific domains."
HIGH YIELD Nativism Domain-Specificity
Q5
According to constructivists (Cohen & Cashon), what IS innate in infants?
Nothing - infants are blank slates
Domain-general information processing systems that detect low-level features like color and motion, from which higher-level units are hierarchically constructed
Specific knowledge about objects, agents, and number
Only reflexes and basic motor patterns
Correct Answer: B
Constructivists are NOT blank-slate theorists. They argue infants are born with perceptual systems (can detect color, edges, motion) and general learning mechanisms. The key difference from nativism: these systems aren't specialized for objects vs. language vs. faces - they're domain-GENERAL. The infant uses the SAME learning processes to figure out objects, social partners, and everything else. Cohen & Straus (1979) showed older infants could form higher-level generalizations from lower-level features.
Why other answers are wrong:
A: "Blank slate" is a strawman - no modern constructivist holds this
C: This is the NATIVIST position (core knowledge)
D: Understates constructivist claims - they acknowledge sophisticated perceptual systems
HIGH YIELD Constructivism Domain-General Learning
Q6

They contradict each other, so one must be wrong

The fact that you learn English or Mandarin depending on exposure shows learning matters. The ACTUAL question is: Does your genetic endowment give you specialized modules for language, objects, etc. (nativism), or just general-purpose learning machinery (constructivism)?

Q7
What does "information encapsulation" mean in the context of cognitive modules?
The module stores information in a special memory system
The module operates on specific inputs and cannot access information from other cognitive systems or modules
The module's operations are hidden from conscious awareness
Information in the module cannot be forgotten
Correct Answer: B
Information encapsulation is a key property of modules. The object module, for example, can't access your semantic knowledge that "ghosts can pass through walls" - it only operates on spatiotemporal perceptual cues. This is why you might feel surprised even when you know logically that a magic trick has an explanation. The module processes its domain-specific inputs independently of other knowledge.
Why other answers are wrong:
A: This describes memory storage, not information encapsulation
C: While modules may be automatic, encapsulation specifically refers to isolation from other information
D: Encapsulation doesn't relate to memory persistence
Modularity Nativism
Q8
According to the modular view, what is "core knowledge"?
The most important information you learn in school
Knowledge stored in deep brain structures like the hippocampus
The innate knowledge that modules apply when triggered by specific perceptual inputs - such as "objects persist when occluded" or "solid objects can't pass through each other"
Simple factual knowledge that develops first
Correct Answer: C
Susan Carey uses "core knowledge" to describe the innate principles that modules embody. For objects: persistence (they exist when hidden), solidity (can't interpenetrate), continuity (move on connected paths). These aren't learned from experience according to nativists - they're the starting point that enables learning about SPECIFIC objects. You don't have to learn that things continue existing; you learn what specific things look like.
Distinction: Core knowledge = innate principles about domains. Learned knowledge = facts about specific instances within those domains.
HIGH YIELD Core Knowledge Nativism
Q9
What does it mean that modules are "sensitive to specific inputs"?
They only work when the infant is paying close attention
They require particular perceptual cues (like common motion for objects) to activate and apply their core knowledge
They are easily damaged by abnormal sensory experiences
They can detect very subtle differences in stimuli
Correct Answer: B
This is crucial for understanding nativist predictions. The object module has core knowledge about object persistence and solidity, BUT it only applies this knowledge when it receives the right perceptual trigger. For object unity, that trigger is COMMON MOTION (surfaces moving together in the same direction). Without motion, the module doesn't activate. This explains why infants show object unity with moving rods but not stationary ones.
Exam Strategy: Nativists can explain developmental changes by saying perceptual systems mature, providing better input to innate modules. This is their response to constructivist evidence of gradual development.
HIGH YIELD Modularity Triggering Input
Q10
The lecture mentions a potential "specialized brain tissue" for faces (fusiform face area). How does this relate to the modularity hypothesis?
It proves modularity is correct
It's consistent with modularity (dedicated neural circuits for specific domains), though whether face processing is truly modular remains debated
It contradicts modularity because faces are processed in one location
It's irrelevant to modularity, which is a psychological not neural concept
Correct Answer: B
Modularity predicts that domain-specific processing might be implemented in specialized neural circuits. The fusiform face area is suggestive - it activates more for faces than other objects. However, the lecturer notes this is "controversial" - some argue it responds to expertise with any category you process frequently. The broader point: neural localization would support but isn't required for modularity. The key is functional specialization (dedicated processing), which could be implemented various ways.
Why other answers are wrong:
A: Too strong - evidence is suggestive but contested
C: Localization supports rather than contradicts modularity
D: Neural implementation is relevant, though not the only evidence
Modularity Neural Basis
Q11
How do nativists explain that infants still LEARN specific things (like "this ball is red") if they have innate core knowledge?
They claim infants don't actually learn anything; all knowledge is innate
Core knowledge provides the FRAMEWORK (objects persist, have properties) that enables learning specific FACTS about particular objects
Learning and innate knowledge are completely separate systems that don't interact
Specific facts are learned through domain-general mechanisms even though core knowledge is domain-specific
Correct Answer: B
This addresses a common misunderstanding. Nativists DON'T claim everything is pre-specified. The innate object module gives you principles: objects persist when hidden, can't interpenetrate, move continuously. This framework ENABLES learning about specific objects - you can learn "red balls bounce" because you already understand objects persist and have stable properties. Without the framework, you couldn't even identify objects as discrete entities to learn about.
Analogy: Core knowledge is like having specialized learning algorithms (object-tracker.exe) pre-installed. You still need data (experience) to run the algorithm on, but the algorithm itself determines HOW you process that data.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Object Unity: Kellman & Spelke (1983) Empirical
Q12
What is "object unity" and how does it differ from "object permanence"?
They're the same thing - understanding objects continue to exist
Object unity is representing PARTS of an object you can't see (inferring the rod is complete behind the occluder); object permanence is representing WHOLE objects you can't see at all
Object unity develops later than object permanence
Object unity is about combining multiple objects; object permanence is about single objects
Correct Answer: B
This distinction is crucial. Object unity: You see a person's head and legs behind a desk - do you represent the torso you CAN'T see? Object permanence: A toy is completely hidden under a cloth - do you represent the toy still existing? Unity involves partial occlusion (some parts visible); permanence involves complete occlusion. Piaget claimed infants lack both initially. Nativists test unity first because it's arguably simpler - you have perceptual evidence (the visible parts) to work with.
Memory Aid: Unity = unified whole from parts. Permanence = permanent existence when fully hidden.
HIGH YIELD Object Unity Object Permanence
Q13
In the Kellman & Spelke (1983) moving rod study, what did infants see during habituation?
A complete rod moving back and forth with no occluder
Top and bottom portions of a rod moving together behind a yellow occluder (partial visibility with common motion)
A broken rod (two separate pieces)
Various objects to prevent formation of specific expectations
Correct Answer: B
During habituation, infants saw a red rod moving horizontally with top and bottom portions visible, middle hidden by yellow occluder. Both visible portions moved in IDENTICAL direction and speed (common motion). This setup tests whether infants INFER the middle exists. Crucially, infants never actually saw a complete rod during habituation - that's why the test is diagnostic.
Why other answers are wrong:
A: If they saw complete rod, there'd be no inference needed
C: This was shown at TEST, not habituation
D: Study used consistent rod stimulus to establish expectation
Kellman & Spelke Method
Q14
At test (after habituation), the occluder was removed and infants saw either a broken rod (two separate pieces) or a complete rod. The 4-month-olds looked LONGER at the broken rod. According to nativists, what does this mean?
They prefer broken objects because they're more interesting
They showed a familiarity preference for the broken rod
They represented a unified complete rod during habituation, so the broken rod was surprising/unexpected (conceptual novelty overrode perceptual familiarity)
They have better memory for broken objects
Correct Answer: C
This is the critical inference. The broken rod is perceptually MORE SIMILAR to what they saw during habituation (two separate moving pieces). Normally, infants should habituate to familiar patterns. But they looked LONGER at it, suggesting CONCEPTUAL surprise - "I thought this was one rod, why is it broken?" The complete rod is perceptually novel (never saw continuous rod) but conceptually expected, so they look at it less. This reversal of novelty preference is the signature of core knowledge overriding perception.
Critical Logic: Longer looking to perceptually familiar = conceptual violation detected. This is the foundation of violation-of-expectation paradigm.
HIGH YIELD Kellman & Spelke Interpretation
Q15
Why did Kellman & Spelke include a STATIONARY rod control condition (where the rod didn't move)?
To test whether infants prefer moving or stationary stimuli
To test whether COMMON MOTION specifically is the critical input that triggers the object module - without motion, module shouldn't activate
To make the study more interesting for infants
To increase the sample size
Correct Answer: B
This control is theoretically essential for nativism. If object unity were just learned from "surfaces that look similar," then stationary rod (same color, texture, alignment) should also produce unity. But nativists claim the specific trigger is COMMON MOTION - coordinated movement of surfaces. Result: With stationary rod, infants showed NO preference (broken vs. complete), suggesting they didn't infer unity. This supports the claim that co-motion is the specific perceptual input the module requires.
Exam Strategy: Control conditions that manipulate the hypothesized "triggering input" are critical evidence for modularity. Be ready to explain why specific inputs matter.
HIGH YIELD Kellman & Spelke Triggering Input
Q16
What does "common motion" mean and why is it theoretically important?
Objects that appear frequently in infant's environment
Motion that is easy for infants to perceive
Two visible surfaces moving in the SAME direction at the SAME speed, which nativists claim is the specific perceptual cue that triggers the innate object module
Any kind of motion that multiple objects share
Correct Answer: C
Common motion (or "co-motion") = surfaces moving together in coordinated fashion. Top of rod moves right, bottom moves right, same speed. This is the "critical input" for nativists - it's what tells the object module "these surfaces are parts of one object." Why this specific cue? In the real world, parts of rigid objects DO move together. If your head moves left but your torso moves right, you're probably two people standing close, not one person. So evolution could have built detectors for this reliable cue.
Why other answers are wrong:
A: "Common" doesn't mean "frequent" here, it means "shared/coordinated"
B: It's a specific type of motion, not just easy-to-see motion
D: Common motion requires coordinated movement, not just multiple objects moving
HIGH YIELD Common Motion Nativism
Q17
In the stationary rod condition, infants initially looked at the stimulus for about 12 seconds (vs. 50 seconds for moving rod), and showed no preference at test. What does this pattern tell us?
Infants don't pay attention to stationary objects
Moving stimuli are inherently more interesting (longer initial looking), but more importantly, without motion the object module doesn't activate (no unity inference, hence no test preference)
Infants habituated too quickly to make any inferences
The study had a methodological flaw
Correct Answer: B
Two findings: (1) Lower initial interest (12s vs. 50s) shows motion attracts attention - this is an attentional/perceptual effect. (2) No test preference shows absence of unity inference - this is the conceptual effect. Even though infants DID look at stationary rod enough to habituate (looking time decreased), they didn't form an expectation about whether it was unified. This dissociation supports the claim that motion isn't just making things interesting; it's specifically triggering object processing.
Kellman & Spelke Control Condition
Q18
If a constructivist (who believes in domain-general learning) saw the Kellman & Spelke results, what alternative explanation might they offer for the moving rod finding?
The results must be wrong; infants can't do this
Infants could have LEARNED from experience that surfaces that look similar and move together are usually parts of the same object - no innate module needed
Infants were just showing a random looking pattern
The broken rod was physically more attractive
Correct Answer: B
Constructivists don't deny the BEHAVIOR - 4-month-olds do show the pattern. They deny it requires INNATE KNOWLEDGE. Their alternative: In the real world, similar-appearing surfaces that move in synchrony ARE usually connected (statistical regularity). With 4 months of visual experience (billions of observations), domain-general learning mechanisms could extract this pattern. No need for a specialized "object module" - just general statistical learning applied to visual input. This debate hinges on whether 4 months is enough time to learn this.
Exam Strategy: For any nativist finding, constructivists ask: "Could this be learned from statistical regularities in the environment?" For any constructivist developmental change, nativists ask: "Could this reflect maturing perceptual systems providing input to innate modules?"

4Ô∏è‚É£ Constructivist Responses to Object Unity Theory & Evidence
Q19
Johnson & Aslin (1998) found that 2-month-olds showed object unity only when a SMALL occluder hid a small portion of the rod, whereas 4-month-olds succeeded even with a LARGE occluder. How do constructivists interpret this developmental pattern?
Two-month-olds have poor vision so they can't see the rod
Object unity ability is being gradually CONSTRUCTED - younger infants need more perceptual support (more visible surface) because their representation is still developing
The study was flawed
Two-month-olds haven't developed the object module yet
Correct Answer: B
This is KEY constructivist evidence. The gradual improvement - newborns need minimal occlusion, 2-month-olds tolerate moderate occlusion, 4-month-olds tolerate substantial occlusion - suggests INCREMENTAL construction of the ability. If there were an innate module that just needed perceptual input, why would amount of occlusion matter AS LONG AS you can perceive the motion? Constructivists say: because the representation itself is being built up through experience, more experience = more robust representation = can infer with less evidence.
Critical Pattern: GRADUAL, GRADED improvements = constructivist signature. SUDDEN emergence at specific age with specific input = nativist signature.
HIGH YIELD Constructivism Developmental Evidence
Q20
How would a NATIVIST (like Carey) explain the Johnson & Aslin finding that 2-month-olds need more visible surface than 4-month-olds?
They would reject the finding as incorrect
They would admit object unity is learned, not innate
The object module is innate, but PERCEPTUAL SYSTEMS (motion tracking, visual acuity) are still maturing - younger infants need more visible surface to reliably DETECT the common motion that triggers the module
Two-month-olds have the module but choose not to use it
Correct Answer: C
This is from Carey's direct quote in the slides: "infants' failure until 2 months of age to create a representation of a complete rod partially hidden... is at least partly explained by their failure to notice the common motion across the barrier. Thus they lack the critical INPUT to the putatively innate computation." Nativists locate development in PERCEPTUAL PREREQUISITES, not in the core knowledge itself. The object module is there from birth, but it can't operate if motion-tracking circuits aren't mature enough to provide reliable input.
Exam Critical: BOTH theories can accommodate developmental changes. Constructivists: knowledge is being built. Nativists: input systems are maturing. The debate is which account is more parsimonious.
HIGH YIELD Nativist Response Performance vs. Competence
Q21
Slater et al. (1996) found that NEWBORNS show a preference for the whole rod. How does each side interpret this?
Nativists: supports innateness (present from birth). Constructivists: could be innate preference for perceptual simplicity (Gestalt grouping), not conceptual object knowledge
Both sides agree it proves core knowledge
Both sides reject the finding
Nativists reject it because newborns can't have innate knowledge
Correct Answer: A
Newborn data seems to favor nativism (how could learning happen before birth?), but constructivists have a counter: low-level perceptual organization principles (Gestalt psychology - proximity, similarity, good continuation) might be innate and produce preference for unified percepts. This is different from conceptual knowledge that "this is one object with hidden middle part." Preference for perceptually simpler/more organized stimuli doesn't require object modules, just basic perceptual preferences. This illustrates how BOTH sides can interpret the same data.
Newborn Studies Theoretical Interpretation
Q22
According to Cohen & Cashon, what changes in the first 4 months to produce increasingly robust object unity?
The object module gradually comes online
Nothing - infants always had the ability
The ability to construct an object percept from visual input develops hierarchically - infants learn to integrate low-level features (color, motion) into higher-level object representations
Memory capacity increases
Correct Answer: C
This captures the constructivist hierarchical learning model. Infants start with domain-general systems that detect low-level features (this patch is red, this edge is moving). Through experience, they learn to group these into mid-level units (surfaces), then into object-level representations. Cohen & Straus (1979) showed older infants can form higher-level generalizations younger infants can't. So object unity emerges as the TOP level of this hierarchy becomes robust enough to maintain unified representations despite missing information.
Constructivist Model: Low-level features ‚Üí Mid-level features ‚Üí High-level objects. Each level constructed from the one below through statistical learning.
HIGH YIELD Constructivism Hierarchical Learning
Q23
Why is the existence of gradual developmental change (newborns ‚Üí 2 months ‚Üí 4 months) more consistent with constructivism than nativism?
Nativism predicts no development should occur
Gradual, graded improvement suggests incremental CONSTRUCTION of knowledge; nativism predicts more step-like emergence once perceptual prerequisites mature
It isn't - gradual change is equally consistent with both theories
Nativists can't explain any developmental changes
Correct Answer: B
Constructivists predict smooth, continuous improvement as knowledge is built. Nativists predict: as long as perceptual input is insufficient, module can't operate (flat performance), then once input is adequate, module activates (sudden jump). While nativists CAN explain gradual changes (gradual improvement in perception), the GRADED nature (need 90% visible at 0mo, 70% at 2mo, 50% at 4mo) is more naturally explained by gradual strengthening of constructed representations than by threshold effects in perception.
Exam Strategy: Look for whether development is step-like (favor nativism) or graded (favor constructivism). But remember, neither pattern is definitive - both theories have ways to accommodate both.

5Ô∏è‚É£ Object Permanence: Piaget's Timeline Empirical
Q25
According to Piaget, when do infants develop object permanence, and what was his evidence?
Birth - innate ability
3-4 months based on looking time studies
8-9 months based on manual search tasks (reaching for hidden objects), with fragility until 12 months
18 months based on language reports
Correct Answer: C
Piaget's classic evidence: Before 8-9 months, if you hide a toy under a cloth, infants don't reach for it ("out of sight, out of mind"). At 9 months, they immediately reach and retrieve it. The video in lecture showed a 7-month-old who seemed confused when the toy was hidden, not reaching for it. This is BEHAVIORAL evidence - can the infant use knowledge of the hidden object to guide action? Piaget interpreted failure as lack of conceptual understanding that objects persist.
Historical Context: Piaget established 8-9 months as the timeline. Nativists challenge this with looking-time measures claiming to show permanence at 3-4 months. Constructivists defend Piaget's timeline with distinctions between memory and true permanence.
HIGH YIELD Piaget Object Permanence
Q26
In the video shown in lecture, a 7-month-old sees a toy hidden under a cloth but doesn't reach for it. How do nativists explain this failure despite claiming infants have innate object permanence?
Seven-month-olds genuinely don't understand objects persist
Manual search tasks have PERFORMANCE DEMANDS (motor planning, means-end reasoning, inhibiting reach to visible cloth) that develop later than the underlying COMPETENCE of representing hidden objects
The infant wasn't interested in the toy
The study was poorly designed
Correct Answer: B
This is the COMPETENCE vs. PERFORMANCE distinction crucial for nativism. Reaching requires: (1) representing the hidden object (competence), (2) planning a motor sequence (performance), (3) understanding that removing the cloth will reveal the object (means-end reasoning), (4) inhibiting the prepotent response to reach for the VISIBLE cloth. Nativists argue (1) is present but (2-4) develop later. So reaching tasks UNDERESTIMATE infant knowledge. This is why they developed looking-time measures.
Nativist Strategy: When infants fail a task despite hypothesized knowledge, invoke performance limitations. Critics call this unfalsifiable - any failure can be explained away.
HIGH YIELD Competence vs. Performance Nativism
Q27
What is the relationship between object unity (Section 3-4) and object permanence?
They're unrelated abilities
Both involve representing occluded aspects of objects, but unity is about PARTIAL occlusion (some parts visible) while permanence is about COMPLETE occlusion (object entirely hidden)
Object unity is more advanced than object permanence
Only permanence requires core knowledge
Correct Answer: B
Both abilities concern representing what you can't see, but permanence is arguably deeper. Unity: "I see top and bottom of rod, I infer middle exists." You have perceptual anchors. Permanence: "I see NOTHING, but I maintain representation that object exists at location X." No perceptual support. Nativists claim both come from the same object module. Constructivists expect permanence to develop AFTER unity because it requires stronger representational capacity.
Conceptual Relations Object Knowledge
Q28
The lecture notes that looking-time measures are "more sensitive" than reaching tasks. What does this mean and why does it matter?
Looking time gives more precise measurements in milliseconds
Looking time requires only visual attention and surprise detection, while reaching requires motor execution and planning, so looking time can reveal knowledge that reaching tasks miss due to performance constraints
Infants prefer looking to reaching
Looking time is easier for researchers to code
Correct Answer: B
"Sensitive" = capable of detecting knowledge even when other abilities are immature. Looking time: If infant sees something surprising, look longer. Simple. Reaching: Represent hidden object ‚Üí plan motor sequence ‚Üí execute reach ‚Üí grasp ‚Üí manipulate. Complex. So looking COULD reveal object permanence earlier because it bypasses motor and planning demands. This motivated Baillargeon's violation-of-expectation paradigm. But constructivists counter: maybe looking time measures something shallower (memory) than true permanence (conceptual understanding).
Methodological Trade-off: Sensitive measures may detect early knowledge OR may be measuring something different/simpler than the target concept. This ambiguity fuels the debate.
HIGH YIELD Method Looking Time
Q29
Why might constructivists argue that reaching tasks are actually the BETTER measure of object permanence than looking time?
Reaching is easier to measure reliably
Infants find reaching more engaging
Reaching demonstrates FUNCTIONAL understanding - the infant can USE knowledge to achieve goals - while looking time might reflect only shallow perceptual expectations or memory without deep conceptual understanding
Piaget used reaching tasks so they must be better
Correct Answer: C
This is the constructivist counter to "looking time is more sensitive." They argue: Looking longer at impossible events could mean (a) true understanding of object permanence, OR (b) low-level perceptual surprise without conceptual understanding. Reaching REQUIRES using the representation to guide action - you must represent location, persistence, retrievability. This "functional" criterion is more stringent. If you can't use knowledge in goal-directed behavior, maybe the knowledge isn't fully developed.
Debate Hinge: What counts as evidence of "understanding"? Implicit detection (looking) vs. explicit use (reaching). Neither is obviously right - this is a theoretical choice about what permanence means.
HIGH YIELD Constructivist Response Method
Q30
The distinction between object unity and object permanence maps onto what broader distinction in the debate?
Visual vs. auditory processing
PERCEPTUAL abilities (organizing visible information) vs. CONCEPTUAL abilities (representing and reasoning about things beyond current perception)
Easy vs. hard tasks
Innate vs. learned abilities
Correct Answer: B
Object unity involves visible parts - it's about ORGANIZING PERCEPTION. Gestalt principles (similarity, good continuation) might handle this without conceptual knowledge.
