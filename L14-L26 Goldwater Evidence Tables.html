Habituation Paradigm (General Method) Fantz (1960s pioneer); standardized 1970s-80s Birth-15mo (method endpoint at verbal comprehension) Repeated stimulus presentation until looking time declines to criterion (typically: 3 consecutive trials < 50% baseline). Then test with novel vs. familiar stimuli. PRIMARY DV in infant research. Novelty preference after habituation = discrimination ability. Generalization (no novelty pref. to pattern-matching novel) = pattern learning. Criterion: Baseline = mean of first 3 trials; Threshold = 0.50 √ó baseline; Met when 3 consecutive trials ALL < threshold Foundational method for ALL infant cognition research. Assumes: (1) looking reflects processing, (2) habituation = memory encoding, (3) dishabituation = discrimination "If they get bored of A and perk up for B, they can tell them apart."
Habituation paradigm: PRIMARY DV = looking time. Criterion = 3 consecutive trials < 50% baseline (baseline = mean of first 3 trials). Novelty preference = discrimination ability. Generalization = pattern learning. Valid until ~15-18 months (verbal comprehension endpoint).
Habituation vs. Familiarization Debate Ongoing field controversy (no consensus) Methodological choice (all ages) HABITUATION: Criterion-based (stop when infant meets threshold); individualized exposure. FAMILIARIZATION: Fixed trials (e.g., 10 √ó 20s); controlled exposure. Habituation PRO: Ensures novelty preference state (clear interpretation). CON: Uncontrolled exposure confounds processing speed. Familiarization PRO: Controlled exposure. CON: Uncertain processing state (familiarity vs. novelty phase?); bidirectional predictions. Habituation: exposure varies ~6-15+ trials/infant. Familiarization: identical exposure (e.g., 10 trials) across all infants Trade-off: controlled state (habituation) vs. controlled exposure (familiarization). Choice often driven by reviewer/advisor preference, not empirical superiority. "Habituation knows their state, familiarization knows their dose ‚Äî pick your poison."
Looking Time Measurement (Primary DV) Classic: Manual coding; Modern: Eye-tracking Birth-18mo Classic: Researcher watches camera feed, holds button while infant fixates on screen. Modern: Eye-tracking software automatically detects gaze direction & duration. Duration of visual fixation = proxy for cognitive processing. Longer looking = more interest/novelty/processing effort. Available from birth, non-invasive, requires no motor response. Typical baseline: 15-25s (first trial); habituated trials: 5-8s; dishabituation: 12-18s (recovery toward baseline) Assumes attention ‚Üí encoding ‚Üí memory. Looking time reflects: (1) perceptual interest, (2) encoding effort, (3) novelty detection. NOT fatigue (proven by recovery to novel stimulus). "Eyes are the windows to the baby brain ‚Äî looking time is the dependent variable of infancy."
Validity Controls: Caregiver Blinding Standard practice (1980s+) All infant studies Caregiver wears headphones with masking audio (white noise or music) so they cannot hear stimuli. Prevents inadvertent attention direction based on stimulus content. Eliminates confound: Without blinding, caregiver may shift attention or lean toward screen when "interesting" stimulus appears ‚Üí infant follows caregiver's gaze, not own interest. Cueing effect size: ~15-30% of variance in looking time attributable to caregiver knowledge (measured in studies comparing blinded vs. unblinded) Internal validity essential. If caregiver knows stimulus, infant's looking time reflects caregiver's expectations, not infant's cognition. "Blind the parent or the baby follows the wrong eyes."
Attention Getter (Procedural Element) Standard procedure All habituation studies Between trials: Present central visual/auditory stimulus (e.g., swirling red star + chime) to reorient infant's gaze to screen center before next trial begins. Ensures infant starts each trial attending to screen (not looking at caregiver, room, etc.). Without attention getter, trials start with infant disengaged ‚Üí artificially low looking times. Increases trial-start attention: ~80-90% of trials begin with fixation when attention getter used vs. ~50% without Procedural validity. Habituation measures stimulus-specific boredom, not general disengagement. Attention getter resets engagement before each trial. "Star and chime: bringing baby eyes back to center stage."

Early exposure: Familiarity preference ("I'm trying to get it" - encoding phase). Full habituation: Novelty preference ("I'm bored" - encoded phase). Critical: Null results at short exposure ‚â† discrimination failure - may be wrong curve phase. Curve shape: No pref ‚Üí Familiar pref (peak ~30-50% through encoding) ‚Üí No pref ‚Üí Novelty pref (peak after full habituation) Revolutionized interpretation: Challenged monotonic habituation assumption. "Familiarity first ('getting it'), novelty second ('got it') ‚Äî preference flips as encoding completes."
Hunter & Ames (1988): Preference is NON-MONOTONIC (U-shaped curve). Early exposure = familiarity preference (encoding phase). Full habituation = novelty preference (encoded phase). Null results at short exposure ‚â† discrimination failure - may be wrong curve phase.

Visual Acuity Discrimination: Stripe Width Paradigm Various (classic perceptual development research) Newborn-12mo (developmental trajectory) Habituation: Black-and-white stripes (varying widths: thick ‚Üí thin). Test: Solid gray field. Logic: If stripes perceived as distinct from gray ‚Üí novelty preference. If stripes below acuity threshold (appear gray) ‚Üí NO novelty preference (gray looks familiar). Perceptual threshold mapping. Thick stripes ‚Üí dishabituation to gray (can discriminate). Thin stripes ‚Üí NO dishabituation (stripes blur into gray; below resolution limit). Developmental pattern: Newborns discriminate thick only; older infants discriminate progressively thinner (improving acuity). Newborn threshold: ~30 cycles/degree (wide stripes). 6-month threshold: ~10 cycles/degree. 12-month threshold: ~5 cycles/degree (approaching adult). Demonstrates habituation can map perceptual boundaries without verbal report. Acuity development = maturational (retinal/cortical development), not learning. Shows infant's visual world is blurrier than adults' (critical for understanding early experience). "Thick stripes pop, thin stripes = gray mush ‚Äî acuity improves month by month."
Interpretation Challenge: Null Results Ambiguity Theoretical/methodological issue All infant studies When infant shows no preference (novel ‚âà familiar looking time), what can we conclude? THREE possible interpretations: (1) Under-habituated: Still in familiarity preference phase (Hunter & Ames curve) - increase exposure. (2) Genuine inability: Cannot discriminate stimuli - supports developmental claim. (3) Over-habituated/fatigued: Infant disengaged entirely - methodological failure. Cannot distinguish without diagnostics. Without age comparison or individual differences analysis, null result is uninterpretable. Requires: (1) older age group (if they succeed ‚Üí ability emerges), (2) split by habituation speed (fast hab. shows effect ‚Üí exposure issue), (3) physiological check (HR deceleration ‚Üí still encoding). Falsification problem: Habituation paradigm is easier to confirm abilities than rule them out. Positive result (novelty pref.) = clear. Null result = murky. Explains replication crisis in infant research (many "null = inability" claims likely wrong). "No preference = maybe can't tell, maybe didn't see enough, maybe stopped caring ‚Äî null is a shrug."
