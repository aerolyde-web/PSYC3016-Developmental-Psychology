PSYC3016 - Lecture 22: Language Development I

Overarching Question: How do infants transform continuous acoustic signals into hierarchical, rule-governed symbolic systems—not through passive absorption but through active structural discovery combining perceptual narrowing that tunes phonetic categories to environmental statistics, social-pragmatic inference that leverages communicative intent to solve referential ambiguity, and productive rule extraction that enables grammatical generalization from sparse, noisy input?

Comprehensive Summary

Language acquisition is not passive absorption but active structural discovery, wherein infants transform continuous acoustic signals into hierarchical, rule-governed symbolic systems through componentiality (finite primitives: ~40 phonemes, ~20,000 words) and compositionality (finite combination rules: phonotactics, syntax, discourse coherence) that paradoxically enable infinite expressive capacity because constraints create contrastive structure—if any sound could follow any sound, listeners could extract no regularities; if any word order were equivalent, syntax could convey no relational meaning ("dog bites man" ≠ "man bites dog")—making combinatorial explosion possible precisely because rules filter chaos into structured meaning across hierarchical levels where phonemes assemble into syllables, syllables into morphemes, morphemes into words, words into sentences, sentences into discourses. Newborns arrive with preferences but not biases, demonstrated by high-amplitude sucking studies (Vouloumanos & Werker, 2004) showing newborns suck faster for speech versus acoustically complex non-speech and recognize native language prosody heard in utero, yet during the first six months infants exhibit categorical perception across all world languages—English-learning and Japanese-learning 6-month-olds both discriminate /r/ from /l/ (English contrast) and retroflex /ɖ/ from dental /d̪/ (Hindi contrast)—but by 10 months, before producing first words, infants undergo perceptual narrowing where Japanese 10-month-olds no longer discriminate /r/ vs. /l/ and English 10-month-olds no longer discriminate Hindi dental vs. retroflex stops, reflecting not cognitive decline but learned attention allocation driven by distributional statistics—collapsing irrelevant acoustic variance into functional categories frees resources for word learning while bilingual infants maintain sensitivity to contrasts in both languages as appropriate environmental tuning. Categorical perception operates through voice onset time (VOT) mechanisms where physically equal acoustic steps produce perceptual inequality: 0ms vs. 20ms VOT (both heard as /d/) show poor discrimination, while 20ms vs. 40ms (across the /d/-/t/ boundary) show excellent discrimination, with Eimas et al. (1971) demonstrating 2-day-old infants already perceive categorical boundaries at adult-like locations for both native and non-native contrasts through habituation paradigms, establishing that categorical perception machinery is present from birth but undergoes adaptive specialization by 10 months enabling efficient word learning. Speech segmentation solves the problem that fluent speech contains no consistent silence marking word boundaries—acoustic analysis reveals pauses occur within words ("ba-by") as often as between them—forcing infants to parse continuous streams like /ðəbeɪbiɪzslipɪŋ/ into units /ðə/ + /beɪbi/ + /ɪz/ + /slipɪŋ/ through multiple probabilistic cues: (1) prosodic stress patterns where English words typically have single primary stress syllable (Gambell & Yang, 2003) with consecutive stressed syllables likely signaling word boundaries, (2) phonotactic constraints where sound sequences like /st/ are legal word-initially but /df/ is not, and (3) transitional probabilities where within-word syllable pairs have high conditional probability (P(by|ba) high in "baby") while cross-boundary probabilities drop, with infants sensitive to all these cues by 8 months, though segmentation is language-specific because stress-based strategies work for stress-timed languages like English and Dutch but fail for syllable-timed languages like French (phrase-final stress, not word-level) requiring greater reliance on phonotactic cues and function word detection. The vocabulary explosion begins around 18 months (from ~50 words at 18 months to 5,000–20,000 by age 7), averaging one new word per waking hour at peak, ruling out simple associative learning due to Quine's gavagai problem—when an adult points at a rabbit and says "gavagai," the word could refer to rabbit, ears, hopping, whiteness, or undetached rabbit parts—which infants solve through social-pragmatic constraints assuming speakers are helpful communicators: (1) referential intent tracking where 12-month-olds follow gaze direction to identify speaker's target, prioritizing looked-at objects over equally salient alternatives, (2) mutual exclusivity where hearing novel word "dax" with familiar object (fork) and unfamiliar object (garlic press) produces 95% selection of unfamiliar object (Markman; Wilson & Katsos, 2021) through pragmatic inference that if the speaker intended the familiar object they would have used its known label, (3) shape bias where new object labels extend by shape for count nouns referring to solid objects but comparison overrides shape bias as demonstrated by Graham et al. (2020) showing children generalize by texture instead of shape when shown two exemplars with shared texture, and (4) pedagogical sampling (Xu & Tenenbaum, 2007) where showing three Dalmatians yields subordinate inference ("dax" = Dalmatian), showing three different dog breeds yields basic-level inference ("dax" = dog), and showing dog/cat/bird yields superordinate inference ("dax" = animal) through Bayesian pedagogical reasoning where children assume teachers choose examples to be maximally informative about category boundaries following the formula level of generality ∝ variation within examples / variation outside examples. At 18 months children begin producing two-word combinations that are not random truncations but systematic compressions preserving word order: "Daddy went to work" becomes "Daddy work" not "*work Daddy," showing early sensitivity to syntax despite production limitations, progressing by 24–36 months to master inflectional morphology (plural -s, past tense -ed, progressive -ing) and demonstrating productive mastery through the famous wug test (Berko, 1958) where children reliably produce "wugs" (/wʌgz/) for "This is a wug. Now there are two of them. There are two ___?", proving extraction of abstract rule NOUN + -s → PLURAL with correct allomorph selection (/-s/ after voiceless consonants in "cats" /kæts/, /-z/ after voiced consonants in "dogs" /dɔgz/ and "wugs" /wʌgz/, /-ɪz/ after sibilants in "buses" /bʌsɪz/), exemplifying U-shaped developmental trajectory: (1) young children correctly produce irregular forms like "feet" and "teeth" through imitation, (2) upon discovering regular plural rule they overgeneralize producing "foots" and "tooths," (3) eventually re-learning that some forms are lexical exceptions while maintaining productive rule for regular nouns—this U-shape (correct → error → correct) is diagnostic of rule learning not rote memorization, with overgeneralization errors revealing cognitively sophisticated prioritization of productive generalizations applying to 99% of English nouns over memorized exceptions, ultimately demonstrating children maintain both productive default and lexically-specified exceptions in a dual-route model. Pragmatic development governs appropriateness not grammaticality—"Could you pass the salt?" is grammatically a yes/no question but pragmatically an indirect request where responding "Yes, I could" without passing salt is infelicitous because it ignores communicative intent—with children not mastering these conventions until 6–8 years, well after basic syntax and vocabulary, because non-literal language (metaphor "time is money," idiom "kicked the bucket," irony, sarcasm) requires metarepresentational theory of mind: the listener must represent not just the speaker's mental state but speaker's intention to communicate a belief different from surface meaning, explaining why children with ASD often show intact phonology, vocabulary, and syntax but struggle with pragmatics precisely due to difficulty with higher-order mental state attributions, with pragmatic competence continuing into adulthood as individuals learn register variation (formal vs. informal), politeness conventions, and culturally-specific norms. Language creation reveals children as active regularizers not passive absorbers: deaf children forming communities (Nicaraguan Sign Language emerging 1980s) create fully grammatical sign language within one generation complete with phonology, morphology, and syntax, while Singleton & Newport (2004) documented deaf child whose hearing parents used sign morphology correctly only ~70% of the time but child's signing showed >80% correct morpheme use, exceeding input quality through filtering noise and amplifying signal, with Hudson Kam & Newport (2005) artificial language studies showing adults exposed to 60% determiner consistency reproduced ~60% usage while ~70% of children regularized into systematic users either always using determiners (grammaticalizing as obligatory) or always omitting them (treating as absent) rather than reproducing probabilistic patterns, demonstrating regularization tendency stronger in children than adults with critical threshold where regularization is strongest when input variability is moderate (60%–80% consistency) but struggles below 40% consistency, explaining how children exposed to pidgins (minimal grammars used by adult second-language learners) transform them into creoles (full grammars) in a single generation by imposing structure not present in input, ultimately establishing that language acquisition proceeds through active hypothesis-testing imposing structure on noisy data where perceptual narrowing by 10 months adaptively tunes phonetic space to functionally relevant contrasts freeing cognitive resources, segmentation mechanisms deploy language-specific strategies tuned to prosodic regularities of ambient input enabling word extraction, social-pragmatic constraints (gaze following, mutual exclusivity, pedagogical sampling, comparison) narrow referential ambiguity through convergent multi-cue integration solving Quine's gavagai problem, productive morphosyntactic rule learning enables grammatical generalization demonstrated by wug test performance and U-shaped overgeneralization patterns, pragmatic mastery requiring metarepresentational theory of mind emerges 6–8 years for non-literal language comprehension, and language creation through active regularization (exemplified by Nicaraguan Sign Language and artificial language learning studies) reveals children as structure-seeking agents who extract abstract rules from finite input enabling infinite generativity, collectively demonstrating that language development is not passive environmental imprinting but active structural discovery combining domain-general learning mechanisms (statistical sensitivity, social inference) with domain-specific constraints (universal phonetic categories, hierarchical compositional architecture) to transform acoustic streams into symbolic systems supporting thought and communication.
