LO1 The Missing Heritability Problem: Defining the Gap
"Explain the missing heritability problem and the challenges it creates for understanding developmental mechanisms..."
Method Variance Explained What It Measures
Twin Studies
(Quantitative Genetics)		 ~50%
for most complex traits		 Compares MZ vs DZ correlations to estimate TOTAL genetic influence on population variance. Shows THAT genetics matter.
GWAS
(Molecular Genetics)		 2-3%
identified variants		 Scans genome for specific SNPs associated with traits. Identifies WHICH genetic variants. Huge samples, tiny effects.
THE GAP 47%
mechanistically unexplained		 "We know genetics matters (twin studies) but can't find the genes (GWAS)." Replications rare. Origin of behaviours remains elusive.

‚ö° WHY THIS MATTERS FOR DEVELOPMENTAL PSYCHOLOGY

We want to understand the MECHANISMS explaining child development. Twin studies tell us h¬≤ = 0.50 for trait X, but that's a black box ‚Äî we don't know HOW genes influence development or WHICH genes are responsible. Without mechanism, we can't design targeted interventions. The missing 47% is the puzzle L04 aims to solve.
LO2 Theoretical & Methodological Reasons for Missing Heritability
"Describe the theoretical and methodological reasons why GWAS fails to account for heritability identified by twin studies..."
Category Problem Explanation
1. NOT LOOKING AT THE CORRECT GENES
Wrong hypothesis Hypothesis-driven research fails Candidate gene approach assumes we know which genes to look at ‚Äî but our hypotheses may be wrong or incomplete.
Gene functionality Genes don't do what we think Genes may have different functions than assumed, OR do what we think but so do MANY other genes (gene networks, gene-gene interactions).
Protein function Proteins don't behave as expected Even if gene ‚Üí protein is correct, the protein may not influence brain function as predicted. Wrong tissue, wrong location, wrong timing.
2. NOT LOOKING IN THE RIGHT SAMPLES
Phenotype imprecision Overlap between disorders Samples need to be "pure" ‚Äî but phenotypes with different aetiologies get lumped together (e.g., CD with vs without CU traits).
Sample characteristics Ethnicity, age range not tracked Gene effects may vary by population or developmental stage ‚Äî collapsing across heterogeneous samples dilutes signals.
3. NOT THINKING ABOUT IT THE RIGHT WAY
Interactions missed Main effects ‚â† interactions GWAS tests additive main effects. Gene-gene (GxG) and gene-environment (GxE) interactions produce effects INVISIBLE to main-effect analyses.
Epigenetics Expression ‚â† sequence Heritable changes to gene EXPRESSION (methylation) don't change DNA sequence ‚Üí invisible to GWAS but contribute to twin h¬≤.
‚ö†Ô∏è KEY INSIGHT: The missing heritability isn't a measurement error ‚Äî it reveals that genetic effects operate CONDITIONALLY (through interactions, context-dependence, and expression changes) rather than additively. GWAS assumes main effects; reality involves complexity.

‚ö†Ô∏è BIGGEST EXAM HINGE: Students constantly confuse mediation and moderation.
MEDIATION = sequential causal chain explaining the MECHANISM (X causes M causes Y)
MODERATION = boundary condition showing effect DEPENDS ON another variable (X effect varies by Z level)
GxE interactions = MODERATION (genotype moderates environmental effect)
LO3 Differential Susceptibility: Orchids vs Dandelions
"Explain the differential susceptibility hypothesis and how it differs from the diathesis-stress model..."
üå∏ Ellis et al. (2011) Differential Susceptibility Theory
Core Claim: The SAME genetic variant produces BOTH worst outcomes in negative environments AND best outcomes in positive environments. Genes confer environmental SENSITIVITY, not just vulnerability.
Key Pattern: Crossover interaction ‚Äî lines for different genotypes CROSS, not just diverge.
Contrast with Diathesis-Stress: Diathesis-stress predicts vulnerability ONLY (worse in bad environments, same in good). Differential susceptibility predicts "for better AND for worse."

üå∏ ORCHID Children (High Susceptibility ‚Äî A-carriers in Fast Track)

Negative environment: WORST outcomes (75% externalizing at age 25)

Positive environment: BEST outcomes (18% externalizing ‚Äî BELOW dandelions!)

Metaphor: Orchids wilt in bad soil but bloom spectacularly with care. Highly sensitive to environmental quality.

Mechanism: Carry "susceptibility alleles" (plasticity genes) that amplify environmental effects in BOTH directions.

üåª DANDELION Children (Low Susceptibility ‚Äî GG genotype in Fast Track)

Negative environment: Moderate outcomes (50% externalizing)

Positive environment: Same moderate outcomes (50% ‚Äî NO BENEFIT from intervention)

Metaphor: Dandelions grow in cracks in pavement ‚Äî resilient to adversity but don't flourish from enrichment.

Mechanism: Stable genotype resistant to environmental programming. Not "better" genes ‚Äî just LESS SENSITIVE genes.

‚ö° CRITICAL INTERPRETATION

Without genotype split: Results would show "small/modest intervention effect" ‚Äî averaging 57-point effect (orchids) with 0-point effect (dandelions) = diluted signal. The GxE interaction is INVISIBLE without stratifying by genotype.

Clinical implication: Intervention only effective for genetically susceptible children. Could genotype to TARGET interventions ‚Äî but ethically problematic (who decides? stigmatization?).

Theoretical confirmation: A-carriers do WORST without intervention AND BEST with intervention = differential susceptibility, NOT just diathesis-stress vulnerability.

LO2/LO4 Epigenetics: Heritable Changes Without DNA Sequence Change
"Explain how epigenetic mechanisms contribute to missing heritability and gene-environment interactions..."
üß¨ Core Concept: DNA Does NOTHING Unless READ
Analogy: DNA = library of books on shelves. Books do nothing sitting there. Must be READ (transcribed) to produce proteins that affect behaviour.
Transcription: Transcription factor binds to PROMOTER REGION ‚Üí reads gene ‚Üí creates RNA ‚Üí protein produced
Epigenetics: Changes which books are READ (gene expression) without changing the text (DNA sequence)
Feature GENETIC MUTATION EPIGENETIC METHYLATION
Alters DNA sequence? YES ‚Äî changes ATCG nucleotide order NO ‚Äî sequence unchanged
Mechanism Base substitution, insertion, deletion Methyl groups (CH‚ÇÉ) attach to cytosine ‚Üí block transcription
Reversibility Permanent (barring additional mutations) Dynamic, potentially REVERSIBLE
Causes Inherited, replication errors, radiation Environmental stress, diet, prenatal adversity
Effect Alters protein structure ‚Üë methylation = ‚Üì gene expression (less protein)
Detected by GWAS? YES (sequence variants) NO ‚Äî invisible to GWAS ‚Üí contributes to MISSING heritability
‚ö†Ô∏è WHY EPIGENETICS SOLVES MISSING HERITABILITY:
Twin studies capture TOTAL genetic influence including methylation effects (MZ twins share similar environments ‚Üí similar methylation). GWAS only sees DNA sequence. The 47% gap includes epigenetic effects: environment ‚Üí methylation ‚Üí expression ‚Üí phenotype. Invisible to sequence-based methods but contributes to heritability.
LO4-B Key Study: Quebec Ice Storm (Epigenetic Programming)
"Describe the Quebec ice storm study and what it reveals about environmental influences on epigenetic programming..."
Component Details
Event 1998 Quebec ice storm ‚Äî extended power outages in subzero winter. Families without heating, extreme stress.
Design Natural experiment: Women pregnant during storm ‚Üí offspring followed into adolescence. Compared to non-exposed controls.
Measure T-cell (immune cell) DNA methylation in adolescent offspring ‚Äî genome-wide analysis of CpG sites
Key Findings Ice storm offspring showed altered methylation at genes for:
(1) Immune regulation ‚Äî hypermethylation ‚Üí autoimmune risk
(2) Insulin signalling ‚Äî diabetes risk via altered glucose metabolism
Persistence Methylation differences persisted >10 years after prenatal exposure ‚Äî durable programming
Implication "May have very good genes" (normal sequence) but expression "locked into" pathological patterns by prenatal environment
‚ö†Ô∏è METHODOLOGICAL CAUTIONS:
(1) T-cell methylation ‚â† brain methylation (measured peripheral blood for accessibility)
(2) Correlational ‚Äî participants hadn't developed predicted diseases yet (biomarkers, not confirmed outcomes)
(3) Confounds: families unable to evacuate may differ systematically (SES, resources)
Despite limitations: proof-of-concept that extreme prenatal stress alters human methylation in functionally plausible patterns.

‚ö° WHAT CAN PSYCHOLOGY DO TO ALTER GENES?

Answer: Psychology can't change DNA SEQUENCE ‚Äî but can change gene EXPRESSION through environmental intervention.
Fast Track implicitly demonstrates epigenetic intervention: comprehensive support likely prevented stress-driven hypermethylation of NR3C1 in orchid children ‚Üí maintained healthy gene expression ‚Üí prevented psychopathology.
Early intervention is NOT only about reducing environmental risk factors ‚Äî it's about programming healthy gene expression during sensitive periods.
