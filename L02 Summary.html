PSYC3016 - Lecture 02: Nature vs Nurture

Overarching Question: How does the historical nature-versus-nurture dichotomy dissolve under the recognition that all complex psychological traits emerge through P = G + E interactions, where heritability quantifies current variance partitioning (not modifiability or individual determinism), and where systematic debunking of five persistent myths—genes dictate outcomes, tabula rasa blames society, warrior gene determines violence, heritability is irrelevant if universal, and genetic literacy fosters fatalism—reveals that understanding genetic mechanisms should sharpen rather than undermine psychological intervention by enabling stratified treatment targeting specific neurobiological vulnerabilities during critical windows?

Color-Coded Legend

Philosophical Foundations: Nativism/rationalism vs empiricism/behaviourism

Myths/Misconceptions: Five systematic fallacies debunked

Comprehensive Summary

The nature-nurture debate originates in philosophical traditions where nativism/rationalism (Chomsky's poverty-of-stimulus argument proposing innate universal grammar because linguistic input is too impoverished to explain complex syntax acquisition) opposes empiricism/behaviourism (Watson's tabula rasa claiming "give me a dozen healthy infants...and I'll guarantee to train any one to become any specialist I might select" attributing all development to environmental shaping), yet this historical dichotomy dissolves under contemporary recognition that all complex psychological traits arise from genetic predispositions (G) interacting with environmental contexts (E) to produce observable phenotypes according to the formula P = G + E, where heritability quantifies the proportion of phenotypic variance in a population attributable to genetic variance under current environmental conditions, critically operating as population-level statistic (not individual prediction tool) that describes current variance partitioning (not modifiability or determinism) because high heritability indicates which interventions work best (biological targets, early intensive intervention during sensitive periods) not whether change is possible, exemplified by height being 80% heritable yet highly responsive to nutritional intervention where Japanese average height increased 15cm across three generations despite genetic stability, demonstrating that heritability measures relative G versus E contributions under existing environments but environmental improvement can shift entire population distributions even for highly heritable traits. Genetic contributions are measured via SNP (single nucleotide polymorphism) genotyping identifying variants across individuals' genomes where any given location shows three possible patterns (AA homozygous for common allele, Aa heterozygous, aa homozygous for rare allele), enabling GWAS (genome-wide association studies) comparing allele frequencies between groups differing on phenotype (e.g., comparing 5,000 individuals with schizophrenia versus 5,000 controls across millions of SNP locations to identify genetic variants statistically associated with diagnosis), while environmental contributions require measurement through direct observation (expensive, time-intensive but ecologically valid capturing real-time parent-child interactions), questionnaires/surveys (cheap, scalable but subject to social desirability bias and retrospective distortion), and archival records (hospital admissions, arrest records, school transcripts providing objective data but missing subjective experiences), with both genetic and environmental measurement facing the challenge that association does not imply individual-level causation—population patterns reveal risk factors, not deterministic outcomes for any particular person. The lecture systematically debunks five persistent myths obstructing proper understanding of nature-nurture interactions: Myth 1 (genes dictate outcomes) falsely conflates genetic contribution with genetic determinism, refuted by Martha Farah's poverty study showing rich and poor children have identical brain structure at birth but poverty produces 3-4% smaller hippocampal and prefrontal cortex volumes by age 12 (demonstrating environmental cause of biological difference) and by MAOA-L "warrior gene" data where 30% of males carry the low-activity variant yet only 5% of carriers become violent by age 25 (versus 2% of MAOA-H carriers), meaning 95% of carriers are non-violent and gene possession does not determine behavior, establishing that genes set reaction ranges requiring environmental realization—genetic potential must be triggered and shaped by experience; Myth 2 (tabula rasa blames society) falsely assumes humans are born identical with all differences environmental, refuted by systematic attribution bias where parents prefer ADHD over ODD diagnosis (both ~40% heritable) because ADHD is perceived as "more biological" and less parenting failure despite equivalent genetic contributions, revealing that all complex traits (even prosocial behaviors like generosity) show G + E contributions, and denying genetic variance distorts causal understanding by attributing all negative outcomes to environmental failures while crediting positive outcomes to individual merit; Myth 3 (warrior gene determines violence) falsely interprets population-level genetic association as individual determinism, refuted by MAOA-L statistics where positive predictive value = 5% (95% of gene carriers non-violent) and 48% of violent offenders carry normal MAOA-H variant (gene neither necessary nor sufficient for violence), compounded by gene-environment interaction where MAOA-L increases violence risk primarily among maltreated children (genetic predisposition requires environmental trigger), establishing that courtroom defenses using "warrior gene" to reduce sentences commit ecological fallacy by applying population statistics to individual responsibility despite gene's poor individual prediction; Myth 4 (heritability is irrelevant if all traits heritable) falsely assumes that because all complex traits show some heritability (typically 30-70%), the concept provides no useful information, refuted by conduct disorder heritability variation where low callous-unemotional (CU) traits show 30% heritability versus high CU traits show 81% heritability despite identical surface behaviors (aggression, defiance, rule-breaking), with treatment response matching heritability structure: 60% of low-CU children respond to parent training (environmental intervention addresses environmentally-driven variance) versus only 20% of high-CU children respond to same intervention (genetic factors dominate variance requiring different treatment targets like affective education, intensive early intervention, or pharmacological approaches), demonstrating that heritability magnitude reveals mechanistic heterogeneity enabling stratified treatment—same phenotype with different etiologies requires different interventions; and Myth 5 (genetic literacy fosters fatalism) falsely concludes genetic risk information undermines behavior change, exemplified by public health campaign stating "Type 2 diabetes is 70% genetic" producing reduced diet/exercise adherence in high-risk individuals who misinterpreted "70% genetic" as "70% unchangeable," shifting locus of control from internal (my behavior matters) to external (my genes dictate outcome) and by 14-year-old ADHD boy believing "my brain doesn't work properly and never will" creating self-fulfilling prophecy where fatalistic interpretation undermines treatment engagement, establishing that problem is communication failure (not genetic information itself) requiring reframing that pairs genetic risk with intervention efficacy: "You have elevated genetic risk AND here's the intervention proven effective for your risk profile" preserving agency by showing behavior determines outcome. Across all five myths, the unified reality is that P = G + E where phenotypic variance partitions into genetic and environmental sources but this partitioning describes population-level variance (how much of the differences between people stem from genetic versus environmental differences) not individual modifiability (whether any particular person can change), meaning high heritability should inform intervention design by revealing mechanisms, neurobiological targets, and population heterogeneity requiring differentiated approaches, exemplified by conduct disorder stratified treatment where low-CU subgroup (30% heritable, environmentally-driven) receives parenting interventions while high-CU subgroup (81% heritable, genetically-driven affective deficits) receives intensive affective training, pharmacological interventions, or residential treatment targeting neurobiological vulnerabilities, with the critical insight that heritability identifies which interventions work best and when (during sensitive periods when neural plasticity peaks) not whether change is possible, because genetic predispositions set reaction ranges (bounded potential) requiring specific environmental inputs for realization—someone genetically predisposed to 6'2" height won't reach that potential under chronic malnutrition (demonstrating environmental constraint on genetic potential) while someone genetically predisposed to 5'6" won't reach 6'2" under optimal nutrition (demonstrating genetic constraint on environmental impact), establishing that genes and environment interact multiplicatively not additively where genetic variants influence which environments produce which outcomes, illustrated by MAOA-L conferring violence risk primarily under maltreatment (gene-environment interaction: genetic vulnerability × environmental stressor = phenotypic outcome) rather than main effect where gene operates identically across all contexts. The resolution of the historical nature-nurture debate thus rejects both pure nativism (genes dictate development independent of environment) and pure empiricism (environment determines development independent of genes) in favor of interactionist models recognizing that genetic predispositions require environmental triggers for expression, environmental impacts depend on genetic susceptibilities, and developmental outcomes emerge from continuous dynamic transactions between organism and context across time, with clinical implications prioritizing genetic literacy communicating that genetic risk is probabilistic (not deterministic), modifiable (not fixed), and informative for intervention selection (not fatalistic rationalization for inaction), operationalized through messaging frameworks like "You have genetic variants affecting leptin signaling (30% higher obesity risk), which means your body stores calories more efficiently—diet and exercise remain effective and identifying this helps us tailor nutrition plans to your biology" (agency-preserving framing pairing genetic mechanism with intervention efficacy) versus fatalistic framing like "You have fat genes, so weight loss will be harder" (undermines locus of control by omitting intervention efficacy), ultimately establishing that understanding G + E should sharpen psychological intervention by enabling personalized medicine approaches matching treatment modality to etiological mechanism rather than one-size-fits-all approaches that ignore population heterogeneity, because heritability variation within disorders reveals mechanistically distinct subtypes (conduct disorder low-CU versus high-CU requiring different treatments despite identical symptoms) enabling precision intervention targeting specific biological vulnerabilities or environmental risk factors, while maintaining emphasis that high heritability indicates biological involvement requiring biological or early-intensive interventions not that outcomes are predetermined or unmodifiable, as demonstrated by height (80% heritable) responding to nutritional interventions, intelligence responding to educational enrichment during sensitive periods, and depression (40-50% heritable) responding to CBT, exercise, and social support interventions particularly in genetically high-risk individuals, collectively resolving the false nature-versus-nurture dichotomy by recognizing that the question is never "whether nature or nurture" but always "how genetic predispositions and environmental inputs interact dynamically across developmental time to produce phenotypic outcomes, and how understanding these interactions enables more effective intervention by identifying mechanisms, targets, timing windows, and population heterogeneity requiring stratified treatment approaches."
