L14: Classic Theories of Cognitive Development (12 Findings)

1. Piaget's Six Sensorimotor Stages
0-24 months PIAGET

Method: Longitudinal diary observations of own children + clinical method

Key Finding: Universal 6-stage sequence: (1) Reflex modification 0-1mo, (2) Primary circular 1-4mo (body-centered), (3) Secondary circular 4-8mo (object-focused), (4) Coordination of schemes 8-12mo (means-ends), (5) Tertiary circular 12-18mo (experimentation), (6) Mental combinations 18-24mo (symbolic thought)

Universal age ranges (±2mo); logically necessary sequence - CANNOT skip stages

Theory Link: CONSTRUCTIVISM - Each stage builds on prior; sensorimotor action schemes gradually internalize into mental operations. No innate symbols.

"Six steps from reflex to thought: body builds mind, 0 to 24 months."
2. Lucienne Matchbox Problem - Mental Representation
18 months PIAGET Stage 6

Method: Hide chain in matchbox with small opening; child must retrieve it

Key Finding: Lucienne PAUSES, opens/closes her MOUTH (physical analogue), then solves problem. Mouth-opening gesture = symbolic stand-in for "opening" action. Internalized representation.

Single-trial solution after pause; mouth gesture = first symbol

Theory Link: Transition from action to thought. Physical exploration → mental simulation. Symbol created THROUGH action schemes, not innate.

"Matchbox opens when mouth opens — body becomes the first symbol system."
3. Object Permanence & A-not-B Error
8-12 months PIAGET Stage 4

Method: Hide object at location A (infant retrieves); visibly move to B. Measure search location.

Key Finding: Stage 4 infants search at A despite seeing object move to B. Object permanence still ACTION-BOUND, not fully representational.

A-not-B error: ~80-90% at 8-10mo; ~10-20% by 12mo

Theory Link: Gradual construction of mental representation. Object concept tied to motor schemes → decontextualized representation.

"Eight months: 'out of sight, out of mind'; two years: object has a life of its own."
4. Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)
2-7 years VYGOTSKY

Method: Compare child's solo performance vs. performance with adult/peer assistance on same task

Key Finding: ZPD = gap between what child can do alone vs. with help. Learning LEADS development (opposite of Piaget: development enables learning).

Solo score vs. assisted score; size of gap predicts learning potential

Theory Link: SOCIOCULTURAL TRANSMISSION - Adult provides cultural tools (language, strategies) that child internalizes. Scaffolding bridges ZPD.

"The gap between 'I can't' and 'I can' is filled by a teacher."
5. Egocentric Speech → Inner Speech
3-7 years VYGOTSKY

Method: Observe children talking aloud during problem-solving; code frequency and task-relevance

Key Finding: Peak ~3-5yr: egocentric speech = INTERNALIZATION IN PROGRESS. Social speech becoming inner thought. INCREASES with task difficulty.

Frequency peaks ~age 4-5 (~40% of utterances); declines by age 7 (~5%) as internalized

Theory Link: CRITIQUE OF PIAGET - Not egocentric/autistic; it's social problem-solving turning inward. Language = cognitive tool from culture.

"At four they solve problems out loud; at seven the voice goes underground."
CONTRAST: Piaget says egocentric = immaturity (declines with age). Vygotsky says = internalization (increases with difficulty, then covert).
6. Vygotsky's Critique: "Wall of Facts"
THEORETICAL DEBATE

Critique: Piaget describes symbolic development beautifully but "hides behind a wall of facts" — doesn't explain HOW symbols originate. Sensorimotor → symbols is a DESCRIPTIVE GAP, not explanation.

Vygotsky's Answer: Symbols don't emerge from individual action — they're PROVIDED BY CULTURE and internalized through social interaction.

"Piaget tells us WHAT happens at 18 months; Vygotsky asks WHERE the symbols come from — and answers: culture."
7. Poverty of Stimulus Argument
2-5 years CHOMSKY - UG

Method: Theoretical argument: analyze linguistic input vs. linguistic knowledge

Key Finding: Children acquire complex grammatical rules despite IMPOVERISHED INPUT: no negative evidence, fragmentary sentences, limited examples. Yet they NEVER make certain errors (structure-dependent rules).

Grammar acquisition complete by ~4-5yr; error types NEVER observed (e.g., linear-order rules)

Theory Link: NATIVISM - Linguistic knowledge UNLEARNABLE from experience → must be innate (Universal Grammar). If language is innate, SYMBOLS are innate (refutes Piaget).

"Children know rules never taught, never corrected, never violated — genes wrote the grammar book."
8. Pronoun Binding Constraints
3-5 years CHOMSKY - UG

Method: Test comprehension: "John believes HE is smart" vs. "John believes HIM to be smart"

Key Finding: "He" can = John OR someone else (ambiguous). "Him" CANNOT = John (obligatory disjoint reference). Children NEVER violate this constraint despite no explicit teaching.

Error rate on "him=John" interpretation: ~0-2% by age 4

Theory Link: Universal Grammar constraint is part of innate linguistic endowment. Not learned via reinforcement or observation.

"'John believes him to be smart' — every four-year-old knows 'him' isn't John, though no one told them."
9. Structure Dependence (Auxiliary Questions)
3-4 years CHOMSKY - UG

Method: Test question formation: "The man who is tall is in the room" → Question form?

Key Finding: Children ALWAYS use structure-dependent rule (move auxiliary from main clause) despite linear-order rule being simpler and consistent with input. NEVER produce linear-order errors.

Error rate on linear-order strategy: ~0%

Theory Link: Human language rules operate on ABSTRACT STRUCTURE, not surface order. Constraint NEVER violated → unlearnable, hence innate.

"Children move the right 'is' every time — grammar isn't about counting words, it's about seeing trees."
10. No Negative Evidence Problem
2-4 years CHOMSKY Brown & Hanlon 1970

Method: Analyze parental corrections: grammar errors vs. truth-value errors

Key Finding: Parents RARELY correct grammar (~10% of errors); correct TRUTH/POLITENESS more (~40%). Yet children converge on correct grammar → cannot rely on negative evidence.

Grammar corrections: ~10% of errors; truth corrections: ~40%

Theory Link: LEARNABILITY ARGUMENT - Without negative evidence, incorrect hypotheses cannot be ruled out → unless innate constraints prevent them.

"Parents fix lies, not grammar — yet kids' grammar fixes itself."
11. Chomsky vs. Piaget: Debate Core
THEORETICAL DEBATE

Chomsky's Critique: NO BRIDGE from pushing objects to understanding abstract syntactic constraints. Linguistic structures are QUALITATIVELY DIFFERENT from action schemes.

Piaget's Response: "Biologically inexplicable" — how can genes specify abstract grammatical rules? Where is the evolutionary pathway?

"Chomsky: 'Pushing blocks doesn't teach island constraints.' Piaget: 'Show me the gene that codes syntax.'"
12. Cultural Variation in Cognitive Tools
VYGOTSKY

Method: Compare problem-solving strategies across cultures (e.g., abacus vs. number line)

Key Finding: Different cultures provide DIFFERENT COGNITIVE TOOLS → different cognitive profiles. Abacus experts show different mental arithmetic strategies than Western-schooled children. NOT universal stages.

Theory Link: REFUTES PIAGET'S UNIVERSALISM - Cognitive development is CULTURALLY SHAPED, not biologically predetermined sequence.

"Give a child an abacus or a pencil — they'll think in beads or symbols."

L15: Infant Research Methods (8 Findings)

13. Habituation Paradigm - Primary Method
Birth-15mo METHODOLOGICAL

Method: Repeated stimulus presentation until looking time declines to criterion (3 consecutive trials < 50% baseline). Then test with novel vs. familiar.

Key Finding: NOVELTY PREFERENCE after habituation = DISCRIMINATION ability. GENERALIZATION (no novelty pref. to pattern-matching novel) = PATTERN LEARNING.

Baseline = mean of first 3 trials; Threshold = 0.50 × baseline; Met when 3 consecutive trials ALL < threshold

Theory Link: Foundational method. Assumes: (1) looking reflects processing, (2) habituation = memory encoding, (3) dishabituation = discrimination.

"If they get bored of A and perk up for B, they can tell them apart."
14. Hunter & Ames (1988): Non-Monotonic U-Shaped Curve
CRITICAL METHOD

Method: Systematically vary familiarization duration; measure preference direction at each exposure level.

Key Finding: Preference is NON-MONOTONIC (U-shaped): No pref → FAMILIARITY pref ("I'm trying to get it" - encoding phase) → No pref → NOVELTY pref ("I'm bored" - encoded phase).

Peak familiarity pref: ~30-50% through encoding. Novelty pref: After full habituation.

Theory Link: CRITICAL - Explains familiarity preferences (previously puzzling). Makes null results AMBIGUOUS (insufficient exposure? genuine inability? wrong phase?).

"Familiarity first ('getting it'), novelty second ('got it') — preference flips as encoding completes."
APPLIES TO: Cohen's critique of Baillargeon - familiarity preference could explain VoE results if infants incompletely habituated.
15. Habituation vs. Familiarization Debate
METHODOLOGICAL CONTROVERSY

HABITUATION: Criterion-based (stop when infant meets threshold); individualized exposure. PRO: Ensures novelty preference state. CON: Uncontrolled exposure confounds processing speed.

FAMILIARIZATION: Fixed trials (e.g., 10 × 20s); controlled exposure. PRO: Controlled exposure. CON: Uncertain processing state (familiarity vs. novelty phase?).

Theory Link: Core of Cohen's critique of Baillargeon - familiarization means uncertain infant state at test.

"Habituation knows their state, familiarization knows their dose — pick your poison."
16. Cohen & Strauss (1979): Generalization Conditions I-III
30 weeks PATTERN EXTRACTION

Method: Vary habituation breadth across three conditions; measure generalization at test.

Key Finding:

  • Condition I (1 face, 1 angle): NARROW - dishabituation to both new angle AND new face ("this exact thing")
  • Condition II (1 face, many angles): MODERATE - generalize to new angles, discriminate new face ("this person from any view")
  • Condition III (many faces, many angles): BROAD - generalize to both new angle AND new face ("faces in general")
Linear relationship: As habituation variation increases, generalization increases proportionally.

Theory Link: CORE PRINCIPLE - Infants habituate to PATTERNS, not individual stimuli. Generalization scope = habituation variability.

"Show them more kinds, they learn broader patterns — habituation teaches what features matter."
17. Pattern Extraction Emerges at ~30 Weeks
<30 weeks vs. 30+ weeks DEVELOPMENTAL MILESTONE

Method: Test infants <30 weeks on Cohen & Strauss paradigm (all three conditions).

Key Finding: Younger infants FAILED to show generalization. In ALL conditions, showed novelty preference to all test stimuli. Could not extract patterns - treated every stimulus as individual instance.

No differentiation across conditions - all showed ~60-70% novelty preference

Theory Link: DEVELOPMENTAL MILESTONE - Before 30 weeks: process stimuli as ISOLATED INSTANCES. After: extract INVARIANTS/COMMONALITIES. Critical for category learning, language, face recognition.

"Before 30 weeks: 'Every face is new.' After 30 weeks: 'I see the pattern.'"
18. Generalization as ABILITY (Not Failure)
CONCEPTUAL SHIFT

Key Insight: When infant shows NO dishabituation to novel stimulus that fits learned pattern, this is PATTERN LEARNING SUCCESS, not discrimination failure.

Logic: Infant correctly treats pattern-matching novel as familiar because they abstracted the common structure. Discrimination ability is INTACT (proven by dishabituation to pattern-breaking novel).

Theory Link: INTERPRETATION SHIFT - Pre-1979: generalization = "infant failed to notice difference." Post-Cohen & Strauss: generalization = "infant abstracted similarity."

"When they yawn at the new example, they learned the rule — generalization is the goal, not the problem."
19. Null Results Ambiguity Problem
METHODOLOGICAL ISSUE

Issue: When infant shows NO preference (novel ≈ familiar looking time), THREE possible interpretations:

  1. Under-habituated: Still in familiarity preference phase (Hunter & Ames) - increase exposure
  2. Genuine inability: Cannot discriminate stimuli - supports developmental claim
  3. Over-habituated/fatigued: Infant disengaged entirely - methodological failure

Theory Link: FALSIFICATION PROBLEM - Habituation paradigm is easier to CONFIRM abilities than RULE THEM OUT. Positive result (novelty pref.) = clear. Null result = murky.

"No preference = maybe can't tell, maybe didn't see enough, maybe stopped caring — null is a shrug."
20. Replication Crisis in Infant Research
ManyBabies 2010s+

Method: Large-scale replication efforts (N>500) with pre-registered designs across multiple labs.

Key Finding: Many classic findings DON'T replicate. Effect sizes smaller than original reports; some effects disappear entirely.

Original studies: typical N=24, reported p<0.05. Replications: N=500+, many p>0.10 or smaller d (~0.2 vs. original ~0.8)

Theory Link: Likely causes: publication bias, flexibility in method choice (researcher degrees of freedom), small original samples.

"The old studies were tiny and tweakable — big replications are humbling the field."

L16: Nativism vs. Constructivism (14 Findings)

21. Kellman & Spelke (1983): Moving Rod - Object Unity
4 months NATIVIST d = 0.8

Method: Habituation: Red rod moves horizontally behind yellow occluder (top & bottom visible, moving together in COMMON MOTION). Test: Complete rod vs. Broken rod.

Key Finding: Moving condition: Infants look longer at BROKEN rod (novelty preference despite perceptual familiarity with disconnected surfaces). Common motion TRIGGERED unity representation.

Broken > Complete (p < 0.01, d = 0.8). Looking time: Broken ~35s, Complete ~22s

Theory Link: NATIVIST MODULE ACTIVATION - Common motion is SPECIFIC TRIGGER that activates innate object module. Module infers unity → applies core knowledge.

"Common motion at 4 months triggers object unity — broken rod surprises because module inferred wholeness."
22. Kellman & Spelke (1983): Stationary Rod Control
4 months NATIVIST

Method: Same rod setup but STATIONARY during habituation (no motion). Test: Complete vs. Broken rod.

Key Finding: NO PREFERENCE between complete and broken (p = 0.6). Infants cannot infer unity from static occlusion alone. MOTION IS NECESSARY.

Complete ≈ Broken looking time (~27s both conditions)

Theory Link: TRIGGER SPECIFICITY - Object module requires COMMON MOTION INPUT to activate. Without trigger, module doesn't fire → no unity inference. Demonstrates INFORMATION ENCAPSULATION.

"Static rod = no unity inference — module needs motion trigger, just like a key needs a lock."
23. Johnson & Aslin (1998): Graded Occlusion
2mo vs. 4mo CONSTRUCTIVIST

Method: Vary occluder size: Small occluder (most surface visible) vs. Large occluder (less surface visible). Test unity perception.

Key Finding: GRADUAL development: 2-month-olds succeed with SMALL occluder only (~75%). 4-month-olds succeed with BOTH small and large (~80% each). Younger infants need more perceptual support.

2mo: Small 75%, Large 40%. 4mo: Small 80%, Large 80%

Theory Link: CONSTRUCTIVIST - Graded improvement suggests HIERARCHICAL CONSTRUCTION. NOT sudden module activation (would predict step-like emergence).

"Two-month-olds need training wheels (small occluder); four-month-olds ride solo — gradual not modular."
24. Slater et al. (1996): Newborn Unity - Gestalt vs. Module
Newborn CONSTRUCTIVIST

Method: Test newborns (hours old) with moving rod paradigm (minimal occlusion).

Key Finding: Newborns prefer COMPLETE rod (OPPOSITE of 4-month-olds who prefer broken). Suggests GESTALT GROUPING (innate perceptual organization), NOT object module.

Complete > Broken (p < 0.05, d = 0.4). Looking ~8s complete, ~5s broken

Theory Link: DISTINGUISHES perceptual from conceptual: Newborns have GESTALT SIMILARITY GROUPING (low-level perception), not object unity via module. By 4mo, learning transforms Gestalt into robust object representation.

"Newborns like complete rods (Gestalt), four-month-olds surprised by broken rods (object knowledge) — different mechanisms."
25. Baillargeon (1987): Drawbridge Paradigm - VoE
4.5 months NATIVIST d ≈ 0.7-0.9

Method: Familiarization: Drawbridge rotates 180°. Box placed behind. Test: (a) POSSIBLE: Rotation stops at ~120° (contacts box) = perceptually novel. (b) IMPOSSIBLE: Full 180° rotation (passes through box) = perceptually familiar.

Key Finding: Infants look longer at IMPOSSIBLE event (45s vs. 33s; p < 0.01). CONCEPTUAL SURPRISE overrides perceptual familiarity.

Impossible: 45s; Possible: 33s (12s difference, ~36% increase). d ≈ 0.7-0.9

Theory Link: VIOLATION OF EXPECTATION (VoE) LOGIC - By making impossible = familiar, separates conceptual from perceptual. Longer looking to familiar-but-impossible = CORE KNOWLEDGE OF SOLIDITY violated.

"Familiar rotation through box = longer looking — they KNOW it shouldn't pass through (solidity constraint)."
26. Cohen & Cashon Critique #1: Incomplete Habituation
CONSTRUCTIVIST CRITIQUE

Critique: Baillargeon used FAMILIARIZATION (fixed trials), not HABITUATION (criterion-based). Included infants who did NOT reach 50% reduction.

Alternative Explanation: Per Hunter & Ames, incompletely habituated infants show FAMILIARITY PREFERENCE. Longer looking to impossible (familiar 180° rotation) = FAMILIARITY PREFERENCE, not conceptual surprise.

Familiarity preference would produce SAME PATTERN as VoE

Theory Link: METHODOLOGICAL CONFOUND - If incomplete habituation → familiarity preference mimics conceptual surprise. Cohen argues Baillargeon's findings may reflect incomplete encoding, not core knowledge.

"Cohen: 'They're not surprised by physics — they're just still staring at the cool rotation because they haven't habituated yet.'"
27. Cohen & Cashon Critique #2: Perceptual Confound
CONSTRUCTIVIST CRITIQUE

Critique: Impossible event (180°) has MORE MOTION than possible event (120°). Full rotation = more motion, longer duration, perceptually more complex.

Alternative Explanation: Infants may look longer for LOW-LEVEL PERCEPTUAL REASONS (motion attracts attention), unrelated to representing hidden object.

Control Test Needed: Run same paradigm with VISIBLE box. If same preference with visible box → perceptual confound. If no preference → supports object representation.

"More motion = more looking — maybe they just like watching things move, not thinking about solidity."
28. Meltzoff & Moore (1998): Feature Memory SUCCESS at 5mo
5 months CONSTRUCTIVIST

Method: Object (cylinder/prism) goes behind screen. Test: Same shape vs. different shape emerges.

Key Finding: 5-month-olds look longer at shape change (28s mismatch vs. 22s match). SUCCESS: Infants have PERCEPTUAL MEMORY for object features across brief occlusion.

5mo: Mismatch 28s, Match 22s (6s difference, p < 0.05)

Theory Link: PARTIAL PERMANENCE - 5mo have PERCEPTUAL MEMORY ("I saw a cylinder go behind there") but NOT full object permanence. Memory ≠ Permanence.

"Five-month-olds remember WHAT went behind (features) but not WHERE it should be (space)."
29. Meltzoff & Moore (1998): Spatial Continuity FAILURE at 5mo
5mo vs. 9mo CONSTRUCTIVIST

Method: Object goes behind Screen 1, emerges from Screen 2 WITHOUT appearing in gap between (object "teleported"). Test: Violation vs. Continuous trajectory.

Key Finding: 5-month-olds: NO longer looking at violation (18s vs. 17s; p > 0.5). FAILURE. 9-month-olds: Look longer at violation (35s vs. 27s; p < 0.01). SUCCESS.

5mo: No difference. 9mo: Violation 35s, Continuous 27s (8s difference, ~30% increase)

Theory Link: CRITICAL DISSOCIATION - 5mo have feature memory BUT NOT TRUE OBJECT PERMANENCE (no spatiotemporal continuity). SUPPORTS PIAGET'S 8-9 MONTH TIMELINE. Piecemeal development contradicts nativist co-emergence.

"Five months: 'I remember the cylinder' but not 'it should travel between screens' — space understanding lags features."
CRITICAL: This dissociation (features yes, space no) is KEY evidence for constructivism - development is PIECEMEAL, not modular co-emergence.
30. Competence-Performance Gap: Looking vs. Reaching
5mo looking vs. 8-9mo reaching DEBATE CORE

The Dissociation: Infants succeed in looking-time tasks at 3.5-5 months (Baillargeon) but fail manual search until 8-9 months (Piaget). ~4-month lag.

Two Interpretations:

  • NATIVIST: Knowledge exists early (COMPETENCE) but motor/executive functions immature (PERFORMANCE)
  • CONSTRUCTIVIST: True permanence develops at 8-9mo; earlier looking = shallow memory

NO CONSENSUS.

"Eyes know at four, hands know at nine — performance gap (nativist) or knowledge gap (constructivist)?"
31. Modularity: Domain Specificity & Co-emergence
NATIVIST - FODOR/CAREY

Theory: Modules process only SPECIFIC CONTENT. Prediction: Object-related abilities should CO-EMERGE.

Evidence: Unity (common motion), solidity understanding, object individuation (spatiotemporal cues) all emerge ~4 MONTHS.

Carey's Argument: Co-emergence supports unified module activating. Constructivism has no principled reason for simultaneity.

Constructivist Counter: Could be coincidence, shared prerequisites, or hierarchical construction reaching threshold together.

"When three abilities unlock at once, it's one key turning three locks — module, not learning."
32. Core Knowledge Principles
NATIVIST - SPELKE

Four Innate Principles:

  1. PERSISTENCE: Objects exist continuously
  2. SOLIDITY: Objects don't interpenetrate
  3. COHESION: Object parts move together
  4. CONTACT: Objects act on each other via contact

Evidence: VoE paradigms show surprise at principle violations at 4-5mo.

Theory Link: Principles are UNLEARNED (too abstract, too early, too little experience). Constructivists counter: Massive experience by 4mo; principles could emerge from lower-level regularities.

"Core knowledge: persistence, solidity, cohesion, contact — four rules the module knows from birth."
33. Statistical Learning: 150 Million Visual Samples by 4 Months
CONSTRUCTIVIST

Calculation: By 4 months, infants have ~14,000 waking hours. If visual sampling at 3 fixations/second × 60% awake time engaged → ~150 MILLION visual samples.

Argument: "Too early to be learned" (nativist claim) underestimates MASSIVE EXPERIENCE. Statistical learning from millions of samples can extract regularities rapidly. Co-motion predicts unity 99%+ in natural environment → learnable.

Nativist Response: Sheer quantity doesn't matter if mechanism is domain-general (can't extract abstract principles from low-level correlations).

"150 million looks by four months — that's a PhD in object physics from the University of Life."
34. Current Status: Theoretical Stalemate
META-THEORETICAL

Both theories accommodate most evidence via auxiliary assumptions:

  • Nativists: Gradual = input maturation, dissociations = performance limits
  • Constructivists: Early abilities = shallow memory/familiarity preferences, co-emergence = coincidence

Key debates unresolved: (1) Is co-emergence real or artifact? (2) Do VoE results reflect conceptual surprise or familiarity preference? (3) Is looking-reaching gap competence-performance or knowledge levels?

"Two theories, same data, different stories — the debate isn't over, it's evolved into interpretation war."

L17: Dynamic Systems & Statistical Learning (11 Findings)

35. Sticky Mittens Intervention
2 months → 6-month level EMBODIED

Method: Velcro-like mittens enable "grasping" through contact (bypassing precision grip requirement); compare to controls.

Key Finding: Accelerated visual inspection and oral exploration patterns to 6-MONTH LEVEL. 4-month developmental jump.

Significant group differences in systematic visual inspection (p < .05); controlled manipulation patterns emerge 4 months early

Theory Link: Motor ability CAUSALLY DRIVES perceptual development via feedback loop: enhanced motor control → more controlled manipulation → richer multi-sensory input → accelerated learning.

"Two-month-olds with sticky mittens reach 6-month perceptual level — motor affordances cause cognitive acceleration."
36. A-not-B Error: Postural Reset (Linda Smith 1999)
8-10 months EMBODIED

Method: Standard A-not-B task, but infant STANDS UP AND SITS DOWN between A-trials and B-trial.

Key Finding: DRAMATICALLY REDUCES OR ELIMINATES perseverative error. Postural reset disrupts motor memory, allowing visual info to guide behavior.

Perseveration rate: Standard ~80%; Postural reset ~20-30% (p < .01)

Theory Link: Object representation is BODY-STATE-DEPENDENT. Encoded as "reaching-from-sitting-posture → toy at A." NOT incomplete object permanence (Piaget) — cognition remains embodied, not abstract.

"Stand-sit postural reset eliminates A-not-B error — object memory is embodied in motor state, not abstract."
REINTERPRETS PIAGET: A-not-B error isn't about object permanence failing — it's about embodied motor memory.
37. Gap Crossing: Posture Transfer Failure (Adolph)
9 months DYNAMIC SYSTEMS

Method: Test SAME infant with identical gap width: first while sitting (reaching), then while crawling across.

Key Finding: SITTING: Refuse to reach across gap (learned safe distance). CRAWLING: Attempt to crawl across SAME gap previously avoided.

Within-subject design: Same infant shows avoidance while sitting but attempts while crawling (p < .001)

Theory Link: Spatial understanding is NOT ABSTRACT. "How far is too far" must be RELEARNED for each motor configuration. PIECEMEAL development with minimal cross-context generalization.

"Nine-month-olds refuse gaps while sitting but attempt same gaps while crawling — spatial knowledge is posture-specific."
38. Thelen Twin Training Study
DYNAMIC SYSTEMS

Method: Intensive roller-skating training for Johnny; no training for twin Jimmy; track milestone acquisition.

Key Finding: Johnny developed exceptional skating skills but NO ACCELERATION in sitting, crawling, or walking compared to untrained Jimmy.

Motor milestone timing: No significant differences (all p > .05)

Theory Link: MINIMAL CROSS-TASK GENERALIZATION — skills learned in one bodily configuration don't transfer to related configurations. Proves piecemeal development principle.

"Intensive roller-skating training doesn't accelerate other motor skills — development is piecemeal, not general."
39. Embodied Semantics: Action Words (Pulvermüller fMRI)
Adults EMBODIED

Method: Read action words ("kick," "punch," "lick") during neuroimaging.

Key Finding: Activate SOMATOTOPIC MOTOR CORTEX regions controlling relevant body parts (leg for "kick," arm for "punch," mouth for "lick").

Significant somatotopic activation (p < .001); spatial organization preserved

Theory Link: Word meanings ARE DISTRIBUTED SENSORY-MOTOR ACTIVATIONS (constitutive, not correlational). No separate abstract semantic layer required. Supports PERMANENT EMBODIMENT.

"Reading 'kick' activates leg motor cortex — word meanings are embodied activations, not abstract symbols."
40. ATL: Multi-sensory Integration Hub
STATISTICAL LEARNING

Method: fMRI during semantic tasks; case studies of ATL damage (semantic dementia).

Key Finding: ATL activates for virtually all concrete nouns; INTEGRATES ACROSS MODALITIES; damage impairs concepts while preserving modality-specific processing.

ATL activation for >90% of concrete nouns regardless of sensory profile (p < .001)

Theory Link: ATL encodes HIGHER-ORDER STATISTICAL REGULARITIES — co-activation patterns across modalities. Solves binding problem by learning feature correlations (yellow + small + bird-shape + tweeting = canary).

"ATL integrates distributed features across modalities — semantic hub encoding co-occurrence patterns."
41. Younger & Cohen (1983): Feature Correlation Learning
4, 7, 10 months STATISTICAL

Method: Habituation to line-drawing animals; 4 features vary, 2 pairs correlated. Test: C (correlated), U (uncorrelated), N (novel).

Key Finding:

  • 10mo: C low, U HIGH, N high (LEARNED CORRELATIONS)
  • 4mo & 7mo: C low, U LOW, N high (features only)
10mo: C=5s, U=12s, N=13s (U vs C: p < .01). 4/7mo: C=5s, U=6s, N=12s (U vs C: n.s.)

Theory Link: Demonstrates HIERARCHICAL CONSTRUCTION: 10mo construct Level 2 representations (feature correlations); 4/7mo limited to Level 1 (features). Age-related capacity increases.

"Ten-month-olds treat uncorrelated test items as novel despite familiar features — learned statistical structure."
42. Younger & Cohen (1986) Exp 4: Cognitive Overload → Reversion
7 months DYNAMIC SYSTEMS

Method: Medium task (3 features, 2/3 correlated) with EXTENDED habituation trials (more time to process).

Key Finding: 7mo: C low, U LOW, N high — REVERTED TO FEATURE-ONLY LEARNING. Extended time produces WORSE outcomes.

Extended trials: C=5s, U=6s, N=12s — pattern matches 4mo despite older age

Theory Link: CRITICAL FINDING — Extended time produces WORSE outcomes when capacity exceeded. Infants recognize correlation learning failed → shift to achievable goal (features). ADAPTIVE STRATEGIC SHIFT.

"Seven-month-olds revert to feature learning with extended time — more processing can produce worse outcomes."
KEY PRINCIPLE: Cognitive overload → reversion to lower-level processing. This applies to CAUSAL perception (L18) and SAME/DIFFERENT learning too.
43. Shape Bias Development
15-24 months STATISTICAL

Method: Measure productive vocabulary (noun count); test shape bias strength with novel noun generalization task.

Key Finding:

  • 0-25 nouns: ~30% shape generalization (weak/absent bias)
  • 26-50 nouns: ~60-70% (emerging bias)
  • 51+ nouns: >90% (strong bias)
Correlation: r = .72 between noun count and shape generalization (p < .001); age correlation weaker (r = .45)

Theory Link: Correlation with vocabulary SIZE (not age) proves LEARNED, not innate. Higher-order pattern requires sufficient category-learning experiences (~25-50 noun threshold).

"Shape bias correlates with vocabulary size, not age — emerges from statistical learning, not innate constraints."
44. Dynamic Systems vs. Piaget: Permanent Embodiment
THEORETICAL

PIAGET: Sensorimotor foundation TRANSCENDED by symbolic operations (abstraction emerges).

DYNAMIC SYSTEMS: Embodiment is PERMANENT; apparent abstraction is illusion from complex integration.

fMRI evidence: Adults show sensory-motor activation during "abstract" reasoning (supports Dynamic Systems)

Theory Link: Rejects Piaget's "symbol problem" — how to explain emergence of discrete symbols from continuous sensorimotor experience — by DENYING SYMBOLS EXIST.

"Piaget says symbols emerge and transcend body; Dynamic Systems says symbols don't exist — only embodied patterns."
45. Hierarchical Brain Architecture
STATISTICAL

Three-Level Hierarchy:

  1. Level 1: Modality-specific (motor, visual, auditory cortex)
  2. Level 2: ATL multimodal hub (feature correlations)
  3. Level 3: aPFC highest integration (relations among relations)

Key Insight: NO DISCRETE SYMBOLS — just increasingly abstract statistical regularities detected via hierarchical integration.

"Brain hierarchy: modality-specific features, ATL cross-modal correlations, aPFC meta-level relations — no symbols, only patterns."

L18: Abstract Relational Learning (10 Findings)

46. Natural Partitions Hypothesis (Gentner 1982)
THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

Framework: Cognition parses world into OBJECTS (stable perceptual entities) vs. RELATIONS (dynamic connections between objects).

Key Insight: Objects are cognitively PRIVILEGED (stable, cohesive, persistent). Relations are HARDER to perceive (dynamic, context-dependent, transient, require abstraction).

Cross-cultural universals: nouns learned earlier than verbs; abstract relational matching nearly impossible for non-humans

Theory Link: Relational learning is computationally demanding, UNIQUELY HUMAN, and vulnerable to cognitive overload.

"Objects are stable and perceptual; relations are dynamic and require abstraction — explaining human uniqueness."
47. Ferry et al. (2015): Same/Different Learning
7-9 months RELATIONAL d = 0.78

Method: Habituation to pairs showing "same" relation. Test: novel objects in same vs. different relation.

Key Finding:

  • 1 pair repeated (AA, AA, AA): NO generalization — encode "A-ness," not "sameness"
  • 4 diverse pairs (AA, BB, CC, DD): SUCCESSFUL — look longer at novel relation (EF) than familiar relation with novel objects (EE)
4 pairs: Looking to different (EF) > same (EE); p < .01; d = 0.78

Theory Link: FOUR PAIRS forces abstraction across instances while remaining within capacity. Optimal exemplar count enables relational representation independent of object identity.

"Four diverse pairs optimal for 7-9-month-olds — abstract same vs. different with novel objects."
48. Ferry et al. (2015): Object Experience Effect
7-9 months CONSTRUCTIVIST

Method: Infants played with test objects in waiting room (familiar but NOT habituated); then standard 4-pair habituation with different objects; test with familiar-but-not-habituated objects.

Key Finding: COMPLETE FAILURE despite succeeding with: (a) completely novel objects, (b) objects that were both played with AND habituated to.

No discrimination (p > .05); pattern identical to 1-pair failure despite optimal exemplar count

Theory Link: CRITICAL EVIDENCE — Mere familiarity without habituation increases object SALIENCE → consumes resources → blocks relational processing. HIERARCHICAL DEPENDENCY: Unexpected re-encounter grabs attention to object level.

"Familiar-but-not-habituated objects block relational learning — salience interference consumes cognitive resources."
FORMULA: Relational success = f(Available capacity - Object salience)
49. Anderson et al. (2018): Same/Different at 3 Months
3 months RELATIONAL

Method: Test relational abstraction with varying exemplar counts at 3 months.

Key Finding:

  • 6 pairs: FAILURE — cognitive overload (tracking 12 objects + relation exceeds capacity)
  • 2 pairs: SUCCESS — 3-month-olds discriminate same vs. different
2 pairs: Significant novelty preference for novel relation (p < .05); EARLIEST evidence of relational abstraction

Theory Link: TWO PAIRS optimal for 3mo — provides sufficient variability to force abstraction while remaining within capacity limits. Age-capacity relationship: Optimal exemplar count increases with development.

"Two pairs optimal for 3-month-olds — earliest abstract same/different learning with minimal load."
50. Leslie (1984): Causal Module with Simple Shapes
6 months NATIVIST

Method: Michotte launching paradigm with SIMPLE GEOMETRIC SHAPES. Habituation to causal (direct launch) vs. non-causal (delayed reaction, spatial gap).

Key Finding: CAUSAL CATEGORIZATION — infants habituated to direct launching dishabituated to delayed reaction; generalized habituation across different non-causal types.

Habituate to causal → look longer at both non-causal types (p < .01)

Theory Link: LESLIE INTERPRETATION — Innate causal module triggered by spatiotemporal cues (contact + immediate motion). 6-month-olds categorize by causal status, not perceptual features.

"Six-month-olds categorize causal vs. non-causal with simple shapes — Leslie claims innate module."
51. Oakes & Cohen (1990): Complexity Effect at 6mo
6 months CONSTRUCTIVIST

Method: Same launching paradigm but with COMPLEX REALISTIC TOYS (dinosaur, airplane) instead of simple shapes.

Key Finding: FEATURAL PROCESSING ONLY — infants discriminated based on spatial-temporal features (gaps vs. delays), NOT causal status. Failed to generalize across different non-causal types.

No evidence of causal categorization (p > .05 for causal grouping)

Theory Link: CRITICAL — Same 6-month age, identical spatiotemporal structure, only object complexity changed → OPPOSITE RESULTS. Complex objects consume capacity for object encoding, preventing causal abstraction.

"Six-month-olds show featural processing with complex toys — object complexity blocks causal perception."
52. Oakes & Cohen (1990): 10mo Succeeds with Complex
10 months CONSTRUCTIVIST

Method: Same complex realistic toys as 6-month condition.

Key Finding: CAUSAL PERCEPTION RESTORED — 10-month-olds showed causal categorization with complex toys (pattern matching Leslie's simple shapes at 6mo).

Habituate to causal → dishabituate to both non-causal types (p < .01 for causal grouping)

Theory Link: INCREASED CAPACITY by 10mo supports causal processing despite object complexity. Same stimuli that overloaded 6mo now processable — demonstrates CAPACITY-DEPENDENT ACCESS, not module maturation.

"Ten-month-olds show causal perception with complex toys — increased capacity handles object complexity."
53. Cohen & Oakes (1993): Variability Effect at 10mo
10 months CONSTRUCTIVIST

Method: Complex realistic toys with DIFFERENT OBJECTS ON EVERY TRIAL (trial-by-trial variability).

Key Finding: REVERSION TO FEATURAL PROCESSING — 10-month-olds who showed causal perception with repeated objects FAILED when objects varied trial-by-trial.

Same 10mo succeed with repeated objects, fail with varying objects

Theory Link: CRITICAL EVIDENCE — Object variability exceeds even 10-month capacity. Continuous encoding of "what are these objects?" consumes resources, preventing "how are they causally related?"

"Ten-month-olds revert to featural with varying objects — trial-by-trial variability exceeds capacity."
54. Three-Factor Decision Rule for Relational Processing
FRAMEWORK

Causal/Relational perception succeeds when ALL THREE factors favorable:

  1. Age capacity sufficient (10mo > 6mo for complex)
  2. Object complexity manageable (shapes > toys)
  3. Object variability low (repeated > varying)

If ANY factor increases load beyond threshold → predict REVERSION to featural processing.

Pattern across studies: 6mo simple ✓, 6mo complex ✗, 10mo complex-repeated ✓, 10mo complex-variable ✗

Theory Link: FORMULA: Relational processing = f(Capacity_age - Load_cognitive), where Load = Object Complexity + Object Variability.

"Causal perception needs favorable age, complexity, and variability — any factor exceeding threshold causes featural reversion."
55. Neoconstructivism: Hierarchical Architecture
NEOCONSTRUCTIVISM

Five Principles:

  1. Innate domain-GENERAL system (not modules)
  2. Higher units built from lower (hierarchical construction)
  3. Hierarchical dependency (must complete lower level first)
  4. Use highest level possible (adaptive flexibility)
  5. Revert to lower when overloaded (signature prediction)

Signature Prediction: INCREASING COGNITIVE LOAD LOWERS INFORMATION PROCESSING LEVEL from relational → featural. Same infant shows relational with low-load, featural with high-load.

Theory Link: Explains complexity effects (6mo), variability effects (10mo), exemplar count non-monotonicity, object experience interference.

"Neoconstructivism predicts load lowers processing level — explains systematic capacity-dependent variation."
CRITICAL COMPARISON: Nativism predicts modules fire consistently when triggered — CANNOT explain load-dependent performance patterns (same age, different load = opposite results).
