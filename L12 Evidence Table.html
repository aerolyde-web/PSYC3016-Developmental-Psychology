Comfort Factor 1: Familiarity All contexts Reducing novelty stress Multiple sessions building relationship, consistent location eliminating surprises, pre-tour showing space/materials, familiar interviewer Novelty-induced stress suppresses recall and disclosure. Familiarity reduces cognitive load allowing child to focus on communication not environment assessment. Multiple sessions build trust. Consistent location eliminates orienting demands. Success marker: child initiating conversation and exploring independently indicates comfort established (not sitting frozen waiting for direction). Success marker: child initiates conversation unprompted, explores room/materials independently, asks questions freely = comfort achieved. Comfort Factor 2: Appropriate Environment Physical space design Physical comfort needs Child-sized furniture (not adult furniture causing discomfort), clear exit paths (preventing trapped feelings), soundproof rooms (privacy/confidentiality), appropriate lighting/temperature Physical discomfort disrupts attention and communication. Child-sized furniture prevents dangling feet, back pain, awkward postures that distract from conversation. Clear exit paths prevent trapped feelings triggering fight/flight responses. Success marker: child settling physically within 5 minutes (stops squirming, fidgeting decreases, maintains comfortable posture throughout session). Success: child settles physically <5 minutes, maintains comfortable posture >80% session. Physical discomfort reduces disclosure 30-40% in studies. Exit visibility reduces anxiety 25-35% Physical comfort prerequisite for psychological comfort; discomfort signals threat activating stress responses; exit visibility provides sense of control reducing anxiety "Comfort-Environment: child-sized furniture (no discomfort distraction) + clear exit (no trapped feeling). Success = child settles physically <5 minutes."
Comfort Factor 3: Adequate Explanation Process transparency Reducing uncertainty Explicit process maps (what will happen), duration clarity (how long), role definition (who is who and why), expectations (what child should do) Uncertainty creates anxiety blocking disclosure. Success = child repeats back demonstrating comprehension."

Reliability Hierarchy: Free Recall Highest accuracy questioning Initial information gathering "Tell me everything that happened from beginning to end." Open-ended prompting without constraints. Exhaustively use first before moving to other question types Free recall produces low quantity yet maximum accuracy with minimal contamination. Always use first, exhaustively. "Tell me everything" allows child to control narrative, report salient details, use own structure/vocabulary. Quantity-veracity trade-off: fewer details but highest reliability. No adult suggestion influencing responses. Accuracy: 85-95% for free recall details. Quantity: typically 20-40% of total information via free recall alone. Contamination: <5% false details. Effect: exhaustive free recall foundation before other questioning Child-controlled narrative minimizes suggestion; child reports what they find salient not what adult directs attention to; own vocabulary prevents adult concept introduction; spontaneous recall most resistant to contamination "Reliability-Free Recall: 'tell me everything' produces low quantity (20-40%) but maximum accuracy (85-95%), minimal contamination (<5%) - ALWAYS USE FIRST exhaustively."

Maintains conversational flow while directing to specific topics Cued invitations generate moderate yield maintaining high accuracy IF using child's language. After free recall exhausted, return child to topics they mentioned using their exact words. Still open-ended (not yes/no). Still child-controlled (they elaborate how they choose). Accuracy remains high when properly implemented. Risk: adult introducing new vocabulary. Accuracy: 75-85% if using child's exact words. Quantity: additional 20-30% information. Contamination: 10-15% if adult words introduced. Success: maintaining child's vocabulary prevents suggestion Focuses attention without constraining response; child's own words ensure topic is their concept not adult imposition; open-ended elaboration maintains control; prevents leading while enabling focus "Reliability-Cued Invitations: 'tell me about [their word]' after free recall exhausted, 75-85% accuracy if child's language, moderate yield 20-30% more info."

NEVER "why" (implicates motivation child may not understand). Reliability Hierarchy: Option-Posing Last resort for essential facts Forced choice minimum use "Was it X or Y or something else?" Forces choice yet provides "something else" option.
