Full Model Answer (~270 words)

Kohlberg's theory posits six discrete stages across three levels of moral reasoning. The fundamental assumption is that stages form an invariant sequence—each represents a qualitatively more adequate way of thinking about moral issues.

Level 1 (Pre-Conventional) centers on external consequences. Stage 1 (Punishment Avoidance) employs reverse logic: punishment indicates wrongdoing. Stage 2 (Self-Interest) asks "What's in it for me?" with moral decisions becoming transactional. Both stages remain egocentric.

Level 2 (Conventional) introduces social perspectives. Stage 3 (Social Approval)—the "good girl/good boy" orientation—judges rightness by perceived social expectations. Stage 4 (Law and Order) focuses on maintaining social order through rule adherence, reasoning "beyond the individual" to societal functioning. This level requires theory of mind.

Level 3 (Post-Conventional) is reached by only 10-15% of adults. Stage 5 (Social Contract) recognizes laws as changeable agreements benefiting the majority. Stage 6 (Universal Principles) involves self-chosen ethical principles that supersede laws—exemplified by Dietrich Bonhoeffer, a pacifist who attempted to assassinate Hitler when universal principles (stopping genocide) overrode both personal beliefs and societal laws.

Kohlberg measures moral reasoning—what one thinks should be done—through hypothetical dilemmas, not actual behavior or emotional responses. This focus on cognitive justification distinguishes his rationalist approach from sentimentalist alternatives emphasizing emotional moral development.

REASONING-BEHAVIOR GAP
Identical behaviors can emerge from different reasoning stages. A man punching a harasser could stem from Stage 2 (impressing the woman), Stage 3 (meeting expectations), or Stage 6 (protecting the vulnerable). Conversely, identical reasoning produces different behaviors based on personality, situation, and social factors.
GENDER BIAS
Carol Gilligan (1982) revealed Kohlberg's original sample contained only males. She identified two moral orientations: ethics of care (relationships, contextual response) vs ethics of justice (rules, fairness). Kohlberg's hierarchy ranks justice (Stage 4) above care (Stage 3), making female reasoning appear less developed.
NON-HIERARCHICAL DEVELOPMENT
Longitudinal data shows individuals reason at multiple stages simultaneously. The same person might use Stage 2 for minor infractions, Stage 3 for interpersonal conflicts, and Stage 4 for legal matters. Development is a shift in reasoning repertoire, not clean progression.
Exam Insight

Don't confuse Gilligan's care/justice distinction with the moral/conventional distinction. Care vs justice are different APPROACHES to morality. Moral vs conventional are different TYPES of transgressions. Both criticize Kohlberg but from different angles.

Full Model Answer (~280 words)

The most fundamental criticism of Kohlberg's theory is that moral reasoning does not predict moral behavior. Consider a man who punches someone harassing a woman: this identical action could stem from Stage 2 self-interest (impressing the woman), Stage 3 social approval (meeting perceived expectations), or Stage 6 universal principles (protecting the vulnerable). Identical behaviors emerge from different reasoning stages, while identical reasoning produces different behaviors depending on personality, situation, and group dynamics.

Carol Gilligan's (1982) analysis revealed that Kohlberg's original sample contained only males, fundamentally skewing the model. Her research identified two distinct moral orientations: ethics of care (emphasizing relationships and contextual response) versus ethics of justice (emphasizing rules and abstract fairness). Females more frequently employ care-based reasoning. Since Kohlberg's hierarchy ranks justice (Stage 4) above care (Stage 3), female moral reasoning appears less developed—an inherent gender bias.

Longitudinal data contradicts the invariant sequence assumption. Individuals reason at multiple stages simultaneously, shifting based on context. The same person might use Stage 2 reasoning for minor infractions (taking office supplies), Stage 3 for interpersonal conflicts (lying for a friend), and Stage 4 for legal matters (reporting a crime). Development is a gradual shift in reasoning repertoire, not clean hierarchical progression.

Additional criticisms include poor ecological validity (hypothetical dilemmas don't capture real decision pressures), cultural bias (collectivist societies value Stage 3 harmony over Stage 4 abstract justice), and articulation demands that disadvantage young children who understand moral distinctions but cannot verbally justify them.

THE DISTINCTION
Moral transgressions involve actions causing harm, suffering, or injustice (hitting, stealing). Conventional transgressions violate social rules that coordinate group behavior without inherent harm (not sitting on mat, improper greetings). Moral prescriptions are perceived as unalterable by consensus and universal across contexts.

Exam Insight

The "harm principle" is key: when conventional transgressions cause harm, they shift domains. Not wearing clothes is conventional, but forcing someone unclothed in the Arctic causes harm, making it moral. Context determines whether rule violations remain conventional or become moral through harm causation.

Full Model Answer (~280 words)

Social domain theory (Turiel, Smetana) demonstrates that children distinguish between moral and conventional transgressions remarkably early. Moral transgressions involve actions causing harm, suffering, or injustice (hitting, stealing, not sharing), while conventional transgressions violate social rules that coordinate group behavior without inherent harm (not sitting on the mat, improper greetings).

Smetana (1981) tested children aged 30-57 months and found no age differences—even 2.5-year-olds made consistent distinctions. Children judged moral transgressions as: (1) more wrong in the absence of rules, (2) more serious and deserving punishment, (3) context-independent—wrong everywhere, and (4) unmodifiable by authority. This early emergence challenges Kohlberg's assumption that moral understanding develops slowly through stages.

The defining feature is the harm principle: moral prescriptions stem from "factors intrinsic to the event"—the wrongness of hitting emerges from the victim's pain, not from prohibition. Perception of moral transgression "is not contingent on the presence of a rule." Conventions, by contrast, are arbitrary, alterable by consensus, and contextually bound.

This creates a development paradox: if 2.5-year-olds understand hitting is inherently wrong, why do they continue hitting? The gap between moral understanding and behavior suggests knowing right from wrong differs fundamentally from consistently choosing right. The disconnect involves immature impulse control, emotional regulation, and gradual internalization processes—moral knowledge develops before behavioral compliance.

Full Model Answer (~260 words)

The lecture establishes a fundamental distinction between moral reasoning (Kohlberg's cognitive approach asking what should be done) and moral appraisal (social domain theory's evaluative approach asking what is right/wrong). These represent the rationalist tradition emphasizing cognitive development through stages versus the sentimentalist tradition emphasizing intuitive moral understanding based on harm perception.

Kohlberg measures cognitive sophistication and perspective-taking through responses to hypothetical dilemmas. Social domain theory measures harm perception and rule contingency understanding through judgments about transgressions. Competence emerges at different ages: conventional reasoning in adolescence versus moral-conventional distinction by 30 months.

Neither approach alone explains moral development completely. Reasoning without emotional understanding produces empty principles—knowing what should be done without motivation to act. Intuition without reasoning cannot handle complex moral dilemmas requiring principled resolution of competing values.

The critical missing element is emotional binding. A child can know hitting is wrong (moral appraisal), articulate principled justifications (moral reasoning), yet still hit when angry (absent emotional binding). True moral development requires all three: intuitive appraisal, cognitive reasoning, AND the emotional transformation that makes moral violations feel personally aversive rather than merely externally punished. This integration typically develops through childhood, explaining the gap between early moral knowledge and later behavioral compliance.

VIM FUNCTION
The VIM is a cognitive mechanism that, when activated by non-verbal distress cues (sad faces, tears, fearful expressions), initiates a withdrawal response that terminates aggressive behavior. This creates a negative reinforcement loop where actions causing distress become progressively less likely.
PSYCHOPATHY CONNECTION

MORAL-CONVENTIONAL IMPACT

Key distinction: VIM is specifically an INHIBITORY mechanism triggered by distress cues, not general empathy. Assess both recognition accuracy (can they identify the emotion?) and response modulation (does identifying it change behavior?) separately. Blair later abandoned pure VIM theory because some psychopathic individuals CAN experience empathy under certain conditions.

Full Model Answer (~270 words)

James Blair's Violence Inhibition Mechanism (VIM) represents an attempt to explain moral development through psychopathy research. The VIM is a cognitive mechanism that, when activated by non-verbal distress communications (sad facial expressions, tears, fearful expressions), initiates a withdrawal response that terminates ongoing aggressive behavior.

In typically developing children, VIM creates a negative reinforcement loop: actions causing distress become progressively less likely through emotional conditioning. When a child sees their victim's tears, VIM activates withdrawal, and the association between the action and aversive distress cues strengthens with each repetition, effectively teaching moral behavior through emotional rather than cognitive mechanisms.

Blair proposed individuals with psychopathic traits fail to form associations between their behavior and socially-relevant aversive stimuli. Research shows children with callous-unemotional traits demonstrate selective impairment in recognizing fear while recognizing happy and angry faces normally. Since VIM specifically requires distress cue recognition (fear, sadness) for activation, this selective deficit prevents the inhibitory mechanism from functioning.

The consequence for moral development is reduced moral-conventional discrimination. Children with impaired VIM rate moral violations (hitting, stealing) as less serious rather than rating conventional violations (rule-breaking) as more serious. Since moral transgressions are defined by inherent harm to victims, absent distress recognition undermines the harm signal that distinguishes moral from conventional—everything becomes merely rule violation rather than genuine harm recognition.

THE TWO FORCES
Darwin's model describes two competing forces: social instincts that maintain steady affiliative pressure toward group cohesion, and appetites that spike intensely but briefly to satisfy immediate needs (food, aggression, sex).

When appetite overwhelms social instinct momentarily—leading to antisocial behavior—the subsequent return of social instinct dominance creates what Darwin termed "dissatisfaction." This mismatch between past appetitive behavior and current social instinct produces guilt or remorse.

Exam Insight

The key insight is the TEMPORAL sequence: appetite spikes and fades quickly, but social instinct persists. After the appetite fades, the constant social instinct creates the mismatch that generates guilt. Young children feel appetite satisfaction but haven't yet developed the emotional binding that produces remorse—explaining Happy Victimizer responses.

Full Model Answer (~260 words)

The sentimentalist tradition, rooted in Hume and elaborated by Darwin, proposes that moral behavior emerges from the tension between constant social instincts and sudden appetitive drives. Darwin's model describes two competing forces: social instincts maintain steady affiliative pressure toward group cohesion, while appetites spike intensely but briefly to satisfy immediate needs.

The dynamics create moral emotions through temporal mismatch. When an appetite (aggression, greed) overwhelms the social instinct momentarily—leading to antisocial behavior—the appetite quickly fades while the social instinct persists. The subsequent return of social instinct dominance creates what Darwin termed "dissatisfaction"—a discrepancy between "what I did" (appetitive action) and "what I feel now" (affiliative pressure). This mismatch is experienced as guilt or remorse.

This emotional discomfort serves as the teaching signal that gradually strengthens inhibitory control over appetitive impulses. With repeated experiences, the anticipation of post-transgression dissatisfaction begins to counterbalance appetitive urges before action occurs, progressively building moral self-regulation.

Young children experience the appetite-instinct conflict but haven't yet learned the emotional binding. They feel appetite satisfaction (getting the toy) without the subsequent social-instinct-driven remorse. This explains Happy Victimizer responses: children correctly identify transgression as wrong (cognitive) but attribute positive emotions to the transgressor (absent emotional binding). Moral development involves learning to anticipate and internalize the dissatisfaction signal.

Argument Scaffold

THE PARADIGM
Children observe a stealing scenario where the thief successfully obtains what they wanted. They are asked: (1) Was stealing wrong? (2) How does the thief feel? 4-year-olds correctly identify theft as wrong but predict the thief feels "good" because they got what they wanted.
THE SHIFT
By age 8, children predict guilt or shame despite the material gain. This reveals that moral knowledge develops before emotional binding. Children know rules before feeling bound by them, suggesting cognitive and emotional moral development follow separate trajectories that converge around age 7-8.
BEHAVIORAL IMPLICATIONS
Meta-analysis shows stronger moral emotion attribution predicts less antisocial behavior (r = -0.35) and more prosocial behavior (r = +0.20). The asymmetry suggests moral emotions primarily function as behavioral inhibitors rather than prosocial motivators.
Exam Insight

The HV response at age 4 is NOT psychopathy—it's developmental immaturity. Young children are HONESTLY reporting motivational satisfaction without emotional binding. The critical clinical flag is HV responses AFTER age 8, which may indicate impaired moral-emotional integration. Also: priming with moral evaluation increases guilt attribution; priming with desire increases happiness attribution.

Full Model Answer (~280 words)

The Happy Victimizer paradigm (Nunner-Winkler & Sodian) exposes a profound developmental discontinuity in moral development. Children observe a scenario where a transgressor (thief) successfully obtains what they wanted. They are asked whether the action was wrong AND how the transgressor feels.

4-year-olds correctly identify theft as wrong but predict the thief feels "good" or "happy" because they obtained what they wanted. By age 8, children predict guilt or shame despite the material gain. This paradigm brilliantly separates cognitive understanding (knowing the rule) from emotional attribution (predicting the transgressor's feelings).

The developmental shift reveals that moral knowledge develops before emotional binding. Children know rules before feeling bound by them, suggesting cognitive and emotional moral development follow separate trajectories that converge around age 7-8. The young child's "happy" response is honest motivational reporting—goal achievement DOES feel good—without the subsequent guilt that makes moral violations personally aversive.

Malti & Krettenauer's meta-analysis (42 studies, 8000+ participants) demonstrates behavioral consequences: stronger moral emotion attribution correlates r = -0.35 with antisocial behavior and r = +0.20 with prosocial behavior. The stronger antisocial correlation suggests moral emotions primarily function as behavioral inhibitors—they stop harm more effectively than they compel helping. Children who develop a moral self-concept where transgressions feel personally aversive show dramatically different behavioral trajectories.

Explain how children's weighting of intention versus outcome in moral judgments changes with development. What does Heyman & Gelman's water hose study reveal?

L09-LO2 HIGH YIELD EXAM PREDICTED

Active Recall Prompt

Before revealing: (1) What's the young child's weighting ratio? (2) What's the adult ratio? (3) Can young children understand intentions at all?

Heyman & Gelman Water Hose Study

P+/O+ Condition Good intention, good outcome 8/10 nice
P+/O- Condition Good intention, bad outcome 3/10 nice
P-/O+ Condition Bad intention, good outcome 7/10 nice
P-/O- Condition Bad intention, bad outcome 2/10 nice

Argument Scaffold

THE FINDING
Young children recognize both intention and outcome but systematically overweight outcome severity. While adults judge primarily on intention with outcome as minor modifier, young children treat outcome as primary with intention as the modifier.
HEYMAN & GELMAN ANALYSIS
The study crossed intention (good/bad) with outcome (good/bad). Kindergarteners show outcome effect = 5.0 units but intention effect = 1.0 unit—a 5:1 outcome-dominant ratio. Adults show the reverse: intention effect = 4.0, outcome effect = 1.0—a 4:1 intention-dominant ratio.
DEVELOPMENTAL EXPLANATION
Young children's limited theory of mind makes outcomes more reliable than inferred intentions. Outcomes are directly observable; intentions must be inferred from context. This may be evolutionarily adaptive: avoiding outcome-causing individuals regardless of intention serves as a safety heuristic.
Exam Insight

Critical point: young children DO understand intentions—they just WEIGHT them less. The classic broken cups paradigm (1 cup intentionally vs 15 cups accidentally) initially suggested children IGNORE intention, but factorial designs reveal they USE both dimensions with different WEIGHTS. Don't say children "can't understand intention"—say they "weight outcome more heavily."

Full Model Answer (~270 words)

Young children's moral judgments reveal a fascinating developmental pattern where they recognize both intention and outcome but systematically overweight outcome severity. The critical insight is that children DO understand intentions—they just weight them differently than adults.

Heyman & Gelman's water hose study crossed intention (positive/negative) with outcome (positive/negative). Kindergarteners rated "niceness" as: P+/O+ = 8/10, P+/O- = 3/10, P-/O+ = 7/10, P-/O- = 2/10. Computing weights: outcome effect = [(8+7)/2 - (3+2)/2] = 5.0 units; intention effect = [(8+3)/2 - (7+2)/2] = 1.0 unit. This yields a 5:1 outcome-dominant ratio.

Adults show the reverse pattern: intention effect = 4.0, outcome effect = 1.0, yielding a 4:1 intention-dominant ratio. What changes developmentally is not the recognition of both factors but their relative weighting (β > α at age 5; α > β by adulthood).

This outcome-weighting may be developmentally adaptive. Young children's limited theory of mind makes outcomes more reliable than inferred intentions. Outcomes are directly observable; intentions must be inferred from context, requiring mental state reasoning that develops gradually. Evolutionarily, avoiding outcome-causing individuals regardless of intention serves as a safety heuristic when intention-reading abilities are immature.

The lecture notes that cancel culture mirrors kindergarten moral reasoning by emphasizing outcome (who was hurt) over intention (what was meant), suggesting cultural regression in moral judgment under social pressure.
