Method

Crain and Nakayama (1987) found zero structural dependence errors across 600+ questions from 3-5yo, demonstrating children never attempt the simpler linear rule—a "parade case" for innate grammatical constraints.

Method
Same paradigm BUT tested different auxiliaries. Compared "is" vs "can" questions.
Finding
SD errors do occur with "can" (7%). "Who smoking" TP≈0; "who smoke" is grammatical bigram.
→ COUNTER: Word-specific variation contradicts universal constraint. SD is emergent from distributional patterns.
Ambridge et al. (2008) found 7% errors with "can" vs 0% with "is"—word-specific variation explained by transitional probabilities contradicts claims of universal innate constraint.
VERDICT: Evidence favors statistical emergence
Nativist: Zero "is" errors is striking—some errors expected if purely statistical.
Constructionist: 7% "can" errors is decisive—universal constraint = NO word-specific variation.
Resolution: SD emerges from high-quality statistical learning. Zero "is" errors because TP=0 makes error implausible.
While Crain's zero errors suggest innate constraints, Ambridge's word-specific variation (7% with "can") indicates SD emerges from statistics—errors occur where TPs support them.

Gertner, Fisher & Eisengart (2006)
21-month-olds succeed
Method
Preferential looking. Warm-up: familiar transitives. Test: novel verb "gorping" in transitive frame.
Finding
21mo looked longer at agent-patient scene—mapped syntax to semantics without knowing verb.
→ POINT: Verb-GENERAL abstract syntax by 21mo. Production conservatism masks early competence.
Fisher (2006) showed 21mo interpret novel verbs using abstract transitive syntax, looking longer at agent-patient scenes.
Tomasello & Brooks (1998)
2;0: 19% → 2;6: 44% → 4yr: 90%
Method
Train novel verb in INTRANSITIVE ("sock tamming"). Test: elicit TRANSITIVE ("tam the sock").
Finding
Only 19% of 2yo extended. Age-graded trajectory, not sudden.
→ COUNTER: Gradual trajectory = gradual abstraction. If abstract, why can't 2yo extend?
Tomasello (1998) found only 19% of 2yo extend novel verbs (rising to 90% by 4yr)—gradual trajectory supports verb islands.
VERDICT: Comprehension vs production dissociation—but see Dittmar
Nativist: Comprehension reveals competence; production masks it.
Constructionist: If truly abstract, production should show SOME success.
Key test: Does Fisher's success depend on priming? → YES (Dittmar).
The Fisher-Tomasello contrast suggests either competence-performance dissociation or method-dependent findings—evaluated by Dittmar.

Priming Confound

Does Fisher's "early abstraction" require recent exemplar priming?
Nativist Position
Constructionist Counter
Synthesis / Verdict
Structural Priming
Recent exposure increases likelihood of using that structure.
Exemplar-Based Retrieval
Success reflects similar recent examples, not abstract schema.
Fisher vs Dittmar: The Critical Control Condition
Fisher (2006): Original Design
Dittmar (2008): Critical Control
What This Means
Fisher et al. (2006) — Original
+ transitive warm-up = SUCCESS
Method
Warm-up: transitive frames with same nouns ("frog washing bear"). Test: novel verb "gorping."
Claim
21mo have abstract transitive syntax. Warm-up activates pre-existing competence.
→ CLAIM: Abstract syntax exists; warm-up merely activates it, doesn't CREATE it.
Fisher (2006) argued warm-up trials activated pre-existing abstract transitive knowledge in 21mo.
Dittmar et al. (2008) — Replication
− transitive warm-up = FAILURE
Method
SAME paradigm but generic warm-ups ("This is called washing"). Identical test trials.
Finding
21mo FAILED without transitive priming. No agent-patient preference.
→ DECISIVE: Fisher's "abstraction" requires exemplar priming. Knowledge is ITEM-SPECIFIC.
Dittmar (2008) showed Fisher's result disappears without transitive warm-ups—"abstract" syntax is actually exemplar-based retrieval.
VERDICT: Strong evidence against early abstraction
Constructionist wins: If truly abstract, should manifest WITHOUT priming.
Nativist response: Generic warm-ups may have confused children.
Resolution: Priming-dependence is signature of exemplar-based, not abstract knowledge.
Dittmar's priming-dependency finding provides strong evidence against early abstraction—truly abstract syntax shouldn't require recent exemplar exposure.
