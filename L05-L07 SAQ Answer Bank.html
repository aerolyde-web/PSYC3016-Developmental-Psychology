Q1: "To what extent do great apes possess Theory of Mind? Argue with reference to empirical evidence."
ARGUE

Opening Thesis (~40 words)

Great apes possess implicit but not explicit Theory of Mind. They can automatically track others' mental states but cannot use this knowledge for deliberate reasoning—a dissociation revealing the two-pathway architecture of social cognition.

Deploy 3 Findings (~200 words total)

F1: IMPLICIT EXISTS 70%
Krupenye et al. (2016)
Apes anticipate where agent will search based on false belief
"Krupenye et al. (2016) demonstrated that great apes anticipate an agent's false-belief-based reach location 70% of the time via eye-tracking, showing implicit ToM is phylogenetically ancient."
F2: EXPLICIT MISSING 0% behavioral
Call & Tomasello (2008)
Apes cannot use FB knowledge for strategic deception
"However, Call and Tomasello's (2008) review found 0% success on behavioral false belief tasks—apes cannot translate implicit tracking into deliberate action planning, unlike 4-year-old humans."
F3: IGNORANCE YES, FB NO 42% vs 27%
Bräuer et al. (2007)
Chimps track ignorance but not misinformation
"Bräuer et al. (2007) showed chimps preferentially steal from ignorant (42%) versus knowledgeable competitors (27%), but perform at chance (33%) when competitor holds false belief—they track information access, not representational states."

Closing Synthesis (~60 words)

Thus, apes possess the evolutionarily ancient implicit ToM system enabling rapid social tracking, but lack the human-unique explicit system requiring language and executive function. The cognitive bottleneck between implicit processing and behavioral planning represents the key divergence point—what makes human social cognition qualitatively different is not awareness of minds, but deliberate reasoning about them.

Q2: "Evaluate the two-pathway hypothesis of Theory of Mind using evidence from atypical populations."
EVALUATE

Opening Thesis (~45 words)

The two-pathway hypothesis—positing independent implicit (automatic, fast) and explicit (deliberate, slow) ToM systems—receives strong support from double dissociation evidence: apes show implicit-only ToM while HFASD individuals show explicit-only ToM, proving the pathways are neurally and functionally separable.

Deploy 3 Findings (~180 words total)

F1: HFASD DISSOCIATION 50% implicit / 100% explicit
Senju et al. (2009)
Autism shows intact explicit but impaired implicit ToM
"Senju et al. (2009) found HFASD adults performed at chance (50%) on implicit gaze-anticipation tasks but achieved 100% on explicit verbal FB questions—demonstrating compensatory top-down reasoning cannot replace the automatic system."
F2: PROCESSING SPEED 150-300ms vs 2000-3000ms
Implicit vs Explicit Timeline
Pathways operate on different timescales
"The implicit pathway operates in 150-300ms (automatic gaze shifts), while explicit reasoning requires 2000-3000ms—natural conversation demands implicit speed, explaining why HFASD individuals struggle socially despite intact FB knowledge."
F3: MIRROR IMAGE Apes: +/- | HFASD: -/+
Double Dissociation Logic
Opposite patterns prove independence
"The double dissociation—apes (implicit+/explicit-) mirroring HFASD (implicit-/explicit+)—proves these are independent systems, not a single developmental continuum. Damage to one leaves the other intact."

Closing Evaluation (~55 words)

The two-pathway hypothesis successfully explains why explicit FB knowledge fails to predict real-world social competence. Limitation: The model may oversimplify—typical development likely involves integration between pathways, and the implicit/explicit boundary may be graded rather than categorical. However, the dissociation evidence remains the model's strongest empirical support.

Evaluate Structure

  1. State the hypothesis clearly
  2. Present supporting evidence (2 findings)
  3. Present confirming/converging evidence (1 finding)
  4. Acknowledge limitation + conclude on balance
Q3: "Compare affective and cognitive empathy with reference to their developmental and evolutionary origins."
COMPARE

Opening Framework (~40 words)

Affective empathy (emotional contagion) and cognitive empathy (perspective-taking) differ in mechanism, developmental trajectory, and evolutionary history—yet both are shared with great apes, challenging assumptions that prosocial behavior requires human-unique capacities.

Deploy 3 Findings (~190 words total)

F1: AFFECTIVE = IN-GROUP 15× stronger
Campbell & de Waal (2011)
Contagious yawning shows social modulation
"Campbell and de Waal (2011) showed chimps yawn 15× more to familiar versus unfamiliar individuals—affective empathy is automatic but socially gated, requiring perceived safety/affiliation. This in-group bias is identical in humans."
F2: COGNITIVE = GOAL-DIRECTED 74% helping
Warneken & Tomasello (2006)
Altruistic helping requires understanding goals, not sharing emotions
"Warneken and Tomasello (2006) found 18-month-olds help adults 74% of the time with no reward—and crucially, chimps show identical rates (40-60%). Cognitive empathy (understanding needs) emerged before explicit ToM and is phylogenetically conserved."
F3: DEVELOPMENTAL PARADOX 18mo help → 24mo self
Developmental Sequence
Helping others precedes self-recognition
"Infants help others (18mo) before recognizing themselves in mirrors (24mo)—the intuitive 'building block' sequence is reversed. This suggests cognitive empathy doesn't require explicit self-concept, challenging traditional theories."

Closing Comparison (~50 words)

Affective: automatic, emotion-matching, in-group biased, present from infancy. Cognitive: goal-directed, response-selecting, stranger-capable, emerges 18mo. Both are shared with apes, but only humans integrate them with explicit ToM for strategic prosociality. The distinction maps onto the implicit/explicit ToM architecture.

  2. Finding 1: Illustrate Dimension A (affective)
  3. Finding 2: Illustrate Dimension B (cognitive)
  4. Finding 3: Show interaction/paradox
  5. Synthesize: What does comparison reveal?
Q4: "Debate whether infants possess 'true' Theory of Mind, using evidence from implicit false belief paradigms."
DEBATE

Opening Position (~45 words)

Whether infant implicit FB responses constitute 'true' ToM depends on definitional criteria. If ToM means tracking mental states that differ from reality, infants possess it by 15 months. If ToM requires conscious metarepresentation, only 4-year-olds qualify. The debate reveals conceptual ambiguity, not empirical dispute.

Deploy 3 Findings (~180 words total)

F1: PRO - INFANTS TRACK FB 20s vs 11.5s
Onishi & Baillargeon (2005)
15-month-olds show FB-congruent looking times
"Onishi and Baillargeon (2005) found 15-month-olds looked significantly longer (20s vs 11.5s) when agents searched inconsistently with their false belief—suggesting implicit tracking of others' representational states far earlier than verbal tasks indicate."
F2: CON - NO BEHAVIORAL USE 15%→85% (3→4yr)
Sally-Anne Task
Explicit FB shows sharp developmental transition
"Classic Sally-Anne performance jumps from 15% to 85% between ages 3-4—if infants had 'true' ToM, why can't 3-year-olds use it? The gap suggests implicit responses may reflect simpler tracking mechanisms (behavior prediction) rather than belief attribution."
F3: RESOLUTION r = 0.66
Carlson & Moses (2001)
EF mediates implicit→explicit transition
"Carlson and Moses (2001) showed EF correlates r=0.66 with FB performance—infants may possess the representational capacity but lack executive resources to deploy it explicitly. The transition is about expression, not emergence."

Closing Resolution (~50 words)

The debate is partly terminological. Infants possess implicit ToM (automatic mental state tracking), which is continuous with ape cognition. Explicit ToM (conscious metarepresentation) emerges at 4 years and is human-unique. Both are 'real' ToM—they're different systems, not developmental stages of one capacity.

Debate Structure

  1. Acknowledge the debate's structure (what's contested?)
  2. Present PRO position with evidence
  3. Present CON position with evidence
  4. Resolve with synthesis/reframing finding
  5. Take a nuanced final position
