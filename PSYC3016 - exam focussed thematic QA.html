SECTION A: COGNITIVE DEVELOPMENT

Theme 1: Nature vs Nurture Dialectic PRIORITY 1 SEC A

The Three Positions

Position Core Claim Key Figures Modern Status
Nativism Innate knowledge/abilities present at birth; development = maturation Plato, Descartes, Chomsky, Spelke Core Knowledge Theory
Empiricism Blank slate; all knowledge from experience Locke, Watson, Skinner Statistical Learning
Interactionism G×E interaction; neither alone sufficient Piaget, Gottlieb, Meaney Dominant modern view

Why This Debate Matters

  • Intervention timing: If nativist → early windows critical; if empiricist → plasticity throughout
  • Responsibility attribution: Nature = determinism concerns; Nurture = blame concerns
  • Educational implications: Nature = differentiated curricula; Nurture = equal opportunity focus
EXAM TIP: Modern consensus is interactionist. Questions often ask you to evaluate both extremes and synthesize. Never argue purely for one side.

Theme 2: Heritability & Behavior Genetics PRIORITY 1 SEC A

Core Formulas

Phenotype Equation: P = G + E
(Phenotype = Genotype + Environment)

Falconer's Formula: h² = 2(rMZ - rDZ)
(Heritability = 2 × difference in twin correlations)

ACE Model: Variance = A + C + E
A = Additive genetic | C = Common/Shared environment | E = Unique environment

Critical Misunderstandings (EXAM GOLD)

Heritability IS Heritability IS NOT
Population statistic (variance explanation) Individual genetic destiny
Context-dependent (changes across environments) Fixed biological constant
About variation in population About proportion of individual's trait due to genes
High h² possible with strong environmental effects Indicator that environment doesn't matter
Turkheimer et al. (2003)
Finding: IQ heritability varies by SES
Low SES: h² ≈ 10%
High SES: h² ≈ 70%
Implication: Genes express differently across environments; poverty suppresses genetic potential
Height Example
Heritability: ~90% in Western populations
Yet: Average height increased 10cm over 100 years
Lesson: High heritability ≠ unchangeable; environment (nutrition) drove secular trend

Twin Study Logic

Natural Experiment: MZ twins share 100% genes; DZ share ~50%
Assumption: Equal Environment Assumption (EEA) - environments equally similar for both types
Critique: EEA often violated - MZ twins treated more similarly, share more environments
EXAM TRAP: "High heritability means genes determine the trait" - FALSE. High h² just means genetic differences explain variation IN THAT POPULATION. The trait itself might be highly modifiable.

SAQ Template: Heritability Interpretation

Opening: "Heritability (h²) quantifies the proportion of phenotypic variance attributable to genetic variance within a specific population. Critically, h² is NOT a fixed property of traits but varies across environments..."

Evidence 1: Turkheimer (2003) - SES moderation of IQ heritability
Evidence 2: Secular height trends despite 90% heritability
Evidence 3: Twin study methodology + EEA critique

Conclusion: "Thus, heritability statistics inform population-level variance decomposition but cannot predict individual outcomes or preclude environmental intervention."

Theme 3: Gene × Environment Interactions PRIORITY 1 SEC A

Key Distinction: Moderation vs Mediation

Moderation (G×E) Mediation
Gene CHANGES the effect of environment Variable explains HOW effect occurs
Different slopes for different genotypes Causal pathway mechanism
"The effect depends on..." "The effect works through..."
Caspi et al. (2002) - MAOA "Warrior Gene"
Design: Longitudinal; maltreated boys tracked to adulthood
Finding: Low MAOA + maltreatment → 85% antisocial outcomes
Low MAOA + no maltreatment → No elevated risk
G×E Pattern: Gene = vulnerability factor ONLY in adverse environment
Critique: Replication inconsistent; publication bias concerns; MAOA effect sizes vary widely
Caspi et al. (2003) - 5-HTTLPR & Depression
Finding: Short allele + stressful life events → increased depression
Major Critique: Risch et al. (2009) meta-analysis FAILED to replicate
Lesson: G×E claims require robust replication; early excitement often premature
Conduct Problems Prevention Research Group - Fast Track RCT
Design: 10-year intervention for high-risk children
G×E Finding: Intervention most effective for those with high genetic risk
Implication: Genetic vulnerability can mark intervention responsiveness, not just risk
Quebec Ice Storm Study (King & Bhaumik)
Natural Experiment: Prenatal stress exposure (1998 ice storm)
Finding: Stress × timing × genetic factors → differential developmental outcomes
Value: Ethically impossible to manipulate; natural disaster = random assignment

Differential Susceptibility Theory

Belsky & Pluess - "Orchids vs Dandelions"
Core Idea: Some individuals more sensitive to BOTH positive AND negative environments
Orchids (High Susceptibility) Dandelions (Low Susceptibility)
Thrive in optimal environments Moderate outcomes regardless
Wilt in adverse environments Resilient to adversity
Marked by "risk" alleles (5-HTTLPR short, DRD4-7R) Less reactive genotype
Reframe: "Risk genes" → "Plasticity genes" - same gene = risk OR advantage depending on environment
Links to: Temperament, Intervention targeting, CU traits

SAQ Template: G×E Interactions

Opening: "Gene-environment interactions (G×E) occur when genetic variation moderates environmental effects on phenotypes, producing different developmental trajectories for different genotypes exposed to the same environments..."

Evidence 1: Caspi MAOA study - maltreatment interaction
Evidence 2: Fast Track RCT - intervention × genetic risk
Evidence 3: Differential susceptibility reframing (Belsky)

Critical Evaluation: Include 5-HTTLPR replication failure; note methodological challenges of detecting G×E

Conclusion: "G×E research has transformed our understanding from genetic determinism to probabilistic developmental pathways moderated by environmental context."

Theme 4: The Great Debate - Nativist vs Constructivist PRIORITY 1 SEC A

Modern Framing

Core Knowledge (Nativist) Constructivism
Spelke, Baillargeon Piaget, Karmiloff-Smith
Innate domain-specific modules Domain-general learning mechanisms
Infants have core representations Knowledge constructed through action
Continuity: same system, enriched Discontinuity: qualitative stage changes

Spelke's Core Knowledge Domains

1. Object Representation
Principles: Cohesion, Continuity, Contact
Baillargeon (1987): Drawbridge paradigm - 3.5mo infants expect solidity
2. Number
Approximate Number System (ANS)
Wynn (1992): 5mo track small number arithmetic (1+1=2 vs 1+1=1)
3. Agency
Self-propelled motion = agent
Infants attribute goals to moving entities
4. Geometry
Reorientation by geometric properties
Shared with non-human animals

Piaget's Stage Theory (Constructivist Classic)

Stage Age Key Achievement Limitation
Sensorimotor 0-2 years Object permanence No mental representation
Preoperational 2-7 years Symbolic thought Egocentrism, no conservation
Concrete Operational 7-11 years Conservation, reversibility Bound to concrete
Formal Operational 11+ years Abstract reasoning Not universal
Piaget Critiques (EXAM ESSENTIAL):
  • Underestimated infant competence - modern methods reveal earlier abilities
  • Stages too rigid - domain-specific variation, not global stages
  • Neglected social/cultural factors - Vygotsky's critique
  • Task demands confused competence/performance

SAQ Template: Nativist vs Constructivist

Opening: "The nativist-constructivist debate concerns whether cognitive development reflects maturation of innate knowledge (core knowledge theory) or active construction through experience (Piagetian constructivism)..."

Evidence for Nativism: Spelke core knowledge, Baillargeon violation of expectation
Evidence for Constructivism: Piaget stage transitions, learning-dependent change
Integration: Karmiloff-Smith's "beyond modularity" - innate constraints + constructive processes

Conclusion: "Contemporary consensus favors integration: minimal innate architecture provides starting points for domain-specific learning mechanisms to construct increasingly sophisticated representations."

Theme 5: Infant Research Methods PRIORITY 2 SEC A

Key Methods & What They Measure

Method What It Measures Key Paradigm Limitation
Habituation Discrimination, categorization Familiarize → test novel Doesn't show what infant knows, only that they distinguish
Preferential Looking Preference, discrimination Two stimuli side by side Looking ≠ understanding
Violation of Expectation (VOE) Expectations about events Possible vs impossible events Rich interpretation vs perceptual novelty
Anticipatory Looking Prediction, expectations Where will object reappear? May reflect perceptual learning, not concepts

Competence vs Performance Problem

Core Issue: Failure on task may reflect:
  • Lack of competence (don't have the knowledge)
  • Performance limitations (have knowledge but can't express it)
Example: Piaget's A-not-B error - is it lack of object permanence or motor/inhibition failure?
Solution: Use multiple methods; if looking measures show competence before action measures, suggests performance factors

Links to: Implicit vs Explicit ToM, Executive Function development

Theme 6: Executive Function as Master Predictor PRIORITY 1 SEC A

The Three Components (Miyake Model)

1. Inhibitory Control
Suppress prepotent responses
Tasks: Day-Night, Stroop, Go/No-Go
Neural: Lateral PFC, ACC
2. Working Memory
Hold/manipulate information
Tasks: Backward digit span, N-back
Neural: Dorsolateral PFC
3. Cognitive Flexibility
Switch between rules/perspectives
Tasks: DCCS, Wisconsin Card Sort
Neural: PFC-parietal networks

Why EF is "Master Predictor"

Predicts across domains:
  • Academic achievement (math, reading)
  • Theory of Mind (FB task passing)
  • Social competence
  • Health outcomes (Dunedin study)
  • Criminal behavior
Moffitt et al. (Dunedin): Childhood self-control predicts adult health, wealth, crime 40 years later
Links to: Adolescent risk-taking (Dual Systems), ToM development, CU traits

Development of EF

  • 3-5 years: Rapid EF development; DCCS transitions
  • Protracted development: PFC not fully mature until ~25 years
  • Hot vs Cool EF: Hot (emotional context) develops differently than Cool (abstract)

SAQ Template: Executive Function

Opening: "Executive functions (EF) comprise a set of top-down cognitive control processes - inhibitory control, working memory, and cognitive flexibility - that enable goal-directed behavior and are subserved by prefrontal cortex..."

Evidence 1: Miyake et al. - three-factor model with unity/diversity
Evidence 2: Dunedin study - childhood EF predicts adult outcomes
Evidence 3: EF-ToM relationship (e.g., inhibition required to suppress own knowledge)

Conclusion: "EF serves as a domain-general cognitive foundation enabling success across academic, social, and health domains."

SECTION B: SOCIAL/EMOTIONAL DEVELOPMENT

Theme 7: Self-Recognition & The Developmental Paradox PRIORITY 2 SEC B

Mirror Self-Recognition (MSR)

Rouge Test (Amsterdam, 1972; Gallup adaptation)
Method: Surreptitiously mark face → observe mirror behavior
Developmental Timeline:
  • 15-18 months: ~25% pass
  • 18-24 months: ~50% pass
  • 24 months: ~70% pass
Cross-species: Great apes, elephants, dolphins, magpies - very few species pass

The Developmental Paradox

Paradox: Infants understand OTHERS before understanding SELF
  • Joint attention to others' focus: 9-12 months
  • Understanding others' goals: ~12 months
  • Mirror self-recognition: 18-24 months
Implication: Self-knowledge may be CONSTRUCTED through social interaction, not innate
"We come to know ourselves through the eyes of others" (Mead, Cooley)

What MSR Actually Measures - Debate

Claim Counter-Claim
Self-awareness / self-concept May just be visual-proprioceptive matching
Necessary for empathy/ToM Some show empathy before MSR
Universal milestone Cultural variation (Keller studies)

Theme 8: Empathy - Affective vs Cognitive PRIORITY 2 SEC B

Critical Distinction

Affective Empathy Cognitive Empathy
Emotional resonance/sharing Understanding others' mental states
Automatic, early emerging Requires ToM, later developing
Mirror neurons, insula mPFC, TPJ
Present in infancy (contagious crying) Develops with ToM (3-5 years)
Warneken & Tomasello (2006) - Altruistic Helping
Finding: 18-month-olds spontaneously help adult who drops object/can't reach
40-60% help without instruction or reward
Key: No training, no reward offered - suggests intrinsic prosocial motivation
Control: Don't help when adult intentionally throws object
Chimp Comparison (Warneken)
Finding: Chimps also help in simple tasks BUT
Limited scope - only instrumental helping, not informational
Human-unique: Pointing to inform (declarative), helping with information

Empathy-Sympathy-Prosocial Pathway

Perception of distress → Affective sharing (empathy) → Other-oriented concern (sympathy) → Prosocial action

Alternative pathway: Empathy → Personal distress → Self-focused → Avoidance (not prosocial)
Links to: CU Traits (empathy deficit), Moral Development, ToM

Theme 9: Joint Attention & Social Referencing PRIORITY 1 SEC B

Joint Attention Development

Stage Age Behavior
Dyadic 0-6 months Face-to-face interaction, no third object
Triadic Emergence 6-9 months Gaze following begins
True Joint Attention 9-12 months Coordinated attention to object + person

Two Types of Pointing

Imperative Pointing Declarative Pointing
"I want that" "Look at that!"
Request/demand Share attention/interest
Present in apes Human-unique
~9-10 months ~11-14 months
Liszkowski et al. (2006) - Perception-Knowledge Link
Paradigm: Adult either sees or misses event; infant points
Finding: 12-month-olds point MORE when adult missed event
Implication: Infants track what others have/haven't perceived; point to INFORM
Significance: Suggests understanding of knowledge states before explicit ToM

Social Referencing

Visual Cliff + Emotional Expression
Method: Ambiguous deep side; mother shows happy vs fearful face
Finding:
  • Happy face: 74% cross
  • Fear face: 0% cross
Age: Robust by 12 months
Implication: Infants use others' emotional reactions to guide behavior in uncertain situations
EXAM TIP: Joint attention is foundational for: language learning, ToM development, social learning. Deficits associated with autism.

Theme 10: Theory of Mind - Two Pathway Model PRIORITY 1 SEC B

The Classic False Belief Task

Sally-Anne Task (Baron-Cohen, 1985)
Scenario: Sally puts marble in basket → leaves → Anne moves marble to box
Question: "Where will Sally look for her marble?"
Correct answer: Basket (where Sally BELIEVES it is, not where it actually IS)

Classic finding: ~4 years = pass; 3-year-olds typically fail
Failure could be: lack of ToM, memory demands, inhibition failure, linguistic confusion

Two Systems Model - Implicit vs Explicit

Implicit ToM (System 1) Explicit ToM (System 2)
Early emerging (~15 months) Later developing (~4 years)
Automatic, fast Deliberate, slow
Measured by looking time Measured by verbal response/action
Limited representational flexibility Full representational flexibility
May not survive signature limits tests Passes explicit reasoning tests
Onishi & Baillargeon (2005) - Implicit FB
Method: VOE paradigm with belief-consistent vs inconsistent actions
Finding: 15-month-olds look longer at belief-inconsistent outcomes
Implication: Infants track beliefs implicitly before they can report them
Southgate et al. (2007) - Anticipatory Looking
Method: Infants anticipate where agent with false belief will search
Finding: 25-month-olds look to belief-location before search
Implication: Predictive representation of false beliefs before explicit verbal tasks

Critiques of Infant ToM Claims

  • Replication concerns - some infant FB findings fail to replicate
  • Lower-level explanations - could be statistical learning, behavioral rules
  • "Signature limits" problem - if truly same system, should show same limits
  • Rich vs lean interpretation - do infants represent BELIEFS or just track behavior?

Tomasello's Shared Intentionality

Tomasello - "We-mode"
Claim: Human-unique capacity for shared goals, joint commitments
Components:
  • Joint attention (shared focus)
  • Joint intention (shared goal)
  • Joint commitment (shared obligation)
Evidence: Chimps collaborate but don't form joint commitments; children protest when partner defects
"Humans cooperate in ways qualitatively different from other apes"

SAQ Template: Theory of Mind

Opening: "Theory of mind (ToM) refers to the ability to attribute mental states - beliefs, desires, intentions - to oneself and others, understanding that these states can differ from reality and guide behavior..."

Evidence 1: Classic FB task - 4-year transition (Baron-Cohen)
Evidence 2: Implicit ToM - Onishi & Baillargeon looking time
Evidence 3: Two-systems debate - implicit vs explicit representations

Critical Evaluation: Replication concerns, rich vs lean interpretation

Conclusion: "ToM development may involve multiple systems, with early implicit sensitivity to belief-like states preceding explicit, flexible belief reasoning."

Theme 11: Moral Development PRIORITY 1 SEC B

Kohlberg's Stage Theory

Level Stage Orientation Age Range
Pre-conventional 1 Punishment avoidance Young children
2 Instrumental exchange
Conventional 3 Good boy/girl approval Adolescents/Adults
4 Law and order
Post-conventional 5 Social contract Some adults (rare)
6 Universal ethical principles

The Seven Critiques of Kohlberg (EXAM ESSENTIAL)

1. Gender Bias (Gilligan)
Based on male sample; women score lower but may use different (care-based) morality
2. Cultural Bias
Western, individualistic assumptions; collectivist cultures may prioritize different values
3. Moral Reasoning ≠ Moral Behavior
Knowing what's right doesn't guarantee doing what's right (action gap)
4. Underestimates Young Children
Infants show moral-like preferences (helper vs hinderer studies)
5. Overemphasis on Justice
Neglects care, relationships, context
6. Methodological Issues
Hypothetical dilemmas; verbal demands confound moral competence
7. Stage Assumptions Problematic
Cross-situational variability suggests no fixed stage

Moral-Conventional Distinction (Turiel)

Moral Rules Conventional Rules
Harm/fairness-based Social coordination
Authority-independent Authority-dependent
Universally wrong Context-relative
"Wrong even if no rule" "Only wrong if there's a rule"
Smetana Studies
Finding: Children as young as 3-4 distinguish moral from conventional
Method: "Would it be OK if the teacher said it was OK?"
Moral: "No" | Conventional: "Yes"
Implication: Early moral competence; not just rule-following

Violence Inhibition Mechanism (VIM) - Blair

Blair's Theory
Proposal: Innate mechanism triggered by distress cues; inhibits aggression
Deficient in: Psychopathy, CU traits
Evidence: Psychopaths don't make moral-conventional distinction; reduced autonomic response to distress
Links to: CU Traits, Empathy deficits

Happy Victimizer Phenomenon

Pattern: Young children judge transgressor as "happy" after gaining through harm
4-6 year olds: Focus on material outcome
8+ years: Begin attributing guilt/negative emotion
Interpretation: Integration of moral evaluation with emotion attribution develops
Alternative: Young children may KNOW action is wrong but focus on salient outcome

Infant Moral Cognition

Hamlin et al. (2007) - Helper vs Hinderer
Method: Shape characters helping or hindering another's goal
Finding: 6-10 month olds prefer helper
Debate: Is this moral evaluation or preference for positive outcomes/competent agents?

SAQ Template: Moral Development

Opening: "Moral development encompasses the emergence of understanding right and wrong, including moral reasoning, emotion, and behavior. Kohlberg's influential stage theory proposed invariant progression through pre-conventional, conventional, and post-conventional levels..."

Evidence 1: Kohlberg's stages + longitudinal evidence
Evidence 2: Moral-conventional distinction (Turiel/Smetana)
Evidence 3: Infant preference for helpers (Hamlin)

Critical Evaluation: Include 3-4 Kohlberg critiques

Conclusion: "Contemporary understanding recognizes early moral competencies while acknowledging that mature moral functioning integrates emotion, reasoning, and behavior in culturally situated ways."

Theme 12: CU Traits & Pathways to Conduct Disorder PRIORITY 1 SEC B

What Are CU Traits?

Callous-Unemotional Traits:
  • Callousness: Lack of empathy, disregard for others' feelings
  • Uncaring: Lack of concern about performance
  • Unemotional: Restricted emotional expression
DSM-5: "Limited Prosocial Emotions" specifier for Conduct Disorder
~30% of youth with CD have elevated CU traits

Two Pathways to Conduct Disorder

Hot CD (Low CU) Cold CD (High CU)
Reactive aggression Proactive/instrumental aggression
Emotion dysregulation Emotion deficit
High autonomic reactivity Low autonomic reactivity
Amygdala hyper-activation Amygdala hypo-activation
Better prognosis Worse prognosis
Responsive to threat-based intervention Need reward-based approaches

The Snowball Effect

Concept: Early CU traits → reduced punishment sensitivity → miss socialization cues → fail to develop conscience → increasingly antisocial trajectory
Mechanism: CU children don't respond to typical parenting (time-out, disappointment expression)
Implications: Early identification crucial; standard parenting interventions may be ineffective

Neurobiological Markers

Amygdala: Reduced response to fearful faces, distress cues
VIM deficit (Blair): Distress cues don't trigger inhibition
Autonomic: Low resting heart rate, reduced skin conductance to threat
Links to: Moral-conventional distinction (psychopaths fail), Empathy development

Intervention Implications

Low CU (Hot) High CU (Cold)
Emotion regulation training Reward-based learning
Standard behavioral intervention Novel reward-based protocols
Responsive to punishment May need different reinforcement schedules

SAQ Template: CU Traits

Opening: "Callous-unemotional (CU) traits - comprising callousness, uncaring, and unemotional features - identify a distinct subgroup of youth with conduct disorder characterized by reduced empathy, fearlessness, and poor response to punishment..."

Evidence 1: Two pathway model (Hot vs Cold CD)
Evidence 2: Neurobiological markers (amygdala, autonomic)
Evidence 3: Treatment implications - reward vs punishment-based

Conclusion: "CU traits represent an important specifier with distinct etiological pathways and treatment implications, highlighting the heterogeneity within conduct disorder."

Theme 13: Adolescent Brain Development PRIORITY 1 SEC B

The Dual Systems Model (Steinberg)

Steinberg's Imbalance Model
Core Idea: Mismatch between early-maturing reward/emotional systems and late-maturing control systems
System Brain Region Maturation Function
Socioemotional Limbic (amygdala, VS) Peaks mid-adolescence Reward seeking, emotion
Cognitive Control Prefrontal Cortex Matures ~25 years Impulse control, planning
Result: "Accelerator without brakes" - heightened sensation-seeking with immature impulse control

Peer Influence Amplification

Gardner & Steinberg (2005)
Method: Driving simulation game; alone vs with peers
Finding:
  • Adolescents take 2-3x more risks with peers present
  • Adults: No peer effect
Neural: Peer presence activates reward regions in adolescents
Implication: Real-world risk (driving, crime) elevated in peer contexts

Puberty Timing Effects (Tanner Stages)

Tanner Stages: 1-5 scale of pubertal development
Early Puberty Effects:
  • Girls: Early maturation → higher depression, eating disorders, delinquency risk
  • Boys: Early maturation → mixed effects (some social advantages but also more risk)
Maturity Gap Theory (Moffitt): Physical maturity before social privileges → frustration → delinquency

Maturity Gap Theory (Moffitt, 1993)

Moffitt's Taxonomy
Core Idea: Gap between biological maturity and social role → adolescence-limited offending
Adolescence-Limited Life-Course Persistent
Majority of offenders Small minority (~5-7%)
Peak in adolescence, desists Starts early, continues
Social mimicry of delinquent peers Neuropsychological deficits
Good prognosis Poor prognosis

Legal/Policy Implications

Roper v. Simmons (2005): US Supreme Court banned juvenile death penalty citing developmental immaturity
Arguments from developmental science:
  • Immature impulse control (PFC not developed)
  • Greater susceptibility to peer influence
  • Greater capacity for change
Links to: EF development, Forensic interviewing

SAQ Template: Adolescent Development

Opening: "Adolescence involves distinctive neurodevelopmental changes characterized by an imbalance between early-maturing socioemotional systems and late-maturing cognitive control systems (Steinberg's dual systems model)..."

Evidence 1: Dual systems model - limbic vs PFC timing
Evidence 2: Peer influence study (Gardner & Steinberg)
Evidence 3: Moffitt's taxonomy - AL vs LCP offending

Conclusion: "Understanding adolescent brain development has significant implications for education, mental health intervention, and juvenile justice policy."

Theme 14: Forensic Interviewing with Children PRIORITY 2 SEC B

NICHD Protocol (5 Phases)

Phase Purpose Key Elements
1. Introduction Build rapport, explain process Ground rules, truth/lie discussion
2. Rapport Building Practice narrative recall Non-threatening topics; "Tell me about your birthday"
3. Transition Move to substantive topic Open-ended invitation: "Tell me why you're here"
4. Free Recall Maximize child's account Open prompts: "Tell me everything"
5. Questioning Follow-up specific details Hierarchy: open → directive → option-posing → (avoid suggestive)

Question Hierarchy (Most to Least Preferred)

Type Example Quality
Open-ended "Tell me more about that" Best - most accurate
Directive "Where did this happen?" Good - focused but non-leading
Option-posing "Did X happen or Y happen?" Adequate - use sparingly
Suggestive "He hurt you, didn't he?" Avoid - contaminates

Four Principles Framework

1. Consent
Child understands purpose; voluntary participation; can refuse
2. Comfort
Child at ease; supportive but neutral; appropriate setting
3. Competence
Assess child's ability; developmental appropriateness; memory capacity
4. Reliability
Minimize contamination; avoid suggestion; document process

Why Children Are Vulnerable

  • Suggestibility: Young children more susceptible to leading questions
  • Authority compliance: Tendency to agree with adults
  • Source monitoring: Difficulty distinguishing sources of information
  • Memory: Less strategic encoding; more susceptible to misinformation
Links to: Memory development, EF (source monitoring requires inhibition)

SAQ Template: Forensic Interviewing

Opening: "Forensic interviewing of children requires specialized protocols that balance the need for accurate information with developmental considerations regarding memory, suggestibility, and authority compliance..."

Evidence 1: NICHD Protocol structure and effectiveness
Evidence 2: Question hierarchy research (open > directive > option > suggestive)
Evidence 3: Developmental vulnerabilities (suggestibility, source monitoring)

Conclusion: "Evidence-based protocols like NICHD improve accuracy while protecting children's wellbeing, representing crucial application of developmental research to legal contexts."

Cross-Lecture Synthesis Threads

Thread 1: Two-Systems Architecture HIGH YIELD

This pattern appears across multiple domains:

Domain System 1 (Fast/Implicit) System 2 (Slow/Explicit)
Theory of Mind Implicit ToM (~15mo) Explicit FB (~4yr)
Adolescent Brain Limbic (reward/emotion) PFC (control)
Moral Processing Intuitive/emotional Reasoned/deliberate
Executive Function Hot EF (emotion-laden) Cool EF (abstract)

Thread 2: Competence vs Performance HIGH YIELD

Pattern: Implicit measures reveal earlier competence than explicit tasks suggest

  • ToM: Looking time shows 15mo belief tracking; verbal FB passes at 4yr
  • Object Permanence: VOE shows earlier knowledge than Piaget's reaching tasks
  • Moral Knowledge: Infant helper preference vs explicit moral reasoning
  • Number: ANS functional early; explicit counting later
EXAM TIP: When discussing developmental transitions, always consider whether change is in competence or just performance (task demands, EF requirements).

Thread 3: Human Uniqueness Question HIGH YIELD

What makes human cognition unique? Multiple candidates:

  • Declarative pointing (informing others) - chimps do imperative only
  • Shared intentionality (Tomasello's "we-mode")
  • Cumulative culture - ratchet effect, not just social learning
  • Teaching - active pedagogy vs passive observation
  • Language - recursive syntax, displacement

Thread 4: EF as Foundational Mechanism IMPORTANT

Executive function underlies success across domains:

  • ToM: Inhibition needed to suppress own knowledge; WM holds both perspectives
  • Moral behavior: Inhibition of selfish impulse; WM for moral reasoning
  • Academic: Attention, planning, persistence
  • Adolescent risk: Immature EF + heightened reward = poor decisions
  • CU traits: EF deficits compound emotional deficits

Thread 5: Double Dissociation Pattern METHODOLOGY

Evidence for distinct systems when:

  • Population A shows Pattern X but not Pattern Y
  • Population B shows Pattern Y but not Pattern X

Examples in course:

  • CU traits: Intact cognitive empathy, impaired affective empathy (vs autism: opposite pattern)
  • Hot vs Cold CD: Different neural profiles, different treatment response
  • Implicit vs Explicit ToM: Different developmental trajectories, may dissociate in autism
