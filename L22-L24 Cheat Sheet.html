1. FOUR LEVELS OF LANGUAGE (L22)

LevelGovernsExample
PhonologySound structure & legal combos/st/ OK, /pf/ not in English
MorphologyWord-internal structure-s (plural), -ed (past), -tion (V→N)
SyntaxWord order & phrase structure"dog bites man" ≠ "man bites dog"
SemanticsMeaning & reference"dog" = canine; "bachelor" = unmarried
PragmaticsSocial use & context"Could you pass salt?" = request
  • Exam Trap: "Goed" = MORPHOLOGICAL error (NOT syntax—word order correct!). Sarcasm = PRAGMATIC (NOT semantic—literal meaning preserved).
  • Hierarchical Productivity: Constraints ENABLE infinity. ~40 phonemes + rules → infinite utterances. No constraints = noise; constraints = contrastive structure.
  • Descriptivism (our approach): Study how people DO use language, NOT how they SHOULD. "Goed" = rule learning evidence, not "error."
Distinguish morphology vs syntax: "I goed to park" = MORPHOLOGICAL overgeneralization (syntax is correct: I-verb-location). Error reveals productive rule extraction, NOT delay.

2. PERCEPTUAL NARROWING & VOT (L22)

AgePhoneme Capacity
6 moUniversal: discriminate English + Hindi + Japanese
10 moLanguage-specific: lose non-native contrasts
  • Mechanism: Adaptive specialization, NOT cognitive loss. Collapse irrelevant variance → optimize for native language.
  • Bilingual: Maintain BOTH phoneme sets—NOT delay, appropriate tuning to both statistical environments. Total vocabulary comparable across languages.
  • Vouloumanos & Werker (2004): Newborns prefer speech > acoustically complex non-speech. Recognize native prosody from womb exposure.
  • 10mo babbling: Shifts from universal → language-specific patterns (Japanese babies stop /l/, English babies stop /ð/).
Categorical Perception (Eimas 1971): 0-20ms = both /d/; 20-40ms = /d/→/t/ (BOUNDARY); 40-60ms = both /t/. Equal acoustic steps → unequal perceptual distances. From 2 days old!
Perceptual narrowing = optimization NOT regression. Adults struggle with L2 phonemes due to tuned categorical boundaries, NOT sensory loss. Bilinguals maintain both—no delay.

4. MORPHOSYNTAX DEVELOPMENT (L22)

DistinctionDefinitionExample
InflectionalNO category change-s, -ed, -ing (dog/dogs = both nouns)
DerivationalCHANGES categorydestroy(V)→destruction(N)
Wug Test (Berko 1958): "This is a wug. Two ___?" → "wugs" /wʌgz/. Productive rule + correct allomorph: /-s/ voiceless, /-z/ voiced, /-ɪz/ sibilants. Double generalization!
  • U-Shaped Learning: feet (imitate) → foots (overgeneralize) → feet (rule + exceptions). Error = EVIDENCE of rule extraction!
  • 18 mo: Two-word combos ("Daddy work")—telegraphic but preserve ORDER. 24-36 mo: Productive morphology; overgeneralize to irregulars.
Overgeneralization ("tooths", "goed") = ADVANCED cognition showing abstract rule extraction, NOT delay. Child has NOUN+-s rule, not memorized forms.

5. NATIVIST VS CONSTRUCTIONIST DEBATE (L23-24)

AspectNativist (UG)Constructionist
What's innate?Language-SPECIFIC: phrase structure, recursion, structural dependenceDomain-GENERAL: statistical learning, analogy, social cognition
Initial stateAbstract & verb-general from 18moConcrete & item-specific; abstract by 36mo
Input qualityIMPOVERISHED (poverty of stimulus)RICH (statistical patterns sufficient)
  • Poverty of Stimulus (5-step): (1) Children acquire knowledge NOWHERE in input; (2) No negative evidence; (3) Should overgeneralize→correct; (4) BUT never make certain errors (island violations); (5) ∴ constraints INNATE.
  • Constructionist 3 Claims: (1) No pre-specified categories—emerge from learning; (2) Input RICH with statistical patterns; (3) Domain-general mechanisms (same as non-language).
Nativist: Language-SPECIFIC innate constraints (UG). Constructionist: Domain-GENERAL mechanisms applied to linguistic input. Know evidence for BOTH sides!

6. STRUCTURAL DEPENDENCE: "A Parade Case" (L23-24)

TypeRuleOutput
Declarative--"The boy who is smoking is crazy"
CORRECTMove MAIN aux"Is the boy who is smoking crazy?"
*ERRORMove FIRST aux"*Is the boy who smoking is crazy?"
  • Crain & Nakayama (1987): Ages 3-5, 600+ questions → 0% SD errors. Perfect despite minimal exposure to complex embeddings in child-directed speech.
  • Nativist interpretation: Innate UG constraint—children NEVER consider linear-order rules. "Parade case of innate schematism" (Chomsky 1971).
Structural dependence: Operations reference HIERARCHICAL structure, NOT linear order. Crain & Nakayama: 0% errors = UG constraint? But see Ambridge...

7. AMBRIDGE COUNTER-EVIDENCE (L23-24)

AuxiliaryError RateWhy?
"is" questions0%"who smoking" NEVER occurs (TP = 0)
"can" questions7%"who smoke" IS grammatical elsewhere!
  • Ambridge et al. (2008): Example error: "Can the boy who smoke can drive?" SD error occurs because "who smoke" is a legal bigram (e.g., "people who smoke").
  • Challenge to UG: If SD were UNIVERSAL INNATE CONSTRAINT, should NOT show word-specific variation. 7% with "can" suggests transitional probability (statistical learning), NOT innate constraint!
Ambridge: 7% SD errors with "can" vs 0% with "is". Word-specific variation challenges universal innate constraint—suggests TP-based avoidance instead of UG.

8. STATISTICAL LEARNING - Saffran et al. (1996)

Transitional Probability: TP(B|A) = freq(AB) / freq(A)
High TP (~1.0) = WITHIN word | Low TP (~0.33) = ACROSS boundary
  • Gambell & Yang (2003): English words = 1 primary stressed syllable. Infants use stress patterns by 8mo (BAbuy, spaGHETti).
  • Method: 8-mo-olds, 2 min exposure to "bidaku-padoti-golabu..." NO pauses. bi→da = 1.0 (within); ku→pa = 0.33 (boundary).
  • Result: Infants extracted word boundaries from TP dips alone! Preferred novel non-words over "words".
  • Mintz (2003): Distributional learning—"the ___ is" frame → nouns; "you ___ it" → verbs (87% accuracy).
  • LLM Evidence: GPT acquires syntactically complex language from statistics alone—proves statistical learning CAN acquire syntax in principle.
Saffran: 8-mo-olds segment speech using TPs (high = within word, low = boundary). Domain-general statistical learning challenges poverty of stimulus claim.

9. SENSITIVE PERIODS & NSL (L23-24)

WindowIf Missed
0-7 yrNo sign exposure = permanent complex syntax deficits
Before pubertyFirst L2 after = never native morphology
  • Senghas et al. (2004) NSL: 1980s Nicaragua deaf schools. Cohort 1 (adults): ~25% componential. Cohort 2 (children): ~75% componential. Children EXCEED adult input!
  • Compositional structure: Sequential "roll, then down" vs simultaneous "rolling-down". Children generated TRUE linguistic compositionality beyond their models.
NSL Cohort 2: Children generated compositional structure BEYOND Cohort 1 adult input (25%→75%). Key evidence for innate drive to impose linguistic structure during sensitive period.

10. VERB ISLANDS: Gradual Abstraction (L23-24)

StageAgeExample
Wholly Concrete18-24mo"I kick it" (rote, no slots)
Item-Specific24-30mo"I kick [OBJECT]" (verb island)
Partially Abstract30-36mo"I [ACTION] [OBJECT]"
Fully Abstract36mo+"[SUBJ] [VERB] [OBJ]"
  • Tomasello & Brooks (1998): Teach "tamming" intransitive, elicit transitive. Age 2;0: 3/16 (19%); Age 2;6: 7/16 (44%); Age 4: success.
  • Noun vs Verb asymmetry: Children READILY generalize novel nouns but NOT verbs. Verb schemas item-specific; noun slots abstract early.
Tomasello: Only 19% of 2yo extend novel verb to transitive. If UG provides abstract syntax from start, why fail? Supports gradual abstraction via analogical comparison.

11. EARLY ABSTRACTION DEBATE (L23-24)

  • Gertner, Fisher & Eisengart (2006): 21-mo-olds, preferential looking. "The frog is gorping the bear" + novel verb → children looked LONGER at frog-as-agent scene. Evidence for abstract syntax at 21mo?
StudyConditionResult
Dittmar et al. (2008)Fisher's (transitive warm-up)SUCCESS

  • Dittmar's confound: Fisher warmed up with SAME NOUNS in transitive ("The frog is washing the bear"). When priming removed → children FAIL!

Fisher vs Dittmar: 21mo transitive comprehension depends on PRIMING (same nouns in warm-up). Without priming = fail. Challenges early abstraction claim.

16. HIGH-YIELD EXAM FACTS: Know These Cold

  • Eimas et al. 1971: 2-day-olds show categorical perception (VOT). Cross-boundary discrimination from birth.
  • Vouloumanos & Werker 2004: Newborns prefer speech > non-speech; recognize native prosody from womb.
  • Perceptual Narrowing: 6mo universal → 10mo language-specific. Adaptive specialization, NOT loss. Bilinguals maintain BOTH.
  • Berko Wug Test 1958: "wugs" /wʌgz/ = productive rule + correct allomorph selection.
  • Mutual Exclusivity: 95% of 3yo select unfamiliar object for novel word (Wilson & Katsos 2021).
  • Gambell & Yang 2003: English words = 1 primary stressed syllable. Stress patterns for segmentation by 8mo.
  • Crain & Nakayama 1987: 0% SD errors across 600+ questions (ages 3-5).
  • Ambridge 2008: 7% SD errors with "can" vs 0% with "is"—word-specific challenges UG!
  • Saffran 1996: 8-mo-olds, 2 min exposure, extract words from TP dips (1.0 vs 0.33).
  • Mintz 2003: "the ___ is" → nouns; "you ___ it" → verbs (87% accuracy). Distributional learning.
  • NSL Senghas 2004: Cohort 1 ~25%, Cohort 2 ~75% componential. Children EXCEED adult input!
  • Tomasello 1998: Novel verb transitive: 2;0 = 19% (3/16); 2;6 = 44%; 4yo = success.
  • Gertner/Fisher 2006: 21-mo-olds interpret novel verb transitively (looking time).
  • Dittmar 2008: Fisher's result FAILS without transitive priming with same nouns.
  • Singleton & Newport 2004: Child 80%+ vs parents 70%. Exceeds input quality.
  • Hudson Kam & Newport 2005: Adults match 60%; children regularize to 100%/0%.
  • LLM Evidence: GPT acquires syntax from statistics alone—proves statistical learning CAN suffice in principle.
  • Common Errors: "Goed" = morphological NOT syntactic. Narrowing = optimization NOT regression. Overgeneralization = rule learning NOT delay. SD errors show word-specific variation (TP-based).
