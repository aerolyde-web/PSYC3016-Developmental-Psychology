PSYC3016: Moral Development I - Comprehensive Study Guide

Conceptual Backbone

Moral development encompasses the cognitive and emotional processes through which children construct their understanding of right and wrong, progressing from externally-driven compliance to internally-motivated ethical reasoning. The lecture establishes a fundamental tension between Kohlberg's rationalist tradition, which views moral development as a hierarchical progression through stages of reasoning about what one should do, and social domain theory, which demonstrates that even toddlers distinguish between moral transgressions (causing harm) and conventional violations (breaking social rules). This distinction emerges remarkably early—by 30 months—suggesting that moral understanding may be more innate than Kohlberg's stage theory implies, yet the translation from understanding to consistent moral behavior remains a prolonged developmental achievement requiring the integration of self-awareness, perspective-taking, and emotional regulation across childhood and adolescence.

Learning Outcomes Mapped

The four learning outcomes structure your understanding hierarchically: LO1 requires you to articulate Kohlberg's six-stage developmental pattern from pre-conventional (punishment avoidance) through conventional (social conformity) to post-conventional (universal principles) morality. LO2 demands critical evaluation of Kohlberg's methodology, particularly his male-only sample, hypothetical dilemmas lacking ecological validity, and the assumption of hierarchical progression when evidence shows contextual variation in moral reasoning. LO3 focuses on the moral-conventional distinction, requiring you to explain how children as young as 30 months judge hitting (moral transgression) as more serious and universally wrong than not sitting on the mat (conventional violation), with moral transgressions defined by harm to others rather than rule violation. LO4 distinguishes moral reasoning (what should be done) from moral appraisal (judging right/wrong), highlighting that Kohlberg measures cognitive justification while social domain theory measures intuitive evaluation.

Section 1: Prerequisites - Self-Conscious Emotions and Moral Agency

The Emergence of Moral Prerequisites

Before moral development can begin, children must achieve self-awareness, typically emerging around 18 months as measured by the Rouge Test, where children recognize that a mark on their face in a mirror belongs to their own body rather than the reflection. This self-awareness enables moral agency—the recognition that one exists as a responsible entity whose actions have consequences. Without a concept of self, there can be no moral responsibility because responsibility requires an agent who can be held accountable. The development progresses from complete egocentricity toward increasing self-other differentiation, allowing children to understand that their actions affect distinct others who have their own experiences and feelings.

Critical Distinction: Self-conscious emotions (pride, shame, embarrassment) emerge around age 2 but initially require an audience for activation. By age 8, children can experience these emotions through internal self-appraisal without external feedback, marking the internalization of moral standards. This internalization process is crucial because it transforms external rules into internal conscience.

A 3-year-old performs a "show" for adults and displays obvious pride when applauded, but shows no pride when completing the same performance alone. This demonstrates:
  • A) Lack of self-awareness
  • B) Audience-dependent self-conscious emotions

Answer: B - At age 3, self-conscious emotions like pride require external feedback for activation. The child has self-awareness (eliminating A) but hasn't yet internalized the capacity for autonomous self-evaluation, which typically emerges around age 8.
Section 2: Kohlberg's Stage Theory - Architecture and Assumptions

The Hierarchical Model of Moral Reasoning

Kohlberg's theory posits six discrete stages across three levels of moral reasoning, measured through responses to hypothetical dilemmas in structured interviews. The fundamental assumption is that moral reasoning develops through qualitatively distinct stages that form an invariant sequence, with each stage representing a more adequate way of thinking about moral issues. Crucially, Kohlberg measures what people think should be done in moral situations, not what they would actually do or how they feel about actions—this distinction between moral reasoning and moral behavior proves central to later criticisms.

Level 1: Pre-Conventional Morality (Early-Mid Childhood)

Stage 1 - Obedience and Punishment Orientation: Moral reasoning centers on avoiding punishment as the primary determinant of right and wrong. Children at this stage employ reverse logic: if someone is punished, they must have done something wrong. The child cannot yet consider intentions or circumstances, only concrete consequences. A child believes drawing on walls is wrong solely because "I got told off for it", not because it damages property or disrespects others' spaces.

Stage 2 - Self-Interest Orientation: Reasoning shifts from punishment avoidance to personal benefit maximization. The child asks "What's in it for me?" without considering how others view their behavior. Moral decisions become transactional. "I should do homework because then I get to go to the cinema", not because homework enhances learning. This stage remains egocentric, with others' perspectives absent from moral calculation.

Pre-Conventional Decision Logic

Moral Dilemma Presented →
Stage 1 asks: "Will I be punished?" → If yes, action is wrong
Stage 2 asks: "What do I gain?" → If reward > cost, action is right
Neither stage considers: Others' feelings, societal impact, or abstract principles

Level 2: Conventional Morality (Adolescence-Adulthood)

Stage 3 - Interpersonal Accord and Conformity: For the first time, external social expectations enter moral reasoning. Often called the "good girl/good boy" stage, individuals judge rightness by what they believe others expect or approve. This marks the crucial transition from egocentric to socially-aware moral reasoning. A teenager helps an elderly person cross the road because "that's what good people do", seeking approval from an internalized social audience.

Stage 4 - Authority and Social-Order Maintaining: Moral reasoning focuses on maintaining social order through law and convention. The individual reasons "beyond the individual" to consider societal functioning. Actions are right if they uphold laws and wrong if they violate them, regardless of personal cost. A teenager reports a friend's shoplifting to authorities despite social consequences because stealing violates laws that maintain social order.

Theory of Mind Connection: The question "Would someone without theory of mind be able to develop conventional morality?" highlights that Stage 3 and 4 reasoning requires perspective-taking abilities that emerge around age 4-5. Without understanding that others have different thoughts and expectations, conventional morality remains inaccessible.

Level 3: Post-Conventional Morality (Some Adults, 10-15%)

Stage 5 - Social Contract Orientation: Laws become changeable agreements rather than fixed rules. The individual recognizes that rules can be modified by majority consensus to benefit the majority. Moral dilemmas involve weighing competing principles. Stealing food to feed a starving child might be justified if preservation of life outweighs property rights. Different opinions and beliefs are respected as legitimate even when disagreeing.

Stage 6 - Universal Ethical Principles: Considered extremely rare by Kohlberg, this stage involves self-chosen ethical principles that supersede laws and social expectations. Personal ideals guide behavior consistently across contexts. Dietrich Bonhoeffer, a pacifist pastor who attempted to assassinate Hitler, exemplifies how universal principles (stopping genocide) can override both personal beliefs (pacifism) and societal laws (against murder) when they conflict fundamentally.

Section 3: Critical Limitations - Why Kohlberg's Model Breaks Down

The Reasoning-Behavior Gap

The most fundamental criticism is that moral reasoning does not predict moral behavior. Consider the bus harassment scenario: a man punches another man who is harassing a woman. His action could stem from Stage 2 self-interest (impressing the woman), Stage 3 social approval (meeting perceived expectations), or Stage 6 universal principles (protecting the vulnerable). Identical behaviors can emerge from entirely different moral reasoning stages, while identical reasoning can produce different behaviors depending on personality factors like social anxiety, group dynamics, and situational pressures.

Gender Bias and the Care/Justice Dichotomy

Carol Gilligan's 1982 analysis revealed that Kohlberg's original sample contained only males, fundamentally skewing the model. Her research identified two distinct moral orientations: ethics of care (emphasizing relationships and contextual response) versus ethics of justice (emphasizing rules and fairness). Females more frequently employ care-based reasoning, focusing on maintaining relationships and minimizing harm through contextual consideration. Males more frequently employ justice-based reasoning, focusing on abstract principles and consistent rule application. Kohlberg's hierarchy ranks justice (Stage 4) above care (Stage 3), creating an inherent gender bias where female moral reasoning appears less developed.

Non-Hierarchical Development and Context Dependency

Longitudinal data contradicts the invariant sequence assumption, showing individuals reason at multiple stages simultaneously and shift between stages based on context. The same person might use Stage 2 reasoning for minor infractions, Stage 3 for interpersonal conflicts, and Stage 4 for legal matters. Social factors, consequence severity, and cultural context all influence which reasoning stage emerges. The graph showing overlapping stage percentages across ages demonstrates that development is not a clean progression but a gradual shift in reasoning repertoire.

Example: Context-Dependent Moral Reasoning

Scenario A (Minor): Taking office supplies home
→ Stage 2 reasoning: "Everyone does it, and I need pens"
Scenario B (Interpersonal): Friend asks you to lie about their whereabouts
→ Stage 3 reasoning: "A good friend would help"
Scenario C (Legal): Witnessing a hit-and-run accident
→ Stage 4 reasoning: "I must report this to maintain justice"
Same individual, different stages: Context overrides hierarchical progression

Methodological Criticisms

Ecological validity remains questionable because hypothetical dilemmas don't capture real-world moral decision-making pressures. Nancy Eisenberg argued that people respond differently when actually facing moral dilemmas versus imagining them. Cultural bias pervades the model, as collectivist societies value Stage 3 (interpersonal harmony) more highly than Stage 4 (abstract justice), challenging the universal hierarchy assumption. Articulation demands disadvantage young children who may understand moral distinctions but cannot verbally explain their reasoning, particularly problematic for children under 7 who struggle to articulate abstract justifications.

A woman from a collectivist culture consistently prioritizes family harmony over legal requirements. According to Kohlberg she's at Stage 3, but in her culture this represents the highest moral ideal. This exemplifies which criticism?
  • A) Gender bias in stage definitions
  • B) Cultural invalidity of hierarchical rankings
  • C) Reasoning-behavior disconnect
  • D) Articulation limitations
Answer: B - The criticism is that Kohlberg's hierarchy reflects Western individualist values where abstract justice (Stage 4) ranks above interpersonal harmony (Stage 3). Collectivist cultures may reverse this hierarchy, viewing relationship maintenance as more morally sophisticated than rule following.
Section 4: Social Domain Theory - The Moral/Conventional Distinction

Turiel's Revolutionary Discovery

Social domain theory, developed by Elliot Turiel and Judith Smetana, demonstrates that children as young as 30 months distinguish between moral and conventional transgressions, challenging Kohlberg's assumption that moral understanding develops slowly through stages. Moral transgressions involve actions that cause harm, suffering, or interpersonal tension (hitting, stealing, not sharing), while conventional transgressions violate social rules that coordinate group behavior without inherent harm (not sitting on the mat, not saying grace, improper greetings). This distinction appears so early and consistently that it suggests moral understanding may be more innate than culturally constructed.

Defining Characteristics of Each Domain

Social conventions are behavioral uniformities that coordinate interactions within social systems—they're arbitrary, alterable by consensus, and contextually bound. Their violation disrupts social coordination but causes no inherent harm. Conventions involve "coordinations at the level of social organization" where uniformities enable stable interactions by creating predictable expectations. Shaking hands when meeting someone, queuing for services, or wearing clothes in public represent conventions that could theoretically be different without moral consequence.

Moral prescriptions are perceived as unalterable by consensus, non-arbitrary, and universal across contexts. They stem from "factors intrinsic to the event" rather than external rules—the wrongness of hitting emerges from the victim's pain, not from prohibition. Moral prescriptions are "determined by factors inherent to social relationships" involving welfare, rights, and justice. Hitting causes pain regardless of rules; stealing violates fairness regardless of context. The perception of moral transgression "is not contingent on the presence of a rule" but stems from harm intrinsic to the action.

The Harm Principle: When conventional transgressions cause harm, they shift domains. Not wearing clothes is conventional, but forcing someone to remain unclothed in the Arctic causes harm, making it moral. Context determines whether rule violations remain conventional or become moral through harm causation.

Empirical Evidence from Smetana (1981)

Smetana's groundbreaking study tested children aged 30-57 months (2.5 to 4.75 years) on their ability to distinguish moral from conventional transgressions. Results showed no age differences—even the youngest children made consistent distinctions. Children judged moral transgressions as: (1) more wrong in the absence of rules than conventional transgressions, (2) more serious and deserving of punishment, and (3) less contextually bound—wrong at home AND at school, while conventional transgressions were wrong only in specific contexts.

The Development Paradox

The early emergence of moral-conventional distinction creates a paradox: if 2.5-year-olds understand that hitting is inherently wrong, why do they continue hitting? This gap between moral understanding and moral behavior suggests that knowing right from wrong differs fundamentally from consistently choosing right. The disconnect likely involves immature impulse control, emotional regulation, and the gradual internalization process whereby external moral understanding becomes internal behavioral control. This paradox challenges both rationalist (Kohlberg) and social domain approaches by highlighting that moral knowledge alone doesn't produce moral behavior.

A 3-year-old says hitting is wrong "everywhere" but not putting toys away is only wrong "at school." The child then hits their sibling during an argument. This pattern demonstrates:
  • A) Failure to understand moral-conventional distinction
  • B) Understanding distinction but lacking behavioral control
  • C) Context-dependent moral reasoning
  • D) Pre-conventional moral development
Answer: B - The child correctly identifies hitting as universally wrong (moral) versus contextually wrong toy storage (conventional), showing understanding of the distinction. However, understanding doesn't guarantee behavioral compliance, especially when emotional regulation and impulse control remain immature.
Section 5: Integration - Reasoning vs. Appraisal

Two Complementary Approaches to Moral Development

The lecture establishes a fundamental distinction between moral reasoning (Kohlberg's cognitive approach asking what should be done) and moral appraisal (social domain theory's evaluative approach asking what is right/wrong). These represent the rationalist tradition emphasizing cognitive development through stages versus the sentimentalist tradition emphasizing intuitive moral understanding based on harm perception. Neither approach alone explains moral development completely—reasoning without emotional understanding produces empty principles, while intuition without reasoning cannot handle complex moral dilemmas.

Developmental Timeline Synthesis

Integrating both theories reveals a complex developmental trajectory: 18 months brings self-awareness enabling moral agency. By 30 months, children distinguish moral from conventional transgressions despite limited articulation ability. During early childhood (3-7 years), pre-conventional reasoning dominates (punishment avoidance, self-interest) while moral-conventional distinction remains stable. Middle childhood (7-11) sees emerging conventional reasoning as theory of mind enables perspective-taking. Adolescence brings formal operational thought enabling abstract moral principles, though most remain at conventional levels. Adulthood occasionally yields post-conventional reasoning (10-15%) while moral-conventional distinction persists unchanged from toddlerhood.

Critical Exam Distinctions

Moral reasoning ≠ Moral behavior ≠ Moral appraisal. Reasoning involves cognitive justification for what should be done. Behavior involves actual actions taken. Appraisal involves evaluative judgments of right/wrong. The same behavior (helping someone) can stem from different reasoning stages (self-interest, social approval, universal principles) and identical reasoning can produce different behaviors based on personality, situation, and social factors.

Common Exam Errors to Avoid:
1. Confusing what Kohlberg measures (reasoning about "should") with behavior or feelings
2. Assuming hierarchical stages are fixed rather than context-dependent
3. Missing that 30-month-olds distinguish moral/conventional despite being "pre-conventional"
4. Conflating Gilligan's care/justice distinction with moral/conventional distinction
5. Believing Stage 6 is common (only ~1% of adults) or that everyone thinks they're Stage 5-6
A researcher finds that children judge "pushing someone off a swing" as wrong even if teachers allowed it, but "wearing shoes indoors" as okay if teachers allowed it. This finding:
  • A) Supports Kohlberg's pre-conventional stage
  • B) Demonstrates moral-conventional distinction
  • C) Shows Stage 3 reasoning
  • D) Indicates post-conventional thinking
Answer: B - This demonstrates social domain theory's moral-conventional distinction. Pushing (moral) remains wrong regardless of rules because it causes harm. Wearing shoes (conventional) is only wrong when rules prohibit it. This distinction appears by 30 months, well before Kohlberg's conventional stages.
