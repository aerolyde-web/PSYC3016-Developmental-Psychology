SAQ 1: Two-Pathway Theory of Mind Architecture

L05-L07 High Yield Integration Question

"Explain the two-pathway model of Theory of Mind. Describe each pathway's characteristics and use evidence from TWO different populations to demonstrate that these pathways can be dissociated."

8-10 marks

🔍 What Examiners Want

  • Clear distinction between implicit and explicit pathways
  • Specific characteristics for each (speed, brain region, emergence)
  • Evidence from TWO populations showing dissociation
  • Understanding of what "double dissociation" means

✅ Model Answer

Introduction & Framework:

The two-pathway model of Theory of Mind proposes that humans possess two distinct systems for understanding others' mental states, which operate via different neural mechanisms and developmental trajectories.

Implicit Pathway Characteristics:

The implicit pathway is supported by the TPJ and subcortical structures. It operates rapidly (150-300ms), functions automatically without conscious effort, and emerges early in development around 9 months of age. Critically, this pathway is phylogenetically ancient and shared with great apes.

Explicit Pathway Characteristics:

The explicit pathway relies on the prefrontal cortex (PFC). It operates slowly (2000-3000ms), requires conscious, effortful reasoning, and emerges later around 4 years of age. This pathway is uniquely human, dependent on language and working memory.

Evidence 1 - Great Apes (Implicit+/Explicit-):

Krupenye et al. (2017) used eye-tracking to demonstrate that great apes (chimpanzees, bonobos, orangutans) show approximately 70% anticipatory looking to where an agent with a false belief would search. However, apes show 0% success on explicit, behavioural false belief tasks requiring strategic food retrieval. This demonstrates intact implicit ToM with absent explicit ToM.

Evidence 2 - High-Functioning ASD (Implicit-/Explicit+):

Senju et al. (2009) tested high-functioning adults with ASD (mean IQ ~115). On explicit tasks like Strange Stories and verbal false belief questions, they achieved 100% accuracy. However, on implicit eye-tracking false belief tasks, they performed at chance level (~50% anticipatory looking), while neurotypical controls looked significantly above chance to the false-belief-consistent location. This demonstrates intact explicit ToM with impaired implicit ToM.

Conclusion - Double Dissociation:

Together, these findings constitute a double dissociation: apes show implicit+/explicit- while HF-ASD shows implicit-/explicit+. This pattern proves the two pathways are neurologically and functionally independent, normally integrated in typical adults but capable of operating separately. Importantly, explicit reasoning cannot fully replace implicit processing for real-time social interaction due to its slow processing speed.

🎯 Marking Criteria

2 marks Implicit pathway characteristics (speed, brain region, emergence age) 2 marks Explicit pathway characteristics (speed, brain region, emergence age) 2 marks Evidence from apes (Krupenye + 70%/0% numbers) 2 marks Evidence from HF-ASD (Senju + 100%/50% numbers) 2 marks Explicit integration: double dissociation conclusion

⚠ Common Errors to Avoid

"ASD individuals have no ToM" → HF-ASD have intact explicit ToM via compensatory PFC reasoning
"Implicit ToM emerges at 15 months" → 9 months (15mo is when looking-time FB is tested, but implicit starts earlier)
Confusing Sally-Anne as implicit measure → Sally-Anne is EXPLICIT (verbal response); Watermelon task is implicit

🚀 Upgrade to Top Marks

Add: "This explains why HF-ASD individuals report social interaction as exhausting - they must consciously compute what neurotypicals process automatically, creating cognitive load that accumulates over extended social contact."

SAQ 2: Violence Inhibition Mechanism & Psychopathy

L08-L09 High Yield Mechanism + Evidence

"Describe Blair's Violence Inhibition Mechanism (VIM) model. What evidence from children with psychopathic traits supports this model? Include at least TWO specific findings."

8 marks

🔍 What Examiners Want

  • Clear explanation of VIM mechanism (cue → threshold → withdrawal)
  • Understanding of how VIM teaches morality through emotion
  • TWO specific empirical findings with numbers
  • Awareness of VIM revision (response modulation, not pure recognition deficit)

✅ Model Answer

VIM Mechanism:

Blair's (2005) Violence Inhibition Mechanism is a sentimentalist model proposing that moral learning occurs through emotional conditioning rather than explicit reasoning. When an individual perceives distress cues (fear, sadness) from others, these signals exceed a threshold that triggers an automatic withdrawal response. Through negative reinforcement, the association between harmful actions and aversive distress signals becomes conditioned, effectively teaching the individual that harming others produces unpleasant emotional consequences.

Distress Cues → Threshold → Withdrawal Response → Negative Reinforcement (ΔR = -0.2/episode)

Evidence 1 - Selective Emotion Recognition Deficit:

Blair (2005) compared children with high versus low psychopathic traits on emotion recognition tasks. Children with high psychopathic tendencies made 2.14 times more errors recognizing fear and sadness (high PSD: 4.73 errors vs low PSD: 2.20 errors). Crucially, recognition of happiness and anger was normal. This selective deficit in processing distress cues means the VIM input signal is absent.

Evidence 2 - Impaired Moral-Conventional Distinction:

Blair (1997) found that children with high psychopathic traits showed a 33% smaller gap between moral and conventional violation ratings (high PSD: 1.23 vs low PSD: 1.84). High PSD children treated moral transgressions (hitting) more like conventional rule violations - endorsing that hitting would be acceptable if no rule prohibited it. This demonstrates that without emotional input from VIM, "rules replace feelings" as the basis for moral judgment.

Evidence 3 - Predictive Validity:

Children who failed the moral-conventional distinction task had significantly higher psychopathic trait scores (21.95) compared to those who passed (18.39), a difference of 3.56 points. This bidirectional relationship validates the emotion-morality link proposed by VIM.

Important Revision:

Later research revealed that children with psychopathic traits CAN feel empathy under certain conditions. The deficit is better characterized as a response modulation deficit rather than a pure recognition failure - they can perceive distress but fail to generate the appropriate withdrawal response. VIM therefore does not explain ALL antisocial behaviour.

🎯 Marking Criteria

2 marks Clear VIM mechanism explanation (cue → threshold → withdrawal → conditioning) 2 marks Evidence 1: Emotion recognition deficit with specific numbers 2 marks Evidence 2: M/C distinction impairment with specific numbers 2 marks Understanding of VIM revision (response modulation)

⚠ Common Errors to Avoid

"Psychopaths can't recognize any emotions" → SELECTIVE deficit for fear/sadness only; happiness/anger normal
"Psychopaths can't feel empathy at all" → CAN feel empathy under conditions; deficit is response modulation
"VIM explains all moral development" → VIM is one sentimentalist mechanism; doesn't explain all antisocial behavior

SAQ 3: Intention-Outcome Developmental Shift

L08-L09 Medium Yield Numbers Heavy

"Describe the developmental change in how children weight intention versus outcome when making moral judgments. Explain why Piaget's original methodology was problematic and how later research addressed this."

6-8 marks

🔍 What Examiners Want

  • Piaget's finding AND its methodological flaw
  • Water hose study as improved methodology
  • Specific ratios showing the reversal (5:1 → 4:1)
  • Understanding it's continuous reweighting, not categorical shift

✅ Model Answer

Piaget's Original Finding:

Piaget (1932) presented children with story pairs: John breaks 15 cups accidentally while Henry breaks 1 cup intentionally. Children aged 5-7 years judged John as "naughtier" (outcome dominance), while children aged 8-10 years judged Henry as naughtier (intention dominance). Piaget concluded that children shift from outcome-based to intention-based moral reasoning.

Methodological Problem:

However, Piaget's design was fundamentally confounded: the stories varied on BOTH intention (accidental vs deliberate) AND outcome severity (15 vs 1 cup) simultaneously. It was therefore impossible to determine whether children were responding to intention, outcome, or some combination.

Improved Methodology - Water Hose Study:

Heyman & Gelman (1998) used a 2×2 factorial design crossing intention (positive/negative) with outcome (positive/negative), allowing independent assessment of each factor's contribution.

Condition Kindergarten Adults
Good intention + Good outcome 8.0 8.5
Good intention + Bad outcome 3.0 7.5
Bad intention + Good outcome 7.0 4.0
Bad intention + Bad outcome 2.0 3.0

Key Findings:

For kindergarteners: outcome effect = 5.0, intention effect = 1.0, creating a 5:1 ratio favoring outcome. For adults: intention effect = 4.0, outcome effect = 1.0, creating a 4:1 ratio favoring intention. This represents a complete developmental reversal in the weighting of these factors.

Judgment = α × Intention + β × Outcome
Age 5: α≈0.3, β≈0.7 | Adults: α≈0.8, β≈0.2

Critical Interpretation:

Importantly, young children do consider both factors - they show sensitivity to intention even at kindergarten age. The development is better characterized as continuous reweighting of both factors rather than a categorical shift from "outcome only" to "intention only." Zelazo et al. (1996) further showed that 3-year-olds CAN use intention when task demands are reduced, suggesting a competence-performance gap mediated by executive function.

🎯 Marking Criteria

1.5 marks Piaget's finding (15 vs 1 cups, age shift) 1.5 marks Identify the confound problem 2 marks Water hose methodology (2×2 design) 2 marks Key ratios (5:1 → 4:1 reversal) 1 mark Conclusion: continuous reweighting, not categorical

⚠ Common Errors to Avoid

"Young children only use outcome" → They use BOTH but overweight outcome (intention effect = 1.0, not 0)
"Children shift at age 7-8" → It's continuous reweighting, not a sudden shift
Citing Piaget ratios → Piaget didn't calculate ratios - that's from Heyman & Gelman

SAQ 4: Happy Victimizer Paradigm

L08-L09 Medium Yield Dissociation Question

"Describe the 'happy victimizer' phenomenon. What does this pattern reveal about the relationship between moral knowledge and moral emotion in development?"

6 marks

🔍 What Examiners Want

  • Clear description of the paradigm (two questions)
  • Age-related pattern with specific numbers
  • Theoretical interpretation: knowledge precedes emotional binding
  • Understanding of "motivational honesty" in young children

✅ Model Answer

Paradigm Description:

The happy victimizer paradigm presents children with scenarios where a character commits a moral transgression to achieve a desired goal. For example, Bob steals candy from another child. Children are asked two questions: (1) "Is it wrong?" - assessing moral knowledge, and (2) "How does Bob feel?" - assessing emotion attribution to the transgressor.

Key Finding:

Nunner-Winkler & Sodian (1988) found a striking dissociation. For Question 1 (moral evaluation), children at ALL ages said "YES, it's wrong" - demonstrating intact moral knowledge. However, for Question 2 (emotion attribution), there was a dramatic developmental shift:

  • Age 4: Children attributed "happy" (+1.35 on scale) to Bob
  • Age 8: Children attributed "guilty" (-0.50 on scale) to Bob
  • Total shift = 1.85 units over development

Theoretical Interpretation:

This pattern demonstrates that moral knowledge precedes moral emotional binding. Four-year-olds display what researchers call "motivational honesty" - they accurately predict that someone who got what they wanted would feel happy, even though they KNOW the action was wrong. The emotional consequence of wrongdoing (guilt) is not yet integrated with the cognitive evaluation.

Evidence for Competing Systems:

Research on children in the transitional period (ages 5-7) reveals priming effects: when moral evaluation is made salient first, guilt attributions increase; when desire is highlighted first ("Bob REALLY wanted that candy"), happy victimizer responses increase. This suggests two competing systems (cognitive-moral and motivational-emotional) that are not yet fully integrated during this period.

Developmental Significance:

The happy victimizer pattern shows that moral development involves not just acquiring moral knowledge, but binding that knowledge to appropriate emotional responses. Full moral competence requires the integration of "knowing it's wrong" with "feeling bad about doing it."

🎯 Marking Criteria

1.5 marks Paradigm description (scenario + two questions) 2 marks Age-related pattern with numbers (+1.35 → -0.50) 1.5 marks Interpretation: knowledge precedes emotional binding 1 mark "Motivational honesty" concept

⚠ Common Errors to Avoid

"4-year-olds think stealing is okay" → They KNOW it's wrong; they just predict happy emotions
"Children are lying about the emotion" → "Motivational honesty" - genuinely predicting emotions based on goal achievement

SAQ 5: Implicit vs Explicit False Belief Understanding

L05-L07 High Yield Developmental Trajectory

"Compare implicit and explicit false belief understanding in children. When does each emerge, what methods are used to assess each, and what explains the developmental gap between them?"

8 marks

🔍 What Examiners Want

  • Clear distinction between implicit and explicit measures
  • Specific ages for each (15mo vs 4yr)
  • Named studies for each type
  • Explanation for the gap (EF, dual representation, inhibition)

✅ Model Answer

Implicit False Belief - Early Emergence:

Implicit false belief understanding emerges remarkably early. Onishi & Baillargeon (2005) demonstrated this using the watermelon task with 15-month-old infants. Using a violation-of-expectation looking time paradigm, infants watched an agent form a belief about an object's location, then the object was moved without the agent's knowledge (creating a false belief). Infants looked significantly longer (~20.0 seconds) when the agent searched where the object actually was (reality) compared to where the agent believed it to be (~11.5 seconds). This demonstrates implicit tracking of others' false beliefs.

Southgate et al. (2007) extended this using eye-tracking with 25-month-old toddlers, showing that children's first anticipatory saccade predicted the agent would search at the false-belief-consistent location, not reality.

Explicit False Belief - Later Emergence:

Explicit false belief understanding, assessed through verbal response tasks, emerges much later. The classic Sally-Anne task (Baron-Cohen et al., 1985) asks children directly where Sally will look for her marble. At age 3, only ~15% pass, while by age 4, ~80-90% succeed by correctly answering "the basket" (where Sally falsely believes it to be).

The "Eyes Know Before Mouth" Gap:

This creates a 2-year developmental gap between implicit success (15-25mo) and explicit success (4yr). Why does this gap exist?

Explanation 1 - Executive Function:

Carlson & Moses (2001) found that inhibitory control measures correlate strongly with false belief success (r ≈ 0.66). Explicit false belief requires inhibiting the prepotent reality response - children must override their knowledge of where the object actually is.

Explanation 2 - Dual Representation:

Explicit tasks require simultaneously holding two representations: reality (where the object IS) and belief (where Sally THINKS it is). This working memory demand exceeds young children's capacity.

Explanation 3 - Language Requirements:

Explicit tasks require understanding and formulating verbal responses. The language demands of comprehending questions like "Where will Sally LOOK?" and formulating appropriate answers add additional processing requirements absent in looking-time measures.

Conclusion:

The dissociation between implicit and explicit false belief demonstrates that understanding mental states and expressing that understanding are distinct competencies. Infants possess implicit ToM competence that is masked by performance limitations on explicit tasks. This aligns with the two-pathway model - implicit processing is available early but explicit, verbally-mediated ToM requires additional cognitive machinery that develops later.

🎯 Marking Criteria

2 marks Implicit FB: method (looking time) + age (15mo) + study 2 marks Explicit FB: method (verbal) + age (4yr) + study 2 marks Gap explanation: EF/inhibition (with correlation) 2 marks Additional explanations (dual representation, language) + integration

⚠ Common Errors to Avoid

"Infants don't understand false beliefs" → They show IMPLICIT understanding by 15mo
"Sally-Anne measures implicit ToM" → Sally-Anne is EXPLICIT (verbal); Watermelon task is implicit
Confusing 15mo with 9mo → 9mo = implicit ToM emerges; 15mo = looking-time FB tested; 25mo = anticipatory looking

SAQ 6: Moral-Conventional Distinction

L08-L09 High Yield Challenges Kohlberg

"Describe Turiel's moral-conventional distinction. How does evidence from young children challenge stage theories of moral development like Kohlberg's?"

6-8 marks

🔍 What Examiners Want

  • Clear definition of moral vs conventional transgressions
  • Smetana's study with the critical age finding (30mo)
  • How this challenges stage theories
  • Distinction between moral reasoning and moral appraisal

✅ Model Answer

The Moral-Conventional Distinction:

Turiel's (1983) social domain theory distinguishes between two types of transgressions:

  • Moral transgressions: Harm-based, universally wrong, unalterable by authority (e.g., hitting someone)
  • Conventional transgressions: Rule-based, context-dependent, modifiable by authority (e.g., dress code violations)

The key criterion distinguishing these domains is harm. When an action causes harm, it enters the moral domain and becomes wrong regardless of rules.

Critical Evidence - Smetana (1981):

Smetana (1981) tested children aged 30-57 months (2.5-4.75 years) on their ability to distinguish moral from conventional transgressions. The critical finding was that there were NO significant age differences within this range. Even the youngest children (30 months) reliably:

  • Rated moral violations as more serious than conventional
  • Judged moral violations as wrong everywhere, not just in their school
  • Said moral violations would be wrong even without a rule prohibiting them

Challenge to Stage Theories:

This evidence poses a significant challenge to Kohlberg's stage theory, which posits that moral understanding develops through invariant stages emerging in adolescence. If 2.5-year-olds already demonstrate sophisticated moral cognition (distinguishing universal harm from arbitrary rules), then moral understanding cannot require the complex reasoning processes Kohlberg described.

Resolving the Paradox - Two Types of Questions:

The apparent contradiction dissolves when we distinguish between:

Dimension Moral Reasoning (Kohlberg) Moral Appraisal (Turiel)
Question asked "What SHOULD be done?" "Is it right/wrong?"
Tradition Rationalist (prescriptive) Sentimentalist (evaluative)
Emerges Adolescence 30 months
Requires Verbal justification Harm-based intuition

Different questions yield different developmental trajectories. Evaluating whether something IS wrong (appraisal) emerges early and may rely on emotional/intuitive processes. Articulating what SHOULD be done and why (reasoning) emerges later and requires linguistic and logical capabilities. Both are valid aspects of moral cognition, but they develop independently.

🎯 Marking Criteria

2 marks Clear M/C distinction with characteristics 2 marks Smetana study with 30mo finding (no age differences) 2 marks How this challenges Kohlberg 2 marks Resolution: reasoning vs appraisal distinction

⚠ Common Errors to Avoid

"M/C distinction emerges at 4 years" → 30 months (2.5 years) - confusing with false belief
"Turiel disproves Kohlberg" → They ask different questions - both valid, different trajectories
"Moral = rules, conventional = no rules" → Moral = harm-based + universal; conventional = rule-based + context-dependent

SAQ 7: Critiques of Kohlberg's Theory

L08-L09 Medium Yield Multiple Critiques

"Evaluate Kohlberg's stage theory of moral development. Discuss at least THREE major critiques, providing specific evidence or examples for each."

8-10 marks

🔍 What Examiners Want

  • Brief summary of Kohlberg's model first
  • THREE distinct critiques with specific support
  • Gilligan's gender bias critique with care vs justice
  • Cultural bias with specific example
  • Reasoning-behavior gap with correlation

✅ Model Answer

Kohlberg's Model Overview:

Kohlberg (1958-1984) proposed six stages of moral reasoning across three levels: pre-conventional (Stages 1-2: punishment avoidance, self-interest), conventional (Stages 3-4: social approval, law and order), and post-conventional (Stages 5-6: social contract, universal principles). Importantly, the model measures reasoning and justification, not whether answers are "right."

Critique 1: Gender Bias (Gilligan, 1982):

Gilligan (1982) noted that Kohlberg's original sample was exclusively male. She argued the stages encode a masculine "ethic of justice" (emphasizing rules, fairness, individual rights) while devaluing a feminine "ethic of care" (emphasizing relationships, context, responsibility to others). In Kohlberg's scheme, care-based reasoning (typical of Stage 3) scores lower than justice-based reasoning (Stage 4), creating systematic gender bias. Gilligan's counter-argument: "different voice, not deficient voice" - care represents an alternative moral orientation, not a lower stage.

Critique 2: Cultural Bias (Cross-Cultural Research):

Cross-cultural studies reveal that Kohlberg's hierarchy reflects WEIRD cultural values. In collectivist cultures, the hierarchy can be reversed: interpersonal harmony (Stage 3) may be valued above abstract justice (Stage 4). For example, a Japanese businessperson prioritizing relationships and group harmony over legal technicalities would score at Stage 3 in Kohlberg's system, despite this representing sophisticated moral reasoning within their cultural framework. The stages therefore reflect Western individualism's privileging of autonomy over collectivist values.

Critique 3: Reasoning-Behavior Gap (Blasi, 1980):

Blasi's (1980) meta-analysis found only a weak correlation (r ≈ 0.30-0.40) between moral reasoning stage and actual moral behavior. This "talk is cheap" problem suggests that cognitive justification is insufficient for moral action. Knowing what one SHOULD do doesn't guarantee doing it. This critique points to the necessity of emotional and motivational factors that Kohlberg's rationalist approach neglects.

Additional Critique: Rarity of Higher Stages:

Kohlberg claimed post-conventional reasoning represents moral maturity, yet only 10-15% of adults ever reach Stage 5, and Stage 6 is virtually non-existent (~1%). If the "endpoint" of moral development is never reached by most humans, is it a valid developmental endpoint, or an idealized philosophical position?

Conclusion:

While Kohlberg's work was pioneering in establishing moral development as a serious research domain, these critiques reveal that his stages may better describe one particular (Western, male, rationalist) moral orientation rather than universal human moral development. Contemporary approaches incorporate multiple moral foundations, emotional processes, and cultural variability.

🎯 Marking Criteria

1 mark Brief accurate summary of Kohlberg's model 2-3 marks Gilligan critique with care vs justice distinction 2-3 marks Cultural bias with specific example (e.g., Japanese hierarchy reversal) 2-3 marks Reasoning-behavior gap with correlation (r ≈ 0.30-0.40) 1 mark Integration/conclusion

⚠ Common Errors to Avoid

"Kohlberg measures whether answers are right or wrong" → Measures REASONING/JUSTIFICATION, not the answer itself
"Most adults reach post-conventional stages" → Most adults are conventional (S3-4); only 10-15% reach S5
"Gilligan showed women score lower" → Gilligan argued the SCALE is biased, not that women are inferior

SAQ 8: Affective vs Cognitive Empathy

L05-L07 Medium Yield Distinction Question

"Distinguish between affective and cognitive empathy. Provide evidence for the early emergence of each and explain why this distinction is important for understanding prosocial behavior."

6-8 marks

🔍 What Examiners Want

  • Clear definitions of both types
  • Evidence for early affective empathy (contagious crying/yawning)
  • Evidence for cognitive empathy (altruistic helping)
  • Why the distinction matters (can overwhelm helping, dissociable pathways)

✅ Model Answer

Defining the Distinction:

de Waal (2008) distinguished two forms of empathy:

  • Affective empathy: Emotional resonance - automatically experiencing what another feels (mirroring anxiety, distress)
  • Cognitive empathy: Understanding what someone feels/needs and responding appropriately (e.g., smiling to calm an anxious speaker rather than mirroring their anxiety)

Evidence for Early Affective Empathy:

Contagious crying in newborn humans demonstrates the earliest form of affective empathy. Newborns cry more intensely and for longer durations when hearing other infants cry compared to neutral sounds. This represents pure emotional contagion - feeling distress in response to another's distress - without any understanding of the cause.

Campbell & de Waal (2011) demonstrated that contagious yawning in chimpanzees is socially modulated: chimps yawned ~3.1 times per session for in-group members but only ~0.3 times for out-group (15× more for friends). This shows affective empathy is "social and safety-gated", not mere motor mimicry.

Evidence for Cognitive Empathy:

Warneken & Tomasello (2006) showed that 18-month-old human toddlers engage in altruistic helping. When an adult reached toward an out-of-reach object (indicating need), toddlers helped in ~40-60% of trials. When no reaching occurred, helping dropped to baseline (~5-20%). Crucially, 74% of helping was reward-independent, demonstrating genuinely altruistic motivation. This requires cognitive empathy - understanding another's goal/need - not just emotional resonance.

Why the Distinction Matters:

First, the pathways are dissociable: one can feel another's distress (affective) without understanding what they need (cognitive), or understand their situation without feeling emotional resonance. Second, affective empathy can overwhelm helping - experiencing too much emotional distress can be paralyzing rather than motivating prosocial action (personal distress vs empathic concern). Third, cognitive empathy enables appropriate responses: a nurse who mirrors every patient's pain would be ineffective; effective care requires understanding needs while regulating one's own emotional response.

This distinction is particularly relevant for understanding ASD, where cognitive empathy (perspective-taking) may be impaired while affective empathy (emotional contagion) can be intact or even heightened.

🎯 Marking Criteria

2 marks Clear definitions of affective vs cognitive empathy 2 marks Evidence for affective (contagious crying/yawning with numbers) 2 marks Evidence for cognitive (altruistic helping with numbers) 2 marks Why distinction matters (overwhelming, dissociable, appropriate response)

⚠ Common Errors to Avoid

"Affective empathy requires understanding" → Affective = automatic resonance; cognitive = understanding
"More empathy = more helping" → Too much affective empathy can overwhelm and prevent helping
"Contagious yawning is just motor mimicry" → It's socially gated (15× more for in-group) - not automatic
