Sample Answer (~295 words)

Working memory capacity constrains infant relational learning through a load-dependent processing mechanism: when cognitive load exceeds available capacity, infants revert from relational abstraction to featural encoding.

The clearest evidence comes from Anderson et al. (2018), who showed that 3-month-olds succeed at same/different discrimination with 2 exemplar pairs but fail with 6 pairs. This demonstrates an inverted-U function: too few exemplars leads to object-specific encoding rather than relational abstraction, while too many exemplars exceeds tracking capacity. The optimal number balances sufficient variability to force abstraction against capacity limits for object tracking.

Ferry et al. (2015) extended this by showing 7-9-month-olds require 4 pairs for successful relational learning (d = 0.78), compared to only 2 pairs for younger infants. This age-related increase in optimal exemplar count directly reflects expanding WM capacity‚Äîolder infants can track more objects while simultaneously extracting relational structure.

Critically, Oakes & Cohen (1990) demonstrated that the same 6-month-olds who succeed at causal perception with simple geometric shapes fail with complex realistic toys, despite identical spatiotemporal causal structure. Object complexity consumes capacity needed for relational processing, causing reversion to featural encoding. This pattern cannot be explained by nativist module accounts‚Äîif an innate causal module existed, it should fire regardless of object complexity given identical triggering input.

Together, these findings establish that hierarchical dependency‚Äîwhere relational representations are built atop object representations‚Äîcreates capacity constraints that determine whether infants achieve relational abstraction or remain at the featural level.

Sample Answer (~285 words)

Language is necessary for abstract relational reasoning because it reifies transient perceptual relations into stable, retrievable symbolic representations that can transfer across contexts.

The decisive evidence comes from Gentner et al.'s (2013) study of Turkish deaf homesigners‚Äîindividuals who developed self-invented gestural communication systems that include nouns and actions but lack systematic spatial prepositions. On a purely nonverbal spatial matching task, homesigners showed only 45% accuracy compared to 73% for hearing children‚Äîa 28 percentage point deficit. Critically, this task required no language production, proving the deficit reflects impaired relational representation, not communication difficulty.

The cross-mapped condition provides even stronger evidence. When object features competed with relational structure (e.g., matching "on top of" while object similarities suggested a different response), homesigners collapsed to 35%‚Äîchance performance‚Äîwhile hearing children maintained 53%. This represents 90% normalized impairment, demonstrating that without linguistic labels for spatial relations, individuals cannot maintain relational representations against competing object-level information.

Gentner's two-roles framework explains this pattern. Language serves as (1) invitation‚Äîshared labels signal hidden commonality, prompting comparison‚Äîand (2) reification‚Äîconverting fleeting perceptual relations into stable symbols that persist across contexts. The homesigner deficit specifically implicates reification: these individuals can perceive spatial relations in the moment but cannot encode them as portable categories.

Without language, relations remain transient and context-bound. Language creates the stable representational format necessary for abstract reasoning‚Äîexplaining why relational cognition, though present in rudimentary form across species, achieves its full power only in linguistic humans.

Sample Answer (~280 words)

Children behave like scientists, spontaneously generating hypotheses and designing informal experiments through play to test causal structure in their environment.

Gopnik's blicket detector studies demonstrate that young children naturally engage in hypothesis testing. When presented with a novel machine that activates for certain objects but not others, children generate 5 different hypotheses within 2 minutes‚Äîsystematically testing which objects are "blickets" through intervention. They don't simply observe but actively manipulate variables, matching the logic of controlled experimentation.

Schulz and Bonawitz provide striking evidence that children's exploration is strategically directed toward resolving uncertainty. In the confounded condition‚Äîwhere a toy activated but the causal mechanism was ambiguous‚Äî65% of children continued exploring that toy rather than switching to a new one. They selectively invested exploration time where causal structure remained uncertain. In contrast, in the unconfounded condition‚Äîwhere causal mechanism was clear‚Äî62% explored the new toy instead, seeking novel information once the original puzzle was solved.

This pattern precisely mirrors scientific practice: concentrate investigation where knowledge gaps exist, then move on once the phenomenon is explained. Children don't explore randomly but allocate cognitive resources toward maximum information gain.

Lucas et al. further showed that children transfer abstract causal structure across domains. Having learned a one-cause versus two-cause distinction in one context, 4-year-olds showed 27 percentage point transfer to novel scenarios‚Äîdemonstrating they extract generalizable causal schemas, not just context-specific associations.

Together, this evidence establishes that children's play constitutes genuine scientific inquiry: hypothesis generation, strategic experimentation, and abstract knowledge transfer.

Sample Answer (~275 words)

Comparison is essential for relational learning because it enables structural alignment‚Äîthe process of mapping corresponding elements across exemplars to highlight relational commonality while downweighting object-specific differences.

Christie and Gentner (2010) demonstrated the dramatic power of comparison using the "toma" paradigm, where children had to learn that "toma" meant "same spatial relation." With solo examples, 3-year-olds achieved only 2% relational responding‚Äîessentially encoding object features ("pig-ness") rather than the abstract relation ("same-facing"). However, with simultaneous comparison of two exemplars, the same children achieved 57% relational responding‚Äîa 55 percentage point improvement.

Critically, sequential presentation yielded only 11%, despite providing the same information as simultaneous comparison. This proves that comparison's power lies not merely in seeing multiple examples but in having them simultaneously available for alignment. When examples are presented sequentially, working memory cannot fully reconstruct the first exemplar for comparison with the second, preventing structural alignment.

Remarkably, even adults benefit from comparison. Markman and Gentner found that adults shown analogous scenarios achieved 78% relational alignment‚Äîwithout comparison, they preferentially matched based on object features (matching "woman" to "woman" rather than recognizing the woman:squirrel as structurally equivalent to another relational pair).

The mechanism is structural alignment: juxtaposing exemplars with different objects but the same relation forces attention to what's COMMON (the relation) rather than what's DISTINCT (the objects). This process operates across development but is especially critical when WM capacity is limited.

Sample Answer (~295 words)

Nativist accounts, championed by Leslie (1984), argue for an innate causal module triggered by spatiotemporal cues. Leslie showed 6-month-olds distinguish causal launching (direct contact, immediate motion transfer) from non-causal events using simple geometric shapes. This early competence, he argued, reflects domain-specific innate knowledge.

However, constructivist evidence fundamentally challenges this interpretation. Oakes and Cohen (1990) presented the same 6-month-olds with identical spatiotemporal causal structure using complex realistic toys instead of simple shapes. These infants showed featural processing only‚Äîdiscriminating delays from gaps based on perceptual features rather than categorizing by causal status. If an innate causal module existed, it should fire regardless of object complexity given identical triggering input.

Cohen and Oakes (1993) extended this by showing that even 10-month-olds who succeed with complex repeated objects fail when objects vary trial-by-trial. The critical pattern‚Äî"same infant, same age, same causal structure, different cognitive load = opposite results"‚Äîcannot be explained by module maturation.

The neoconstructivist resolution proposes hierarchical construction: causal/relational representations are built atop object representations. When object-level processing consumes capacity (tracking complex or varying objects), higher-level causal abstraction becomes inaccessible. This explains systematic load-dependent variation while preserving early competence as capacity-dependent access rather than module-dependent triggering.

The constructivist account better explains the empirical pattern. The decisive evidence is within-age manipulation: identical relational structure producing opposite outcomes based solely on processing load. This demonstrates competence is present but performance varies with cognitive resources‚Äîa pattern incompatible with modular triggering.

Sample Answer (~290 words)

The competence-performance distinction is essential for understanding executive function development: children often possess cognitive competencies that fail to manifest in behavior due to processing limitations.

The DCCS paradigm provides the clearest demonstration. In the pre-switch phase, both 3- and 5-year-olds achieve 100% accuracy, proving basic attention and comprehension are intact. When asked to state the post-switch rule, 3-year-olds accurately describe it 100% of the time‚Äîthey KNOW "now we're playing the color game." Yet behaviorally, 3-year-olds achieve 0% post-switch accuracy while 5-year-olds achieve 85-95%. This dissociation between verbal knowledge and behavioral execution proves the failure is not about rule comprehension but about cognitive flexibility‚Äîthe capacity to reconfigure stimulus-response mappings when goals change.

Goswami and Brown's analogy research demonstrates the complementary pattern: competence revealed when load is reduced. 3-year-olds who typically fail analogy tasks succeed when given familiar relational content. The analogical reasoning capacity is present; only the working memory load of encoding novel relations masks it.

Additional evidence comes from interference and compensation effects. Test anxiety consumes approximately 2.5 working memory units on novel tasks, degrading performance despite unchanged competence. Conversely, elderly Go experts show no performance decline on familiar patterns despite age-related WM reduction‚Äîaccumulated knowledge compensates for capacity decline.

This distinction has profound implications. Development involves not only acquiring new competencies but also expanding the capacity to express existing ones. Apparent cognitive limitations often reflect performance constraints rather than absent knowledge, and interventions should focus on reducing processing demands rather than teaching already-possessed concepts.

Sample Answer (~285 words)

Evidence suggests pretend play and counterfactual reasoning share underlying cognitive architecture rather than being merely correlated through a common third factor like executive function.

Buchsbaum et al. provide the critical test. 4-year-olds who passed a counterfactual reasoning task ("What if block B were the zando?") showed 78% success on pretense transfer, compared to only 32% for those who failed the counterfactual task. Crucially, this correlation held controlling for executive function measures. If the relationship were merely due to both tasks requiring EF, the correlation should disappear when EF is statistically controlled‚Äîbut it persists, suggesting shared representational demands beyond general cognitive capacity.

Harris's syllogism studies demonstrate that the same logical competence that fails in standard format succeeds in imaginative framing. Children who fail "All cats bark, Rex is a cat, does Rex bark?" succeed when told "Imagine a world where all cats bark..." The pretend frame provides license to depart from reality-based reasoning, enabling counterfactual inference that children otherwise suppress.

The shared architecture is dual-model representation: both pretense and counterfactual reasoning require simultaneously maintaining "what is" (reality) and "what could be" (alternative possibility). In pretense, the banana IS a phone despite being a banana. In counterfactual reasoning, B IS the zando despite actually being inert. Both require decoupling representations from reality while tracking both models.

The evidence favors shared architecture over mere correlation. The EF-controlled correlation (Buchsbaum), the frame-dependent competence (Harris), and the common representational requirement (dual-model) converge on a unified counterfactual cognition system underlying both capacities.

Evaluate the evidence for executive function as a unitary construct versus separable components.
Required Findings

Sample Answer (~275 words)

Evidence supports a "unity and diversity" model: executive functions share common variance while also showing component-specific contributions.

Miyake et al.'s influential framework identifies three core components: inhibition (suppressing prepotent responses), working memory (maintaining and manipulating information), and cognitive flexibility (switching between rules or perspectives). Factor analyses consistently show these components are moderately correlated, suggesting shared underlying processes, yet maintain separable variance that predicts distinct outcomes.

Evidence for unity comes from developmental synchrony: inhibition (Day-Night Stroop: 70-90% errors ‚Üí 10-20%), working memory (span tasks), and flexibility (DCCS: 0% ‚Üí 85-95%) all show dramatic improvement between ages 3-5. This coordinated development suggests a common maturational process, likely tied to prefrontal cortex development (largest volume increase ages 7-11).

Evidence for diversity comes from the hot/cool distinction. Cool EF (abstract, decontextualized reasoning) depends on DLPFC and matures earlier. Hot EF (emotion-laden, reward-related decisions) depends on VMPFC and shows protracted development into the mid-20s. This neuroanatomical dissociation explains why adolescents show adult-equivalent abstract reasoning but elevated real-world risk-taking.

The resolution is that EF components share a common executive attention system (explaining unity) while depending on partially distinct neural circuits and developing along different trajectories (explaining diversity). Neither purely unitary nor purely separable models capture this complexity.

Relational learning follows the same hierarchical principles from infancy through adulthood, with only capacity thresholds changing‚Äîdemonstrating developmental continuity rather than qualitative stage transitions.

Halford's relational complexity theory provides the unifying mechanism. Working memory capacity increases from C‚âà1 (3 years) to C‚âà2 (5 years) to C‚âà4 (adults), determining how many relations can be processed in parallel. This trajectory supports neoconstructivism: continuous capacity growth within constant hierarchical architecture, not discrete stages or maturing modules.
