The Natural Partitions Hypothesis (Gentner, 1982) provides the theoretical foundation for understanding why relational learning is fundamentally more demanding than object learning and why this capacity is distinctively human. The core insight is that objects and relations occupy different levels in a processing hierarchy: objects are perceptually stable, cohesive, persistent across viewpoints, and map directly onto perceptual input, making them cognitively "easy" and accessible to virtually all animals with visual systems. Relations, by contrast, are dynamic, context-dependent, and transient—the spatial relation "to the left of" changes as you move, the causal relation "A causes B" requires integrating temporal succession with spatial contact—and crucially, relations can only be represented by abstracting a common pattern across multiple instances involving different objects. This explains why the identity relation "same" is so theoretically important: recognizing that two blue squares and two red circles both instantiate "sameness" requires representing the relation independently of any specific perceptual features, since the two pairs share literally zero perceptual overlap yet are equivalent at the relational level. The evidence for human uniqueness in abstract relational matching is striking—even adult chimpanzees require extensive symbol training before they can match stimuli by sameness/difference relations, whereas as we will see, 3-month-old human infants demonstrate this capacity under optimal conditions. The asymmetry also appears in language acquisition: nouns are learned earlier than verbs/prepositions cross-linguistically, and noun vocabularies are larger and more consistent across languages, supporting the claim that object concepts are more stable reference points than relational concepts. For exam purposes, recognize that this framework establishes the critical theoretical claim that relations are built on top of objects in a cognitive hierarchy—you must first encode "what are these things" before you can encode "how are they related"—which becomes the foundation for understanding cognitive overload effects when object processing consumes all available capacity.

Ferry, Hespos & Gentner (2015) established the foundational paradigm for testing same/different relational abstraction in 7-9 month-old infants and produced the critical findings that inform our understanding of capacity-dependent relational learning. The experimental logic is straightforward but theoretically powerful: familiarize infants to pairs of objects all instantiating one relation (e.g., "same": two goldfish, two crocodiles, two pigs), then test whether they generalize to completely novel objects in the familiar relation (two elephants) versus the novel relation (elephant and giraffe). If infants have abstracted the relation independently of specific objects, they should dishabituate to the novel relation but not to novel objects in the familiar relation—and this is precisely what 7-9 month-olds demonstrated with 4 diverse exemplar pairs. The failure condition with 1 pair repeated is theoretically important: presenting the same two goldfish over and over provides insufficient variability to force abstraction, allowing infants to encode "two-goldfish-together" rather than "two-things-that-are-the-same," demonstrating that variability across exemplars is essential for extracting the common relational structure. However, the most theoretically diagnostic finding is the object experience effect: when infants had played with objects in the waiting room but those objects were NOT included in the habituation phase, they failed to show relational discrimination at test—even though they succeeded with completely novel objects and with objects they both played with AND habituated to. This pattern reveals that mere familiarity without habituation-based encoding actually INCREASES object salience rather than reducing it, because the unexpected re-encounter triggers recognition ("oh, it's those toys I played with!") that focuses attention on object identity rather than relation. The critical insight for exam purposes is that familiarity ≠ reduced salience—only habituation completes object encoding and reduces salience, freeing capacity for higher-level relational processing. This supports the neoconstructivist principle that relations are hierarchically dependent on objects: when object-level processing is incomplete or attention-grabbing, relational processing becomes inaccessible.

Anderson et al. (2018) pushed the boundary of relational learning to the youngest testable age—3 months—demonstrating that the capacity for abstract same/different learning exists far earlier than previously believed, while simultaneously confirming the capacity-dependent nature of this ability. The critical comparison involves exemplar count: with 6 diverse pairs during habituation, 3-month-olds showed no discrimination between same and different at test—the cognitive load of tracking 12 individual objects while attempting to extract the common relation exceeded their processing capacity, causing system overload and failure at the relational level. However, when the task was simplified to 2 diverse pairs, these same infants successfully discriminated same from different, including with completely novel objects at test, proving genuine relational abstraction independent of specific object identities. This finding has profound theoretical implications: 3-month-old human infants demonstrate more sensitivity to abstract identity relations than adult chimpanzees, who require extensive symbol training and elaborate procedures before they can match stimuli by sameness versus difference. The human capacity for relational thought appears to be present from the earliest measurable ages, though its expression is entirely dependent on cognitive load remaining within age-specific thresholds. The replication of the object experience effect at 3 months—familiar-but-not-habituated objects produce failure even with optimal 2 pairs—confirms that the salience interference mechanism is universal across development, not age-specific. For exam purposes, the Anderson et al. findings establish two critical points: first, that the exemplar count function is non-monotonic (not "more is better" but "optimal is specific to age-dependent capacity"); second, that early competence does NOT mean robust competence—the same infant who succeeds with 2 pairs fails with 6 pairs, demonstrating the fragile, capacity-dependent nature of early relational cognition that neoconstructivism predicts but nativist modularity cannot explain.

The causal perception literature provides the most decisive empirical evidence distinguishing nativist modularity from neoconstructivist hierarchical construction, because it demonstrates systematic task-dependent variation that modules cannot explain. Leslie (1984) found that 6-month-olds habituated to causal launching events (ball A contacts ball B, B immediately moves) dishabituated to BOTH types of non-causal events (temporal delay; spatial gap) and generalized habituation across non-causal types—if habituated to delay, they treated gap as familiar. This pattern indicates categorization by causal STATUS rather than by specific spatiotemporal features, which Leslie interpreted as evidence for an innate causal perception module triggered by the spatiotemporal signature of launching (contact + immediate motion). However, Oakes & Cohen (1990) challenged this interpretation by changing ONLY object complexity while holding spatiotemporal cues constant: with complex realistic toys (dinosaurs, airplanes) instead of simple geometric shapes, 6-month-olds failed to show causal categorization, processing events by features (discriminating delay from gap) rather than by causal status. This is devastating for nativism: if a causal module exists and is triggered by specific spatiotemporal input, it should fire regardless of what objects are undergoing the interaction—the module shouldn't "care" whether it's squares or dinosaurs. Yet 6-month-olds succeeded with simple shapes and failed with complex toys. The constructivist explanation is straightforward: complex objects consume processing capacity for feature encoding, leaving insufficient resources for the higher-level causal abstraction. By 10 months, increased capacity allowed causal perception even with complex objects—but Cohen & Oakes (1993) showed that even 10-month-olds reverted to featural processing when complex objects VARIED trial-by-trial (different toys in each event). This is the critical test: same age, same complexity level, same relational structure, only variability changed—and processing level shifts from causal to featural. Nativism has no mechanism for this; the module should fire whenever triggering input is present. Neoconstructivism explains it perfectly: object variability requires encoding multiple identities across trials, which consumes capacity and leaves insufficient resources for causal relation extraction.

Cognitive Overload: The Signature Neoconstructivist Pattern

Load Factor Low Load → RELATIONAL High Load → FEATURAL Mechanism
Object Complexity Simple geometric shapes → 6mo causal perception (Leslie) Complex realistic toys → 6mo featural only (Oakes & Cohen) Complex objects require more encoding resources, fewer left for relation
Object Variability Same objects repeated → 10mo causal (Oakes & Cohen) Different objects each trial → 10mo featural (Cohen & Oakes) Variability requires tracking multiple identities across trials

Object Salience Novel objects OR habituated familiar → relational success Familiar-but-NOT-habituated → failure despite optimal exemplars Unexpected re-encounter increases salience, blocks relational processing

The cognitive overload pattern is the signature behavioral prediction that uniquely identifies neoconstructivism and distinguishes it from nativist modularity: increasing cognitive load lowers the information processing level, causing the same infant at the same age to succeed with low-load stimuli but fail with high-load stimuli even when the relational structure being tested is held constant. This pattern appears consistently across both same/different learning and causal perception paradigms, with load manipulated through multiple independent dimensions: object complexity (simple shapes vs. complex toys), object variability (repeated vs. changing objects across trials), exemplar count (2 pairs vs. 6 pairs for same/different), and object salience (novel/habituated vs. familiar-but-not-habituated). In every case, the pattern is identical: when lower-level object processing is simple, automatic, or complete, cognitive resources remain available for higher-level relational processing; when object processing is complex, demanding, or incomplete, it consumes available capacity and relational processing becomes inaccessible. The theoretical significance cannot be overstated: nativist accounts predict that if the triggering input for a module is present, the module should fire regardless of task variations—there is no mechanism in modularity theory for why Leslie's 6-month-olds succeed with simple shapes but Oakes & Cohen's 6-month-olds fail with complex toys (same spatiotemporal launching cues), or why 10-month-olds show causal perception with repeated objects but revert to featural processing with variable objects. Neoconstructivism explains all these patterns with a single principle: relations are hierarchically built on top of objects through statistical learning, and when object-level processing exceeds available capacity, the higher-level relational representation cannot be constructed or accessed. The formula is simple: Relational success = f(Capacity_age − Load_cognitive), where cognitive load is the sum of object complexity, variability, exemplar count, and salience.

The empirical evidence from same/different learning and causal perception provides a decisive test between nativist modularity and neoconstructivist hierarchical construction, with the data clearly favoring neoconstructivism. The critical empirical pattern is task-dependent variation in processing level: the same infant at the same age succeeds with low-load stimuli but fails with high-load stimuli, even when the relational structure being tested is held constant. Leslie (1984) found causal perception in 6-month-olds with simple shapes; Oakes & Cohen (1990) found failure in 6-month-olds with complex objects but success in 10-month-olds with the same objects; Cohen & Oakes (1993) found that even 10-month-olds reverted to featural processing when object variability increased. Similarly, Ferry et al. (2015) found same/different learning in 7-9 month-olds with 4 exemplar pairs but failure with 1 pair or with salient un-habituated objects; Anderson et al. (2018) found success in 3-month-olds with 2 pairs but failure with 6 pairs. Nativism cannot explain these graded, load-dependent patterns: if modules exist and are triggered by specific perceptual input, they should fire consistently whenever that input is present, regardless of object complexity, variability, or salience—there is simply no mechanism in modularity theory for task-dependent variation when the triggering input is held constant. Neoconstructivism provides a complete and unified explanation: higher-level relational representations are constructed from lower-level object representations through statistical learning, and this construction requires available processing capacity after object-level encoding is complete. When object processing is simple (geometric shapes, repeated objects, habituated objects), capacity remains available for relational abstraction; when object processing is demanding (complex toys, variable objects, salient un-habituated objects), it consumes available capacity and relational processing becomes inaccessible. This is not a limitation of the system but an adaptive feature: the infant cognitive system is one unified, domain-general statistical learning system that flexibly adjusts processing level based on available resources.

Across same/different learning and causal perception, a unified developmental pattern emerges that confirms the neoconstructivist framework: relational abstraction capacity exists from the earliest testable ages, but its expression is entirely contingent on cognitive load remaining below age-specific thresholds. Anderson et al. (2018) demonstrated that 3-month-old human infants—more sensitive to identity relations than adult chimpanzees—can abstract same/different with 2 exemplar pairs; Leslie (1984) showed that 6-month-olds perceive causality in launching events with simple geometric shapes; Ferry et al. (2015) established that 7-9 month-olds succeed with 4 diverse pairs; Oakes & Cohen (1990) found that 10-month-olds can handle complex objects. But in every case, increasing load—whether through object complexity, variability, exemplar count, or salience—causes systematic reversion to lower-level featural processing. The pattern is not all-or-nothing based on age-locked module maturation but graded and task-dependent, exactly as hierarchical construction predicts. The infant cognitive system is revealed to be one unified, domain-general statistical learning system rather than a collection of independent domain-specific modules: objects are encoded first (lower level), relations are built from patterns across objects (higher level), and when object-level processing consumes available capacity, higher-level relational processing becomes inaccessible. For exam purposes, the signature prediction is: increasing cognitive load lowers information processing level. If you see task-dependent variation where the same relational structure produces success or failure depending on object properties, this supports neoconstructivism; if you see robust, consistent relational processing regardless of object properties once a certain age is reached, this would support nativism—but the empirical evidence consistently shows the constructivist pattern. The critical theoretical insight is that early competence does not mean robust competence: infants have relational capacity from 3 months, but this capacity is fragile, hierarchically dependent on object processing, and subject to overload—a profile that nativism cannot accommodate but neoconstructivism naturally predicts.
