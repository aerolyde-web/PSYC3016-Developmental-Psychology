VERBATIM SAQ PARAGRAPH: Domain Architecture

The nativism-constructivism debate is NOT "nature versus nurture"‚Äîboth acknowledge genes and learning matter‚Äîbut concerns whether learning mechanisms are domain-specific (triggered modules) or domain-general (hierarchical statistical construction). Nativist evidence comes from Baillargeon's (1987) drawbridge paradigm, where 4.5-month-olds look longer at physically impossible events (d ‚âà 0.7-0.9), and Kellman & Spelke's (1983) object unity study, where 4-month-olds perceive unity with common motion (d = 0.8) but not stationary displays‚Äîsuggesting specific perceptual triggers activate innate modules. However, constructivist critiques challenge this interpretation: Johnson & Aslin (1998) found gradual development (2-month-olds need ~70% visible vs 4-month-olds needing only ~40%), and Oakes & Cohen (1990) demonstrated the decisive load manipulation‚Äî6-month-olds perceive causality with simple shapes but fail with complex toys despite identical spatiotemporal cues. This load-dependent variation is the signature of hierarchical construction: when the same infant succeeds and fails depending on cognitive load while triggering input remains constant, domain-specific modules cannot explain the pattern‚Äîonly capacity-limited hierarchical processing can.

VERBATIM SAQ PARAGRAPH: Core Knowledge

Core knowledge theory proposes that infants possess innate representational primitives‚Äîdomain-specific expectations about objects, number, and agents‚Äîthat constrain learning from birth. Spelke's core principles include persistence (objects exist when occluded), solidity (cannot interpenetrate), and unity (common motion = one object), all evident by 4-4.5 months in violation-of-expectation paradigms. Carey's number systems analysis identifies two innate mechanisms: System 1 (subitizing) provides precise small-set representation (1-3 items, >95% accuracy), while System 2 (analog magnitude) enables approximate large-quantity discrimination following Weber's Law (~30% threshold). Critically, neither system supports precise large-number representation‚Äîthere is no innate way to represent "exactly 47." The Pirah√£, who lack number words beyond ~3, have intact subitizing and analog magnitude but cannot perform exact arithmetic beyond the subitizing range‚Äîdemonstrating that number language is NECESSARY, not merely helpful, for bootstrapping beyond core knowledge to mature mathematical cognition. For Theory of Mind precursors, goal understanding emerges by 6 months, triadic joint attention by 9-15 months, and perception-knowledge tracking by 12-15 months‚Äîfollowing the counterintuitive pattern where understanding OTHERS precedes understanding SELF.

VERBATIM SAQ PARAGRAPH: Hierarchical Construction

Neoconstructivism proposes that representations are built hierarchically‚Äîfeatures before correlations, correlations before relations‚Äîwith each level requiring completion of the previous level before the next becomes accessible. Younger & Cohen (1983) demonstrated this sequence: 4- and 7-month-olds encode individual features but treat uncorrelated test items as familiar, while 10-month-olds show surprise at uncorrelated patterns (U=12s vs C=5s, p < .01)‚Äîproving they learned the statistical structure. Crucially, when task difficulty is reduced, 7-month-olds succeed (Younger & Cohen, 1986), demonstrating capacity threshold rather than absolute inability. The Goldilocks Principle reveals that optimal variability is required for abstraction: Ferry et al. (2015) showed 7-9 month-olds fail with 1 pair (encode object identity) but succeed with 4 diverse pairs (force relational abstraction), while Anderson et al. (2018) found 3-month-olds succeed with 2 pairs but fail with 6 (capacity overload). The object experience effect provides decisive evidence: familiar-but-not-habituated objects block relational learning despite optimal exemplar counts‚Äîsalience without encoding consumes the cognitive resources needed for hierarchical construction. This pattern is the signature of constructivism: the same infant succeeds or fails depending on load, variability, and salience‚Äînot on the presence or absence of triggering input.

FULL-LENGTH SAQ: "How is cognitive knowledge architected in infancy?"

Infant cognitive architecture reflects domain-general hierarchical construction constrained by capacity limits, rather than domain-specific modules triggered by specific inputs‚Äîas evidenced by load-dependent performance variation, the Goldilocks Principle of optimal variability, and systematic developmental progressions from features to correlations to relations.

Domain Architecture (A1): Baillargeon's (1987) VoE paradigm suggests core knowledge of object permanence by 4.5 months (d ‚âà 0.7-0.9), and Kellman & Spelke (1983) demonstrate object unity with common motion at 4 months (d = 0.8). However, constructivist critiques reveal the decisive test: Oakes & Cohen (1990) found 6-month-olds perceive causality with simple shapes but fail with complex toys despite identical spatiotemporal cues. If triggering input is constant but performance varies with load, module theory fails‚Äîhierarchy wins.

Core Knowledge (A2): Spelke's principles (persistence, solidity, unity) and Carey's number systems (subitizing for 1-3, analog magnitude for approximate large quantities) provide representational starting points. Yet neither number system supports precise large-number representation‚Äîthe Pirah√£, lacking number words, cannot perform exact arithmetic beyond 1-3 despite intact core systems. Core knowledge constrains but does not determine; language provides the symbolic structure enabling qualitative conceptual shifts.

Hierarchical Levels (A3): Younger & Cohen (1983) established the features‚Üícorrelations‚Üírelations sequence (4/7mo: features only; 10mo: correlations), and Ferry et al.
