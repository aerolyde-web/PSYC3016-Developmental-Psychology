SAQ 1 L22: Phonological Development
Explain perceptual narrowing in phonological development. Why is the loss of sensitivity to non-native phonetic contrasts considered adaptive rather than a cognitive decline?

Key Points to Include

  • Universal phase (0-6 months): Infants discriminate ?all phonetic contrasts across world languages, whether or not they're meaningful in their native language
  • Narrowing (6-10 months): Sensitivity to ?non-native contrasts is lost while native contrasts are maintained/enhanced
  • Why adaptive: Collapsing irrelevant acoustic variation frees ?cognitive resources for mapping sound sequences to meanings rather than tracking meaningless distinctions
  • NOT cognitive loss: It's ?learned categorical boundary reorganization optimized for environmental statistics
Key Evidence:
Werker studies: English-learning 6-month-olds discriminate Hindi retroflex /ɖ/ vs dental /d̪/. By ?10 months, they no longer discriminate this contrast.
Bilinguals: Infants in bilingual environments ?maintain sensitivity to contrasts in BOTH languages - not developmental delay but appropriate environmental tuning.

Model Answer Structure

Opening: Perceptual narrowing refers to the developmental transition from universal phonemic discrimination (0-6 months) to language-specific perceptual tuning (by 10 months), representing adaptive specialization rather than cognitive decline.

Universal phase: During the first six months, infants exhibit categorical perception across all world languages - they discriminate phonemic contrasts whether those contrasts are meaningful in their native language or not. For instance, English-learning 6-month-olds discriminate the Hindi retroflex /ɖ/ from dental /d̪/ contrast, and Japanese-learning infants discriminate English /r/ from /l/.

The narrowing shift: By 10 months, before producing first words, infants lose sensitivity to non-native contrasts while maintaining discrimination of native-language phonemes. Japanese 10-month-olds no longer discriminate /r/ vs /l/; English infants no longer discriminate Hindi dental vs retroflex stops.

Why adaptive: This is not cognitive regression but learned attention allocation driven by distributional statistics in ambient language input. By collapsing irrelevant acoustic variation into functional phonemic categories, the perceptual system frees cognitive resources for the crucial task of mapping sound sequences onto meanings. Tracking meaningless acoustic distinctions would create noise in word learning. Evidence for adaptiveness comes from bilinguals, who maintain sensitivity to contrasts in both languages because both are statistically frequent and functionally relevant - showing the system tunes appropriately to environmental demands.

Marking criteria: Universal phase description (2 marks) + Narrowing timing/mechanism (2 marks) + Adaptive explanation (2 marks) + Evidence (2 marks)
SAQ 2 L22: Word Learning
Explain how children solve Quine's "gavagai problem" (referential ambiguity) using mutual exclusivity and pedagogical sampling. How do these mechanisms differ in what they solve?

Key Points to Include

  • Gavagai problem: When a speaker labels something, infinite logically possible referents exist - word learning cannot be ?pure association because children must constrain the hypothesis space
  • Mutual exclusivity solves: ?WHICH object is the referent when multiple candidates exist (novel word maps to object lacking known label)
  • Pedagogical sampling solves: ?WHAT level of abstraction the label targets (subordinate vs basic vs superordinate category)
  • Underlying assumption: Both rely on ?pragmatic inference - speakers are helpful communicators who choose informative examples
Mutual Exclusivity (Markman):
Fork + garlic press shown; "Hand me the dax" - ?95% of 3-year-olds select the unfamiliar object.
Pedagogical Sampling Logic:
3 Dalmatians labeled "dax" → Child infers: ?subordinate category (Dalmatian-like)
3 different dog breeds labeled "dax" → Child infers: ?basic level (dog)
Dog + cat + bird labeled "dax" → Child infers: ?superordinate (animal)

Model Answer Structure

Opening: Quine's "gavagai problem" highlights that when a speaker labels something (e.g., pointing at a rabbit and saying "gavagai"), infinite logically possible referents exist - rabbit, ears, hopping, whiteness, even "undetached rabbit parts." Children solve this through pragmatic constraints that assume speakers are helpful communicators.

Mutual exclusivity: This mechanism solves WHICH object is the referent when multiple candidates are present. The inference: if I already know "fork" refers to that object, and the speaker says "dax," they must mean the other object - otherwise they would have used "fork." Evidence: 95% of 3-year-olds shown a fork and unfamiliar garlic press select the garlic press when asked for "the dax." This isn't logical necessity (objects can have multiple names) but pragmatic inference about speaker intention.

Pedagogical sampling: This mechanism solves WHAT level of abstraction the label targets. Children reason about example selection: if shown three Dalmatians labeled "dax," they infer subordinate category (if the speaker meant "dog," why choose such similar examples?). If shown Dalmatian + poodle + bulldog, they infer basic level (dog). If shown dog + cat + bird, they infer superordinate (animal). The logic: level of generality is proportional to variation within examples.

How they differ: Mutual exclusivity operates on object selection in a choice array (contrastive inference). Pedagogical sampling operates on category breadth interpretation (diversity inference). Both assume speakers choose examples to be maximally informative about intended meaning.

Marking criteria: Gavagai problem explanation (2 marks) + Mutual exclusivity mechanism + evidence (2 marks) + Pedagogical sampling mechanism + examples (2 marks) + How they differ (2 marks)
SAQ 3 L22: Morphosyntax
Explain U-shaped development in morphology using the example of past tense learning. Why do overgeneralization errors like "goed" and "tooths" actually demonstrate sophisticated learning rather than failure?

Key Points to Include

  • Phase 1 (correct): Young children correctly produce irregular forms like "went" and "feet" through ?imitation/rote memorization
  • Phase 2 (errors): Upon discovering the regular rule, children ?overgeneralize, producing "goed" and "tooths" by applying VERB+-ed and NOUN+-s to irregulars
  • Phase 3 (correct): Children re-learn that some forms are ?lexical exceptions requiring memorization while maintaining the productive rule for regular forms
  • Why errors are sophisticated: "Goed" shows the child has ?extracted an abstract rule that applies to 99% of English verbs - rule extraction is cognitively advanced, not failure
Wug Test (Berko, 1958):
Shows novel creature: "This is a wug. Now there are two of them. There are two ___?"
Children produce: ?"wugs" (/wʌgz/)
This demonstrates ?productive rule application - they've NEVER heard "wugs" before, so cannot be retrieving stored form. Children also select correct allomorph (/-z/ after voiced consonant, not /-s/ or /-ɪz/).

Model Answer Structure

Opening: U-shaped development describes a trajectory where children produce correct forms, then appear to regress to incorrect forms, before returning to correct production - with the "dip" actually representing cognitive advancement.

Phase 1 - Correct through imitation: Young children correctly produce irregular forms like "went," "feet," "teeth" through rote memorization of stored exemplars. This correct production does not demonstrate grammatical rule knowledge.

Phase 2 - Errors through rule discovery: Upon extracting the productive morphological rules (VERB + -ed for past tense; NOUN + -s for plural), children overgeneralize these rules to irregular exceptions, producing "goed," "tooths," "foots." These errors are diagnostically significant - the child has prioritized the productive rule (which applies to 99% of English verbs/nouns) over memorized exceptions.

Phase 3 - Dual-route mastery: Children eventually re-learn that certain forms are lexical exceptions requiring storage (went, feet) while maintaining the productive default rule for regular forms. This requires maintaining BOTH a productive rule AND lexically-specified exceptions - a dual-route model of morphology.

Why errors are sophisticated: The wug test demonstrates productivity: children who have never heard "wug" reliably produce "wugs," applying both the morphological rule (add plural marker) and the phonological rule (select correct allomorph /-z/ after voiced consonant). "Tooths" is cognitively sophisticated because it shows abstract rule extraction from finite input - the child has generalized beyond their input. Calling this "failure" misunderstands development: rote correct forms require less sophisticated knowledge than productive but temporarily incorrect overgeneralizations.

Marking criteria: Three phases described (3 marks) + Why errors are advanced (2 marks) + Wug test explanation (2 marks) + Dual-route conclusion (1 mark)
SAQ 4 L23-24: Constructionist
Explain how infants use transitional probabilities for speech segmentation (Saffran, Aslin & Newport, 1996). What does this evidence suggest about the role of domain-general vs language-specific learning mechanisms?

Key Points to Include

  • The problem: Fluent speech contains ?no consistent silence marking word boundaries - pauses occur within words as often as between
  • Transitional probability: The ?conditional probability that syllable B follows syllable A (P(B|A))
  • Key insight: Within words, TPs are ?high (1.0); across word boundaries, TPs ?drop (0.33)
  • Saffran finding: After just 2 minutes exposure, 8-month-olds ?discriminated "words" from "non-words" based purely on TP dips at boundaries
Implications for Nativist-Constructionist Debate:
This evidence supports constructionism because: ?word segmentation, previously thought to require language-specific innate knowledge, can be solved by domain-general statistical learning mechanisms
Caveat: This demonstrates statistical learning works for ?segmentation specifically - does not prove it suffices for all aspects of syntax (recursion, structural dependence)

Model Answer Structure

Opening: Saffran, Aslin & Newport (1996) demonstrated that 8-month-old infants can segment continuous speech using transitional probabilities - the statistical regularities in syllable sequences - providing evidence for domain-general learning mechanisms in language acquisition.

The segmentation problem: Fluent adult speech contains no consistent silence marking word boundaries; acoustic analysis shows pauses occur within words as often as between them. Infants must parse continuous streams like /ðəbeɪbiɪzslipɪŋ/ into discrete words without knowing what words exist.

Transitional probability solution: Within words, syllable pairs have high conditional probability (in "bidaku," after "bi," "da" always followed, TP=1.0). Across word boundaries, probabilities drop (after "ku," any of three possible syllables could follow, TP=0.33). Infants can use these statistical regularities to infer word boundaries.

The study: Infants heard 2 minutes of synthesized speech concatenating pseudowords (bidaku-padoti-golabu...) with no pauses. At test, infants preferred listening to novel "non-words" (syllable sequences spanning boundaries: kupado) over "words" (bidaku), demonstrating they had extracted word units purely from TP dips.

Theoretical implications: This evidence supports the constructionist position that domain-general statistical learning mechanisms can solve linguistic problems previously attributed to language-specific innate constraints. The same mechanisms extract patterns from visual and tonal sequences, suggesting nothing language-specific is required for segmentation. However, this does not prove statistical learning suffices for all syntax - structural dependence and recursion may require additional mechanisms.

Marking criteria: Segmentation problem (1 mark) + TP mechanism (2 marks) + Study design + finding (2 marks) + Theoretical implication + caveat (3 marks)
SAQ 5 L23-24: Nativist
Explain structural dependence in question formation and why Crain & Nakayama (1987) considered it "a parade case" for innate constraints. What counterevidence did Ambridge et al. (2008) provide?

Key Points to Include

  • Structural dependence: Syntactic operations reference ?hierarchical phrase structure, not linear word order
  • The example: "The boy who is smoking is crazy" → Question requires moving the ?main clause auxiliary ("is crazy"), not the first auxiliary ("is smoking")
  • Crain & Nakayama argument: Children never make linear errors despite ?no negative evidence (no one produces ungrammatical sentences to teach what's prohibited)
  • Poverty of stimulus: If children learned from statistics alone, they should ?hypothesize the simpler linear rule first, make errors, then correct - but they don't
Crain & Nakayama (1987): 3-5 year-olds produced ?600+ questions with ZERO structural dependence errors - taken as evidence for innate UG constraint.

Ambridge et al. (2008) Counterevidence:
"is" questions: ?0% errors (because "who smoking" never occurs - TP ≈ 0)
"can" questions: ?7% errors (because "who smoke" IS grammatical elsewhere)
This shows errors depend on ?word-level statistics, not universal innate constraints.

Model Answer Structure

Opening: Structural dependence is the principle that syntactic operations reference hierarchical phrase structure, not linear word order - a central claim of Universal Grammar theory.

The test case: Consider "The boy who is smoking is crazy." To form a question, the rule is NOT "move the first auxiliary" (which would yield ungrammatical "*Is the boy who smoking is crazy?") but "move the auxiliary in the main clause VP" (yielding correct "Is the boy who is smoking crazy?"). Children must distinguish main-clause from embedded-clause auxiliaries based on hierarchical structure.

Crain & Nakayama's argument: They called this "a parade case of an innate constraint" because children never produce linear-order-based errors despite never receiving negative evidence - no one produces ungrammatical sentences to teach what's prohibited. Their study found zero structural dependence violations across 600+ questions from 3-5 year-olds. If children learned purely from input statistics, they should initially hypothesize the simpler linear rule, make errors, then correct based on feedback - but this doesn't happen.

Ambridge et al. counterevidence: They found word-specific variation that challenges UG: 0% errors with "is" questions (because "who smoking" has TP ≈ 0 - it never occurs in English) but 7% errors with "can" questions (because "who smoke" IS grammatical in other contexts, e.g., "people who smoke"). This pattern suggests errors depend on transitional probabilities between specific words, not violations of universal innate constraints. A truly innate structural dependence constraint should not show word-specific variation.

Marking criteria: Structural dependence definition (2 marks) + Crain & Nakayama evidence + interpretation (2 marks) + Poverty of stimulus argument (2 marks) + Ambridge counterevidence with numbers (2 marks)
SAQ 6 L23-24: Nativist
Describe the emergence of Nicaraguan Sign Language (NSL) and explain why nativists consider it evidence for innate language constraints and sensitive periods. What alternative explanation do constructionists offer?

Key Points to Include

  • Context: 1980s Nicaragua - deaf children with no prior linguistic community brought together in schools; first cohort invented ?home signs lacking full compositionality
  • Critical finding: Second cohort children systematically ?regularized and complexified the language, introducing componential structure absent in input
  • Componentiality example: Motion events - Cohort 1 produced ?simultaneous manner+path gestures (25%); Cohort 2 produced ?sequential separate gestures (75%)
  • Sensitive period evidence: Cohort 1 adults ?past their critical window could not generate these structures despite being the children's primary models
Nativist Interpretation:
Children's UG ?imposed linguistic structure (compositionality, hierarchy) on deficient input during sensitive period.

Constructionist Alternative:
?Domain-general cognitive biases for segmentation and categorization, combined with iterated learning, optimize communicative systems for learnability - no language-specific UG required

Model Answer Structure

Opening: Nicaraguan Sign Language (NSL) emerged in the 1980s when deaf children were brought together for the first time, creating a natural experiment in language creation that nativists consider powerful evidence for innate constraints and sensitive periods.

The emergence: The first cohort of signers, having had no prior linguistic community, invented home signs - gestural communication with some word-like units but lacking full grammatical structure. The second cohort, children exposed to first-cohort signing during their sensitive period, systematically regularized and complexified the emerging language.

Componential structure evidence (Senghas, Kita & Ozyurek, 2004): For motion events like "ball rolling down," Spanish speakers produce simultaneous manner+path gestures (65% simultaneous). NSL Cohort 1 showed 25% sequential/componential expressions. NSL Cohort 2 showed 75% componential - they separated manner and path into sequential independent components, mirroring established sign languages. This componentiality was absent in Cohort 1's signing that Cohort 2 learned from.

Nativist interpretation: Children generated language exceeding their input, imposing UG-specified structure (compositionality, hierarchy) during the sensitive period. Cohort 1 adults, past their critical window, could not generate these structures themselves - demonstrating language-specific maturational constraints.

Constructionist alternative: Domain-general cognitive biases for segmentation and categorization (also seen in visual/motor learning) plus iterated learning through cultural transmission could produce increasing systematicity without UG. Each generation optimizes for learnability; children's segmentation biases are general, not language-specific. The critical test: whether similar "generation beyond input" occurs in non-linguistic domains.

Marking criteria: NSL context (1 mark) + Cohort comparison with numbers (2 marks) + Componentiality explanation (2 marks) + Nativist interpretation (2 marks) + Constructionist alternative (1 mark)
SAQ 7 L23-24: Constructionist
Explain Tomasello's gradual abstraction account of syntactic development, including verb islands. How do Fisher et al.'s findings challenge this account, and how did Dittmar et al. respond?

Key Points to Include

  • Gradual abstraction: Early syntax is ?lexically specific (verb islands) that gradually abstract through exemplar comparison
  • Verb islands: Children initially learn "kick X," "throw Y" as ?separate schemas tied to specific verbs, not an abstract [AGENT] [ACTION] [PATIENT] rule
  • Evidence: Tomasello & Brooks (1998) - 2-year-olds taught "The sock is tamming" ?rarely produced "He's tamming the sock" (only 3/16), but 4-year-olds succeeded
  • Schematization mechanism: Abstract constructions emerge through ?analogical comparison across multiple verb-specific schemas
Fisher et al. Challenge:
21-month-olds interpreted novel verbs using transitive syntax (looked longer at agent-patient scenes for "The frog is gorping the bear") - suggests ?abstract verb-general knowledge present before verb islands dissolve

Dittmar et al. Response:
Fisher's warm-up used ?familiar verbs in transitive frames with the SAME nouns from test trials. When warm-up was replaced with generic descriptions (no transitive priming), ?children FAILED the task - suggesting exemplar-based retrieval, not abstract syntax.

Model Answer Structure

Opening: Tomasello's gradual abstraction account argues that children's earliest syntactic knowledge is lexically specific, not abstract, and gradually generalizes through exemplar comparison over development.

Verb islands: According to this account, children initially learn verb-specific schemas: "I kick [X]," "I hit [Y]" are separate representational units tied to specific verbs, not instances of an abstract [AGENT] [ACTION] [PATIENT] rule. Evidence: Tomasello & Brooks (1998) taught 2-year-olds "The sock is tamming" (intransitive) then tested transitive production ("Make Big Bird tam the sock"). Only 3/16 two-year-olds succeeded, versus ready success at age 4 - suggesting syntactic knowledge is initially verb-specific.

Schematization: Abstract constructions emerge through analogical comparison: detecting commonalities across verb-specific schemas ("I kick X," "I hit Y," "I throw Z") leads to extraction of the abstract relational structure [AGENT does ACTION to PATIENT].

Fisher et al. challenge: They showed 21-month-olds interpreted novel verbs using transitive syntax in a preferential looking task - suggesting abstract syntax is present earlier than Tomasello claims. Hearing "The frog is gorping the bear," children looked longer at scenes where the frog acted on the bear.

Dittmar et al. response: Fisher's warm-up primed transitive frames using the exact nouns appearing in test trials ("The frog is washing the bear"). When Dittmar et al. replaced this with generic descriptions avoiding transitive priming, 21-month-olds failed - they no longer showed agent-patient preferences. This suggests Fisher's results reflected exemplar-based retrieval (item-specific frames activated by priming) rather than abstract syntax. The debate remains unresolved: nativists claim priming merely "unlocks" pre-existing abstract knowledge; constructionists claim priming constitutes the basis for generalization.

Marking criteria: Gradual abstraction explanation (2 marks) + Verb islands + Tomasello evidence (2 marks) + Fisher challenge (2 marks) + Dittmar response (2 marks)
SAQ 8 L22: Language Creation
Explain the regularization phenomenon demonstrated by Hudson Kam & Newport (2005). How do children and adults differ in their treatment of inconsistent linguistic input, and what does this suggest about language creation?

Key Points to Include

  • Study design: Artificial language with determiner "po" appearing either ?100% (consistent) or 60% (inconsistent/probabilistic) before nouns
  • Children's pattern: In 60% condition, ~70% of children became ?systematic users - either always using or always omitting the determiner (regularized to categorical rule)
  • Adults' pattern: Adults tended to ?reproduce the input statistics, using the determiner ~60% of the time (matched probabilistic pattern)
  • Implication: Children are ?active structure-seekers who impose categorical rules on probabilistic input, not passive input-matchers
Link to Creole Genesis:
This lab result mirrors ?naturalistic creole genesis: when children are exposed to pidgins (variable, inconsistent adult L2 output), they regularize morphology and syntax, creating full creoles in one generation

Singleton & Newport (2004):
Deaf child whose hearing parents used sign morphology ?~70% correctly. Child's signing showed ?>80% correct morpheme use - EXCEEDING input quality by filtering noise and amplifying signal.

Model Answer Structure

Opening: Hudson Kam & Newport (2005) demonstrated that children and adults process inconsistent linguistic input differently - children regularize probabilistic patterns into categorical rules while adults tend to reproduce input variability.

The study: Participants learned a miniature language over multiple sessions. In the critical condition, a determiner ("po") appeared before nouns 60% of the time (probabilistic), not 100% (consistent). The question: would learners reproduce this variability or impose structure?

Children's regularization: Approximately 70% of 5-7-year-old children became "systematic users" - they either always used the determiner (grammaticalizing it as obligatory) or always omitted it (treating it as absent from the language), rather than reproducing the 60% probabilistic pattern. They imposed categorical structure on probabilistic input.

Adults' probability matching: Only about 50% of adults became systematic. Many reproduced the input statistics, using the determiner approximately 60% of the time - matching rather than regularizing the probabilistic pattern.

Implications for language creation: This demonstrates children are active structure-seekers, not passive absorbers. The same mechanism explains creole genesis: when children are exposed to pidgins (minimal grammars with inconsistent second-language output from adults), they regularize and systematize, creating full-fledged creoles in a single generation. Singleton & Newport (2004) documented this naturalistically: a deaf child whose hearing parents used sign morphology ~70% correctly produced >80% correct morphemes - exceeding input quality by filtering noise and amplifying signal. Children preferentially extract categorical rules even from noisy data.

Marking criteria: Study design (2 marks) + Children vs adult pattern with numbers (2 marks) + Regularization interpretation (2 marks) + Creole/Singleton connection (2 marks)
