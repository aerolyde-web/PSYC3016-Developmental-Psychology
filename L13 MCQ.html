Question 2
The term "doli incapax" in the juvenile justice context refers to:
An absolute bar to prosecution for children under 10
A rebuttable presumption that children aged 10-14 lack criminal capacity unless proven otherwise
A requirement that children must understand the legal consequences of their actions
A mandatory diversion program for first-time youth offenders
✓ Correct Answer: B - A rebuttable presumption that children aged 10-14 lack criminal capacity unless proven otherwise
Doli incapax (Latin: "incapable of wrong") applies specifically to ages 10-14. It creates a rebuttable presumption that the child lacks criminal capacity. The prosecution must prove beyond reasonable doubt that the child knew their conduct was "seriously wrong" (not merely naughty or mischievous) at the time of the offense. This is distinct from the minimum age threshold which is absolute.
Critical Distinction: Minimum age (10) = absolute bar below which NO prosecution possible. Doli incapax (10-14) = presumption that can be overcome with sufficient evidence showing moral understanding.
Why other options are incorrect:
A: The absolute bar applies to children under 10 (minimum age), not doli incapax which is a presumption that can be rebutted.
C: Doli incapax requires understanding that the act was MORALLY seriously wrong, not just understanding legal consequences. Knowing "it's illegal" is insufficient.
D: Doli incapax is a legal presumption about capacity, not a diversion program. Diversion is a separate system response.

Question 3
The 2016 High Court decision regarding doli incapax (RP v The Queen) had what effect on prosecution of 10-14 year-olds?
It abolished the doli incapax presumption entirely
It raised the evidentiary bar, making it harder to prove the child knew their act was seriously wrong
It lowered the minimum age of criminal responsibility to 8 years
It mandated automatic diversion for all first-time offenders aged 10-14
✓ Correct Answer: B - It raised the evidentiary bar, making it harder to prove the child knew their act was seriously wrong
The 2016 High Court decision in RP v The Queen clarified that prosecutors must prove the child understood the MORAL GRAVITY of their act, not just factual knowledge that it was prohibited. Evidence might include statements showing awareness ("I knew I'd go to jail"), concealment attempts, or prior police contact—but mere knowledge that something is "illegal" is insufficient. This raised bar has resulted in higher not-guilty rates for 10-14 year-olds, causing police frustration and pressure to raise the minimum age outright.
Exam Trap: Common error is conflating "knew it was illegal" (factual knowledge) with "knew it was gravely wrong" (moral culpability). Post-2016, only the latter suffices to rebut doli incapax.
Why other options are incorrect:
A: The decision strengthened (not abolished) doli incapax by clarifying its requirements and making it harder to rebut.
C: The decision had nothing to do with changing the minimum age threshold—it addressed evidentiary standards for 10-14 year-olds.
D: The High Court ruling addressed legal presumptions about capacity, not diversion policies which are determined by state governments and police.
